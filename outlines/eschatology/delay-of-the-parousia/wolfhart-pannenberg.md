# Wolfhart Pannenberg (1928–2014)

Wolfhart Pannenberg addressed the **delay of the Parousia** by integrating it into his broader theology of **history**, **eschatology**, and **revelation**. He argued that the **resurrection of Jesus** is the proleptic realization of the end, while the final consummation remains open, allowing for the ongoing unfolding of **God’s self-revelation** in history. For Pannenberg, the delay is not a theological embarrassment but a necessary aspect of the **historical process** in which the meaning of all reality is ultimately disclosed.

- "The delay of the parousia is not a failure of promise but the space in which the meaning of history is revealed." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Key Works

- Systematic Theology, Vol. 3
- Jesus—God and Man
- Theology and the Kingdom of God
- Revelation as History (editor and contributor)
- Basic Questions in Theology, Vol. 2

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Eschatology as the Horizon of History**
   - The **Parousia** is the final event that gives meaning to all preceding history.
   - The delay allows for the full unfolding of **God’s purposes** within time.
   - History is the arena in which **revelation** and **truth** are progressively disclosed.
   - The openness of the future is essential to the nature of eschatological hope.
   - "The future of God is the horizon in which all history finds its meaning." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

2. **Resurrection as Prolepsis of the End**
   - The resurrection of Jesus is the anticipatory realization of the eschaton.
   - It provides a **guarantee** of the final consummation despite the delay.
   - The resurrection is both a historical event and a sign of the future.
   - The church lives in the tension between the resurrection and the awaited Parousia.
   - "The resurrection is the proleptic event in which the future has already begun." (Jesus—God and Man)

3. **Revelation in and through History**
   - **Revelation** is not a static deposit but unfolds within historical processes.
   - The delay of the Parousia allows for the ongoing clarification of God’s self-disclosure.
   - Human history is the medium in which God’s ultimate purposes are made manifest.
   - The meaning of revelation is only fully clear from the standpoint of the end.
   - "Revelation is only complete when history itself is complete." (Revelation as History)

4. **Theological Openness and Provisionality**
   - The delay underscores the provisional character of all theological statements.
   - No doctrine is final until the eschatological consummation.
   - The church must remain open to correction and development in light of ongoing history.
   - Theological certainty is eschatological, not present.
   - "All theology is provisional until the final revelation at the end of history." (Basic Questions in Theology, Vol. 2)

5. **Universal Scope of Salvation History**
   - The delay of the Parousia allows for the inclusion of all peoples in salvation history.
   - God’s plan is universal, not limited to a single epoch or nation.
   - The openness of history provides space for the mission and witness of the church.
   - The final judgment will encompass all human history, not just the church.
   - "The universality of salvation history requires the openness of the future." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Legacy and Impact

Pannenberg’s interpretation of the **delay of the Parousia** has significantly shaped modern discussions of **eschatology** and the theology of history. His insistence on the openness of history and the provisionality of doctrine has encouraged a dynamic, historically engaged approach to systematic theology. By rooting eschatological hope in the resurrection and emphasizing the ongoing nature of **revelation**, Pannenberg provided a robust framework for understanding the church’s mission in the time between the times. His influence endures in both academic and ecclesial contexts, especially among those seeking to integrate **historical consciousness** with Christian faith.
