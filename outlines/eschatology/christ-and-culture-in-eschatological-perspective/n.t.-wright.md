# N. T. Wright (1948–)

N. T. Wright approaches the relationship of **Christ and culture** through the lens of **eschatology**, emphasizing the inaugurated yet not fully consummated **Kingdom of God**. He contends that Christian engagement with culture is shaped by the resurrection of Jesus, which signals the beginning of **new creation** and calls the church to embody **anticipatory signs** of God’s ultimate renewal of all things.

- "Jesus’ resurrection is the beginning of God’s new project not to snatch people away from earth to heaven but to colonize earth with the life of heaven." (Surprised by Hope)

## Key Works

- Surprised by Hope: Rethinking Heaven, the Resurrection, and the Mission of the Church
- Jesus and the Victory of God
- The Resurrection of the Son of God
- Simply Christian
- Paul and the Faithfulness of God

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Eschatology as New Creation, Not Escape**
    - Stresses that **resurrection** inaugurates the renewal of creation, not its abandonment.
    - Rejects a dualistic separation between **heaven and earth**.
    - Argues that Christian hope is for bodily resurrection within a restored cosmos.
    - Sees the church as participating in God’s project of cosmic restoration.
    - "God’s plan is not to abandon this world, but to renew it." (Surprised by Hope)

2. **Christ’s Lordship Over All Culture**
    - Affirms that Jesus is **Lord** over every sphere of life, not just the spiritual.
    - Calls Christians to bear witness to Christ’s reign in public and cultural arenas.
    - Critiques privatized or escapist forms of Christianity.
    - Encourages cultural engagement as a sign of the coming kingdom.
    - "The gospel of Jesus points us and indeed urges us to be at the leading edge of the whole culture, articulating in story and music and art and philosophy and education and politics and theology and even—heaven help us—biblical studies, a worldview that will mount the historically rooted Christian challenge to both modernity and postmodernity." (The Challenge of Jesus)

3. **The Already/Not Yet of the Kingdom**
    - Emphasizes the **inaugurated eschatology**: the kingdom is present but not complete.
    - Christians live in the tension between what is and what will be.
    - Cultural work is provisional but meaningful as it anticipates final redemption.
    - The church is called to be a foretaste of God’s future in the present.
    - "We are called to build for the kingdom, not just to build the kingdom." (Surprised by Hope)

4. **Mission as Anticipation of Eschatological Renewal**
    - Mission is grounded in the resurrection and the hope of **new creation**.
    - Social justice, beauty, and reconciliation are signs of God’s future breaking in.
    - The church’s vocation is to embody and proclaim the coming renewal.
    - Cultural transformation is part of the church’s witness to Christ’s lordship.
    - "Every act of love, gratitude, and kindness; every work of art or music inspired by the love of God and delight in the beauty of his creation... will find its way, through the resurrecting power of God, into the new creation that God will one day make." (Surprised by Hope)

5. **Critique of Dualism and Secularism**
    - Rejects both sacred/secular and nature/grace dichotomies.
    - Argues that all of life is under Christ’s redemptive claim.
    - Sees secularism as a distortion of the biblical vision for creation.
    - Calls for a holistic, integrated approach to faith and culture.
    - "The separation of religion from the rest of life is a modern heresy." (Simply Christian)

## Legacy and Impact

N. T. Wright has significantly shaped contemporary discussions on **eschatology** and the church’s cultural engagement, challenging both escapist and triumphalist paradigms. His vision of **new creation** as the telos of redemption has encouraged Christians to see cultural work as meaningful within God’s redemptive plan. Wright’s influence extends across denominational lines, prompting renewed interest in the resurrection and the public implications of the gospel. His work continues to inspire theological reflection on how the church lives as a sign of God’s coming kingdom in the midst of the world.
