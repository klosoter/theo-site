# John Calvin (1509–1564)

John Calvin approached the relationship of **Christ and culture** through the lens of **eschatology**, emphasizing the sovereignty of Christ over all creation and the ongoing transformation of the world in anticipation of the **consummation**. He maintained that while the present age is marked by sin and imperfection, believers are called to engage culture faithfully, seeking the **renewal of all things** under Christ's lordship, yet always with an eye toward the coming **kingdom of God**.

- "The whole world is a theater for the display of the divine goodness, wisdom, justice, and power." (Institutes of the Christian Religion)

## Key Works

- Institutes of the Christian Religion (1559)
- Commentary on Romans
- Commentary on 1 and 2 Corinthians
- Sermons on Ephesians
- Calvin: Institutes of the Christian Religion (ed. McNeill, Battles translation)
- Calvin’s Doctrine of the Christian Life (Ronald S. Wallace)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Christ’s Sovereignty Over Culture**
    - Christ rules over all spheres, including civil and cultural life.
    - No area of human endeavor is outside Christ’s authority.
    - Cultural activities are legitimate and can glorify God.
    - The church and society are distinct but interrelated under Christ’s reign.
    - "There is not one blade of grass, there is no color in this world that is not intended to make us rejoice." (Institutes II.2.15)

2. **Already/Not Yet Eschatology**
    - The kingdom of God is inaugurated but not yet consummated.
    - Believers live between the times, participating in renewal while awaiting fulfillment.
    - Cultural engagement is provisional, marked by hope and patience.
    - The present world is passing away, but its goodness is affirmed.
    - "We must make it our aim that Christ’s kingdom may flourish among us, and that the world may be renewed by the Spirit of God." (Commentary on Romans 12:2)

3. **Common Grace and Cultural Good**
    - God’s **common grace** restrains evil and enables cultural achievements.
    - Non-believers can contribute to culture through God’s gifts.
    - The arts, sciences, and civil order are expressions of God’s goodness.
    - Christians can learn from and participate in the broader culture.
    - "Whenever we come upon these matters in secular writers, let that admirable light of truth shining in them teach us that the mind of man, though fallen and perverted from its wholeness, is nevertheless clothed and ornamented with God’s excellent gifts." (Institutes II.2.15)

4. **Pilgrim Mentality and Cultural Engagement**
    - Christians are sojourners, not fully at home in this world.
    - Engagement with culture is marked by discernment and detachment.
    - Earthly life is good, but ultimate hope is in the new creation.
    - The church witnesses to the coming kingdom through its life and works.
    - "We must use this world as though we did not use it, for the fashion of this world passes away." (Institutes III.9.4)

5. **Transformation and Anticipation of Consummation**
    - The Spirit enables believers to work for the transformation of society.
    - Cultural renewal is a sign of the coming **restoration** in Christ.
    - All efforts are penultimate, pointing to the final redemption.
    - The eschatological hope shapes Christian action in the present.
    - "The restoration of the world is commenced by the gospel, and carried forward every day by the Spirit, until it is fully completed at Christ’s last coming." (Commentary on 2 Corinthians 5:17)

## Legacy and Impact

Calvin’s vision of **Christ and culture** in eschatological perspective has profoundly shaped Reformed approaches to cultural engagement, emphasizing both the legitimacy of cultural pursuits and the provisional nature of all human achievements. His doctrine of **common grace** provided a theological foundation for Christian involvement in the arts, sciences, and public life. Calvin’s influence endures in the ongoing Reformed emphasis on the lordship of Christ over all creation and the hope of the coming kingdom. His eschatological realism continues to inform contemporary discussions on the church’s mission in the world.
