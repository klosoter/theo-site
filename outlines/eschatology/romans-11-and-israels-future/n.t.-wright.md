# N. T. Wright (1948–)

N. T. Wright interprets **Romans 11** as a pivotal text for understanding the ongoing role of **Israel** in the unfolding of **God’s covenant purposes**. He argues that Paul envisions a future for ethnic Israel within the renewed people of God, but this future is always framed within the **Messiah’s fulfillment** and the **unity of Jew and Gentile** in the **new covenant**. Wright resists both supersessionism and a simplistic restorationism, emphasizing the continuity of God’s promises through **Jesus Christ**.

- "Paul’s vision in Romans 11 is not of a separate future for Israel over against the church, but of the one family of God, Jew and Gentile together, brought to fullness in the Messiah." ("Paul and the Faithfulness of God")

## Key Works

- Paul and the Faithfulness of God (2013)
- Romans (New Interpreter’s Bible Commentary, 2002)
- The Climax of the Covenant (1991)
- Paul: In Fresh Perspective (2005)
- Surprised by Hope (2007)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **God’s Covenant Faithfulness Remains Central**
   - Emphasizes that **Romans 11** is about God’s unwavering commitment to His promises to Israel.
   - Argues that Paul’s language of “all Israel will be saved” is rooted in the narrative of covenant fulfillment.
   - Sees the **remnant motif** as evidence of God’s ongoing faithfulness.
   - Insists that the inclusion of Gentiles does not nullify God’s promises to ethnic Israel.
   - "God has not abandoned his covenant people; rather, he is fulfilling his promises in a new and unexpected way." (Paul and the Faithfulness of God)

2. **Christological Fulfillment of Israel’s Story**
   - Maintains that Jesus as **Messiah** is the climax of Israel’s history.
   - Interprets Israel’s future as inseparable from participation in Christ.
   - Argues that Paul’s vision is not a return to Torah observance but entry into the renewed covenant in Christ.
   - Sees the church as the reconstituted people of God, Jew and Gentile together.
   - "The Messiah is the one in whom Israel’s destiny and vocation are summed up and fulfilled." (Paul and the Faithfulness of God)

3. **Rejection of Supersessionism**
   - Denies that the church has simply replaced Israel in God’s purposes.
   - Emphasizes the ongoing significance of ethnic Israel in Paul’s argument.
   - Argues that Paul’s olive tree metaphor points to continuity, not replacement.
   - Sees the church as grafted into Israel’s story, not vice versa.
   - "Paul does not envisage the church as a separate entity from Israel, but as its fulfillment and extension." (Romans, NIB Commentary)

4. **Eschatological Inclusion of Israel**
   - Interprets “all Israel will be saved” as a future event within the messianic age.
   - Suggests that Israel’s hardening is temporary and will be reversed.
   - Connects Israel’s future salvation to the final ingathering of the nations.
   - Argues that this inclusion is through faith in Christ, not apart from him.
   - "Paul’s eschatological vision is of a single, renewed people of God, with Israel’s fullness as a crucial part." (Paul and the Faithfulness of God)

5. **Unity of Jew and Gentile in the People of God**
   - Stresses that the ultimate goal is the **one new humanity** in Christ.
   - Argues that the distinction between Jew and Gentile is overcome in the Messiah.
   - Sees the church as the locus of God’s renewed people, incorporating both groups.
   - Maintains that this unity is the fulfillment of the Abrahamic promise.
   - "The unity of Jew and Gentile in the Messiah is the sign and means of God’s faithfulness to his promises." (Paul: In Fresh Perspective)

## Legacy and Impact

Wright’s interpretation of **Romans 11** has significantly shaped contemporary debates about **Israel’s future** and the relationship between **covenant, eschatology, and ecclesiology**. His rejection of both supersessionism and dual-covenant theology has provided a nuanced framework for understanding Paul’s vision of the people of God. Wright’s scholarship continues to influence Reformed and evangelical discussions on the unity of **Jew and Gentile** and the ongoing significance of Israel in redemptive history.
