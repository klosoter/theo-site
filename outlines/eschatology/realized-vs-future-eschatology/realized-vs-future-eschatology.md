### TITLE
Already and Not Yet: Holding Realized Kingdom and Future Consummation in Christian Hope

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Realized versus future eschatology names the tension between the present realization of the kingdom and the promised consummation at the end, framing Christian hope as both gift and goal. The churches confession holds together the **inaugurated kingdom** that has come in Christ and the **consummated kingdom** that arrives at the *parousia*. Within dogmatics, this belongs to **eschatology** but radiates into **Christology**, **Pneumatology**, and **Ecclesiology**, since the exalted Christ reigns even now, the Spirit is given as *arrabon*, and the church is a proleptic community of the age to come. The key claim is that the one eschatological reality is temporally stretched, the same **kingdom of God** appearing as present power and future fullness.

This definition rejects the false alternative that either everything is realized or everything is deferred. The church lives in the overlap of the **two-age schema**, *aion houtos* and *aion mellon*, such that believers are already transferred to the new age while still suffering the old. The tension is dogmatically anchored by **union with Christ**, where believers share in his resurrection life by the Spirit and await their bodily resurrection, rooting **realized eschatology** in participation and **future eschatology** in hope.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The Synoptic Gospels announce an eschatological arrival that is simultaneously present and promised. Jesus proclaims in Mark 1:15 that the time is fulfilled and the kingdom is at hand, and declares in Matt 12:28 that exorcisms show the kingdom has come upon you. These texts ground **realized kingdom** claims. Yet the Lord teaches the petition your kingdom come and speaks of the **coming of the Son of Man**, sustaining **future consummation**. The parables present the kingdom as seed and leaven, realities of hidden present power and future harvest, displaying **eschatological tension** intrinsic to Jesus mission.

Johannine literature intensifies the present dimension. In John 5:24 the one who hears has eternal life and has passed from death to life, and in John 11:25–26 Jesus is the resurrection and the life now. Yet the Fourth Gospel holds a robust **last day** hope, as in John 6:39–40, while 1 John 3:2 confesses a not yet appearing of what we shall be. This double horizon represents a **realized eschatology** of life in the Son and a **future eschatology** of eschatophany. The Spirit in John 14–16 is the **eschatological Paraclete**, whose presence anticipates the glory to come.

Paul gives the most explicit grammar. Believers are new creation in 2 Cor 5:17 and taste the powers of the age to come, receiving the **firstfruits** *aparche* of the Spirit in Rom 8:23 and the **down payment** *arrabon* in 2 Cor 1:22. Yet 1 Cor 15 insists on a future bodily resurrection, the last enemy death to be destroyed at the end. Romans 8 holds present adoption and future sonship in tension, articulating a creation-wide **future liberation**. The Pauline **two-age schema** thus grounds inaugurated soteriology and a climactic eschatology in one coherent story.

Hebrews 2:8–9 observes that all things are subjected to Christ though we do not yet see it, a classic formulation of **already/not yet**. Revelation 1 announces Jesus as ruler of kings, yet Revelation 20–22 moves toward final judgment and **new creation**. The canonical synthesis binds **realized eschatology** to Christ's resurrection and **future eschatology** to his return, ensuring that proleptic participation never collapses the promised consummation.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Early Christians expected the **imminent parousia**, yet stabilized their hope through catechesis and liturgy. The Didache and 1 Clement already exhibit both urgent expectation and structured leadership, while second-century **chiliasm** expressed a concrete future reign. After the crisis of Montanism in 156–200, the church distanced itself from excesses without renouncing **future eschatology**. Origen emphasized spiritual transformation, but **Irenaeus** retained the integrity of bodily resurrection and **new creation**, refusing a purely **realized eschatology**.

The decisive patristic turn came with **Augustine's City of God** written 413–426, which refuted civic eschatologies and embedded hope in the distinction of the two cities. Augustine rejected a carnal millennium and offered a classic **amillennial** reading in which the millennium symbolizes the present reign of Christ and the saints. This move safeguarded **realized eschatology** in terms of Christ's present session while securing the **future judgment and resurrection**. Medieval theology continued this synthesis, developing doctrines of **beatific vision** and purgation within a teleological order aimed at consummation.

The Reformation reaffirmed creedal futures, the **general resurrection** and **last judgment**, while re-situating realized elements within **justification by faith** and the **means of grace**. Anabaptist radicalizations, such as Münster in 1534–1535, warned against politicized **over-realization**. Post-Reformation Reformed scholasticism embedded eschatology within **covenant theology**, with **Witsius 1677** drawing lines from promise to eschaton. Early modernity saw **postmillennial optimism** among some Protestants, culminating at Old Princeton with **Warfield**, while nineteenth-century **dispensational futurism** coalesced with **Darby in the 1830s** and the **Scofield Reference Bible in 1909**, stressing discontinuity and a strongly deferred kingdom.

Twentieth-century debate crystallized around three poles. **Johannes Weiss 1892** and **Albert Schweitzer 1906** argued for consistent eschatology, reading Jesus apocalyptically and explaining the church by a failed expectation. **C. H. Dodd 1935** replied with **realized eschatology**, claiming that fulfillment had occurred in Jesus ministry. **Oscar Cullmann 1946–1947** forged the salvation-historical analogy of D-Day and V-Day to name **inaugurated eschatology**, while **G. E. Ladd 1964** synthesized biblical evidence in The Presence of the Future. In Reformed circles, **Geerhardus Vos 1930** and **Herman Ridderbos 1966** deepened Pauline **two-age** exegesis, and **Anthony Hoekema 1979** offered a systematic account of **amillennial inaugurated eschatology**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Reformed tradition binds eschatology to **covenant and kingdom**, grounding realization in union with Christ and securing futurity by the Creator's fidelity to the created order. **Old Princeton** combined a high doctrine of Scripture with divergent millennial schemes, yet converged on **resurrection realism** and **final judgment**. **Charles Hodge 1871–1873** affirmed the resurrection's bodily integrity, and **B. B. Warfield** famously leaned **postmillennial**, arguing for gospel progress in history while maintaining a definitive **future consummation**.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** stream, from **Abraham Kuyper** to **Herman Bavinck**, emphasized **creation's eschatological renewal** rather than escape, with Bavinck in 1895–1901 insisting that grace restores and elevates nature. **Geerhardus Vos** crystalized the Pauline **two-age schema**, while **Ridderbos 1966** framed the kingdom as present power and future dominion. This line yields a robust **inaugurated eschatology** that is thoroughly amillennial yet culture-affirming, integrating **new creation** with vocation in the saeculum.

The **Westminster Standards 1646–1647** confess the intermediate state, the resurrection of the just and unjust, and the **last judgment**, while locating realized blessings in effectual calling, adoption, and sanctification. In this view, the Spirit is **firstfruits and down payment**, the sacraments are **signs and seals** of the age to come, and the Lord's Day anticipates the **eighth day** of new creation. **Hoekema 1979** and later Reformed writers articulate amillennial inaugurated eschatology against both over-realized social utopias and under-realized quietism.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology integrates **realized eschatology** through sacramental realism and **future eschatology** through the beatific vision. **Lumen Gentium 1964** names the church as the **pilgrim People of God**, already endowed with sanctity yet journeying toward consummation. **Joseph Ratzinger's Eschatology 1977** clarifies the intermediate state and purgation as sanctifying encounters with Christ, preserving the **resurrection of the body** and **new creation** hope, while insisting that Eucharist is **eschatological prolepsis**.

Lutheran theology emphasizes the **theology of the cross** and the **two kingdoms**, giving **realized eschatology** a cruciform shape while retaining the **future parousia** as comfort for the afflicted. The Augsburg and later confessions teach **amillennialism** with strong sacramental presence as pledge. Modern biblical theology offered contending proposals: **Bultmann's 1941 demythologizing** construed eschatology existentially as decision in the now, while **Moltmann 1964** and **Pannenberg 1961** re-centered hope on the **future of God**, drawing the present into proleptic participation through the Spirit and the resurrection.

In analytic and contemporary philosophical theology, debates turn on **personal identity in resurrection**, the **intermediate state** and **soul-body** relations, and the metaphysics of **new creation**. Some defend **Christian physicalism** with a divinely preserved continuity, others uphold **dualistic survival** toward resurrection, and still others explore **annihilationism** as a coherent **future eschatology** within biblical judgment. These discussions often assume an **already/not yet** structure, asking how present sanctification relates to the identity of the future glorified person.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The first objection targets **the delay of the parousia**, noting texts like Mark 13:30 and 2 Peter 3. Inaugurated eschatology responds by reading apocalyptic discourse through the **two-stage fulfillment** of judgment on Jerusalem in 70 and the **final consummation**, avoiding **hyper-preterism** while taking realized elements seriously. The canon itself models patient hope, as 2 Peter reframes delay as divine mercy and **faithful endurance**.

A second debate concerns **kingdom ethics** and social engagement. An over-realized eschatology can fuel utopian projects, while an under-realized stance can license quietism. Reformed and catholic voices commend **sacramental prolepsis** and **vocation** as measured anticipations, where justice and mercy are authentic signs yet not the consummation. Here **new creation** functions as both criterion and horizon, testing all programs against cruciform love and the promise of resurrection.

A third dispute concerns **Israel and the church** within **future eschatology**. Dispensational schemes defer the kingdom and posit separate destinies, while **inaugurated eschatology** in Reformed and many catholic readings sees **one olive tree**, with the mystery of Rom 11 forecasting future mercy without disjoining the singular **people of God**. Related are millennial debates, where **amillennial inaugurated eschatology** resists both realized triumphalism and speculative timetables, and contends that the millennium symbolizes the present age of the gospel under the **reign of Christ**.

Finally, pneumatological disputes surface in charismatic-cessationist tensions, argued in terms of **already/not yet** gifts. Some claim Pentecost as paradigmatic realization authorizing ongoing **charismata**, others delimit sign gifts to the apostolic foundation while affirming the **future fullness**. The deeper agreement is that the Spirit gives **firstfruits**, not fullness, and that love as the greatest gift is the most **eschatologically durable**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinitarianly, **Rahners Rule** presses that the economic missions reveal the immanent relations, so the Son's resurrection and the Spirit's presence are the **eschatological self-giving of God**. The already is the procession of the **Spirit of adoption**, while the not yet is the **beatific vision** of the Father in the Son. Christologically, the two states of **humiliation and exaltation** ground the structure: in his exaltation Christ inaugurates the kingdom, and in his return he consummates it, uniting **realized and future eschatology** in his person.

Anthropology depends on **resurrection realism**, the body redeemed in continuity with creation. The church's sanctification is **eschatological sanctification**, participation in the powers of the age to come, while justification is **proleptic verdict** ahead of time on the basis of Christ. Ecclesiology sees the church as **firstfruits community**, gathered by the Word and sacraments, where Baptism and Eucharist are **signs and seals** of the kingdom's arrival and pledge of the **wedding feast** to come. Creation and providence culminate in **new creation**, not abandonment, so stewardship and Sabbath are lived foretastes of the **coming rest**.

Soteriology as union with Christ makes the Spirit the **down payment** and freedom from sin the **already**, with glorification as the **not yet**. The doctrine of Scripture is implicated, since the prophetic and apocalyptic word functions as **promise-fulfillment** witness that shapes a canonical **eschatological horizon**. In short, each locus either receives or refracts the **already/not yet** logic, testifying to a single divine economy moving toward **consummation**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, **realized eschatology** grounds assurance, for believers possess eternal life now, while **future eschatology** secures perseverance, for we await redemption and groan with creation. The church should teach both, resisting triumphalism and despair. Preaching should name Christ's present reign and call for hope in suffering, practicing **patience as eschatological virtue** and **joy as foretaste**. The Eucharist is celebrated as **Maranatha meal**, where the Spirit lifts the church into Christ's presence as pledge of the **wedding supper** still ahead.

Liturgically, the calendar embodies the tension. Advent trains desire for the **coming King**, Easter proclaims **resurrection firstfruits**, Ascension celebrates Christ's **present session**, and Pentecost bestows **Spirit firstfruits**. Funerals confess **now with the Lord** and **resurrection then**, and the Lord's Day as the **eighth day** rehearses **new creation**. Catechesis should teach children to pray your kingdom come and to recognize in baptism their **eschatological identity** as those who have died and been raised with Christ, awaiting the **redemption of the body**.

Ethically, **inaugurated eschatology** authorizes witness marked by cruciform service rather than coercion. Justice projects are neither the kingdom nor irrelevant, but **signs and instruments** of the kingdom's power. Suffering is reframed as sharing in Christ's **eschatological victory**, while lament is legitimized as the language of the not yet. Mission proceeds with urgency, since the gospel of the kingdom is announced now, and with patience, because the **harvest** belongs to the Lord of the end. This double vision cultivates resilient communities of hope.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Christian hope is the Spirit-given capacity to live now from the powers of the age to come while awaiting with patience and joy the resurrection and the renewal of all things under the reign of the risen Christ.
