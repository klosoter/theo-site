# Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834)

Friedrich Schleiermacher approached **eschatology** not as a discrete future event but as an expression of the ongoing realization of **redemption** within the life of the **Christian community**. He emphasized the **immanence of the Kingdom of God**, interpreting eschatological hope as the progressive actualization of **God-consciousness** and ethical transformation in history, rather than a dramatic, future consummation. For Schleiermacher, the tension between **realized** and **future eschatology** is resolved by focusing on the present experience of salvation.

- "The kingdom of God is not a future state, but a present reality in the fellowship of believers." (The Christian Faith)

- On Religion: Speeches to Its Cultured Despisers

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Eschatology as Present Reality**
    - The **Kingdom of God** is primarily experienced in the present through the communal life of the church.
    - Emphasizes the **actualization of redemption** in the ongoing ethical and spiritual transformation of believers.
    - Downplays apocalyptic or catastrophic future events in favor of present spiritual realities.
    - Eschatological hope is realized in the growth of **God-consciousness** within humanity.
    - "The consummation of the kingdom of God is not to be expected as a sudden event, but as a gradual process." (The Christian Faith)

2. **Redefinition of Resurrection and Immortality**
    - Interprets **resurrection** and **immortality** symbolically, as the persistence of the God-consciousness in the community.
    - Rejects literal bodily resurrection in favor of spiritual continuity.
    - The future life is understood as the perfection of the individual's relationship to God.
    - Focuses on the ethical implications of immortality for present conduct.
    - "Immortality is not the continuation of personal consciousness, but the enduring influence of the God-consciousness awakened in us." (The Christian Faith)

3. **Immanence of Salvation**
    - Salvation is not a future reward but a present participation in the divine life.
    - The work of Christ is understood as the ongoing mediation of **God-consciousness** to the community.
    - The church is the locus where salvation is realized and experienced.
    - Eschatology is thus integrated into soteriology and ecclesiology.
    - "Salvation is nothing other than the state of being in communion with God here and now." (The Christian Faith)

4. **Ethical Progress as Eschatological Fulfillment**
    - The ethical transformation of individuals and society is the true fulfillment of eschatological hope.
    - The **Kingdom of God** grows as love and righteousness increase in the world.
    - Eschatology motivates ethical striving rather than speculative curiosity about the future.
    - The ultimate goal is the universalization of God-consciousness and moral unity.
    - "The true end is the complete moral transformation of humanity in the image of Christ." (Christian Ethics)

5. **Critique of Apocalypticism**
    - Rejects traditional apocalyptic expectations as incompatible with modern thought.
    - Views biblical apocalyptic language as symbolic of spiritual realities.
    - Emphasizes the continuity of divine action in history rather than discontinuous future interventions.
    - Encourages reinterpretation of eschatological texts in light of contemporary experience.
    - "Apocalyptic imagery must be understood as the symbolic expression of the inner life of faith." (The Christian Faith)

## Legacy and Impact

Schleiermacher’s **immanentist eschatology** profoundly shaped modern Protestant thought, shifting the focus from speculative future events to the present experience of redemption. His approach influenced later **liberal theology** and the development of **realized eschatology** in the twentieth century. While often criticized for minimizing the future dimension of Christian hope, his integration of eschatology with ethics and ecclesiology remains a significant contribution to systematic theology. Schleiermacher’s legacy endures in ongoing debates about the relationship between present and future aspects of Christian salvation.
