### TITLE
Embodied Glory: The New Heavens and New Earth as the Consummation of Redemptive History

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core conceptual issue: How the promised **new heavens and new earth** relate to creation, redemption, and consummation, especially the continuity and discontinuity between this world and the world to come.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, John Calvin, Geerhardus Vos, N. T. Wright.
- Traditions: Patristic eschatology, medieval scholasticism, Reformation and Reformed orthodoxy, and modern biblical-theological developments.
- Debates:
  - Internal: spiritual versus robustly creational views of the final state, continuity versus discontinuity, already–not yet in inaugurated eschatology.
  - External: Platonizing and Gnostic trajectories, secular progressivism and annihilationism, dispensational premillennialism.
  - Contemporary: ecological theology, physicalism versus dualism, and the charge that Christian eschatology is “otherworldly” and ethically evasive.
- Defended position: The Bible teaches that God will bring a radical, transformative renewal of the present creation into a concrete, embodied **new heavens and new earth**, in which resurrected saints enjoy the beatific vision in communion with the triune God within a glorified, yet genuinely continuous, cosmos under the everlasting kingship of Christ.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of the **new heavens and new earth** concerns the final form of reality under the lordship of the risen Christ. It answers the question: What is the ultimate telos of creation and redemption? To formulate it with precision, one must hold together several poles: the goodness of the original creation, the depth of sin and curse, the definitiveness of Christ’s cross and resurrection, the inaugurated yet not consummated kingdom, and the final victory of God over every enemy including death. Conceptually, the new heavens and new earth denotes the eschatological state in which God, having judged and purged all evil, renovates and glorifies the entire created order so that righteousness dwells and God is all in all. This is not a mere “location” but the consummate order of reality in which redeemed human beings, in resurrected bodies, live in covenant communion with the triune God forever within a renewed cosmos.

The decisive turning point for Western Christian reflection on this doctrine is Augustine. In the City of God, particularly books 20–22, Augustine confronts pagan cyclical theories of history and Platonizing spiritualism. His context is crucial: he writes in the wake of Rome’s sack, accused that Christianity has weakened the empire by directing attention to heaven instead of the earthly city. Augustine’s method is to oppose pagan pessimism and cyclical recurrence with a linear, teleological history ordered by God toward a final judgment and eternal city. He insists on bodily resurrection and the goodness of created embodiment, against both Platonist contempt for matter and crude materialism. Yet his language is often read as if the final hope were essentially “heaven” understood as the soul’s contemplation of God, with the new earth more a backdrop than an integral field of human vocation. Examiners will press here: does Augustine give us enough creational thickness?

One must handle Augustine carefully. On the one hand, he clearly affirms the renewal of creation. He expounds Romans 8 to say that creation, now subjected to futility, will share in the liberty of the children of God. He anticipates a “spiritual body” that is nevertheless numerically identical with the present body, restored beyond decay. He does not propose annihilation of the present cosmos but its transformation. Yet, shaped by Neoplatonic concerns, his center of gravity lies in the soul’s **beatific vision** of God, with the created order almost entirely subordinated to that contemplative end. The supreme good is the enjoyment of God; other goods of creation are relativized as means or vestiges. This does not deny the **new earth**, but it can render it the stage rather than the content of final life. Internal to the Augustinian tradition arises therefore a tension: can one affirm the robust goodness of creation’s final form without undermining the primacy of direct communion with God?

Thomas Aquinas intensifies this contemplative orientation while giving some of the most precise metaphysical analysis of the final state. In the Summa Theologiae, especially the questions on the resurrection and the consummation, Aquinas integrates **divine simplicity**, the hierarchy of being, and the doctrine of grace. For Thomas, the ultimate end of rational creatures is the **beatific vision**, a direct intellectual intuition of the divine essence granted by created light of glory. The resurrected body is necessary not as the direct vehicle of this vision, which is intellectual rather than sensory, but as the fitting completion of human nature as an embodied rational soul. He affirms a renewed earth and heavens, but their role is subordinate, ordered to the delight and perfection of resurrected bodies that belong to souls primarily perfected by their intellectual vision of God.

In Aquinas, the ontological and volitional structure is asymmetric: the proportionate end of nature is some natural knowledge of God through creatures, but the supernatural end by grace is the direct vision of God which supersedes and, in a sense, leaves behind the mediated knowledge through creation. Examiners may challenge: does such a schema risk undermining the abiding significance of the **new creation** as creation? Does the earth matter once we “see” God directly? Aquinas would answer that the renewed cosmos still manifests God’s glory and supplies supplementary joy; yet the hierarchy is clear. The decisive contribution of Augustine and Aquinas, then, is to clarify the finality of God himself as the supreme object of human fulfillment and to insist on bodily resurrection and transformation while, in practice, marginalizing the positive role of a renewed creation order.

By contrast, the Reformation, and especially John Calvin, re-centers the notion of the new heavens and new earth within a redemptive-historical framework that strongly affirms creation’s destiny. Calvin’s context is polemical against both medieval speculation and Anabaptist apocalypticism. In the Institutes and his commentaries, particularly on Romans 8, 2 Peter 3, and Revelation 21–22, Calvin repeatedly insists that the world is not to be annihilated but purified by fire. He explicitly rejects the idea that God will create a new world ex nihilo at the end as if this one were discarded. Instead, he reads the prophetic and apostolic language of “new” as referring to a radical renovation of the same creation. Here Calvin’s commitment to a robust doctrine of creation, undergirded by **divine providence**, shapes his eschatology: God does not abandon his works but perfects them.

Calvin’s portrait of the final state keeps together two goods without competition. First, he too affirms the centrality of seeing God, but this vision is always mediated by the incarnate Christ and framed within **covenant communion** rather than a bare metaphysical intuition of the divine essence. Second, he takes with new seriousness the biblical imagery of cultural and creational restoration: the nations bringing their glory into the New Jerusalem, the healing of the nations by the river of life, the curse removed from the ground. The resurrection of the body is the hinge by which personal eschatology and cosmic eschatology are joined. The same creation that now groans will share in the resurrectional freedom of the sons of God. Calvin thus pushes further than Augustine and Aquinas toward an integrated vision where the renewed cosmos is not a mere stage but the home, field, and structure of eternal life under God.

Internal debates arise here within the Reformed tradition. Some strands, influenced by a strong emphasis on the beatific vision and fear of “earthly” eschatology, read Calvin through an Augustinian lens, treating the new earth as significant but not central. Others, especially in the Dutch Reformed trajectory, accentuate the creational and cultural dimension, sometimes in ways that verge on collapsing eschatology into an endless extension of cultural projects. At this point, Geerhardus Vos is decisive, because he articulates a controlled, biblical-theological account of the new heavens and new earth as the eschatological goal built into creation from the beginning while safeguarding the transcendence of the eschaton.

Vos, working in the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, is responding to liberal Protestant reduction of eschatology to ethics and consciousness and to premillennial literalism. His biblical theology, summarized in works like The Pauline Eschatology and the posthumous Biblical Theology, is marked by the category of **inaugurated eschatology**: the age to come has already entered history in Christ’s death and resurrection, but its consummation awaits his return. Vos argues that from the outset, creation was ordered toward a higher, eschatological state symbolized by the Sabbath and the tree of life. Adam’s probation in the covenant of works held forth a consummate, unlosable life transcending the mutable natural state. The fall did not create eschatology; it complicated it by inserting the need for redemption, but the goal remains a glorified, Sabbath-consummated creation.

On this view, the new heavens and new earth are not a divine “plan B” after sin but the realization of the original intention for creation, achieved through the second Adam. Vos thus resists both annihilationist readings of 2 Peter 3 and any notion that the eschaton is a mere return to Eden. Instead, the cross and resurrection bring about a higher form: a creation incapable of defection, suffused with the glory of God, with a transformed structure no longer under the possibility or actuality of curse. Vos is also impatient with purely spatial readings of “heaven” as somewhere else; “heavenly” in Paul is eschatological and Christological rather than simply supra-spatial. The saints are now seated in the heavenlies in Christ, which anticipates their embodied life in the consummate kingdom.

Here examiners will test your grip on continuity and discontinuity. Vos insists that continuity is grounded in **covenant history**: the same world God created good, the same human race fallen in Adam, is the arena in which Christ acts and the Spirit renews. Discontinuity is not metaphysical obliteration but eschatological transformation. Passage through judgment is purgative and re-creative, not annihilative. The flood is a type: the world is judged and cleansed, not replaced. To those who appeal to 2 Peter’s language of the elements melting, Vos responds that biblical language for divine judgment often uses cosmic hyperbole to depict the severity of God’s action while in context speaking of purification for righteousness to dwell. The category of **new creation** in Paul is already present in regeneration and sanctification; the final cosmic new creation stands as the ultimate horizon of this same renewing work.

N. T. Wright, as a contemporary figure, has popularized and in some respects extended this creationally robust eschatology, albeit with certain distinct emphases that provoke internal debate. Writing against a background of popular “heavenism” and disembodied afterlife imagery, Wright stresses that the Christian hope is not “going to heaven when you die” but resurrection of the body and a renewed world. In Surprised by Hope and his major work on resurrection, Wright argues that the biblical narrative moves from creation to new creation, with resurrection as the hinge. He exposes the Platonizing misreadings that reduce salvation to escape from the material world and shows how both Old and New Testaments present a unified story where God comes to dwell with his people in a renewed world.

Wright’s insistence that “heaven” in the New Testament is primarily God’s dimension of reality rather than the ultimate human destination can be a useful corrective. His reading of Romans 8 and Revelation aligns with the Reformed emphasis on creation’s liberation and the descent of the New Jerusalem. Moreover, his portrayal of the church’s present mission as anticipatory of and congruent with the future new creation leads him to affirm the enduring significance of certain cultural labors. However, examiners will rightly probe where Wright’s program may overreach. Does his emphasis on “building for the kingdom” risk underplaying the discontinuity introduced by final judgment and the sheer gratuity of consummation? Does he sufficiently maintain that only what is united to Christ by the Spirit will pass through the fire? A Reformed answer would affirm that present obedience in vocation has eschatological significance because it participates in Christ’s kingdom, but it would resist any suggestion that our work, as such, accumulates into the kingdom by its own intrinsic value.

Against this backdrop, several internal debates must be confronted sharply. First, the question of the nature of “new”: is the new heavens and new earth a brand-new ex nihilo creation or the transformed present creation? A classical Reformed position, following Calvin and Vos, reads the biblical data in favor of transformation. The global flood as typological judgment, Romans 8’s language of liberation of the same creation now groaning, and Revelation’s imagery of continuity in the nations and kings entering the city suggest that God renews rather than discards. An examiner may press 2 Peter’s language that “the earth and the works done on it will be exposed” and that “the heavens will be set on fire and dissolved.” The response should note the textual variant between “burned up” and “found” and then stress Peter’s emphasis on the moral dimension of this burning: the works are exposed to judgment. The imagery of fire in scripture is as much purifying as destructive. Thus, one can affirm a radical, even unfathomable, transformation, such that the new world will be beyond our current categories, without positing metaphysical discontinuity that severs God’s redemptive faithfulness to this creation.

Second, the debate over the relative centrality of the **beatific vision** versus creational flourishing. Here Augustine and Aquinas are often invoked against more “earthy” eschatologies. A sound Reformed response does not set these against each other but orders them rightly. The ultimate blessedness of the saints is to see and know the triune God in and through the incarnate Christ by the Spirit. This is the heart of the covenant formula: “I will be their God, and they shall be my people.” Yet this communion is not exercised in a void. Because God created humans as embodied, cultural creatures placed in a world to cultivate, rule, and offer it back to God, the final state must be the place where this integrated vocation is fully and eternally realized without sin, curse, or sorrow. There is no competition between the vision of God and the enjoyment and stewardship of the **new creation**; the latter is the sphere in which and the means through which the former is continually expressed. Any examiner suggestion that a robust new-earth eschatology diminishes the visio Dei should be met by insisting that creation was always ordered to be the theater of divine glory, not a distraction from it.

Third, the already–not yet structure. Vos and contemporary biblical theologians emphasize that believers already participate in the powers of the age to come. Union with Christ, the indwelling Spirit, and our being seated with Christ in the heavenlies mean that eschatological life has broken in. The Lord’s Day worship is an anticipatory participation in the heavenly assembly. Yet death still reigns, creation groans, and believers await the redemption of their bodies. The danger is either an overrealized eschatology that expects present conditions to match the new earth or an underrealized one that treats eschatology as almost irrelevant to present life. A Reformed approach ties sanctification, mission, and perseverance directly to the hope of the **new heavens and new earth**, while recognizing that the full realization awaits the parousia and final judgment.

External debates further clarify the doctrine. Historically, Christianity has opposed Gnostic and Manichaean trajectories that either demonize matter or reduce salvation to escape from materiality. The insistence on bodily resurrection and a renewed earth is one of the strongest anti-Gnostic claims in Christian doctrine. Modern forms of spiritualized Christianity, that speak only of “souls in heaven,” functionally align with these older errors. Against this, the biblical promise that “the meek shall inherit the earth” is not fulfilled by giving them something else. The new heavens and new earth doctrine also stands over against secular progressivism, which promises a kind of immanent eschaton through technological and political advance. Here the Christian insists that the final state is not the product of historical evolution but the result of divine intervention, judgment, and resurrection. The new creation is gift, not achievement.

Another external debate concerns dispensational premillennialism, which tends to treat the millennial kingdom and the eternal state as sharply distinct, sometimes with a focus on national Israel’s land promises and an earthly kingdom before the final new earth. The Reformed amillennial reading tends to view Revelation 20 as a recapitulation rather than a chronological step, with the new heavens and new earth corresponding to the single consummate state. On this view, the land promises to Israel are fulfilled typologically and escalated in the inheritance of the entire renewed earth by the whole people of God in Christ. For oral defense, one must show how the new creation fulfils the Abrahamic and Davidic covenants in Christ, thereby integrating **covenant theology** and eschatology.

Contemporary debates raise further questions. Ecological theology sometimes accuses traditional eschatology of underwriting environmental abuse: if the world will be destroyed, why care for it? A doctrine of the **new earth** as transformation rather than annihilation undercuts this objection. If God intends to renew this creation, our present care for it participates in and anticipates that renewal. Moreover, the cultural mandate is not revoked but reframed in Christ. Yet we also insist that ecological activism is not itself the kingdom; it is obedience within the old age destined for transformation. Exam questions may press whether one can derive specific environmental policies from eschatology. The answer is that eschatology gives an orientation and valuation of creation, not a technocratic blueprint.

The rise of physicalist accounts of human nature in some Christian circles adds another dimension. Some propose that the intermediate state is a kind of unconscious sleep, with personal identity preserved in God’s memory until resurrection. Others deny any disembodied state. The classic Reformed view, in continuity with Augustine and Calvin, affirms a conscious intermediate state in which the souls of believers are with Christ in heaven, awaiting the resurrection of the body. A robust new creation doctrine requires maintaining bodily resurrection as nonnegotiable. Yet one must also avoid making the intermediate state the main focus. Properly ordered, the intermediate state is “far better” than this life, but it remains penultimate; the final hope is resurrection in the **new heavens and new earth**.

Finally, the accusation that Christian eschatology is “otherworldly” and ethically evasive must be addressed. When rightly understood, the hope of the new creation grounds and intensifies ethical seriousness. Knowing that our labor in the Lord is not in vain, because God will raise and renew, leads to steadfastness and abounding in the work of the Lord. The certainty of final judgment and the removal of all injustice energizes the church’s pursuit of righteousness and mercy now. An amorphous “heaven” detached from creation can invite quietism; a concrete hope in the **new earth** under the visible reign of Christ calls believers to live now as citizens of that coming kingdom, embodying its righteousness and peace in anticipation.

The position to be defended in examination is therefore as follows. God, in his **eternal decree**, purposed not only to create a good world but to bring it to eschatological consummation in Christ. The fall introduced curse and death into that same creation. In the fullness of time, the eternal Son assumed a true human nature, died, and rose bodily as the firstfruits of the resurrection, inaugurating the new creation. At his return, he will raise the dead, judge all men, purge creation by fire, and transform it into the **new heavens and new earth**, in which righteousness dwells. This final state is continuous with the present creation in its underlying identity but discontinuous in its glorified, incorruptible, Spirit-filled form. The redeemed, united to Christ, will enjoy the **beatific vision** of the triune God in and through the incarnate Mediator, and they will exercise holy, joyful rule over the renewed cosmos, fulfilling at last the original human vocation in an unbreakable covenant of consummate peace. Any account that either spiritualizes this hope into an immaterial heaven or collapses it into a secularized extension of present history misses the biblical balance.

Within this framework, Augustine and Aquinas are valued for grounding the final state in the enjoyment of God, though their accounts are corrected by Calvin’s and Vos’s stronger integration of creation’s destiny. Wright’s contemporary emphasis on bodily resurrection and new creation usefully counters popular distortions but must be disciplined by a high view of divine judgment and the gratuity of consummation. The doctrine of the **new heavens and new earth**, thus articulated, secures the unity of God’s purposes in creation and redemption, glorifies the person and work of Christ as the second Adam, and offers the church a concrete, embodied, and God-centered hope to confess and defend.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The **new heavens and new earth** are the final, eschatological state in which God, through Christ, radically renovates and glorifies this present creation after judgment, so that resurrected saints dwell in embodied covenant communion with the triune God in a renewed cosmos where righteousness permanently dwells.
- Decisive figures:
  - Augustine: linear eschatology and strong **beatific vision**, affirming renewed creation but often subordinating it to contemplative heaven.
  - Aquinas: precise account of resurrection and visio Dei, with renewed cosmos as subordinate delight for perfected souls.
  - Calvin: insists on transformational, not annihilational, renewal of creation and integrates cosmic and personal eschatology via resurrection.
  - Geerhardus Vos: frames new creation within **inaugurated eschatology** and covenant history, emphasizing transformation and the original eschatological goal of creation.
  - N. T. Wright: contemporary stress on bodily resurrection and new creation against disembodied “heavenism,” with strong creational and missional emphases.
- Debates:
  - Internal: annihilation versus transformation of creation, primacy of **beatific vision** versus creational flourishing, balance of already–not yet.
  - External: Gnostic or Platonizing spiritualism and secular progressivist eschatologies, plus dispensational premillennial schemes separating earthly kingdom from final state.
  - Contemporary: ecological ethics and the charge of “otherworldliness,” physicalism and the intermediate state, cultural labor and its eschatological significance.
- Defended position: God will not discard but radically purify and transform this creation into a **new heavens and new earth**, continuous in identity yet gloriously discontinuous in mode, where resurrected believers, beholding God in Christ by the Spirit, exercise perfected human vocation forever in a concrete, embodied, and holy cosmos.
- Likely examiner questions and one-line answers:
  - Does 2 Peter 3 teach annihilation of the world? No, its fire imagery depicts comprehensive judgment and purification, not metaphysical replacement, consistent with Romans 8 and flood typology.
  - How do you relate the **beatific vision** to the new earth? The visio Dei in Christ is the central blessedness, exercised within and through embodied life in the renewed creation rather than apart from it.
  - Are you saying human cultural products survive into the new creation? Only what is purified and taken up in Christ’s kingdom endures; the mode of continuity is eschatologically reconstituted by God, not mechanically carried over.
  - How does this doctrine affect environmental ethics? It grounds respect and care for creation as destined for renewal, while keeping ultimate hope in divine consummation, not human management.
  - What is the intermediate state in relation to the new earth? A conscious, blessed, but provisional being-with-Christ in heaven, awaiting bodily resurrection and full life in the **new heavens and new earth**.
