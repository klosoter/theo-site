### TITLE
Corruption and Covenant: Interpreting the Fall and Its Consequences in Reformed Dogmatics

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How the historical Fall grounds universal human sin, guilt, and misery while preserving divine goodness, justice, and the coherence of **federal theology**.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, John Calvin, Francis Turretin, Karl Barth.
- Traditions: Augustinian–Western anthropology, medieval scholasticism, Reformation and post-Reformation Reformed orthodoxy, modern Protestant theology; engagement with Pelagian and semi-Pelagian alternatives.
- Debates: Internal Reformed disputes over **original guilt**, **concupiscence**, and covenant structures; external challenges from Pelagianism, Roman Catholic nature–grace schemas, and modern liberal or evolutionary accounts of sin; contemporary questions about historicity of Adam, structural evil, and pastoral psychology of depravity.
- Position: I will defend a historically grounded, Augustinian–Reformed account of a historical Fall, entailing **original sin** as guilt and corruption propagated in Adam as federal head, with pervasive noetic, moral, and social consequences, yet without destroying the integrity of human nature or evacuating moral responsibility.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of the Fall and its consequences is the dogmatic hinge between **creation**, **anthropology**, and **soteriology**. It explains why a good God creates a good world yet we inhabit one marked by radical evil and misery. To treat it adequately one must hold together three claims that examiners will press relentlessly: first, that the Fall is a real event in history, not a mere mythic cipher; second, that its effects are universal, profound, and inescapable; and third, that these effects do not render God the author of sin nor nullify human responsibility. The category that bears this weight in Western theology is **original sin**, understood as both an inherited guilt and an inborn corruption of nature, grounded in a primal act of covenant-breaking by Adam as **federal head** of humanity.

Augustine is the decisive architect of this structure. Situated in late antiquity, battling Pelagius in the early fifth century, he had to explain why infants die and why grace must be sovereign for anyone to turn to God. His key texts, especially his anti-Pelagian writings such as On the Grace of Christ and On Original Sin and On the Spirit and the Letter, articulate the central Augustinian claims that will shape the Western tradition. Adam was created in a state of original righteousness with the posse non peccare, the ability not to sin, and endowed with what later tradition calls **donum perseverantiae**, a supernatural gift enabling steadfastness. In Adam’s first sin, a disordered love of self and a turning from God, the entire human race participated. Augustine insists on the solidarity of humanity in Adam by appeal to Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15: in Adam all die, in Adam all sinned. Consequently, all are born with a **massa peccati**, a mass of sin, involving both liability to divine judgment and an inward bent away from God.

Examiners will immediately probe: Augustine speaks of concupiscence transmitted through procreation. Does that make sexuality itself sinful or God the author of a corrupt propagation mechanism? Augustine’s own account is often awkward and tied to his pre-modern biology, but the core conceptual move is sound: he distinguishes between nature as created and good and a vitiating quality inhering in that nature after the Fall. The corruption is not a substance or a positive created entity but a privation of original righteousness and a disordered orientation of the will. This allows Augustine to protect divine goodness. God creates the nature; sin is a defect arising from a free created will, not a positive thing God makes. Yet this defect is now propagated by ordinary generation, so that there is no point in the human story untouched by sin. The result is a strongly monergistic view of salvation: grace must precede and enable any good movement of the will.

Against Augustine, Pelagius argued that sin consists only in imitation and habit, not in inheritance. Each human is born in the same state as Adam, with the full ability to obey or disobey. Examiners will expect a crisp response why this is inadequate. The answer lies both exegetically and pastorally. Exegetically, Paul’s parallel between Adam and Christ in Romans 5 presupposes a representative structure: condemnation comes through one man’s disobedience, justification through one man’s obedience. If Adam’s sin affects us merely by example, by parity Christ’s righteousness would be merely illustrative. This guts forensic **justification**. Pastorally, a Pelagian account cannot do justice to the depth and universality of moral bondage that human experience and Scripture attest. Augustine’s emphasis on the radicality of sin protects the absolute necessity of grace and the glory of Christ’s work.

Thomas Aquinas receives and refines Augustine but within the broader Roman Catholic nature–grace schema. In the Summa Theologiae, especially the questions on original sin in the Prima Secundae, Aquinas distinguishes sharply between human nature as metaphysical essence and the **habitual grace** or original righteousness superadded in Eden. For Thomas, Adam was created with a rational nature ordered to God as its end, but the rectitude of his passions and the harmonious ordering of lower powers to reason derived from a gratuitous gift, not from bare nature. When Adam sinned, this gratuitous gift was withdrawn. The consequence is a loss of original justice and the onset of disorder in the appetites, which Thomas calls concupiscence. For him, **original sin** is formally the privation of original justice and materially the disordered disposition of the powers of the soul that follows.

This duality serves several functions. First, it intends to safeguard that God does not create a corrupt nature. Nature, as such, remains good, though wounded, weakened, and inclined to sin. Second, it allows Aquinas to affirm a natural capacity in humans to know and desire God as the author of nature, even in fallen condition, though incapable of attaining the beatific vision without grace. Examiners will point out that this structure differs from later Reformed emphases and may ask whether it softens the Fall by locating original righteousness not in nature as such but in a superadded gift. The critical Reformed response, while acknowledging the insight that grace is gratuitous, is to insist that the loss in the Fall is not merely of an accidental donum but of that righteousness without which human nature is not functioning as it ought before God. In other words, what Thomism calls superadded is from a covenantal standpoint essential to human flourishing. This does not deny the essence–accident distinction metaphysically but resituates it within a **covenant of works** framework: Adam is created upright, placed under a positive law, and promised eschatological life upon perfect obedience. Losing that righteousness is not just removal of a bonus but the forfeiture of a divinely ordained relation necessary for reaching the intended telos.

John Calvin represents the Reformation reappropriation of Augustine with a sharper emphasis on the covenantal and forensic dimensions. In the Institutes, especially book 2, Calvin defines **original sin** as the hereditary depravity and corruption of our nature, diffused into all parts of the soul, which first makes us guilty of God’s wrath and then brings forth the works which Scripture calls works of the flesh. Several correlative themes appear here that examiners will test. First, the order: guilt and corruption are inseparable but there is a priority of legal standing leading to moral bondage. Second, the extent: human depravity is total in the sense of pervasiveness, touching intellect, will, affections, and body, though not in the sense that humans are as evil as possible or lack all civil virtue. Third, Calvin intensifies the noetic effects of sin. Fallen humans know God in a sense yet constantly suppress this knowledge. Thus **noetic corruption** is central for a Reformed account of why natural theology is limited and why Scripture and the internal testimony of the Spirit are necessary.

Calvin is also decisive in clearly articulating Adam as federal head. Building on Romans 5, he maintains that Adam’s person contained the whole human nature; thus when he fell, all fell. This is not mere biological transmission, though that is the ordinary means by which we are joined to Adamic nature, but a judicial imputation of Adam’s guilt to his descendants. Examiners may object that this seems unjust: why should one be condemned for another’s act? The Reformed answer, which Calvin anticipates and later scholastics like Turretin develop, lies in the concept of **covenantal representation**. In civil life, covenant heads act for their corporate bodies, and their actions impose consequences on the many. We do not consider it unjust that a treaty signed by a ruler binds citizens or that a family’s fortunes are affected by a parent’s decisions. Moreover, the parallel with Christ is instructive. If one rejects alien guilt in Adam, one undermines the structure of alien righteousness in Christ. As Turretin will later insist, federal headship is the very structure of God’s dealings with humanity.

Francis Turretin, as a mature representative of Reformed orthodoxy in the seventeenth century, systematizes these themes with high precision. In his Institutes of Elenctic Theology, he treats **original sin** under the locus of the Fall with a series of carefully posed questions. Turretin distinguishes between the imputation of Adam’s first sin and the inherent corruption that follows. The former is the ground of condemnation even in infants who have not yet committed actual sins; the latter is the habitual inclination to evil. Internal Reformed debates appear here. Some Reformed theologians, often under the influence of John Owen or the covenantal refinement of Cocceius, stress slightly different orders between imputation and inborn corruption or debate the precise covenantal conditions God established with Adam. Turretin, representing a more classical line, strongly emphasizes the **covenant of works** as a real legal arrangement in which Adam’s obedience would have merited eschatological life by virtue of divine promise, not condign merit.

Examiners may push on whether the notion of imputing Adam’s guilt before any personal act on our part makes God arbitrary. Turretin’s response, and one worth defending, is that God’s justice is not measured by creaturely intuitions of autonomy but by the wisdom of his own counsel and revelation. The natural solidarity of the race, the covenantal structure of Scripture, and the symmetrical relation between Adam and Christ together form a coherent framework. Furthermore, Turretin is careful that the corruption derived from Adam is itself truly ours. It is not a foreign entity imposed extrinsically but our own heart’s disposition. Thus the doctrine sustains both a legal and an existential realism: we are condemned justly both for what we are in Adam and for what we willingly are and do.

Turning to Karl Barth introduces a modern reconfiguration of the Fall and its consequences, one that both illuminates and challenges classic formulations. In Church Dogmatics III and IV, Barth insists that one cannot speak of a self-contained state of pure nature or a primal covenant other than the covenant of grace in Christ. For Barth, election in Christ is the primal divine decision, and Adam is not a first covenant head set over against Christ but the human whose fallenness is already comprehended within Christ’s gracious election. Barth resists the idea of a prelapsarian Adam under a legal covenant of works, seeing in it a threat to the primacy of grace and the Christocentric character of all theology. On sin, Barth speaks more of an impossible possibility, a contradiction of the true being of humanity in Christ, than of an inherited guilt in a juridical sense. The Fall becomes paradigmatic for every human, depicting the universal turning from God, rather than a once-for-all covenantal rupture whose guilt is imputed.

Examiners influenced by Barthian sympathies will press whether a federal covenant of works unjustly subordinates Christ to Adam and whether a robust doctrine of inherited guilt is pastorally dangerous. A Reformed response must affirm Barth’s proper concern that Christ is the center and telos of creation and that grace is not an afterthought. Yet it must reject the collapsing of protology into soteriology in a way that obscures the historical reality of a good creation and a real Fall. To deny a distinct **covenant of works** is to evacuate the forensic structure of both sin and salvation. If there is no prelapsarian probation with a real threat and promise, then one cannot meaningfully say that Christ fulfills all righteousness as the second Adam by his active obedience. Moreover, if sin is defined primarily as an ontic impossibility or contradiction rather than as actual lawlessness under a violated covenant, the sense of real culpability before a holy God is dulled. In pastoral terms, people burdened by sin do not need to be told merely that their condition is an ontological contradiction but that they stand condemned under a broken covenant and that Christ as the second Adam has borne that curse and obeyed in their stead.

Within these broad trajectories we can now clarify the conceptual core of the Fall and its consequences. The Fall is the historical event in which the first human, Adam, under a positive divine command, turned from God in unbelief and disobedience, thereby violating the **covenant of works**. As a result, he lost communion with God, incurred the curse of the law, and became subject to death. Because he stood as **federal head** of the human race, his guilt is imputed to all his natural descendants, and from him they receive a nature corrupt in every faculty. This **original sin** has two inseparable aspects: **original guilt**, the judicial liability to condemnation before God, and **original pollution**, the inward corruption of soul and body that manifests in actual sins. The consequences are manifold: spiritual death and estrangement from God, physical mortality, disordered desires and **concupiscence**, noetic darkening, social alienation and injustice, and subjection of creation itself to futility.

Internal debates within the Reformed tradition revolve around the intensity and structure of these consequences. One issue concerns the status of concupiscence. Is every movement of desire toward created things, even involuntary, already sin, or only when consented to by the will? Augustine and later Reformed voices tend to treat concupiscence itself as sin, though distinguishable from more voluntary acts. This underlines the depth of corruption but raises questions about how Christ could assume a true human nature without sin. The Reformed answer is that Christ, though like us in all things, did not descend from Adam by ordinary generation and so did not inherit original sin. His human nature is true yet assumed in the hypostatic union without corruption.

Another internal dispute concerns the exact formal cause of our condemnation. Realist accounts emphasize a metaphysical participation in Adam; federal accounts stress covenantal imputation. Most Reformed theologians, following Calvin and Turretin, combine both, though they give priority to **imputation** to preserve the forensic structure of Romans 5. Examiners may ask whether this makes sin purely external. The answer is no: imputation always comes clothed with an inherited corruption, and the two are never separated in experience, though they can be distinguished conceptually for theological clarity.

Externally, the chief historical rival has been Pelagian and semi-Pelagian attempts to soften the consequences of the Fall. Semi-Pelagian positions, which became influential in certain medieval currents and in aspects of the Roman Catholic tradition prior to the Council of Trent, admit that the Fall wounds or weakens nature but deny that it entails total inability. They maintain that the first movement toward God can be made by free will assisted by general grace, with God then granting further grace in response. The Reformed critique here, building on Augustine, is that such a view misconstrues the consequence of the Fall as mere sickness instead of death. Ephesians 2 describes fallen humanity as dead in trespasses and sins. Deadness is not partial. Moreover, the semi-Pelagian framework blurs the line between **nature** and **grace**, making grace in effect a cooperative supplement to a still autonomous will rather than a resurrecting power. This has knock-on effects for assurance and for the doxological focus of salvation.

Contemporary external challenges arise not only from explicit Pelagianism but from modern liberal theology, existentialism, and evolutionary accounts of human origins. Denial of a historical Adam or of a punctiliar Fall functions to reinterpret sin as merely the universal but inevitable result of finite freedom in a process of development. In such accounts, there is no state of original righteousness; so-called Fall narratives are mythic projections of adult self-consciousness. Examiners will press here on how a Reformed dogmatician engages questions of science and history. The position I defend is that confessional commitments to a historical Adam and a real transition from innocence to guilt are non-negotiable for maintaining the biblical doctrines of **federal representation**, **original sin**, and the parallel between Adam and Christ. While there is room for intramural discussion on the mode of the narrative and the length of prelapsarian history, the dogmatic stakes of evacuating historicity are severe. Without a real Fall there is only gradual emergence of moral awareness, and sin becomes inevitable and therefore excusable rather than culpable lawlessness.

At the same time, contemporary theology has brought helpful nuances, particularly in highlighting structural and systemic dimensions of sin. The Fall’s consequences do not terminate at the level of individual hearts but permeate institutions, cultures, and creation itself. **Structural evil** is not an alternative to original sin but one of its social expressions. Romans 8 speaks of creation subjected to futility because of human sin. A Reformed account can and should affirm that fallen humans, acting corporately, generate unjust systems, entrenched patterns of exploitation, and cultural liturgies that further enslave. Yet a biblical anthropology insists these structures are derivative: they are generated by sinful persons under Adamic headship, not autonomous entities bearing guilt on their own. This guards against shifting blame entirely to “systems” or “structures” and preserves the personal character of sin before God.

Another contemporary question concerns the psychological and pastoral use of the doctrine of total depravity. Critics argue that strong language about human corruption destroys self-worth and can fuel abuse. A confessional response must be clear that **total depravity** names the pervasiveness of sin relative to God, not the absence of all worth or dignity. Because humans remain image bearers even after the Fall, there is real though fractured goodness and capacity for civil righteousness. The doctrine humbles pride and undermines self-salvation projects, but it also equalizes all humans under sin and opens the door to a robust doctrine of **common grace**. Pastoral deployment should avoid morbid introspection and instead drive sinners to the sufficiency of Christ as the second Adam. Far from crushing, the awareness of one’s deep corruption, when paired with the greater grace of God in Christ, becomes liberating.

Finally, the doctrine of the Fall intersects with eschatology. The story is not creation–Fall–redemption as mere restoration of Eden, but creation–Fall–redemption–consummation. The **covenant of works** that Adam broke finds its fulfilment in Christ’s **active and passive obedience**, and the eschatological life held out to Adam is realized in a higher, irrevocable state in the new creation. The consequences of the Fall are therefore not merely reversed but overcome and transfigured. Yet the seriousness of those consequences explains the Cross: only a representative who is both truly God and truly man, free from original sin and actual sin, bearing imputed guilt as federal head of a new humanity, can undo what Adam did and more. If one weakens the Fall, one inevitably diminishes the Cross.

In summary, a Westminster candidate must hold and defend that the Fall is a historical, covenantal rupture by a real first man, Adam, whose representative disobedience plunges the race into guilt and corruption. Augustine’s emphasis on inherited sin and the bondage of the will, Aquinas’s careful distinction of nature and grace, Calvin’s focus on total depravity and federal headship, Turretin’s precision on imputation and the **covenant of works**, and Barth’s Christocentric protest against any non-gracious order together form the field of debate. The Reformed position I defend appropriates Augustine’s realism, criticizes Aquinas’s superadded schema at the covenantal level while learning from his metaphysical clarity, follows Calvin and Turretin in affirming **original guilt** and **pollution** under a real covenant of works, and acknowledges Barth’s insistence on Christ’s primacy while resisting his dissolution of protology into soteriology. The Fall and its consequences thus stand as a central test case for one’s doctrine of God, man, Christ, and salvation, demanding a coherent, courageous confession that can withstand both historical scrutiny and contemporary pressure.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The Fall is the historical, covenantal disobedience of Adam as **federal head**, by which humanity lost original righteousness, incurred **original guilt**, and inherited pervasive **corruption** of nature with universal spiritual, moral, social, and cosmic consequences.
- Decisive figures: Augustine defends inherited guilt and bondage of the will against Pelagius; Aquinas frames **original sin** as loss of superadded justice and resultant concupiscence within a nature–grace distinction; Calvin sharpens total depravity, noetic corruption, and federal headship; Turretin systematizes **covenant of works**, imputation, and original pollution; Barth reconfigures the Fall within a Christocentric election, questioning a distinct covenant of works and juridical imputation.
- Debates: Internal Reformed disputes over nature of concupiscence, order of imputation and corruption, and the precise structure of the **covenant of works**; external conflicts with Pelagian and semi-Pelagian minimization of inherited sin, Roman Catholic nature–grace schemas, and modern liberal or evolutionary denials of a historical Fall; contemporary issues around historicity of Adam, structural evil, psychological reception of depravity, and theodicy.
- Defended position: An Augustinian–Reformed account affirming a historical Adam and Fall, Adam’s federal headship, **original sin** as imputed guilt and inherent corruption, total but not absolute depravity, and the necessity of Christ as second Adam to fulfill the broken covenant of works and inaugurate the new creation.
- Likely examiner questions and one-line answers:
  - Is concupiscence itself sin? Yes, as a disordered inclination contrary to God’s law, though differing in voluntariness and aggravation from deliberate acts.
  - Why is imputation of Adam’s sin just? Because God ordained a wise **federal** arrangement where Adam truly represented us, paralleling the gracious imputation of Christ’s righteousness.
  - How does the Fall affect the image of God? The image is marred and corrupted but not destroyed; functional righteousness is lost, structural capacities remain.
  - Can one be Reformed and deny a historical Adam? No, because doing so undercuts **federal theology**, **original sin**, and Paul’s Adam–Christ parallel.
  - Does total depravity eliminate responsibility? No, inability is moral, not coercive; humans freely choose according to their corrupt nature and are accountable to God’s revealed law.
