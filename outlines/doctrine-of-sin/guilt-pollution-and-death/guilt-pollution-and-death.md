### TITLE
Guilt, pollution, and death: the threefold wound of sin and the one Christic cure

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Doctrine of Sin within Anthropology and Soteriology**, framed by creation-fall-redemption-consummation across 2nd–21st centuries.
- Core question: How do **guilt, pollution, and death** describe the effects of sin, and how does **union with Christ** remedy each in justification, sanctification, and glorification.
- Principal figures or schools: **Augustine vs Pelagius** at Carthage 418 and Orange 529; **Aquinas** on privation; **Luther and Calvin** on forensic and ontic damage; **Trent** 1546–1563; **Westminster** 1643–1649; **Old Princeton and Dutch Neo-Calvinism** 19th century.

- Legacy cue or continuing debate: **Historical Adam, death before the fall, structural sin, penal substitution debates**, and **analytic grounding of imputation**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The doctrine names the threefold effect of sin as **guilt, pollution, and death**. Guilt speaks to objective liability before **divine justice**, the standing of the sinner under law and wrath. Pollution speaks to disordered inclination and the stain of **concupiscence**, a corruption that both disables and inclines the will. Death speaks to the penal consequence of sin as **spiritual, physical, and eternal separation**, culminating in the second death. These three belong to **Anthropology** as the condition of fallen humanity and to **Soteriology** because salvation answers them with forgiveness, cleansing, and life.

Dogmatically, guilt is ordered to **justification**, pollution to **sanctification**, and death to **resurrection and glorification**. In classic Reformed idiom, union with Christ is the fountainhead from which flow a double grace, **justification and sanctification**, with glorification as the telos. The marring of the imago Dei through **original sin** establishes the pervasiveness of the problem across persons and powers, and the triune work of God answers it through **the Father’s justifying verdict, the Son’s atoning mediation, and the Spirit’s purifying indwelling**.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture portrays guilt with juridical clarity. In **Romans 3.9–26**, all are under sin and the law shuts every mouth, so that God might be just and the justifier through **propitiation in Christ**. The Greek *hamartia* names transgression, and terms like *opheilēma* and *paraptōma* evoke debt and trespass, grounding **forensic liability**. **Romans 5.12–21** narrates Adam and Christ as two heads, so that condemnation comes through the one and **justification of life** through the other, a matrix for imputation and corporate guilt.

Pollution is narrated in cultic and moral idioms. Leviticus 16 and the Day of Atonement portray defilement of persons and sanctuary, with *kippēr* covering and *taher* cleansing, mapping toward **sacrificial cleansing**. The Greek *akatharsia* and *miasma* show in **Mark 7.20–23** that defilement arises from the heart, not merely from ritual contact, and **Psalm 51** unites guilt and stain, asking for both forgiveness and cleansing. **Isaiah 6** combines woe for guilt with the purging coal, a canonical emblem of **atoning purification**.

Death is the announced wage. **Genesis 2.17** warns that in the day of eating there is dying, and **Genesis 3** inaugurates alienation and mortality. **Romans 6.23** declares the wages of sin is death, while **Ephesians 2.1–3** depicts the living dead under wrath, a description of **spiritual death**. **1 Corinthians 15.21–22, 45–49** binds death to Adam and life to the last Adam, linking **resurrection life** to the defeat of death’s reign.

Exegetically, the three are distinct yet interlocked. The idiom of **legal guilt** grounds the necessity of pardon, the idiom of **staining defilement** grounds the necessity of purification, and the idiom of **death’s dominion** grounds the necessity of vivification. The Old Testament’s *asham* guilt offering and *hattat* sin offering foreshadow Christ’s cross as both **expiation and purification**, while the New Testament speaks of sprinkling and washing, *katharismos* and *palingenesia*, grounding **baptismal cleansing and new creation**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic theology framed the basic contours. **Irenaeus** articulated recapitulation, by which Christ retraces Adam to heal **corruption and death**, while **Athanasius** in On the Incarnation argued that the Word assumes flesh to rescue humanity from **corruption and mortality**. **Augustine** systematized the relation of guilt and concupiscence, naming **original sin** as both inherited fault and damaged will. The condemnation of Pelagius at **Carthage 418** and the anti-semi-Pelagian canons of **Orange 529** affirmed the necessity of grace for cleansing and life, and the reality of inherited **culpability and corruption**.

In the medieval West, **Anselm’s Cur Deus Homo** depicted sin as a failure to render due honor, intensifying **objective guilt** requiring satisfaction. **Aquinas** presented sin as privation of order, loss of original justice, and consequent **concupiscence** as a disordered appetite. He held that baptism removes guilt and eternal punishment, leaving concupiscence as a remnant of **pollution** to be healed through infused virtues and sacramental life, thus coordinating **forgiveness and healing**.

Reformation theology sharpened forensic and sanctificatory distinctions. **Luther** insisted on **simul iustus et peccator**, the believer as righteous by imputed righteousness and yet polluted in the old Adam. **Calvin** stressed duplex gratia from union with Christ, linking **justification for guilt** and **sanctification for pollution**, while naming death the last enemy conquered by **resurrection**. Post-Reformation scholastics like **Turretin** and **Owen** refined **immediate imputation** and total depravity, integrating penalty, pollution, and death within federal theology and **covenant of works and grace** frames.

The **Council of Trent 1546–1563** responded by affirming that original sin is the deprivation of original justice, that guilt is removed by **baptism**, and that concupiscence remains but is not sin properly in the baptized, thereby distinguishing ongoing **pollution** from culpable fault. Early modern and modern voices diversified. **Edwards** explored moral inability and the nature of true virtue, analyzing **pollution as dispositional bent**, while **Schleiermacher** construed sin as sickness of God-consciousness, reframing **guilt** as internal to piety. **Barth** reoriented sin under the doctrine of reconciliation, treating **nothingness** as parasitic, while keeping death as the great reality unmasked by **the cross and resurrection**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Westminster tradition codified the triad. **Westminster Confession of Faith 1646, chapter 6** teaches that by Adam’s fall we are made guilty, the guilt imputed, the nature corrupted, and all parts of soul and body defiled, therefore subject to **death in all miseries**. The Larger Catechism specifies **original sin** as the sin of our first parents imputed and the corruption of nature conveyed, from which proceed actual transgressions, and assigns the miseries of **this life, death itself, and the pains of hell** as the wages of sin.

Old Princeton, represented by **Charles Hodge 1871–1873** and **B. B. Warfield 1902**, defended **immediate imputation** of Adam’s guilt grounded in federal headship, while arguing that depravity is real and pervasive **pollution** inherited through generation. **W. G. T. Shedd 1872** strengthened a traducian account to explain the propagation of corruption, though he kept **forensic guilt** under federal unity. Their hallmark was to coordinate legal and ontic accounts under **covenantal representation**.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism, through **Abraham Kuyper 1898** and **Herman Bavinck 1895–1901**, emphasized the organic solidarity of humanity in Adam and the cosmic reach of **sin’s pollution**. Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics binds guilt, pollution, and death to the history of creation and re-creation, with **common grace** restraining corruption and **particular grace** renewing in Christ, and with **resurrection** as the definitive victory over death. Kuyper’s antithesis highlighted structural and cultural expressions of **defilement**, while insisting that **justification** as verdict and **sanctification** as renewal flow from union with Christ.

Across these streams, three claims hold. First, **guilt is objective liability** remitted by Christ’s satisfaction and righteousness imputed. Second, **pollution is inherent corruption** progressively mortified and vivified by the Spirit. Third, **death is penal and tyrannical**, defeated by Christ’s bodily resurrection and ours in Him. The Reformed coherence rests on **federal headship, union with Christ, and the ordo salutis**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic teaching, classically at **Trent 1546**, names original sin as loss of original justice. **Guilt is removed by baptism**, restoring friendship with God, yet **concupiscence remains** as a wound rather than sin properly in the regenerate. Penitential practice addresses post-baptismal guilt, while infused charity and the virtues heal **pollution**. Death remains as temporal penalty that becomes medicinal in the saints, with **purgation** purifying persistent stains.

Lutheran theology centers **simul iustus et peccator** and insists that concupiscence is sin. **Forensic justification** removes guilt entirely by the alien righteousness of Christ, while sanctification begins but does not eradicate **pollution** in this life. Death is conquered in promise through **baptismal union** with Christ and in fact at the last day, with a robust insistence that the law condemns in order that the gospel might absolve, preserving the law-gospel distinction across **guilt and cleansing**.

Eastern Orthodoxy emphasizes **ancestral sin** as mortality and corruption rather than inherited culpability. Humanity inherits **death and corruption** from Adam, which then engender actual sins, and the emphasis falls on healing and deification through **theosis**. The liturgy and sacramental life cleanse **pollution** and transfigure death through Christ’s Pascha, while the West’s strong claims about **imputed guilt** are often resisted.

Modern projects vary. **Schleiermacher 1799** prioritizes religious consciousness, recasting **guilt** as alienation within the communal life of the Redeemer. **Niebuhr mid-20th century** retrieves a realist account of pride and sensuality as social and personal **pollution**, while **Girard** interprets sin and death through mimetic violence, with **guilt** displaced onto scapegoats. In analytic theology, debates focus on the metaphysics of **imputation**, the distinction between **liability and blameworthiness**, and corporate responsibility, with proposals for grounding **original guilt** in divine-covenantal appointment rather than biological transmission.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Scientific accounts of human origins pressure readings of **Romans 5** and **1 Corinthians 15**. The question whether there was **death before the fall** in animal realms probes how to frame death as a wage of sin. Reformed theologians increasingly distinguish human death as judicial within the covenant from biological mortality in creation, attempting to preserve **penal death** without denying prelapsarian creaturely finitude.

Penal substitution is contested by those who fear it exaggerates **guilt** and neglects **pollution**, or reduces death to mere penalty. Defenders emphasize a both and account, holding that **Christ as sin-bearer** both removes liability and breaks the enslaving power of sin and death, uniting **expiation and liberation**. Trauma-informed pastoral theology worries that an exclusive focus on guilt crushes the wounded, arguing for robust accounts of **defilement and cleansing** drawn from biblical purity imagery.

Social and structural accounts of sin underline **pollution** as embedded in institutions, economies, and cultures. The Reformed neo-Calvinist legacy provides resources for naming **structural defilement** while maintaining **personal guilt**, arguing that Christ delivers from both. Analytic discussions of free will and moral luck revisit whether inherited corruption compromises **culpability**, prompting nuanced accounts of responsibility under **enslaved wills** and liberating grace.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Under **Theology Proper**, the triune God addresses the triad of sin according to the divine missions. The Father judges and justifies, the Son atones and mediates, the Spirit indwells to purify, so that **divine justice and mercy** meet in the cross. This safeguards that **guilt** is not erased by fiat but answered by the **obedience and blood of Christ**, and that **pollution** is not merely overlooked but sanctified by the **Spirit of holiness**.

In **Christology**, the incarnate Son assumes our nature to the root of its **corruption and mortality**. By His passive and active obedience He bears **guilt** and attains righteousness, by His death He undergoes **death’s curse**, and by His resurrection He begins the new creation. The communicatio of benefits in union with Christ distributes **forgiveness, cleansing, and life**, mapping precisely onto guilt, pollution, and death.

In **Anthropology**, the image of God remains real though marred. **Original sin** as privation and depravation explains human incapacity and inclination, and locates the will’s bondage within a broader disorder of affects. In **Ecclesiology**, the church administers the Word and sacraments, declaring **absolution** for guilt, nurturing **sanctification** for pollution, and sustaining hope in **the resurrection of the body**. In **Eschatology**, the defeat of the last enemy secures **death’s end** and the cleansing of creation, so that righteousness dwells.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, distinguishing **guilt, pollution, and death** allows discriminating care. To the guilty, we preach **justification by faith** and the objective verdict of pardon. To the polluted, we catechize in practices of **mortification and vivification**, cultivating virtue by the Spirit’s power. To those fearing death, we proclaim **resurrection hope**, the defeat of the enemy and the presence of Christ at the graveside.

Liturgically, Christian worship dramatizes the triad. Confession and assurance enact **forgiveness of guilt**, baptism enacts **washing and regeneration**, and the Eucharist nourishes unto **life everlasting**. The calendar trains penitence in Lent, joy in Easter, and hope at All Saints, while funerals tell the truth that **death is an enemy** that has lost its sting. Reformed liturgies often include an **assurance of pardon** after confession, and catechisms like the **Heidelberg 1563** teach comfort in belonging to Christ in **life and in death**.

Practically, counseling must name shame and stain with the Bible’s vocabulary of **defilement and cleansing**. Social ministries must address **structural pollution**, systems that deform desire and action, while never eclipsing **personal guilt**. The church should teach believers to confess specifically, to trust the **alien righteousness of Christ**, to pursue holiness concretely, and to grieve with hope in the face of **mortality**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
In Adam we incur **guilt, pollution, and death**, and in Christ crucified and risen we receive **pardon, purgation, and life**.
