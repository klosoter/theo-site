# Cornelius Van Til (1895–1987)

Cornelius Van Til approached the doctrine of **atonement** within the framework of **Reformed orthodoxy**, emphasizing its **necessity** as rooted in the character of the **triune God** and the demands of **God’s justice**. He affirmed the **definite (or limited) atonement** of Christ, insisting that the atonement is efficacious for the elect alone, and critiqued alternative theories as inconsistent with a truly biblical, presuppositional approach.

- "The atonement is necessary because God cannot deny himself, and his justice must be satisfied." ("The Defense of the Faith")

## Key Works

- The Defense of the Faith
- Christian Theistic Ethics
- An Introduction to Systematic Theology
- Common Grace and the Gospel
- Essays on Christian Education

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Atonement is grounded in the self-consistency of God**
    - God’s **justice** and **holiness** require satisfaction for sin.
    - The atonement is not arbitrary but flows from God’s immutable character.
    - Any denial of the necessity of atonement undermines the coherence of the Christian worldview.
    - The atonement is a demonstration of both God’s love and justice, not one at the expense of the other.
    - "God cannot forgive sin without satisfaction to his justice." (An Introduction to Systematic Theology)

2. **Necessity of penal substitution**
    - Van Til upheld the **penal substitutionary** theory as biblically mandated.
    - Christ bears the penalty due to the elect, satisfying divine wrath.
    - Alternative theories (moral influence, governmental) are seen as concessions to autonomous human reasoning.
    - The cross is the only solution consistent with the antithesis between belief and unbelief.
    - "The cross is the place where the justice and mercy of God meet in perfect harmony." (The Defense of the Faith)

3. **Extent of the atonement is definite**
    - Christ’s atonement is intended for and applied to the **elect** alone.
    - Universal atonement theories compromise the efficacy and purpose of Christ’s work.
    - The atonement actually secures salvation, not merely makes it possible.
    - This commitment is rooted in the **covenantal** structure of redemption.
    - "The atonement is particular, not general; it is for those whom the Father has given to the Son." (Christian Theistic Ethics)

4. **Atonement and the antithesis**
    - The atonement is intelligible only within a presuppositional, Christian worldview.
    - Non-Christian theories of atonement inevitably distort its meaning.
    - The antithesis between belief and unbelief is manifest in how the atonement is understood.
    - Apologetic engagement must presuppose the necessity and particularity of Christ’s work.
    - "The natural man will always misconstrue the meaning of the cross unless his presuppositions are challenged." (The Defense of the Faith)

5. **Trinitarian context of the atonement**
    - The atonement is the work of the **triune God**: planned by the Father, accomplished by the Son, applied by the Spirit.
    - Any theory that neglects the Trinity fails to do justice to the biblical data.
    - The unity and diversity of the Godhead are reflected in the atonement’s design and application.
    - The atonement is not merely a moral example but a redemptive act rooted in intra-Trinitarian covenant.
    - "The atonement is the outworking of the eternal counsel of the triune God." (An Introduction to Systematic Theology)

## Legacy and Impact

Van Til’s approach to the **atonement** reinforced the centrality of **penal substitution** and the necessity of a presuppositional framework for understanding its meaning and extent. His insistence on the **definite atonement** and the antithesis between Christian and non-Christian thought has shaped Reformed apologetics and systematic theology. Van Til’s legacy endures in the ongoing defense of a robust, confessional doctrine of atonement within the Reformed tradition.
