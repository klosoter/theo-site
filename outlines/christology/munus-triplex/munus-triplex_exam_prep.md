### TITLE
One Mediator, Three Offices: Integrating Prophetic Revelation, Priestly Reconciliation, and Kingly Rule without Fragmenting Christ

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core conceptual issue: whether the **munus triplex** is a merely heuristic grid or a biblically grounded, dogmatically necessary account of the one Mediator’s unified work that safeguards the relation of revelation, redemption, and reign.
- Decisive figures: John Calvin, John Owen, Francis Turretin, Herman Bavinck, Karl Barth, Geerhardus Vos.
- Traditions: Reformed confessional and scholastic theology, Puritan exegesis, Reformed biblical theology, modern dialectical theology.
- Debates:
  - Internal: the primacy and coordination of the offices, their relation to the states of humiliation and exaltation, and their extension to church offices under **federal theology**.
  - External: Lutheran and Roman alternatives, Socinian and liberal reductions, and political-theological distortions of kingship.
  - Contemporary: atonement disputes, church and public square, charismatic-prophetic claims, and the scope of Christ’s present reign.
- Position: Defend the classic Reformed account as biblically warranted and necessary for dogmatic coherence, with the priestly office as the soteriological center and the prophetic and kingly offices organically coordinated, executed by the one person of the Son in the economy of salvation, and extended to the church only analogically and ministerially.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of the munus triplex names the unified mediatorial work of Christ under three coordinated offices: Prophet who reveals God’s will, Priest who reconciles sinners through sacrifice and intercession, and King who conquers enemies and rules his people. The point is not to portion Christ into functional fragments but to identify, within the one incarnate person acting according to both natures, the distinct modes by which he fulfills the entire economy of revelation, redemption, and consummation. The claim is that Scripture does not present a loose collage of Christ’s acts but a coherent Messianic vocation grounded in anointing with the Spirit for Word, altar, and throne. When rightly understood under **divine simplicity** and the **inseparable operations** of the Trinity, the munus triplex secures both the unity of Christ’s work and the ordered relations among its aspects.

John Calvin stands as the decisive modern catalyst. In Institutes 2.15, Calvin situates the offices in the biblical logic of Messiah as the anointed one. He does not speak of three disparate roles but of three lines of Israel’s history that converge on the Son, each grounded in promise. Deuteronomy 18 provides the prophetic expectation, Psalm 110 and Leviticus anticipate a priest after the order of Melchizedek, and 2 Samuel 7 and Psalm 2 center Davidic kingship. For Calvin, the offices are not an imported schema but the grammar of how the covenant peoples’ needs are fulfilled. Calvin’s method is not speculative but exegetical and pastoral. Christ must be Prophet because sinners stand in ignorance and rebellion; he must be Priest because guilt demands expiation and intercession; he must be King because the enemies of God, internal and external, must be subdued and the new life ordered. Calvin’s contribution is to make the munus triplex the architectonic of Christ’s mediatorial work, arguing that without it doctrines like **atonement**, sanctification, and ecclesial authority float unmoored from the person and mission of the Son. He decisively resists any suggestion that one office could be abstracted from the others. Christ’s prophetic Word interprets his priestly cross, and his kingly rule applies and protects the benefits revealed and won.

To defend the thesis that the munus triplex is biblically warranted rather than a Calvinist invention, one must show how Scripture presents these offices not as metaphors but as covenantal offices gathered up in the Messiah. Isaiah 61 and Luke 4 display the anointing of the Spirit as the commissioning of the Servant to proclaim, to bind up, to liberate. Acts 3 explicitly names Jesus the prophet like Moses. Hebrews 5 to 10 is explicit on his priesthood, grounding it in divine oath and eternal intercession. The Gospels crown him King in David’s city and at the cross ironically enthroned. Zechariah 6 foresees priestly and royal unification in one person. The biblical-theological development of these strands is given classic articulation in Geerhardus Vos, whose work demonstrates that the offices are not prooftexts but the crescendo of redemptive history. Vos shows that Christ as the eschatological Prophet does not merely deliver new information but inaugurates the last days in which the knowledge of the Lord covers the earth; as Priest he offers the once-for-all sacrifice that renders obsolete the Levitical shadow; as King he inaugurates the kingdom that is already present yet not yet consummated. Vos’s method secures the eschatological unity of the offices and guards against dehistoricized dogmatics.

The Puritan exegete and theologian John Owen intensifies the priestly center of the doctrine without disjoining it from the other offices. In his Hebrews commentary and The Death of Death, Owen locates Christ’s priesthood in the **covenant of redemption**, the eternal counsel in which the Father appoints the Son as Mediator and Surety and the Son willingly undertakes to fulfill the law and bear the penalty due to the elect. Owen’s concern is both exegetical and polemical. Against Socinians who deny vicarious satisfaction, Owen argues that priesthood by definition entails oblation and intercession and that Scripture attributes to Christ a sacrificial act that is substitutionary, penal, and definite. The office is not a bare metaphor for divine love but a public, forensic act under God’s justice. Moreover, Owen insists that Christ’s priestly oblation is coordinated by his kingly authority and interpreted by his prophetic Word. The cross is not a tragic interruption of kingship but its paradoxical enthronement, and the prophetic proclamation of the gospel is not external to the oblation but the Spirit’s authorized interpretation of its efficacy. Owen’s precision about persons and natures is important here. The priesthood belongs to the person of the Son incarnate. He offers himself according to his human nature yet with the infinite worth of his person. This guards the munus triplex against Nestorian partition. When examiners press whether Christ sacrificed as man only or as God and man, the answer is that the act is theandric, belonging to the person, performed through the human nature, and of infinite value because of the dignity of the person. The same unity obtains for prophecy and kingship. As Prophet, he reveals the Father not from a merely human perspective but as the eternal Son who knows the Father and makes him known through a sanctified human mind. As King, he exercises royal authority by virtue of his exaltation according to his human nature into participation in the royal administration of the kingdom while never divesting his divine sovereignty.

Francis Turretin systematizes the doctrine under the scholastic rubric with polemical clarity. In the Institutes of Elenctic Theology, he treats the offices in correlation with the states of humiliation and exaltation. For Turretin, Christ executes prophetic, priestly, and kingly offices in both states, though in diverse modes. His prophetic office culminates in the earthly ministry and continues in heavenly teaching through the Spirit’s illumination of the Word. His priestly office centers in the once-for-all oblation in humiliation and continues perpetually in intercession in exaltation. His kingly office is exercised paradoxically at the cross where he triumphs over principalities and powers and then in manifest rule from the right hand. Turretin’s contribution is the orchestration of the offices with the two states and the precise identification of what belongs to satisfaction and what belongs to application. He also clarifies how the munus triplex relates to church offices. Pastors are not prophets in a revelatory sense; their ministry is ministerial, declarative, and derivative. The church’s discipline exercises keys of the kingdom under Christ’s kingship but wields no civil sword. This guards against both Anabaptist enthusiasms and Erastian capture of the church. When examiners ask whether the munus triplex implies an ecclesial theocracy, the scholastic answer is that Christ’s kingship is spiritual in its present administration, real and cosmic, but his church’s authority is strictly ministerial under the Word.

Herman Bavinck retrieves and balances the doctrine in the modern era, insisting on both its biblical-theological rootedness and its dogmatic necessity. For Bavinck, the offices are not arbitrary titles but the form of Christ’s mediation. He stresses that the three offices are mutually implicative. The prophetic Word does not merely precede the work of the priest but is itself redemptive, for revelation is a mode of reconciliation when it brings the knowledge of God that is life. The kingly rule is not mere aftermath but the ongoing application and protection of redemption. Yet Bavinck preserves the soteriological center of the priesthood: without reconciliation there is no durable rule of peace and no saving knowledge. Bavinck uses the munus triplex to integrate ecclesiology, sacramentology, and ethics. The ministry of the Word is the extension of Christ’s prophetic office; the sacraments are the embodied, covenantal speech of the Priest-King, sealing what his oblation procured and his reign administers; Christian ethics flows from union with the Priest who has cleansed, the Prophet who enlightens, and the King who commands. Bavinck also engages the cultural question. Christ’s kingship is cosmic. All authority in heaven and earth has been given to him. But the way this kingship is mediated in history is through the church’s mission and through providential rule, not through ecclesial domination of civil structures. This positions the munus triplex as an antidote to both privatized piety and politicized triumphalism.

Karl Barth reconfigures the three offices within his doctrine of reconciliation, and his distinctive appropriation must be confronted because it shapes much contemporary dogmatics. In Church Dogmatics IV, Barth structures reconciliation under Christ as true Witness, true Servant, and true Lord, mapping broadly onto Prophet, Priest, and King. Barth aims to preserve the unity of Christ’s work as God for man and man for God, rejecting older **federal theology** that grounds the munus triplex in an eternal pact between the Father and the Son. He worries that the pactum salutis introduces a quasi-contract that threatens **divine simplicity** and yields a predestinarian logic that abstracts from Jesus Christ. The gain in Barth is his insistence that all of Christ’s offices are functions of his person and that election is fulfilled in him. His emphasis on Christ’s Lordship as the grace that liberates human freedom corrects an anemic account of kingship. Yet Barth’s reconstruction loses the forensic and priestly center when he resists speaking of penal satisfaction and when he universalizes reconciliation in ways that detach the office of Priest from particularity and definite atonement. In response, the Reformed account insists that the munus triplex is not a conceptual matrix imposed on reconciliation but the biblical grammar that ensures that God’s revelation addresses human ignorance, his priestly act resolves guilt in law-governed terms, and his royal claim subdues enemies. The pactum salutis, far from reifying intratrinitarian contracts, articulates the trinitarian willing of redemption such that the Son is appointed Mediator and undertakes freely to fulfill the law. Hebrews speaks of oath and appointment. The economy reveals, without dividing, the **inseparable operations**. Properly construed, **federal theology** protects not threatens **divine simplicity** by locating plurality in the economy, not in the essence.

Internal debates in the Reformed tradition turn on sequencing and extension. Is the priestly office architectonic, or should one center kingship or prophecy? Calvin often places kingship last but insists on unity. Owen urges priestly centrality for soteriology. Bavinck refuses to privilege any in abstraction but tacitly centers the priestly office by arguing that only a reconciled people can be taught savingly and ruled peaceably. The position defended here follows this pattern: the priestly office is the soteriological center because expiation and reconciliation are the hinge without which prophetic light is condemnation and kingly power is judgment. But the priestly office cannot be isolated. The cross is a royal victory and a prophetic disclosure. The resurrected Lord teaches through the Spirit and rules through his Word. The offices are perichoretic in the economy, executed by the same person, and distinguished by their formal objects. Such an account offers coherence across loci. In Scripture, what Christ says is what his cross means. In sacrament, what his Supper seals is what his kingdom sustains.

A second internal debate concerns the relation of the offices to the states of humiliation and exaltation. Some narratives locate prophecy chiefly in humiliation, priesthood at the cross, and kingship in exaltation. Turretin wisely resists this oversimplification. Christ prophesies now from heaven through the Spirit’s ministry of the Word. He rules at the cross and in resurrection. His priestly intercession is a distinct and ongoing act in exaltation. Precision here matters because it directs pastoral application. The believer’s assurance is anchored not only in a past oblation but in a present Priest who ever lives to intercede. The church’s confidence in mission is grounded not only in past teaching but in a living Prophet who opens hearts. And the patience of the saints rests not only in a future kingdom but in a present King who subdues, rules, and defends.

External debates test the doctrine’s coherence. Lutheran dogmatics affirms the three offices but organizes Christology around the genus of the communication of properties and the two states rather than around the three offices. The difference is not merely formal. The Lutheran stress on the ubiquity of Christ’s human nature in exaltation reconfigures priestly presence in the Supper in a way that Reformed theology rejects. The Reformed insist that the priestly office of Christ is exercised from heaven by the Spirit, not by local bodily presence on many altars, and that the sacraments are means of grace exactly because the exalted Priest-King keeps his promise to be present by the Spirit through instituted means. Roman Catholic theology venerates Christ as King and Priest but embeds priesthood within a sacramental economy that multiplies sacrificial acts. Hebrews, however, speaks of once-for-all oblation and an unrepeatable sacrifice. The church’s priesthood is participatory and ministerial, not propitiatory. Socinian and liberal theologies present the most radical external challenge by reducing Christ to a prophetic exemplar of love and kingship to moral influence. In the face of these reductions, the munus triplex functions as a bulwark. If Christ is Prophet only, revelation condemns rather than saves because guilt remains. If he is Priest only, grace becomes unintelligible and arbitrary without explication by the prophetic Word. If he is King only, life becomes sheer command without reconciliation or illumination. The biblical Christ must be all three.

Contemporary debates demand that the doctrine be tested on the field of public theology, ecclesial practice, and soteriological controversy. Advances of Christian nationalism and its reaction expose confusions about Christ’s kingship. The Reformed tradition, drawing from Calvin and developed by Turretin and Bavinck, refuses Erastianism by insisting that Christ’s rule over his church is spiritual and ministerial and that the church’s authority is limited to Word, sacrament, and discipline. The **two kingdoms** framework orients the church to its distinctive mandates under Christ’s universal kingship. The claim that asserting Christ’s kingship licenses coercive ecclesial rule over civil society betrays the nature of his present administration and the weapons he has appointed. On the other hand, the privatization of kingship that confines it to inward piety denies his cosmic authority. The munus triplex gives a balanced path. The church proclaims Christ’s prophetic Word to the nations, calls rulers to justice under the King, and rests in the Priest whose intercession sustains mission in the midst of opposition.

The atonement debate is the sharpest test. Critics allege that priestly language sacralizes violence or reduces God to retributive mechanisms. Owen’s careful exposition responds that priesthood is covenantal, free, and love’s costly form in a moral universe. The cross is not divine child abuse but the self-offering of the Son according to the human nature he assumed in order to bear our sin. Hebrews por-trays this offering as the joy set before him. Moreover, disconnecting priesthood from kingship and prophecy distorts both the nature and aim of atonement. The Priest is King, and his cross disarms the powers. The Priest is Prophet, and the cross is God’s final word of truth about sin and grace. The munus triplex anchors penal substitution within a trinitarian and teleological framework that resists caricature.

Questions about charismatic prophecy and apostolic ministry also press the doctrine. Some argue that Christ’s prophetic office implies ongoing revelation in the church beyond Scripture. The Reformed confessions, however, interpret the prophetic office as normatively executed through the inscripturated Word and the Spirit’s illumination. The church shares in Christ’s offices only analogically. There is a **priesthood of all believers**, but it is not a propitiatory priesthood. There is a prophetic people, but their speech is ruled by the canon. There is a royal people, but their reign is service and cross-bearing until the consummation. The distinction between Christ’s unique mediatorship and the church’s derivative participation guards against both authoritarianism and enthusiasm.

Finally, the doctrine must be connected to adjacent loci with rigor. The munus triplex presupposes a robust Christology. The person who executes the offices is the eternal Son incarnate. The offices belong to the person and are executed according to both natures in the mode proper to each. This guards the doctrine from becoming a functionalist Christology that neglects ontology. The **communicatio idiomatum** underwrites preaching and sacrament as sites where the Mediator himself addresses and feeds his people. The doctrine also depends on and illumines **federal theology**. The **covenant of redemption** clarifies that Christ’s mission is not ad hoc but eternally willed in God, rendering the economy intelligible. Election and atonement are not disconnected events but coordinated under Christ’s priestly suretyship. Union with Christ then becomes the soteriological matrix in which the benefits that flow from each office are communicated. Illumination and adoption derive from the prophetic and kingly offices, justification and sanctification from the priestly office as administered by the King through the means of grace.

A likely examiner objection is that the munus triplex risks functional tritheism within Christ. The answer is that the offices are not parts but aspects of the one mediation. They are formally distinct with respect to their objects but materially one in their subject, the person of the Son. A second objection is that it imposes a structure on the text. The reply is that the prophetic-priestly-kingly pattern grows from covenant history and climaxes in Christ, as Vos shows, and is interwoven into the New Testament’s explicit claims. Another objection is that elevating the priestly office renders the others subordinate. The response is to distinguish soteriological centrality from metaphysical or temporal priority. The priestly office is the hinge of reconciliation, but there is no Priest without the Prophet who speaks God’s truth and no durable peace without the King who rules in righteousness. A final objection is that centering kingship breeds political overreach. The rejoinder is to distinguish Christ’s universal reign from the church’s limited mandate. Christ rules all things for the sake of his church, but the church’s authority is ministerial.

In conclusion, the munus triplex, received and refined in Reformed theology from Calvin through Owen, Turretin, Bavinck, and Vos and critically engaged with Barth, is not a scholastic embellishment. It is the grammar by which the church names and defends the unity and fullness of Christ’s mediation. Prophet without Priest is light without life. Priest without King is pardon without protection. King without Prophet is power without truth. The doctrine insists that the one Mediator reveals the Father, reconciles the guilty, and rules the redeemed, and that he does all this now and to the end of the age. As such, it structures preaching, sacrament, discipline, mission, and public witness. It safeguards penal substitution within a trinitarian horizon, preserves the sovereignty of grace, and steadies the church in contested spaces by directing attention to the living Christ who teaches by his Word and Spirit, intercedes by his indestructible life, and reigns at the right hand of the Father until all enemies are under his feet.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The **munus triplex** is the unified mediatorial work of Christ as Prophet who reveals God’s will, Priest who reconciles by sacrifice and intercession, and King who conquers and rules, executed by the one person of the Son under **inseparable operations** and consistent with **divine simplicity**.
- Decisive figures:
  - John Calvin: architectonic biblical formulation from Messiah’s anointing integrating Word, altar, throne.
  - John Owen: priestly center under the **covenant of redemption**, penal substitution, coordinated with prophetic and kingly acts.
  - Francis Turretin: scholastic precision tying offices to states and to church’s ministerial authority.
  - Herman Bavinck: balanced retrieval linking offices to ecclesiology, sacraments, culture with priestly soteriological center.
  - Karl Barth: restructured offices as witness, servant, lord; gains in unity but loss of forensic priesthood and federal grounding.
  - Geerhardus Vos: biblical-theological development showing eschatological unity of the offices.
- Debates:
  - Internal: which office is central; relation to humiliation and exaltation; analogical extension to church offices under **federal theology**.
  - External: Lutheran and Roman differences on presence and sacrament; Socinian and liberal reductions to prophet or moral influence; political distortions of kingship.
  - Contemporary: atonement objections; scope of Christ’s kingship and the **two kingdoms**; claims of ongoing prophecy; church’s public witness.
- Defended position: The classic Reformed account is biblically grounded and dogmatically necessary, with priestly reconciliation as the soteriological center and prophetic and kingly offices organically coordinated, extended to the church only analogically and ministerially.
- Likely examiner questions and answers:
  - Is the munus triplex an imposed grid? No, it arises from covenant offices fulfilled in Christ attested across Scripture.
  - Does privileging priesthood diminish kingship and prophecy? No, priesthood is soteriological hinge, but offices are perichoretic in one Mediator.
  - How do the offices relate to the two states? All three operate in both states in distinct modes, with ongoing heavenly prophecy and intercession.
  - Does Christ’s kingship justify ecclesial coercion? No, the church’s authority is ministerial and declarative under the Word.
  - How does this guard against atonement critiques? By locating penal substitution within trinitarian love, priestly vocation, and royal victory.
