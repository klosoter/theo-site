### TITLE
Without Confusion or Division: Personal Unity and Natural Distinction at Chalcedon

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How to confess the single subject of the incarnate Son with full divine and full human reality, safeguarding **hypostatic union**, **communicatio idiomatum**, and the integrity of both natures without violating **divine simplicity** or **impassibility**.
- Decisive figures: Cyril of Alexandria, Leo the Great, Nestorius, Eutyches, Maximus the Confessor.
- Traditions: Patristic conciliar Christology culminating in Chalcedon and Constantinople III, Byzantine dyothelite clarification, later Lutheran and Reformed reception touching **communicatio idiomatum** and **extra Calvinisticum**.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Alexandrian–Antiochene tensions, miaphysite readings of Cyril, dyothelitism and two operations, Lutheran–Reformed disputes about genus majestaticum and the ascended body.
  - External: Nestorian and Eutychian alternatives, Apollinarian pressure, Islamic and Unitarian denials of Incarnation, modern kenoticism.
  - Contemporary: Analytic “two minds” models, functionalist or metaphorical christologies, theopaschite theses and divine suffering claims.
- Position: Defend a Chalcedonian–dyothelite account with **anhypostasia–enhypostasia**, the one person of the Logos as subject of all acts, two wills and energies, a sober **communicatio idiomatum**, and the **extra Calvinisticum** to preserve true humanity and immutable deity.

### ESSAY
The Chalcedonian Definition articulates the church’s most rigorous answer to the central christological problem: to confess Jesus Christ as one and the same Son, truly God and truly man, such that the unity of person is neither a fusion that destroys difference nor a coordination that dissolves unity. The formula “in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation” is not a slogan but a set of four fences that mark out how the mystery of the **hypostatic union** should be thought and spoken. At stake is doctrinal coherence across loci. If the Son is not consubstantial with the Father, the Trinity collapses. If he is not consubstantial with us, soteriology dissolves. If the union confuses natures, we lose either a passible, obedient mediator or the immutable Lord. If the union divides into two subjects, we lose the one Lord Jesus Christ as the acting agent of salvation. The Chalcedonian center must therefore be defended as both biblically warranted and conceptually coherent.

Cyril of Alexandria stands at the fulcrum of the pre-Chalcedonian debate. His theological context was the aftermath of Arianism and the pressure of Apollinarian strategies that, in trying to preserve unity, compromised the fullness of Christ’s humanity. Cyril’s decisive insistence is that the subject of the incarnation is the eternal Logos. Key works include his Second Letter to Nestorius and the Twelve Anathemas, as well as On the Unity of Christ. Two controlling convictions frame his contribution. First, the one subject principle: all predicates of Jesus in Scripture, whether divine or human, are predicated of the same hypostasis, the Word. Second, the soteriological axiom that what is not assumed is not healed, inherited from Athanasius and Gregory of Nazianzus. Against Nestorius’s prosopic coordination, Cyril argued for a true **hypostatic union**, not a moral or external conjunction. He embraces Mary as Theotokos precisely to guard the identity of the one born of her as God the Word. His controversial phrase, “one incarnate nature of God the Word,” has often been weaponized by both sides. Read in context, “nature” for Cyril frequently functions as hypostasis, and the “one” is the one who is incarnate, not the obliteration of humanity. He explicitly insists that the Logos “took flesh animated by a rational soul,” refusing Apollinarian truncation. The heart of Cyril’s synthesis is a robust **communicatio idiomatum** at the level of person: we can rightly say that God suffered and died, but always “in the flesh,” because the one who suffered is God the Word who assumed a passible human nature. This is the seed of the later theopaschite formula, “one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh,” which safeguards both the impassibility of the divine nature and the unity of the acting subject.

Nestorius, trained in the Antiochene tradition, feared that Cyril’s emphasis on unity would compromise Christ’s authentic human agency. Nestorius opposed Theotokos in favor of Christotokos, worried that calling Mary the bearer of God implied that the divine nature underwent change or that the Son’s humanity was swallowed. In sermons and letters he developed a prosopic union, effectively a conjunction of two realities under one appearance or moral unity. The examiner will ask whether Nestorius has been caricatured. The fairest reading recognizes that Nestorius wished to protect complete humanity and eschewed Apollinarianism. Yet his account yields two personal subjects loosely coordinated. Exegetically, the New Testament does not alternate agents but speaks of one Lord who is born, grows, speaks, suffers, dies, and reigns. Dogmatically, a prosopic union destroys the ground of **communicatio idiomatum**, making the most decisive soteriological statements equivocal. The church judged that Nestorius could not maintain real union without falling back into a cooperative duality.

Leo the Great, bishop of Rome, authored the Tome to Flavian that became the theological charter for Chalcedon. Leo assimilates the gains of both Alexandria and Antioch while refusing their excesses. His dictum that each nature does what is proper to it, in communion with the other, expresses the four fences elegantly. The divine does not turn into the human, nor the human into the divine. Yet the actions of each nature belong to the one person. The Christ who weeps at Lazarus is the same Christ who raises him. The Logos does not stop sustaining all things in the act of sleeping in the boat. Leo’s method is pastoral and exegetical but conceptually sharp: the act is from the person, the mode is according to nature. This locks in a durable account of **communicatio idiomatum**. We do not communicate properties between natures as if the human becomes omnipresent or the divine becomes passible; we communicate predicates by attribution to the one person who subsists in both natures. Leo thus supplied Chalcedon with doctrinal grammar that avoided Nestorian separation and Eutychian confusion.

Eutyches, by contrast, exemplifies the Alexandrian overcorrection. In the fight against Nestorian duality, he denied that Christ is consubstantial with us after the union and spoke of the human nature as being absorbed into the divine like a drop in the ocean. The result is a Christ neither truly human nor truly divine in the economy, for the immutable God by definition cannot be blended with created changeability. Eutyches precipitated the Robber Council of Ephesus, was condemned at Chalcedon, and his position defined the “without confusion, without change” fences. The church affirmed the unity of person and the duality of natures not as abstract metaphysics but to preserve the Gospel’s reality: the one Lord truly hungers and truly feeds, truly dies and truly raises the dead.

Chalcedon’s Definition is a doctrinal via media in the best sense, but not a compromise. It is a principled synthesis produced by hard-won exegetical and metaphysical clarity. The Definition confesses one and the same Son, consubstantial with the Father according to the Godhead and consubstantial with us according to the manhood, acknowledged in two natures, without confusion or change, without division or separation, the difference of the natures being by no means taken away by the union, but rather the properties being preserved, concurring in one person and one hypostasis. Every clause is a safeguard. The two “without” clauses fence the integrity of each nature. The last clause secures a single subject. The logic is grammatical: who acts is one, how he acts depends on which nature is the principle of the action. This grammar resists both logical contradiction and category errors. When Scripture predicates ignorance or growth of Jesus, we ascribe this truly to the one Son according to the human nature. When Scripture predicates omniscience or divine prerogatives, we ascribe these truly to the same Son according to the divine nature. This is not evasive double-speak but clarity about levels of predication. A likely objection is that this produces a divided Christ. The reply is the principle of **enhypostasia** and **anhypostasia**. The human nature of Christ is anhypostatic, that is, it does not subsist as a personal subject on its own, but is enhypostatic in the Logos, that is, it exists as the concrete human nature of the divine person. The doctrine thereby protects full humanity without a second human person. The subject is one; his human nature is the organ of human experiences and acts.

Maximus the Confessor decisively clarified and defended this synthesis in the monothelite controversy. In the seventh century, to heal ongoing divisions, some proposed that while Christ has two natures, he has only one will and one operation. Maximus saw this as a subtle dissolution of the humanity that undermined soteriology. If will and operation belong to hypostasis rather than nature, then the unity of Christ would indeed seem to require one will and one energy. But Maximus argued from Scripture and from a well-ordered anthropology that will and energy are natural faculties. Christ has a human will because he has a full human nature; he has a divine will because he is truly God. The unity of person does not erase the distinction of natural operations but harmonizes them in the concrete life of the Logos. Gethsemane becomes the luminous theater of orthodoxy: “not my will but yours be done.” Maximus insists that the human will is not contrary to the divine but deified and freely aligned with it, thereby healing our disordered willing. Two ecumenical councils confirmed him, especially Constantinople III, which confessed two wills and two energies in Christ. Maximus expands Chalcedon in two ways of importance for examiners. He nails down that faculty predicates like will and energy are predicated according to nature, not person, protecting a truly human obedience and growth. He also anticipates and corrects sloppy talk of a single theandric energy, by distinguishing cooperative synergy from ontological confusion. This development seals the coherency of **communicatio idiomatum** and forecloses monothelite and monenergist evasions that would reduce Christ’s human life to a mere epiphenomenon of deity. His thought also helps answer modern existentialist or functionalist Christologies by insisting that the soteriological power of Christ’s mission depends on the metaphysical truth of who he is.

Internal debates did not cease at Chalcedon. The miaphysite churches claim to stand with Cyril’s “one incarnate nature” against dyophysite language. The best defense is to show that Chalcedon is the most Cyrillian reading available. Cyril affirms the preservation of properties and their true predication in the one subject. Chalcedon uses the duality of natures to cash out precisely that claim. The two traditions talk past one another at times due to terminological legacies. Yet Maximus’s dyothelitism makes evident that denying duality at the level of natural faculties inevitably ends in a diminished humanity. Proponents may reply that deification transfigures the human so radically that talk of dual operations is obsolete. The rejoinder is that deification perfects nature without destroying its created mode. If a nature is destroyed, deification is evacuated. The patristic axiom holds: grace does not destroy nature, it restores and elevates it.

A later intra-orthodox debate unfolded between Lutheran and Reformed trajectories over **communicatio idiomatum**. The Lutheran genus majestaticum ascribes a communication of divine majesty to the human nature, deployed especially to defend the ubiquity of the body in the Supper. The Reformed insist on the **extra Calvinisticum**, namely that the Logos is never circumscribed by his flesh and continues to exist and operate beyond or extra carnem. The Reformed fear that Lutheran ubiquity erases the finite mode of the human nature and blurs the Chalcedonian fences. The Lutheran fear is that the Reformed extra yields a Nestorian separation and threatens the realism of sacramental presence. The Chalcedonian–Maximian framework directs us to a resolution. Predication and operation are according to nature. The human nature does not acquire omnipresence, for that would be a change in its created properties. The person of the Son is truly present with his body, but the way he is present as God exceeds the mode whereby his human body is present in heaven. To say the Son operates extra carnem is not to deny that he operates in and through his flesh; it is to deny that his divinity is compressed. The one person is undivided; the natural modes remain distinct. This preserves **impassibility**, maintains the reality of the ascended body, and upholds Chalcedon’s “without change” while honoring the pastoral intent of real communion.

External challenges flank the doctrine from both rationalist and religious alternatives. Apollinarianism returns in modern garb whenever the human psychology of Jesus is hollowed out and “the Word takes the place of a human mind.” Against this, the dyothelite teaching asserts that Christ has a true human intellect and will. Nestorian patterns recur in some analytic proposals that treat Christ as the conjunction of two centers of consciousness with no asymmetry. The antidote is to recall that the subject is the Logos, who subsists in two cognitive ranges, human and divine, asymmetrically ordered to the single personal identity. Islamic critique presses a different line: the Incarnation violates divine unity and perfection. The response is that **divine simplicity** and unity are not compromised by the assumption of a created nature, because the union is hypostatic not essential. The divine essence neither changes nor mixes. God does a new thing toward the world without becoming a new kind of being. Christian theopaschite confession is not that the divine nature suffers, but that the divine person suffers in the assumed nature. This is coherent precisely because of Chalcedon’s predication rules. Rationalists object that the doctrine is paradoxical and therefore unintelligible. Yet the charge often rests on illicit property transference. Once we respect the fences, the alleged contradictions evaporate. “Christ is mortal and immortal” is not a contradiction when the predicates are distributed to distinct natures in the one person.

Contemporary theology raises fresh challenges. Kenotic theories assert that the Son relinquished or limited divine attributes in becoming man. But **divine simplicity** blocks the partitioning of essential attributes; God cannot divest himself of what he is. Philippians 2 reads as a descent in the form of a servant, not a subtraction of deity. The Son’s emptying is the assumption of our low condition, not the loss of his high nature. The biblical humiliation is real and occurs according to the humanity. Analytic “two minds” models propose that Christ has a human mind with limited knowledge and a divine mind with unlimited knowledge, with a relation of asymmetric access. Properly chastened by Chalcedon, such models can be serviceable if reformulated as one person operating through two cognitive ranges. The Logos is the subject of both ranges and can, by the mode of his human cognition, truly learn and grow, while as God he eternally knows all. The examiner may worry that this divides the subject. The reply is **enhypostasia**: there is no second human person alongside the Logos. The human range does not introduce a second subject but a second mode of conscious operation belonging to the same subject. Feminist or liberationist approaches sometimes dismiss metaphysical Christology as an abstraction that hinders praxis. Yet the church’s confessing precision was always driven by pastoral and soteriological stakes. Only if the same one is God and man can his solidarity with the oppressed carry divine authority and salvific efficacy. Dispensing with the metaphysical core sheds the moral power it seeks.

Chalcedon is best understood as a harmonization of the Trinity, Scripture, and salvation. Trinitarianly, the subject of the incarnation is the second person who eternally proceeds and shares the divine essence with the Father and the Spirit. He does not cease to be who he is in the economy of salvation, which preserves **impassibility** and **simplicity**. Scripturally, the Gospels present one Jesus who sleeps and stills storms, who does not know the day and yet forgives sins. Paul speaks of God’s own Son, born of a woman, born under the law, and of the Lord of glory crucified. Soteriologically, the representative obedience, atoning death, and priestly intercession require a mediator who is fully human to stand with us and fully divine to render his work of infinite value and universal efficacy. The satisfyingly tight logic of the doctrine is that every error either endangers divine agency in salvation or human solidarity in mediation. The Chalcedonian confession keeps both.

Examiners will press the coherence of suffering and **impassibility**. The most faithful answer is the theopaschite formula refined after Chalcedon: one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh. This refuses to predicate suffering of the divine nature while preserving personal attribution. They will push the question of will: is will a property of person or nature. Maximus’s argument from Christ’s obedience shows that making will a personal property either denies a human will or produces two persons. Thus will and energy are natural requirements; the one person exercises both in harmony. They may query whether the ascended body is enclosed in a locale, and how the Son can be present with the church everywhere. The answer is **extra Calvinisticum**. The Son is present to us as God by his Spirit everywhere, and present bodily in heaven, returning in the same way he ascended. This is not a reduction of presence but an honoring of each nature’s mode. They may ask if the doctrine forbids saying God died. Properly construed **communicatio idiomatum** allows the confession “God died” as a shorthand for “the person who is God died according to the human nature.” The fence of “in the flesh” keeps the claim true and avoids confusion.

A final integration underscores how the doctrine disciplines preaching and piety. Because the person is one, we look to Jesus and see God. Because the natures are two, we learn obedience in a genuinely human register, sanctified by the Spirit in the humanity of Christ. Prayer to Christ is prayer to God. Consolation in weakness is grounded in his true human sympathy. Confidence in salvation rests in his divine sufficiency. All of this rides on Chalcedon’s grammar. To relax it is to loosen the bolts on the entire structure of Christian confession.

In conclusion, I defend a Chalcedonian–dyothelite Christology that takes Cyril’s one-subject insight as determinative, reads Leo’s duality of operations as its proper extension, rejects Nestorian dyarchy and Eutychian fusion, and relies on Maximus to anchor two wills and two energies in nature. The human nature is anhypostatic and enhypostatic in the Logos, so that there is only one acting subject. A disciplined **communicatio idiomatum** frames our language, and the **extra Calvinisticum** preserves the immensity of deity and the integrity of Christ’s body. This account coheres with **divine simplicity** and **impassibility**, upholds biblical exegesis, and secures soteriology. It answers the major internal, external, and contemporary objections not by hedging but by applying the Chalcedonian fences to predication, will, and operation. The Christ who is one in person and two in natures is the Christ who can truly save and truly sympathize, the living center of trinitarian faith and the anchor of Christian hope.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The **hypostatic union** is the personal union of the eternal Logos with a complete human nature, such that Christ is one person in two natures, “without confusion, change, division, or separation,” with a sober **communicatio idiomatum**, two wills and energies, and the human nature as **anhypostatic** yet **enhypostatic** in the Son.
- Decisive figures:
  - Cyril of Alexandria: one-subject Christology, Theotokos, strong **communicatio idiomatum** at the level of person.
  - Leo the Great: each nature does what is proper, one person as subject; Chalcedon’s grammar of predication.
  - Nestorius: prosopic union tending to two subjects; rejection of Theotokos; judged insufficiently unitary.
  - Eutyches: monophysite fusion, loss of consubstantiality with us; condemned at Chalcedon.
  - Maximus the Confessor: two wills and two energies according to nature; Gethsemane as paradigm; anti-monothelite bulwark.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Alexandrian–Antiochene tensions, miaphysite readings of Cyril, dyothelitism, Lutheran–Reformed dispute over **communicatio idiomatum** and the **extra Calvinisticum**.
  - External: Nestorianism, Eutychianism, Apollinarianism; Islamic and Unitarian denials of Incarnation; modern kenoticism.
  - Contemporary: Analytic two-minds proposals, functionalist christologies, theopaschite claims and divine suffering.
- Defended position: A Chalcedonian–dyothelite account with the Logos as sole subject, natural distinction of faculties including **two wills**, disciplined **communicatio idiomatum**, affirmation of **impassibility** and **divine simplicity**, and the **extra Calvinisticum** to honor both natures’ modes.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - How can God suffer if God is impassible? One of the Trinity suffered in the flesh; suffering is predicated of the person according to the human nature.
  - Is will a property of person or nature? Nature; otherwise Christ lacks a human will or becomes two persons.
  - Does Chalcedon divide Christ’s consciousness? No; one subject with two cognitive ranges, human and divine, ordered by **enhypostasia**.
  - Can we say “God died”? Yes, by **communicatio idiomatum** qualified “according to the flesh.”
  - What about the ascended body and omnipresence? The Son is omnipresent as God; his human body remains finite, safeguarded by the **extra Calvinisticum**.
  - Is Chalcedon truly Cyrillian? Yes; it secures the one subject while explicating the preserved properties that Cyril affirmed.
