# Emil Brunner (1889–1966)

Emil Brunner approached the **Chalcedonian Definition** and the **two-natures doctrine** with a critical yet appreciative stance, emphasizing the existential encounter with the living Christ over strict metaphysical formulations. While affirming the importance of **Christ’s true humanity and true divinity**, Brunner critiqued the static categories of classical **Christology**, seeking to recover the dynamic, relational dimension of the Incarnation as the revelation of God to humanity. For Brunner, the heart of the doctrine lies in the **personal encounter** with Christ, rather than in abstract definitions.

- "The real meaning of the doctrine of the two natures is not to be found in the metaphysical sphere, but in the personal relationship between God and man as it is revealed in Jesus Christ." (The Mediator)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Christology as Personal Encounter**
   - Emphasizes that **Christ** is encountered as the living Lord, not as an abstract metaphysical construct.
   - The doctrine of two natures serves to safeguard the reality of this encounter.
   - The Incarnation is fundamentally about **God’s self-revelation** to humanity.
   - Theological formulations must serve the existential relationship between God and the believer.
   - "The Incarnation is the event in which God meets man in person." (The Mediator)

2. **Critical Reception of Chalcedon**
   - Affirms the Chalcedonian concern to maintain both **full divinity and full humanity** of Christ.
   - Critiques the use of Greek metaphysical categories as potentially obscuring the biblical witness.
   - Sees the Definition as a necessary boundary, but not as the final word on Christology.
   - Stresses the need to reinterpret Chalcedon in light of contemporary understanding.
   - "Chalcedon is a fence, not a house." (The Mediator)

3. **Revelation over Metaphysics**
   - Prioritizes **revelation** and the event of Christ over philosophical speculation.
   - The two-natures doctrine is meaningful only as it points to the reality of God’s action in Christ.
   - Rejects attempts to explain the Incarnation in terms of substance or essence.
   - The mystery of Christ is to be confessed, not dissected.
   - "The doctrine is not a metaphysical explanation, but a confession of faith in the event of revelation." (Dogmatics I)

4. **Unity of the Person of Christ**
   - Insists on the **personal unity** of Christ, not a mere juxtaposition of two natures.
   - The divine and human are united in the one person of Jesus, who is the Mediator.
   - Avoids both **Nestorian** separation and **Eutychian** confusion.
   - The unity is dynamic, rooted in God’s redemptive purpose.
   - "In Jesus Christ, God and man are one person, not by nature, but by the will and act of God." (The Mediator)

5. **Doctrine as Safeguard for Soteriology**
   - The two-natures doctrine protects the reality of **atonement** and **reconciliation**.
   - Only one who is truly God and truly man can mediate between God and humanity.
   - Christ’s work is effective because of who He is in His unique person.
   - The doctrine serves the proclamation of the **gospel**, not speculative theology.
   - "The doctrine of the two natures is the safeguard of the reality of redemption." (The Mediator)

## Legacy and Impact

Brunner’s approach to the **Chalcedonian Definition** influenced 20th-century Protestant theology by calling for a renewed focus on the existential and relational dimensions of **Christology**. His insistence on revelation and personal encounter challenged both liberal reductionism and rigid dogmatism. While some criticized his reluctance to affirm traditional metaphysics, Brunner’s work remains significant for those seeking to articulate the meaning of the Incarnation in contemporary terms. His legacy endures in ongoing debates about the relevance of classical doctrinal formulations for modern faith.
