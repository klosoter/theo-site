### TITLE
Distinguish Without Dividing: Law and Gospel in Reformed Federal Theology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How to maintain a principled distinction between **law and gospel** that safeguards **justification sola fide** while preserving the unity of God’s covenantal dealings and the **third use of the law** in sanctification.
- Decisive figures: Martin Luther; John Calvin; Francis Turretin; C. F. W. Walther; John Murray; Norman Shepherd.
- Traditions: Lutheran Reformation; classic Reformed and **federal theology**; Reformed scholasticism; Westminster Standards; modern Reformed debates.
- Debates addressed:
  - Internal: unity of covenant and the **covenant of works**; Sinai and **republication**; the third use; antinomian and neonomian tensions; Shepherd controversy.
  - External: Roman Catholic construal of gospel as “new law”; Lutheran sharpness on the distinction; New Perspective on Paul’s redefinition of “works of the law.”
  - Contemporary: evangelical moralism and therapeutic antinomianism; preaching law and gospel; assurance and sanctification.
- Position: I defend the classic Reformed law–gospel distinction grounded in the **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, centered on Christ’s **active obedience** and **forensic justification**, with the law’s abiding normative role for the regenerate, rejecting both antinomianism and neonomianism.

### ESSAY
The law–gospel distinction names a hermeneutical, theological, and pastoral judgment: God addresses fallen humanity in two fundamental voices. The law articulates God’s holy will and its demand for perfect, personal, perpetual obedience, promising life on condition of entire righteousness and threatening curse for the slightest transgression. The gospel proclaims that this righteousness has been accomplished by Christ apart from our works and is received by faith alone, such that sinners are justified and reconciled to God. The distinction is not a metaphysical split in God, for the one Lord speaks with a single, simple will, but rather a covenantal distinction in the way his one will confronts the sinner in Adam and comforts the sinner in Christ. To get this wrong is to collapse the **ordo salutis**, fracture **federal theology**, ruin assurance, and mute Christian freedom. To get it right is to preach Christ crucified with precision and to lead the saints into the **third use of the law** as the path of grateful obedience.

Martin Luther is decisive because he recovered the distinction as a hermeneutical key that cracked open Scripture for the conscience enslaved to self-justification. In the Heidelberg Disputation and the Galatians commentaries, Luther identified the law as everything in Scripture that commands and threatens, and the gospel as everything that promises and gives Christ. His method was existentially charged and polemically focused against medieval synergy that had turned the gospel into a higher law of charity. His key conceptual contribution was the simul iustus et peccator anchored in **forensic justification**, in which the law always accuses and drives to Christ, and the gospel alone imparts righteousness extra nos. Luther’s disputes with Erasmus on the will emphasized that any synergistic element reimports law into the grammar of grace. This clarity, however, generated internal tensions within his heirs concerning the law’s positive role for the regenerate. Luther himself acknowledged the law’s instructive function, yet in the polemical crossfire he sometimes spoke as if the regenerate did not need the law as guide. The Lutheran scholastics would formulate uses, but the accent remained that the first use dominates and the third is precarious pastorally. This sharpness protects **justification sola fide**, yet it risks, in Reformed judgment, attenuating the law’s positive normativity for those united to Christ.

John Calvin receives extended treatment because he retains the evangelical center while reframing the law–gospel relation within a more architectonic **covenant** scheme that would shape the Reformed tradition. In the Institutes and catechetical writings, Calvin confesses the antithesis of law and gospel for justification: law exposes sin, gospel offers free reconciliation in Christ. Yet he insists that the law as moral norm expresses the unchanging righteousness of God and, once the curse is removed in Christ, becomes principally the rule of gratitude. For Calvin the three uses are real, and the **third use of the law** is primary in the regenerate life. This is not a retreat from the gospel but a consequence of **union with Christ**: the Spirit writes the law on the heart, enabling the justified to walk in the obedience that the law always required. Further, Calvin’s biblical theology binds the distinction to **federal theology** without fragmenting redemptive history. He sees one **covenant of grace** administered through varying economies, with a legal pedagogy under Moses that is not a rival gospel but a preparatory dispensation pointing to Christ who is the telos of the law. Although Calvin did not formalize the **covenant of works** label, he affirmed the substance: Adam was placed under a probation with the promise of life upon obedience, which clarifies why Christ as the second Adam must render the **active obedience** that the law demanded. Calvin thus holds together the antithesis of law and gospel in justification, the unity of God’s covenant dealings, and the law’s abiding normativity for the saints.

Francis Turretin sharpened and stabilized these insights in the high Reformed scholastic period. In the Institutes of Elenctic Theology he offers a crisp, formal distinction: law and gospel differ not materially as to doctrine, since both reveal God and require obedience, but formally as to the mode of acceptance by which life is obtained. The law as covenant demands perfect obedience for life; the gospel announces the free promise of life for the sake of Christ’s righteousness imputed and received by faith alone. By situating the distinction within a precise **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** framework, Turretin secured both poles against collapse. Against Rome he denies that the gospel is a new law commanding evangelical obedience as the matter of justification. Against the Socinians he defends the necessity of satisfaction and the imputation of Christ’s obedience. Against antinomians he upholds the law’s perpetual rule for the regenerate, grounded not in works for justification but in conformity to Christ. Turretin also clarifies the relation of Sinai to the covenant of works. He acknowledges a legal republication at Sinai as to the covenant form, principally for pedagogical and political ends, while insisting that for the elect the Mosaic economy was an administration of the **covenant of grace**. This calibration protects the Pauline antithesis in Galatians 3 without denying the unity of redemptive history.

C. F. W. Walther’s The Proper Distinction Between Law and Gospel remains a Lutheran classic that continues to press Reformed pastors helpfully and critically. Walther’s twenty-five theses are pastoral guardrails against the perennial temptation to mingle law and gospel, whether by softening the law into manageable exhortations or by hardening the gospel into a subtler demand. Walther insists that the law always says do and the gospel always says done, that the comfort of the gospel must never be tied to prior moral renovation, and that preaching must alternate these voices in proper order to smite the secure and heal the broken. Reformed theologians rightly receive this as a salutary admonition. Yet we register two concerns. First, Walther’s construal of the third use tends toward marginalization; whereas the New Testament commands the regenerate to fulfill the righteous requirement of the law by the Spirit, Walther’s paradigm can discourage systematic moral instruction as gospel fruit. Second, Lutheran dogmatics commonly refuses the **covenant of works**, thereby lacking the federal architecture that explains why Christ’s obedience is legally constitutive for our justification. Even so, Walther’s acute pastoral ear amplifies a core truth Reformed theology shares: to the trembling conscience, any condition annexed to the gospel as the ground of acceptance is not merely an error but soul-destroying.

John Murray, as a mid-twentieth-century Reformed exegete and theologian at Westminster Seminary, represents a careful development and, at a few points, recalibration of older federal categories. Murray affirmed **forensic justification** and the necessity of Christ’s obedience in our stead, as well as the **third use of the law**. He emphasized **union with Christ** as the organic matrix of all soteriological benefits, thereby integrating the legal and the transformative without confusion. However, Murray questioned the traditional nomenclature of a prelapsarian **covenant of works**, preferring to speak of the Adamic administration as a divine command with attached promise rather than as a formal covenant with federal arrangements. His motive was exegetical caution, not neonomian drift. He held fixed the law–gospel antithesis for justification, but he recast some federal language. This recasting had consequences. Students and subsequent interpreters split over whether the removal of the traditional term weakened the conceptual clarity needed to oppose later neonomian proposals. Murray’s own writings commend neither antinomianism nor mingling, but debates over Sinai’s character and the function of the Decalogue in redemptive history intensified in his wake.

Norman Shepherd’s controversial formulation of “covenant faithfulness” as the condition for final justification crystallized the Reformed need to draw the law–gospel line with unembarrassed boldness. Shepherd maintained adherence to justification by faith, but he redefined faith by building obedient works into its essence and so made persevering obedience co-instrumental for justification. The effect, whatever the intention, was to treat the gospel as a new law, to confuse the instrument with its fruit, and to relocate assurance from Christ’s righteousness to the believer’s covenant loyalty. This approach reverberated in the broader Federal Vision movement and required a robust restatement of classic **federal theology**. Shepherd’s project also pushed exegetical pressure points: James 2, Romans 2, and the warning passages. But careful attention to Paul’s logic shows that works vindicate faith before men and as eschatological evidence, not as the ground or co-instrument of forensic acceptance. To blend them is to overthrow the law–gospel distinction and to erode the pastoral core of the Reformed confessions.

These figures position us to handle three layers of debate. Internally, Reformed theologians have wrestled over Sinai, **republication**, and the third use. The question of Sinai arises because Paul can speak of the law as a ministry of condemnation and death, yet also claim that the law is holy and good. The best Reformed answer, anticipated by Calvin and sharpened by Turretin, is that Sinai republishes, in its covenant form, the works principle originally revealed to Adam, principally to magnify sin and to govern the theocratic nation, while simultaneously administering the **covenant of grace** through promises, sacrifices, and types pointing to Christ. This avoids the antinomian claim that the Mosaic law is purely condemning with no gospel, and the neonomian claim that the Mosaic economy is a simplified set of doable conditions for life. The abiding moral law, summarized in the Decalogue, continues as the rule of gratitude for the church, not as a means of justification. A Westminster examiner may press: does the third use compromise the antithesis? The answer is no, because the uses answer different questions. The antithesis addresses the ground of acceptance before God. The third use addresses the norm of sanctified life after acceptance. The same law that cannot justify now guides those in Christ. Romans 3 does not abrogate the law but establishes it precisely by situating it within **union with Christ** and the gift of the Spirit.

Another internal debate concerns whether the law–gospel distinction is merely a soteriological rubric or a hermeneutical principle spanning Scripture. The Lutheran tradition, following Luther and Walther, tends to treat it as the master key for all exegesis. Reformed theology affirms its hermeneutical utility but refuses to let it flatten the redemptive-historical development of covenant administrations. In practice, this means we interpret Proverbs as law in its sapiential mode and Isaiah as gospel in its promissory mode, yet we also recognize mixed discourse and multivalent forms where commands are embedded in gracious promises and vice versa. The pastor must learn to speak both voices in the right season without letting one devour the other.

Externally, Rome’s Tridentine articulation treats the gospel as a new law of faith formed by charity, such that justification is both forgiveness and interior renewal, with the final verdict corresponding to inherent righteousness. This collapses the distinction. By rendering the gospel law-like, it conditions acceptance on infused habits and mutilates assurance. The Reformed response rests on the federal logic: since God first demanded perfect obedience in the **covenant of works**, only perfect obedience can answer the law’s claims. Christ’s **active obedience** is the righteousness that answers for us. To make our Spirit-wrought obedience even partially constitutive of the righteousness by which we are justified is to deny both the integrity of the original demand and the sufficiency of Christ’s fulfillment. An examiner may ask whether this makes sanctification optional. The answer is no, because the same Christ who justifies also sanctifies by the Spirit, and the law’s normativity is intensified, not relaxed. But sanctification is the necessary fruit and evidence, never the ground or instrument of **forensic justification**.

A second external debate comes from the New Perspective on Paul. By recasting “works of the law” as primarily boundary markers of Jewish identity, the NPP can imply that Paul’s problem is sociological exclusivism rather than human inability under God’s righteous demand. If so, law and gospel are not antithetical at the level of justification, and law-keeping of a certain sort may belong in the instrumentality of final acceptance. The Reformed reply is exegetical and covenantal. Paul’s argument in Romans 2 through 4 treats “works” as doing what the law requires in its moral depth, not merely ceremonial badges. Abraham is justified by faith apart from works because the promise rests on grace. The curse of the law in Galatians 3 falls on all who do not abide by everything written in the book, not merely boundary markers. Christ redeems from the curse by becoming a curse, fulfilling the original works principle as the second Adam. The law is thus established in its highest demand and the gospel triumphant in Christ’s fulfillment and imputation.

Contemporarily, evangelicalism oscillates between therapeutic antinomianism and anxious moralism. The former assumes the gospel’s comfort without the law’s holy demand, leading to cheap grace. The latter preaches imperatives without the evangel, creating guilt without godly sorrow, despair without deliverance. The law–gospel distinction offers the antidote. Preaching must articulate God’s demand in its full range, exposing not only external acts but heart-level idolatry, thereby silencing evasions. Then preaching must declare the finished work of Christ as objective reality outside the hearer, relieving the conscience and instigating gratitude. Only then can the minister wisely bring the **third use of the law** to bear as pattern, with the Spirit’s enabling, for new obedience. Failing to distinguish confuses order. Failing to connect collapses growth. The sacraments likewise should be administered as gospel signs and seals, not as a new regime of conditions. Pastors must guard assurance by teaching that even our best works need Christ’s blood, while urging holiness by showing that grace trains us to renounce ungodliness.

At this point an examiner may probe particular texts. Does Romans 2:13, that the doers of the law will be justified, contradict **justification sola fide**? No. Paul states a legal principle that no sinner fulfills, setting up the necessity of a righteousness from God apart from the law. Christ alone is the doer whose obedience is counted to us. Does James 2 teach justification by works? It teaches that faith is justified as genuine by its fruits before the forum of observation, not that works enter the instrument of forensic acceptance before God. Does Matthew 5’s fulfillment of the law place the Christian back under law for righteousness? Christ fulfills the law representatively and inaugurates the new creation life wherein the law’s true intent is realized in those united to him by the Spirit, not as a condition to secure acceptance but as the path that flows from acceptance. Does the antithesis between law and gospel degrade the law as if it opposed grace? No. The law is good, because it reflects the character of God whose **divine simplicity** guarantees no tension between justice and mercy. The tension lies in the sinner’s relation to the law outside of Christ. In Christ, the law’s curse is silenced and its pattern is loved.

Against the charge that the Reformed version softens the distinction in favor of sanctification and thereby compromises assurance, we answer that our federal scheme strengthens the distinction precisely at the decisive point. Because we confess a real **covenant of works** with a real promise of eschatological life upon perfect obedience, we can say with legal precision that Christ, as second Adam, has secured that life by his **active obedience**, which is counted to believers. This removes every form of conditionality from the ground and instrument of justification. Only then do we robustly reintroduce the law as norm for the justified without confusion. Lutheran critics worry that this subordinates the distinction to covenant. But covenant is the biblical idiom by which the distinction is explained and its Christological resolution achieved.

What then is the positive account that preserves both distinction and unity? It runs along these lines. In creation, God inscribed his law on human hearts and gave a probationary command to Adam, a **covenant of works** in substance. In the fall, Adam’s guilt and corruption placed the race under the law’s curse and robbed us of ability to fulfill its demands. In promise and fulfillment, God instituted the **covenant of grace**, first shadowed in types and later accomplished by Christ. The Mosaic economy included a formal **republication** of the works principle at the typal level to magnify sin and tutor Israel to Christ, without contradicting its gracious administration to the elect. In the fullness of time, Christ satisfied the law’s penal sanction and fulfilled its positive demand, so that God might be just and the justifier. By faith alone we receive his righteousness, and by **union with Christ** and the Spirit’s indwelling we are renewed to live according to the law’s pattern. Thus the law–gospel distinction functions decisively at the level of justification and instrumentality, whereas the unity of God’s plan grounds the law’s abiding role in sanctification.

The pastoral corollaries are straightforward. Preach the law in its full rigor, not as a ladder to heaven but as the mirror of sin and the map of gratitude. Preach the gospel as a gift, not a task, and keep Christ’s accomplishment front and center. In counseling the tender conscience, do not import demands into the article of justification. In correcting the idle or complacent, do not mute the commands of Christ because of a misunderstood freedom. Administer discipline as a function of the law’s public use. Administer the Lord’s Supper as gospel for the penitent, warning the unrepentant by the law. Shape the liturgy such that confession precedes absolution and thanksgiving flows into consecration, rehearsing the distinction in the life of the church. All of this is not a matter of optional emphasis but the oxygen of Reformed ministry.

The figures treated yield a coherent line. Luther retrieved the hermeneutical antithesis necessary to pierce the conscience. Calvin located it within a comprehensive covenantal vision that dignifies the law as norm for the justified. Turretin supplied the scholastic precision needed to withstand errors both Roman and radical. Walther warns pastors not to dilute the voices in practice. Murray’s exegetical reserve reminds us to argue our federal categories from Scripture, and his focus on **union with Christ** prevents sterile legalism. Shepherd shows how the lines blur when obedience is folded into the instrument of justification. The lesson is clear: distinguish without dividing, unite without confusing. The one God who speaks in law and gospel is faithful; his **federal theology** in Christ holds the tension for the salvation and holiness of his people.

I therefore defend the classic Reformed position. The law and the gospel are distinct as covenants concerning the condition of life before God: the **covenant of works** promises life upon perfect obedience, the **covenant of grace** gives life through Christ’s obedience received by faith alone. This distinction is decisive for **forensic justification** and unaltered by the law’s **third use**. The Mosaic economy, including a real though typological **republication** of the works principle, is an administration of the **covenant of grace** for the elect. Christ’s **active obedience** is imputed to the believer, grounding assurance. The regenerate, united to Christ, delight in the law as norm, not as instrument of acceptance. Both antinomianism and neonomianism are rejected as failures to keep the distinction and the unity in their proper places. This account is exegetically grounded, confessionally faithful, and pastorally fruitful, and it equips a candidate to answer the precise objections examiners will raise.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The law declares God’s demand for perfect obedience and threatens curse; the gospel announces Christ’s accomplished righteousness and offers life by faith alone; they are distinct in covenantal mode, unified under God’s one will, and coordinated so that the law accuses and then guides, while the gospel justifies and transforms.
- Decisive figures:
  - Luther: recovered the antithesis for the conscience; law always accuses; gospel as promise of alien righteousness.
  - Calvin: integrated the distinction into **federal theology**; upheld the **third use of the law**; one **covenant of grace** with Christ as telos.
  - Turretin: formally distinguished law and gospel as covenants of works and grace; defended **forensic justification** and limited **republication** at Sinai.
  - Walther: pastoral theses to avoid mingling; sharp Lutheran emphasis helpful but weak on third use and federal grounding.
  - John Murray: affirmed Reformed soteriology and third use; recast some federal terms; highlighted **union with Christ**.
  - Norman Shepherd: collapsed distinction by making obedient faith co-instrumental; catalyzed Reformed restatement.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Sinai and **republication**; scope of third use; antinomian vs neonomian tensions; precision of covenant terminology.
  - External: Rome’s “gospel as new law”; Lutheran sharpness versus Reformed federal approach; NPP’s boundary-marker thesis.
  - Contemporary: evangelical moralism and therapeutic antinomianism; preaching law and gospel; assurance and sanctification.
- Defended position: Classic Reformed law–gospel distinction grounded in **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, with Christ’s **active obedience** imputed and the law’s abiding normative role for the regenerate; rejects antinomianism and neonomianism.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - Does Romans 2:13 contradict sola fide? Answer: It states the legal principle Christ alone fulfills; it sets up the gospel.
  - Is James 2 denying **forensic justification**? Answer: Works vindicate faith as genuine before men and at judgment, not as ground or instrument.
  - Does third use compromise the antithesis? Answer: No; it governs sanctification, not justification’s ground or instrument.
  - Is Sinai a covenant of works? Answer: It republishes the works principle typologically while administering the **covenant of grace** to the elect.
  - How do you avoid legalism in preaching imperatives? Answer: Preach imperatives after indicatives; law as norm for those united to Christ, never as ground of acceptance.
