# Francis Turretin (1623–1687)

Francis Turretin offered a rigorous and influential account of the **pactum salutis** (covenant of redemption), emphasizing its eternal, intra-Trinitarian character and its foundational role in **Reformed federal theology**. He distinguished the **covenant of redemption** from the **covenant of grace**, arguing that Christ, as federal head, fulfills all covenant obligations for the elect. Turretin’s precise formulations shaped later Reformed understandings of Christ’s mediatorial work and the assurance of salvation.

- "The covenant of redemption is not to be confounded with the covenant of grace, but is to be distinguished from it as the cause from the effect." (Institutes of Elenctic Theology, 2.255)

## Key Works

- Institutes of Elenctic Theology (especially vol. 2, on the covenant of redemption and Christ’s mediatorship)
- Compendium Theologiae Didactico-Elencticae
- Selected Questions on the Covenant (within Institutes)
- Willem J. van Asselt, The Federal Theology of Francis Turretin (secondary)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **The Pactum Salutis as an Eternal Intra-Trinitarian Covenant**
   - The **pactum salutis** is an eternal agreement within the Trinity, especially between the Father and the Son.
   - It precedes and grounds the **covenant of grace** made with the elect in history.
   - The covenant involves distinct roles: the Father as promisor, the Son as mediator and surety, and the Spirit as applier.
   - This covenant is the foundation for the Son’s mission and mediatorial work.
   "The covenant of redemption is nothing else than the agreement between the Father and the Son, by which the Son undertook to redeem the human race." (Institutes, 2.255)

2. **Christ as Federal Head and Surety**
   - Christ represents the elect as their **covenant head** in the pactum salutis.
   - He assumes all legal and moral obligations required for their salvation.
   - His obedience and satisfaction are imputed to those whom he represents.
   - The federal headship of Christ is essential for the efficacy of the atonement.
   "Christ is constituted the head and surety of the elect by the pact made with the Father." (Institutes, 2.257)

3. **Distinction Between Covenant of Redemption and Covenant of Grace**
   - The **covenant of redemption** is eternal and intra-Trinitarian; the **covenant of grace** is historical and with the elect.
   - The former is the cause, the latter the effect.
   - The terms, parties, and conditions differ between the two covenants.
   - This distinction safeguards the initiative and sovereignty of God in salvation.
   "The covenant of grace flows from the covenant of redemption as its fountain." (Institutes, 2.255)

4. **Necessity and Voluntariness of Christ’s Obedience**
   - Christ’s obedience is both necessary (by covenant) and voluntary (by consent).
   - The Son freely agrees to the terms set by the Father in the pactum salutis.
   - This voluntary obedience fulfills all righteousness required by the covenant.
   - The merit of Christ’s work is rooted in his willing submission as covenant head.
   "The Son did not undertake the work of redemption by compulsion, but by the most free consent." (Institutes, 2.256)

5. **Ground of Assurance and Immutability of Salvation**
   - The unbreakable nature of the **pactum salutis** ensures the certainty of redemption for the elect.
   - God’s faithfulness to his own covenant guarantees the application of salvation.
   - The work of Christ as covenant head is the objective ground of assurance.
   - The Spirit’s application of redemption is rooted in the eternal covenant.
   "The immutability of the covenant of redemption is the foundation of the immutability of our salvation." (Institutes, 2.258)

## Legacy and Impact

Turretin’s articulation of the **pactum salutis** and Christ’s federal headship became a cornerstone of later **Reformed covenant theology**, shaping confessional standards and systematic theology. His careful distinctions and robust defense of intra-Trinitarian covenantal relations influenced theologians such as Herman Witsius and Jonathan Edwards. Turretin’s clarity and precision continue to inform contemporary discussions on the nature of Christ’s mediatorial work and the theological grounding of assurance.
