### TITLE
One person, two natures: safeguarding the hypostatic union without collapsing or dividing Christ

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core conceptual issue: How to confess that in the one person of the incarnate Son there subsist two complete, distinct natures, divine and human, without mixture, change, division, or separation, and how that union grounds salvation and worship.
- Decisive figures: Athanasius, Cyril of Alexandria, Leo the Great, Thomas Aquinas, John Calvin.
- Relevant traditions: Nicene and post‑Nicene patristic theology, conciliar Christology of Ephesus and Chalcedon, medieval scholastic synthesis, Reformation and post‑Reformation Reformed orthodoxy.
- Debates addressed:
  - Internal: Alexandrian vs Antiochene emphases, monothelite controversies, Reformed vs Lutheran Christology, Reformed internal debates on extra Calvinisticum and genus idiomaticum.
  - External: Arianism and Apollinarianism, Nestorianism and Eutychianism, modern kenoticism and functional Christologies.
  - Contemporary: analytic models of the incarnation, “two minds” Christology, non‑incarnational or adoptionist tendencies in liberal and analytic theology.
- Defended position: A Chalcedonian, Reformed account of the **hypostatic union** in which the eternal Son subsists as one person in two complete, unconfused, inseparable natures, with **anhypostasia–enhypostasia** and the **extra Calvinisticum** clarifying the union’s mode without denying **divine simplicity** or true human experience.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of the **hypostatic union** answers a precise conceptual question: in what sense is Jesus Christ both God and man such that his person is identical with the eternal Son and yet he lives, acts, suffers, dies, and rises in a genuinely human way? To put it more technically, the church confesses that the one hypostasis of the Son subsists in two distinct, complete natures, divine and human, united without composition into a tertium quid and without creating a second subject of subsistence. This is not a pious paradox that eludes conceptual analysis; it is an ordered judgment, developed to secure the gospel against distortions. A Westminster oral examiner will immediately press whether you can state, unpack, and defend that judgment with respect to the doctrine of God, anthropology, soteriology, and worship.

Historically, the question arises from the confession that the crucified and risen Jesus is Lord, sharing the divine name and prerogatives. Athanasius of Alexandria stands at the threshold of full Christological clarification. His primary battle is with Arian subordinationism, so his central concern is the homoousion of the Son with the Father. Yet his anti‑Arian works, especially On the Incarnation and the Orations against the Arians, are already Christological in the technical sense. Athanasius insists that the one who became man is the very Word through whom all things were made. Soteriologically, this yields his famous axiom: what is not assumed is not healed. Behind that maxim is an incipient account of the **hypostatic union**: the Word must assume the full human nature, not a quasi‑human or incomplete humanity, and that assumption must be personal. It is the Word who acts, suffers, and saves in and through that humanity.

Here the first external debates arise. Against Apollinarius, who denied that the incarnate Christ had a human rational soul, Athanasius’s principle implies the necessity of a complete human nature. If the Logos replaces the human mind, then the human mind is not healed. Against Arianism, Athanasius undermines any scheme that would reduce the incarnation to a kind of partnership between a superlative creature and a man. Yet Athanasius does not yet possess the later technical language of nature and person that will become decisive. His emphasis on the subjectivity of the Word paves the way for Cyril of Alexandria, who will give that intuition conciliar form.

Cyril’s struggle with Nestorius is arguably the furnace in which the doctrine of the **hypostatic union** is forged. Nestorius, anxious to preserve the transcendence of the divine nature, spoke as though in Christ there were two subjects, or at least two loosely coordinated prosopa, divine and human, united morally or by indwelling rather than personally. This led him to reject the title Theotokos for Mary, preferring Christotokos, because in his scheme Mary bore the man who was conjoined to the Word, not the Word himself according to the flesh. Cyril saw that such a pattern dissolves the unity of Christ and thus the unity of redemptive action. His key conceptual move is to fix the unity at the level of hypostasis: the one who is born of Mary, suffers, and dies is the same one who is eternally begotten of the Father.

In his letters, especially the Third Letter to Nestorius and the Twelve Anathemas, Cyril articulates the union as a “natural” or “hypostatic” union, not a mere prosopic or moral union. He speaks of “one incarnate nature of the Word,” a formula that raises immediate concerns about monophysitism. To read Cyril rightly, you must distinguish his use of physis as often functioning where later orthodoxy will say hypostasis. He does not mean there is now only one abstract nature that is a synthesis of deity and humanity, but that there is one concrete existent, the Word, who now exists also as man. The decisive point is that he resists any division of subjects: the incarnate one is the Word himself, and all predicates, divine and human, are uttered of the one person. This grounds the **communication of idioms**: we confess that God shed his blood, that the Lord of glory was crucified, without implying that the divine nature in itself is passible. The examiner may press: does Cyril’s language not collapse the distinction of natures? The answer must insist that Cyril continuously affirms that the properties of the two are not confused or mingled. What is new is not a blended nature but a new mode of subsistence for the eternal Son.

The Council of Ephesus (431) vindicates Cyril against Nestorius, but leaves some ambiguities that will fuel further controversy. The Monophysite reaction, especially in Eutyches, truly collapses the distinction of natures, rendering Christ’s humanity absorbed into the divinity. Here Leo the Great becomes decisive. His Tome to Flavian is perhaps the single most powerful pre‑Chalcedonian statement of the **hypostatic union** in the fully articulated dyophysite sense. Leo insists that in the incarnation “the proper character of both natures was maintained and came together in a single person.” He speaks of each nature performing what is proper to it, yet in communion with the other. In Christ there is one and the same subject of action, but the actions are grounded in the distinct capacities of the two natures. When he hungers, he does so according to the human nature; when he stills the storm, he does so according to the divine nature; yet in both cases it is the one Christ who acts.

Chalcedon (451) crystallizes Leo’s insight and Cyril’s insistence on personal unity in its famous formula: one and the same Christ, Son, Lord, only‑begotten, to be confessed in two natures, without confusion, change, division, or separation. The distinction of natures is by no means removed by the union, but the properties of each nature are preserved and concur in one person and subsistence. This formula is the doctrinal baseline that a Westminster examiner will assume. It gives the grammar: nature indicates what something is, with its essential properties and capacities; person or hypostasis indicates who the subject is, the concrete subsistent. In Christ there is one who and two whats. The union is not at the level of abstract essence but at the level of personal subsistence. This also guards **divine simplicity**, since the divine nature remains what it eternally is, without addition or composition, even as the Son assumes a created human nature into his person.

Internal debates within the patristic and conciliar tradition did not cease at Chalcedon. The monothelite and monenergist controversies, resolved at the Third Council of Constantinople, made explicit what was implicit: if Christ has two complete natures, he must have two wills and two energies, human and divine. A single theandric operation or a single will would again risk either the denial of full humanity or the introduction of change into the divine nature. Dyothelitism secures that Christ’s human obedience is truly and freely human, yet always theandric in the sense that it is the obedience of the person who is the Word. Here one sees how **hypostatic union** governs the doctrine of Christ’s mediatorial work: as the last Adam, he offers a human obedience and suffering to God, but that obedience has infinite worth because of the person who offers it.

The medieval synthesis, especially in Thomas Aquinas, deepens and clarifies this inheritance with exact metaphysical language. For Aquinas, the key is to deploy Aristotelian substance‑accident ontology while respecting Chalcedonian boundaries. In the Summa Theologiae, Third Part, questions 2 through 6, he argues that the union occurs in the person, not in the nature. The humanity of Christ does not subsist as a separate hypostasis; its subsistence is in the person of the Word. This is later captured in the categories of **anhypostasia** and **enhypostasia**: the human nature of Christ is anhypostatic in that it does not have an independent personal subsistence, and enhypostatic in that it subsists in the divine hypostasis of the Son. Aquinas insists on a real distinction of natures, with distinct intellects and wills, but affirms that the human nature is united so intimately to the Word that one can predicate of God what belongs to man according to the human nature and vice versa, always with the backing rule of the **communication of idioms**.

Aquinas also explicitly relates the incarnation to **divine simplicity** and immutability. He argues that the assumption of a human nature represents not a change in God but a new relation of the creature to God. The Word does not undergo intrinsic alteration; rather, the humanity comes to participate in the subsistence of the Word. Examiners will probe this: if the Word becomes flesh, does he not change? The Thomistic and broader classical answer is that becoming flesh is a change in the human nature and in the world’s relation to God, not in the divine essence. This preserves the Creator‑creature distinction and avoids a quasi‑pantheistic collapse.

At this point a key external critique emerges in modern theology: that such classical Christology is “docetic” because the metaphysical guardrails allegedly prevent Christ from sharing in our condition. But precisely the opposite is true. Aquinas, like the Fathers, affirms a complete human nature with body and rational soul, a human intellect and will, human emotions and growth in acquired knowledge, all within the unity of the person of the Son. Without this, Christ would not be consubstantial with us and could not function as second Adam or sympathetic high priest. A good defense here must show that classical Christology guards the full range of human predicates, but locates their subject in the eternal Son.

The Reformation does not reject Chalcedon but re‑appropriates it. John Calvin, as a decisive figure, exemplifies a Reformed commitment to patristic Christology while sharpening certain aspects against both medieval speculation and emerging Lutheran alternatives. In the Institutes, especially book 2, chapters 12 to 14, and in his polemics against Osiander and the Lutheran ubiquity view, Calvin insists on the unconfused distinction of natures and the unity of the person. He repeatedly stresses that the finite is not capable of the infinite in the sense of an essential communication of divine attributes to the human nature. This becomes codified as the Reformed denial of the Lutheran genus maiestaticum. For Calvin, if the human nature of Christ were made omnipresent or omniscient essentially, its finite character would be abolished, and with it the true humanity upon which our salvation depends.

Here arises the internal Protestant debate between Reformed and Lutheran Christologies. Lutherans, driven by a powerful account of sacramental presence, argue that in the **hypostatic union** the human nature is endowed with all the majesty of the divine nature, so that Christ’s body and blood can be present in, with, and under the elements of the Supper everywhere. They appeal to the **communication of idioms** to justify predicating divine attributes of the human nature. The Reformed reply, in Calvin and later orthodoxy, is that communication is of predicates to the person, not of attributes from one nature to the other. Thus we can say that the man Jesus is omnipresent or omnipotent, but only because the person who is man is also the eternal Son according to the divine nature. This precision attempts to maintain Chalcedon’s “without confusion” while still honoring the unity of the agent.

The famous **extra Calvinisticum** expresses another Reformed instinct: while the Son is truly present in and with his assumed humanity, he also continues to exist and act beyond that humanity according to the divine nature. The Word is not enclosed by the flesh. Critics accuse this of Nestorianism, but the Reformed respond that they are simply protecting the transcendence of the Logos and the integrity of divine omnipresence. The examiner may ask whether this implies two persons or a divided Christ. The answer draws on the same Chalcedonian grammar: there is one person subsisting in two natures, and the personal subject is identical in both modes of existence. The fact that the divine nature can operate extra carnem does not generate a second subject; rather, it displays the asymmetry of Creator and creature and thus reinforces **analogical knowledge** of God. We do not know in a univocal way what it is for the Son to live a human life while remaining what he is as God.

Within Reformed theology there are further internal debates that clarify the **hypostatic union**. One concerns how to describe the communion of natures without attributing a quasi‑composite substance to Christ. Post‑Reformation scholastics like Turretin press the notion of personal union and reject any language that suggests a third composite nature. Another concerns the role of the Holy Spirit in Christ’s humanity. Some modern Reformed theologians, wanting to avoid a quasi‑docetic picture of the incarnate life, emphasize the Spirit’s anointing as the proximate cause of Christ’s miracles and obedience, thereby preserving his human development. Here one must carefully maintain that the Spirit’s work does not replace the personal agency of the Son but is the mode by which the Son, as man, lives in perfect dependence on the Father.

Contemporary analytic theology has reopened Christology using tools from philosophy of mind and metaphysics. Proposals such as “two minds” Christology seek to model Christ as having a divine mind and a human mind, related perhaps by something like a functional hierarchy. Kenotic Christologies suggest that the Son divested himself of some divine attributes in the incarnation, limiting omniscience or omnipotence to become truly human. From a classical, Chalcedonian, Reformed standpoint, these external and contemporary models often threaten either **divine simplicity** or the completeness of Christ’s human nature. A two‑minds model may be useful if it is merely an analytic restatement of two intellects in one person, but it easily slides into suggesting two centers of consciousness that approximate two persons. Kenoticism, by positing divestiture of properties, directly collides with God’s immutability. The classical alternative, defended here, insists that incarnation is not subtraction but addition: the Son assumes a human nature without ceasing to possess the fullness of deity. Any limitation in Christ’s human knowledge or power is located in the finite capacities of the human nature, not in a mutilated divine nature.

Modern functionalist Christologies, which define Christ primarily by his role or function rather than by ontological claims about his natures, also press from another angle. Under the guise of biblical fidelity or concern for narrative‑historical categories, they resist metaphysical language of natures and persons as Greek intrusion. The defense of classical **hypostatic union** here must show that the New Testament implicitly demands such language: only if the same subject who pre‑exists in the form of God also takes the form of a servant, and only if the crucified one is Lord and God, can the soteriological and doxological pattern of Scripture be sustained. The church’s metaphysics is an attempt to give disciplined account of these canonical judgments, not to supplant them.

The doctrine of the **hypostatic union** also interfaces with adjacent loci in ways that examiners will exploit. In soteriology, Christ’s mediatorial office presupposes that he is consubstantial with both God and humanity. His priestly sacrifice and intercession, royal rule, and prophetic revelation all depend on the unity of person and distinction of natures. If Christ were merely a Spirit‑filled man, his mediation would lack ontological depth. If his humanity were swallowed by divinity, he could not genuinely represent us. Union with Christ in salvation presupposes our union by the Spirit to this one person, so that by participation in him we share in the benefits of his obedience and resurrection. That union is not a fusion of substances but a covenantal and pneumatic relation to the person whose **hypostatic union** grounds the efficacy of all his works.

In the doctrine of God, the incarnation stress‑tests our confession of **divine simplicity**, immutability, and impassibility. A common objection is that if God truly suffers and dies in Christ, then he is passible and mutable. The classical Christological answer insists that suffering and death are predicated of the person according to the human nature. We can truly say “God suffers” because the person who is God suffers as man. Yet in the divine nature, God remains impassible. This is not a verbal evasion but a disciplined application of Chalcedonian grammar. Any move that attributes intrinsic change to the divine essence in the incarnation undermines the Creator‑creature distinction and the very possibility of grace as free and sovereign.

Finally, in relation to Scripture, the **hypostatic union** grounds the unique authority of Christ’s teaching and the integrity of his human learning. He grows in wisdom according to his human intellect, yet his words are the words of God. This suggests a pattern for understanding **analogical knowledge** in the economy: our human apprehension of God is real yet finite, and in Christ this finite human knowledge is singularly and perfectly ordered to the Father by the Spirit, without collapsing finite and infinite into one.

The position defended, then, is a Chalcedonian, classical, Reformed account of the **hypostatic union**. The eternal Son, of one essence with the Father, assumes in the fullness of time a complete human nature, body and rational soul, so that there is one person in two natures. The natures remain distinct, without mixture, change, division, or separation. The human nature has no independent hypostasis but subsists anhypostatically and enhypostatically in the hypostasis of the Son. The person is the single subject of all the incarnate operations, with each nature operating according to its properties. There is no essential communication of attributes from one nature to the other, but there is a real **communication of idioms** to the person. The Son remains omnipresent and infinite according to his divine nature, even while locally present and finite according to his human nature, as articulated by the **extra Calvinisticum**. This union is the ontological ground of salvation, worship, and revelation, and it can be defended rigorously without surrendering classical doctrines of God.

A robust defense in oral examination will not only recite Chalcedon but also show how Athanasius’s soteriological principle, Cyril’s insistence on personal unity, Leo and Chalcedon’s dyophysite formula, Aquinas’s metaphysical precision, and Calvin’s Reformed clarifications cohere into a single theological judgment. It will also expose the weaknesses of Arian, Apollinarian, Nestorian, Eutychian, kenotic, and functionalist alternatives, and it will show how contemporary analytic models can assist or distort the confession. Above all, it will keep before the mind that this doctrine is not speculative ornamentation but the church’s guarded way of saying who Jesus is, so that the confession “My Lord and my God” is both doxologically fitting and conceptually intelligible.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: The **hypostatic union** is the personal union whereby the one eternal Son subsists as a single person in two complete, distinct natures, divine and human, united without confusion, change, division, or separation, such that all actions and sufferings of Christ are the actions of this one person according to one or the other nature.
- Decisive figures:
  - Athanasius: anti‑Arian defender of the homoousion and “what is not assumed is not healed,” implying full assumption of a complete human nature by the Word.
  - Cyril of Alexandria: against Nestorius, locates unity at the level of hypostasis, defends Theotokos, articulates **hypostatic union** and **communication of idioms**.
  - Leo the Great: Tome to Flavian stresses two natures acting in one person, undergirds Chalcedon’s “without confusion, change, division, separation.”
  - Thomas Aquinas: refines Chalcedon with **anhypostasia–enhypostasia**, two wills and intellects in one person, safeguards **divine simplicity** and immutability.
  - John Calvin: Reformed appropriation of Chalcedon, denies essential transfer of attributes between natures, formulates **extra Calvinisticum**, sharpens **communication of idioms**.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Alexandrian vs Antiochene tendencies, monophysite and monothelite controversies, Reformed vs Lutheran Christology on genus maiestaticum and ubiquity, intra‑Reformed nuances on extra Calvinisticum and Spirit‑Christ relation.
  - External: Arian and Apollinarian denial of full deity or humanity, Nestorian duality of subjects, Eutychian confusion of natures, modern kenoticism and purely functional Christologies.
  - Contemporary: analytic “two minds” proposals, liberal and adoptionist tendencies, critiques of classical doctrines of **divine simplicity**, immutability, and impassibility as incompatible with incarnation.
- Defended position: A classical, Chalcedonian, Reformed account where the one hypostasis of the Son assumes a complete human nature, with real distinction of natures, unity of person, **anhypostatic–enhypostatic** humanity, no essential communication of attributes, but full **communication of idioms** to the person, consistent with **divine simplicity** and grounding salvation and worship.
- Likely examiner questions and one‑line answers:
  - How do you distinguish nature and person in Christ? Nature answers what he is with its properties; person answers who he is as the single subject who subsists in and operates through both natures.
  - Does the incarnation change God? No, the change is in the assumed human nature and in the world’s relation to God; the divine essence remains simple and immutable.
  - How do you avoid Nestorianism while affirming the extra Calvinisticum? By insisting that the same hypostasis acts both in and beyond the flesh, so there is one subject in two modes of existence, not two persons.
  - What does “what is not assumed is not healed” require? It requires that the Word assume a complete human nature, including rational soul and will, so that every aspect of humanity is redeemed.
  - How is the communication of idioms rightly understood? Predicates of either nature are truly predicated of the one person, not because natures exchange properties, but because the person who is man is also God.
