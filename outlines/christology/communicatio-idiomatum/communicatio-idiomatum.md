### TITLE
Predication without confusion: how one person bears two natures in the communication of idioms.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Chalcedonian Christology 451** within **Theology Proper and Christology**, clarifying how the one person of Christ is spoken of after the **hypostatic union**.
- Core biblical or theological question: How can we truly say that **God suffered** and that **the man Jesus is omnipotent**, without collapsing the **two natures without confusion**?

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The communication of idioms, or **communication of properties**, names the rule that what properly belongs to either nature of Christ may be truly predicated of the one person of the incarnate Son. It does not mean that properties transfer between natures, but that the subject of predication is the single **hypostasis of the Son**, who subsists in two natures. The classic formula of **Chalcedonian dyophysitism** in 451 governs the grammar: one and the same Christ, Son, Lord, Only-begotten, known in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation. This rule operationalizes **hypostatic union** in speech, enabling us to say, for instance, that Mary is the **Mother of God** and that God died, while denying any confusion of divine and human properties.

The dogmatic location sits within **Christology** as articulated by **Ephesus 431** and **Chalcedon 451**, with metaphysical underpinnings in the **person-nature distinction** and later clarified by **anhypostasis/enhypostasis** in Leontius of Byzantium. The **Theopaschite formula** received in **Constantinople II 553** guards that one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh, indexing both the unity of person and the integrity of natures. The rule’s function intersects **Theology Proper** whenever divine attributes and **divine impassibility** are invoked, and it bears directly on **soteriology** given the axiom that what is not assumed is not healed.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture regularly ascribes to the one Christ predicates that, according to their mode, belong to either His divinity or His humanity, thereby undergirding the **communication of idioms**. Acts 20:28 speaks of the church of God which He purchased with His own blood, a theologically thick expression of the **Theopaschite insight**. Paul’s confession in 1 Corinthians 2:8 that the rulers crucified the Lord of glory confirms the unity of person as the subject enduring passion according to the **assumed human nature**. Romans 9:5 identifies Christ as over all, God blessed forever, even as the Gospels recount His hunger, thirst, and growth in wisdom, evidencing a coherent pattern of **personal predication**.

At the same time, the New Testament preserves distinct attributions that encourage careful **reduplicative predication**. In Mark 13:32, the Son does not know the day or hour according to His humanity, while in John 16:30 the disciples confess He knows all things, according to His divinity. John 1:14 declares that the Word became flesh, holding together **incarnation** and **immutability** by identifying the Word as the unchanging subject who assumes a human nature. Philippians 2:6–8’s being in the form of God yet taking the form of a servant resists subtraction from deity and teaches the **two-natures Christology** in the economy of humiliation.

The evangelists’ narratives also employ titles that cross expected boundaries, legitimizing **Cyrillian predication**. Luke 1:43 calls Mary the mother of my Lord, echoing **Theotokos** and authorizing speech that predicates of the one person realities sourced in either nature. John 19:28 I thirst and John 8:58 Before Abraham was, I am, are simultaneously true of the one **person of the Son**, though under different respects. This biblical practice becomes dogmatic grammar through **Chalcedon 451**, ensuring our speech neither divides the subject nor confuses **natural properties**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The crucible of the doctrine spans from **Nestorius and Cyril** to **Chalcedon and Constantinople II**. Nestorius’s reluctance to call Mary **Theotokos** and his preference for Christotokos signaled an overly loose junction of prosopa, threatening divided subjectivity; **Cyril of Alexandria** insisted on a unity of person in whom we may predicate divine and human realities without mixing natures. The **Council of Ephesus 431** vindicated Cyril’s concern, while **Leo’s Tome** to Flavian (449, received 451) supplied dyophysite clarity: each nature performs what is proper to it, in communion with the other, yet what belongs to each may be said of the one **person of Christ**.

In the sixth century, **Leontius of Byzantium** developed **anhypostasis/enhypostasis** to state that Christ’s human nature has no independent hypostasis but is enhypostasized in the **divine person** of the Word. This sharpened how **communication of idioms** works: it is the person who is the subject of all predicates, while the modes of enacting them remain nature-specific. The **Theopaschite formula** received definitive conciliar support in **553**, cohering with **divine impassibility** by specifying that the impassible Son suffered impassibly in His divine nature but truly suffered in the flesh according to His humanity.

The Reformation sharpened disputes around the communication of idioms, especially due to **Eucharistic controversies**. **Luther** pressed a strong **genus maiestaticum**, arguing the human nature participates in divine majesty such that Christ’s body is ubiquitous, foundational for the **Formula of Concord 1577**. **Zwingli** and later **Calvin** refused ubiquity to the human nature, insisting on the **extra Calvinisticum**, that the Word is not circumscribed by the flesh and so exists beyond, though never apart from, the assumed humanity. Seventeenth-century **Reformed scholastics** such as **Turretin 1679–1685** codified the Reformed schema of genera, while **Roman Catholic Thomists** maintained the classical **suppositum** account of predication.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Reformed theology affirms the **genus idiomaticum**, that properties of either nature can be truly predicated of the **one person**, and the **genus apotelesmaticum**, that the operations of Christ’s mediatorial work are theandric, belonging to the person acting through both natures. It rejects the **genus maiestaticum** as entailing a transfusion of divine attributes to the human nature. This rejection is tied to the axiom finitium non capax infiniti and the **Chalcedonian without confusion**, protecting real creaturely integrity of Christ’s humanity while preserving **divine simplicity and immutability**.

The **Westminster Confession 1646–1647** teaches that the Son, being very and eternal God, of one substance and equal with the Father, did, when the fullness of time was come, take upon Him man’s nature, yet without sin, so that two whole, perfect, and distinct natures were inseparably joined together in one person without conversion, composition, or confusion. Old Princeton figures like **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield** stressed that all predicates are formally true of the person, not the natures considered abstractly, and so guarded **theopaschite predication** only with the qualifier in the flesh. **W. G. T. Shedd** treated the union as personal, not essential, denying any deification of the human nature while affirming the integrity of **Christ’s human soul and will**.

Dutch Neo-Calvinists such as **Herman Bavinck** and **Abraham Kuyper** echoed the same contours. **Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics** insists that the **extra Calvinisticum** is simply the entailment of **divine infinity**, not a detachment of the Word from His humanity. For Bavinck, the communication of idioms underwrites the unity of Christ’s saving acts while leaving untouched the distinction of **divine and human energies**, thereby anchoring the Reformed understanding of **sacramental presence** as spiritual and real, rather than local and bodily.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology classically follows **Aquinas ST III**, where the **person, not the nature**, is the subject of predication; thus vera Dei Genitrix and God suffered are true, with reduplication when needed. Aquinas avoids any **transference of properties** between natures while affirming a single supposit of action and passion. Post-Tridentine Thomism continued this line, coordinating the **communication of idioms** with **real presence** in the Eucharist without committing to the **ubiquity** thesis characteristic of Lutheranism.

Lutheran scholasticism elaborated three genera: the **genus idiomaticum**, the **genus maiestaticum** (communication of divine majesty to the human nature), and the **genus apotelesmaticum**. The **Formula of Concord 1577** defends the ubiquity of Christ’s human body on the basis of the personal union and the right hand of God motif. This aimed to secure **sacramental union** and the bodily presence of Christ in the Supper. Critics from the Reformed side contended that this violates **Chalcedonian without confusion**, while Lutherans replied that the genus maiestaticum follows from the unique grace of **incarnation** rather than a general deification of the creature.

Modern kenotic Christologies, beginning with **G. Thomasius in the 1840s** and appearing in **Charles Gore 1891**, proposed that the Logos emptied Himself by temporarily laying aside or veiling certain **divine attributes**. These moves often reconceive the **communication of idioms** as grounded in an ontological reduction of divinity in the economy, thereby resolving tensions about **omniscience and human ignorance** by weakening immutability. Classical critics object that this compromises **divine simplicity and aseity**, whereas proponents argue it honors the exegesis of Philippians 2 by giving substantive content to **kenosis**.

Contemporary analytic theology has reframed the doctrine with logical precision. **Thomas V. Morris’s Logic of God Incarnate 1986** and **Oliver Crisp’s Divinity and Humanity 2007** deploy **reduplicative predication** and the qua operator to show how one person may be truly omniscient qua divine and non-omniscient qua human. **Tim Pawl’s In Defense of Conciliar Christology 2016** argues that the **Chalcedonian and later conciliar** claims are consistent and coherent under standard metaphysics, reinforcing that the **communication of idioms** is predication to a suppositum with two complete natures. These accounts often lean on **two-minds models** to handle Christ’s consciousness without implying Nestorian dual subjects.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A recurring objection claims that the communication of idioms is mere verbalism, a linguistic device masking metaphysical opacity. The reply, following **Chalcedonian Christology**, is that language here tracks ontology: the **person-nature distinction** grounds the predications, and predicates are true because the one person subsists in two natures. The **Theopaschite formula** ensures that talk of divine suffering is not equivocation but carefully indexed to the Son’s suffering in the flesh, leaving **divine impassibility ad intra** intact while affirming the reality of the cross.

Another debate concerns whether the **extra Calvinisticum** jeopardizes the unity of Christ. Reformed theologians answer that it simply follows from **divine infinity** and that the Son’s personal union with the assumed humanity is indivisible. Conversely, does the **genus maiestaticum** compromise Christ’s true humanity by attributing ubiquity to a finite body? Lutheran theologians reply that the communication of majesty is unique to Christ’s human nature because of the **hypostatic union**, not a generic blurring of Creator and creature. The dispute thus returns to how **Chalcedonian without confusion** and **without separation** are balanced.

Questions of consciousness and will persist. If Christ has a divine and a human will, per **Constantinople III 680–681**, how do we avoid two acting subjects? The answer, classically, is that there is one **personal agent** enacting operations through two natural principles, and the **genus apotelesmaticum** names the unity of those acts. Analytic appeals to **reduplicative predication** and two-minds models attempt to preserve this, though some worry about Nestorian overtones; here **anhypostasis/enhypostasis** remains crucial.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Within **Theology Proper**, the doctrine safeguards the identity of the Son with the Father and Spirit while specifying the **missions reflect processions** structure that **Rahners Rule** highlights. The Son who is consubstantial with the Father assumes a human nature; the **communication of idioms** allows us to say that God shed His blood without implying change in the divine essence. This dovetails with **divine simplicity** by locating change and passion in the assumed humanity, not within the **ad intra** life of God.

In **Soteriology**, the communication of idioms underwrites the efficacy of atonement: it is the one who is **God and man** who mediates, so that His obedience and blood have infinite worth. This aligns with **Calvins autotheos** and the Reformed insistence on the Son’s full deity, grounding the sufficiency of satisfaction while preserving the integrity of **human obedience** as the mode of redemption. The patristic maxim what is not assumed is not healed is guaranteed by the doctrine’s insistence on full **human nature** with its own mind and will.

In **Ecclesiology and Sacramentology**, the doctrine is decisive for the **Eucharist**. Lutheran appeals to the **genus maiestaticum** justify bodily presence, while Reformed appeals to the **extra Calvinisticum** and the **genus apotelesmaticum** ground a real spiritual presence through the Spirit’s ascent, not Christ’s body’s descent. In **Anthropology**, Christ’s deified but creaturely humanity becomes the eschatological template for our **glorification**, while the **communication of idioms** remains incommunicable to us, underscoring the uniqueness of the Mediator.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the doctrine authorizes robust proclamation: we may say with biblical confidence that **God suffered and died for us**, while catechizing that He did so according to His **assumed humanity**. This guards the affective center of the Gospel without surrendering **divine immutability**, granting believers assurance that the Mediator is truly one of us and truly the Lord. In liturgy, the title **Theotokos** and hymns addressing Christ in both lowliness and glory practice **communication of idioms** as doxological grammar.

It also trains prudence in preaching and prayer. When we say the **Lord of glory was crucified**, we avoid implying change in deity by remembering **Chalcedonian without confusion**. When we confess the ascended Christ’s presence, we discern the difference between **local bodily presence** and **spiritual real presence**, preventing category mistakes. Thus the doctrine yields doxological boldness with metaphysical chastity, empowering pastoral speech that is both tender and theologically exact.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The communication of idioms is the Chalcedonian rule that lets the church speak truly of the one Christ in whom divine and human meet without confusion, division, or diminution.
