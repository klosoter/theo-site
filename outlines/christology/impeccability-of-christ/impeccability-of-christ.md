### TITLE
Able-not-to-sin or Not-able-to-sin? The Person-centered logic of Christ’s sinlessness

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: Patristic roots clarified at 451 and 680–681; refined by Reformed orthodoxy 1560–1700; reconsidered in 19th–21st c. analytic and kenotic debates; locus in **Christology** with implications for **Theology Proper** and **Soteriology**.
- Core biblical or theological question: Was Jesus merely able not to sin, or was he not able to sin, given **hypostatic union** and **dyothelitism**.
- Principal figures or schools (with short roles): **Athanasius** and **Gregory Nazianzen** on sinless assumption; **Maximus the Confessor** on two wills; **Aquinas** on impeccability via **beatific vision**; **Luther** and **Calvin** on sinlessness; **Owen**, **Hodge**, **Warfield**, **Bavinck** defending Reformed impeccability; **Thomasius** kenosis; **Morris**, **Crisp**, **Pawl** analytic models.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **non posse peccare** vs **posse non peccare**; **communicatio idiomatum**; **extra Calvinisticum**; **original righteousness**; **active obedience**; **divine immutability**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: Hebrews 4:15; James 1:13; Luke 4; 2 Cor 5:21; 1 Pet 2:22; **Chalcedon 451**; **Constantinople III 680–681**; **WCF 1646–1648**; **Aquinas ST III 1265–1274**; **Warfield, Person and Work of Christ 1908**; **Bavinck, Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: Reformed insist on **person-based impeccability** and **two-wills harmony**; Roman Catholic emphasize **grace of union** and often **beatific vision**; Lutheran uphold sinlessness within **genus maiestaticum**; modern kenoticists risk **peccability**; analytic philosophers offer **two-minds models**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Can Christ genuinely sympathize if he could not sin, and how does **libertarian freedom vs compatibilism** shape the account of his temptations.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The impeccability of Christ asserts that the incarnate Son was not merely free of sin but was unable to sin, the classic claim of **non posse peccare** rather than **posse non peccare**. The question is not abstract capacity but personal subject: who could or could not sin. Because the acting subject is the one **person of the Son**, anything that would require the person to deviate from the divine will is ruled out by the **hypostatic union**. Impeccability thus sits within Christology yet draws on **Theology Proper** because it presupposes the Son’s immutable goodness.

Dogmatically, this is configured by **dyothelitism**, the confession of two wills in Christ defined at Constantinople III in 680–681. Christ has a created human will and an uncreated divine will, each operating according to its nature, yet without opposition in the one person. Following **Maximus the Confessor**, classical accounts deny a gnomic will in Christ, that deliberative vacillation associated with fallen ignorance. Thus, while Christ’s human will is truly human, it is never morally indeterminate toward evil; by the grace of union, it is impeccably ordered to the divine good, a point integrating **communicatio idiomatum** with Christ’s moral perfection.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The New Testament uniformly witnesses that Christ is sinless and yet tempted. Hebrews 4:15 affirms he has been tempted in every respect as we are, yet without sin, the phrase choris hamartias anchoring **without sin texts**. First Peter 2:22 echoes Isaiah 53:9, that he committed no sin, with 2 Corinthians 5:21 declaring that God made him to be sin for us who knew no sin. These are not literary flourishes; they ground the soteriological logic that only a sinless mediator can be our righteousness, underwriting **active obedience** and substitutionary atonement.

The Gospels narrate real temptations. Luke 4 and Matthew 4 depict Jesus tested by the devil, each temptation offering a messianic shortcut. The verb peirazein evokes both testing and enticement, yet the narratives show victory by Scripture-saturated fidelity. There is no hint of internal concupiscence; rather, Jesus faces external solicitation and responds as the obedient Son, confirming **second Adam** typology in Romans 5:12–19 and 1 Corinthians 15:45–49. The defined parameters of temptation align with James 1:13, which asserts that God is apeirastos kakon, untempted by evil, thereby inviting a precise Christological account that distinguishes the modes of temptation appropriate to his **divine and human natures**.

Crucially, the temptations do not become credible by entailing a real possibility of Christ’s sin but by encompassing genuine suffering, deprivation, and vocational testing. Hebrews 5:8 teaches that he learned obedience through what he suffered, indicating growth in experiential obedience without moral defect. The biblical data take shape as the matrix for **impeccability of Christ** when interpreted within conciliar Christology.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic voices supply the Christological grammar. **Athanasius** insists in On the Incarnation that the Word assumed what is ours to heal it, yet without sin, echoing **Gregory Nazianzen’s** axiom, what is not assumed is not healed. Sinlessness guards the efficacy of the incarnation while honoring **homoousios** and the uniqueness of Christ’s mediatorship. Chalcedon in 451 safeguards the union without confusion or division, implicitly requiring a sinless human nature united to the Son, and gives the metaphysical underpinning for later arguments about **impeccability**.

The decisive development arises with **dyothelitism** at the Third Council of Constantinople in 680–681, guided by **Maximus the Confessor**. Maximus distinguishes natural will from gnomic will and affirms that Christ’s human will never deliberated between good and evil in a fallen way. This secures the simultaneity of real temptation and unerring goodness. The medieval synthesis refines this: **Anselm**’s Cur Deus Homo circa 1098 presumes the fittingness of a sinless God-man for satisfaction; **Thomas Aquinas** in ST III, q.15 and q.18 argues that by the grace of union, the fullness of the Spirit, and, on his view, the **beatific vision**, Christ is impeccable. He is unable to sin because the person is divine and the human will is perfectly ordered to the end of God.

The Reformers maintained sinlessness with nuanced emphases. **Luther** stresses the real humanity and combat with the devil, yet Christ remains the spotless Lamb bearing others’ sins. **Calvin** underscores the Spirit-anointed obedience and resists any hint that Christ’s temptations imply peccability, relating this to **Calvins autotheos** and the majesty of the Son. Post-Reformation Reformed orthodoxy, such as **John Owen** in his Hebrews commentary 1668–1684 and On the Person of Christ 1667, carefully delineates external diabolic testing from internal lust, grounding Christ’s impeccability in his person while expounding the **communicatio idiomatum** and the Spirit’s role. In the nineteenth century, kenotic Christologies beginning with **G. Thomasius** in 1845–1856 suggested a self-limitation in incarnation that endangered classical impeccability. **B. B. Warfield** and Old Princeton replied that such kenosis undermines **divine immutability** and the soteriological necessity of an impeccable Christ.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Westminster standards 1646–1648 confess that the Lord Jesus was made under the law, yet without sin, fit for his office, a concise expression of **Westminster Christology**. The Larger Catechism speaks of Christ’s obedience and sacrifice as a lamb without blemish, insisting that priestly sympathy does not require peccability. Within this framework, impeccability functions to secure both **active obedience** and penal substitution without impugning the reality of temptation.

Old Princeton articulates a stringent **person-based impeccability**. **Charles Hodge** argues in his Systematic Theology 1873 that if the person is divine, he cannot sin; to posit that he could is to deny the divine nature’s moral necessity. **B. B. Warfield** emphasizes that Christ’s temptations were real occasions of suffering and moral triumph precisely because he could not sin, likening the case to a strong swimmer who feels the full force of the current yet cannot be swept away. The integrity of the **extra Calvinisticum** in Reformed thought further guards the transcendence of the Word, ensuring that the incarnate person never ceases to be who he is in the divine life.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism deepens this within a rich doctrine of creation and grace. **Herman Bavinck** argues that by the grace of union and fullness of the Spirit, Christ’s human will is confirmed in original righteousness, rendering him **impeccable** while fully human. He employs the **anhypostasia–enhypostasia** nexus to emphasize the real humanity of Christ subsisting in the person of the Son. **G. C. Berkouwer** in The Person of Christ 1954 wrestles with the phenomenology of temptation and affirms impeccability while warning against a docetic flattening of Christ’s human struggle. Across these strands, the Reformed align **dyothelitism** with divine immutability to affirm that Christ could be tempted and could not sin.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology classically affirms impeccability. **Aquinas** concludes that the grace of union and the assumed **beatific vision** yield impossibility of sin. Many twentieth-century Catholics, following **Pius XII** in Mystici Corporis 1943, maintain the beatific vision in Christ from conception, fortifying impeccability. Others, seeking psychological realism, downplay that claim while preserving impeccability by the grace of union and plenitude of the Spirit, a variant still centered on **hypostatic union**.

Lutheran theology likewise confesses sinlessness, coordinated with the **genus maiestaticum**. Christ’s human nature shares in the majesty of the divine through the personal union, a logic that can yield a robust account of incapacity to sin. In the state of humiliation, Lutherans insist Christ refrains from full use of divine prerogatives without surrendering **impeccability**. Sympathy is vested not in sinful inclination but in the depth of suffering under temptation, especially in the passion and dereliction.

Modern alternatives vary. Nineteenth-century **kenotic Christologies** sometimes flirt with peccability by positing that the Son reduces or suspends divine attributes in becoming man. In the twentieth century, certain process and relational theologies risk a picture of divine becoming that undermines **divine immutability** and so jeopardizes impeccability. In contrast, analytic philosophers and theologians have offered models that protect sinlessness while clarifying psychological coherence. **Thomas V. Morris** proposed a two-minds model in The Logic of God Incarnate 1986 that maintains full human consciousness and a divine mind, with the person as the integrator, thus accounting for **impeccability of Christ** without docetism. **Oliver Crisp** and **Timothy Pawl** defend conciliar Christology, affirming that the person’s essential goodness and the grace of union render sin metaphysically impossible even if temptations present genuine alternative reasons.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A recurring objection claims that if Christ could not sin, his temptations were not genuine. Classical replies distinguish between the reality of an ordeal and the possibility of failure. One can undergo authentic testing that causes suffering and growth in experiential obedience without possessing a power to sin. Here discussions of **libertarian freedom vs compatibilism** arise: is alternative possibility necessary for moral responsibility. Many theologians argue that Christ exhibits the highest freedom, freedom from sin, aligning impeccability with **perfected will** rather than abstract indifference.

Another objection cites James 1:13 that God cannot be tempted, against Gospel accounts that Christ was tempted. The answer is to apply conciliar Christology with **two-natures language**: as God *ad intra* the Son is untemptable, as man *ad extra* he is temptable with respect to created faculties, though not morally susceptible. The person is tempted according to his human nature; the divine person remains **immutable**. This is not a verbal trick but the logic of **dyothelitism** where the human will is the instrument of obedient victory.

A third worry concerns empathy. How can Christ sympathize if he is impeccable. Hebrews 4:15 grounds sympathy not in sinful inclination but in suffering under trial. **John Owen** argues that Christ’s trials were greater precisely because he never anesthetized pain by yielding; resistance intensifies pressure. Analytic proposals employ **two-minds models** to explain how Christ’s human psychology, with limited knowledge and real affective life, could experience temptation’s force while the person remained anchored in divine goodness. These responses converge on the claim that **pastoral empathy vs metaphysical security** is a false dichotomy.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Impeccability safeguards **Theology Proper** by protecting **divine immutability** and moral necessity. If the Son could sin, divine goodness would be contingent. It also consolidates Chalcedonian **Christology**, because the personal unity requires that the predicates of moral action refer to the one subject who is God the Son. Within **Anthropology**, Christ showcases unfallen human nature in **original righteousness**, confirmed and perfected by the grace of union and the Spirit’s anointing.

In **Soteriology**, impeccability grounds the sufficiency of **active obedience** and the spotless worth of the Lamb. Only a sinless Christ can be made sin for us without internal corruption, and only an impeccable Christ offers an indefectible righteousness to be imputed and imparted. In **Pneumatology**, the Spirit’s fullness upon Christ 30 CE at his baptism witnesses to the economy of empowerment that never competes with the Son but instruments his human obedience in history. In **Eschatology**, Christ’s impeccability is the fountain of the saints’ future **eschatological impeccability**, the non posse peccare of the glorified, patterned after the last Adam. In **Ecclesiology and Sacramentology**, the church’s holiness is derivative from union with the impeccable head, communicated by word and sacrament for the church’s sanctification.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, affirming **impeccability of Christ** stabilizes assurance. The High Priest who cannot fail has already triumphed, and his obedience is the believer’s ground of confidence. It also heightens the moral invitation: Christian holiness is not a fragile experiment but participation in Christ’s invincible righteousness, an outflow of **union with Christ** and the Spirit’s sanctifying work. Liturgically, the Lent lections on temptation, the Good Friday passion, and Easter victory rehearse the drama of obedient Sonship, inviting congregations to behold the one who is **holy, harmless, undefiled**.

In preaching, resist portraying temptations as inner corruption; rather present Christ as the warrior who felt temptation’s full weight without capitulating. In catechesis, teach the **non posse peccare** alongside Hebrews 4:15, helping believers see that sympathy is grounded in suffering and obedience. In spiritual counsel, direct the tempted to the impeccable Savior whose perfect prayer and priesthood avail for the weak, integrating **pastoral empathy vs metaphysical security** into practical consolation.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Christ’s impeccability is the personal inevitability of sinless obedience by the incarnate Son, in whom genuine temptation and invincible holiness converge for our salvation.
