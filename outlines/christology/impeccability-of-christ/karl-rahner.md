# Karl Rahner (1904–1984)

Karl Rahner approached the **impeccability of Christ** through his distinctive **Christological anthropology**, emphasizing the unity of Christ’s **divine and human natures** within the framework of **transcendental theology**. For Rahner, Christ’s inability to sin is rooted in the hypostatic union, where the **Son’s divine personhood** guarantees the sinlessness of the human nature, while still affirming the full reality of Christ’s human freedom and temptability.

- "The possibility of sinning is not a necessary consequence of the possession of a human nature, but only of a human person." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

## Key Works

- Foundations of Christian Faith (Grundkurs des Glaubens)
- Theological Investigations, Vol. 1: God, Christ, Mary and Grace
- Theological Investigations, Vol. 4: More Recent Writings
- Christology within an Evolutionary View of the World (in Theological Investigations, Vol. 5)
- Encyclopedia of Theology: A Concise Sacramentum Mundi (editor)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Hypostatic Union as the Ground of Impeccability**
    - Christ’s human nature is assumed by the **divine Logos**, not by a human person.
    - The divine personhood ensures that Christ’s human will is always aligned with the divine will.
    - Impeccability is not a limitation of Christ’s humanity but a consequence of the union.
    - The unity of personhood precludes the possibility of Christ choosing sin.
    - "The human nature of Christ is not a subject existing for itself, but is assumed by the Logos." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

2. **Affirmation of Real Human Freedom**
    - Christ possesses authentic human freedom, including the capacity for genuine temptation.
    - This freedom is exercised without the possibility of sin due to the hypostatic union.
    - Rahner rejects any docetic tendencies that would undermine Christ’s true humanity.
    - The experience of temptation is real, though sin is impossible.
    - "He was really tempted, but not because he could have sinned." (Theological Investigations, Vol. 1)

3. **Transcendental Christology**
    - Christ is the concrete realization of the existential openness to God found in all humanity.
    - The divine self-communication in Christ is unique and definitive.
    - Impeccability is understood within the context of Christ as the absolute self-communication of God.
    - The mystery of Christ’s personhood is the foundation for understanding his sinlessness.
    - "Christ is the one in whom the self-communication of God to the world is irrevocably and definitively realized." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

4. **Rejection of Kenotic Christology**
    - Rahner opposes any notion that the Logos “emptied” himself of divine attributes, including impeccability.
    - The divine attributes remain fully present in the incarnate Christ.
    - Impeccability is not a result of diminished humanity but of the enduring presence of the divine person.
    - The kenosis is a mode of presence, not a subtraction of divine prerogatives.
    - "The Logos does not cease to be what he is in the incarnation." (Theological Investigations, Vol. 4)

5. **Impeccability as Soteriological Necessity**
    - Christ’s sinlessness is essential for his role as the universal mediator of salvation.
    - Only a sinless mediator can effect reconciliation between God and humanity.
    - Impeccability guarantees the efficacy of Christ’s redemptive work.
    - The sinlessness of Christ is both a gift for humanity and a revelation of God’s holiness.
    - "The sinlessness of Christ is the presupposition of his salvific function." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

## Legacy and Impact

Rahner’s approach to the **impeccability of Christ** has shaped modern Roman Catholic and ecumenical Christology by integrating **transcendental anthropology** with classical dogmatic formulations. His insistence on the real humanity and freedom of Christ, alongside the necessity of impeccability, has influenced subsequent debates on the nature of Christ’s temptations and the meaning of the hypostatic union. Rahner’s nuanced synthesis continues to inform contemporary discussions on the relationship between Christ’s person and work.
