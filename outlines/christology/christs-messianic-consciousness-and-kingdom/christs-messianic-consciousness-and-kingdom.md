### TITLE
Abba and the Kingdom: Jesus’ Self-Knowledge as Messiah and the Inaugurated Reign of God

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Christology and Eschatology**, first century origins through twentieth-century retrievals, focused on Jesus’ identity and the nature of the **Kingdom of God**.
- Core biblical or theological question: What did Jesus know about being **Messiah and Son**, and how did that self-consciousness shape his proclamation of the **inaugurated Kingdom**.
- Principal figures or schools with short roles: **Wrede** demystifies messianic claims, **Schweitzer** apocalyptic emphasis, **Dodd** realized eschatology, **Ladd** already and not yet, **Vos and Ridderbos** Reformed kingdom theology, **Aquinas** on Christ’s knowledge, **Rahner and Balthasar** modern Catholic nuance, **Morris and Crisp** analytic two minds.

- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **Wrede, The Messianic Secret, 1901**, **Schweitzer, Quest, 1906**, **Dodd, Parables, 1935**, **Cullmann, Christ and Time, 1946**, **Ladd, Jesus and the Kingdom, 1959**, **Vos, Biblical Theology, 1948**, **Ridderbos, The Coming of the Kingdom, 1950**, **N T Wright, Jesus and the Victory of God, 1996**.

- Legacy cue or continuing debate: The interface of **historical Jesus and canonical Christ**, the scope of the **Kingdom and church**, and the coherence of **Christ’s human and divine knowing**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
By Christ’s messianic consciousness we mean the personal self-awareness of Jesus of Nazareth regarding his identity and vocation as the **anointed Son and kingdom-bearing Servant**, and by the Kingdom we mean the dynamic reign of God in redemptive power rather than a mere spatial realm. The doctrine lies at the intersection of **Christology and Eschatology**, while pressing into **Theology Proper** through **Rahners Rule** that the economic missions reveal the immanent processions. It asks whether Jesus knew himself as **Messiah, Son, and Lord**, how he interpreted his death and resurrection, and how his proclamation of the *basileia tou theou* defines the shape of salvation and the church.

The doctrine also belongs to **Bibliology** because the Gospels are the privileged witness to Jesus’ words and deeds, to **Anthropology** because his human knowing sets the grammar for redeemed cognition, and to **Soteriology** because the Kingdom arrives in his person and work. The classical Christological guardrails remain decisive, namely **communicatio idiomatum** and **extra Calvinisticum**, which together insist that the one person acts in and through two natures without confusion or division while the eternal Son never ceases to be transcendent Lord even as he fully assumes a rational soul and body.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Jesus announces, The time is fulfilled and the *basileia tou theou* is at hand, repent and believe the gospel, Mark 1, 15, signaling **eschatological fulfillment** rather than mere moral reform. His Isaianic manifesto, The Spirit of the Lord is upon me, Luke 4, 18 to 21, appropriates **Isaiah 61** to himself and so declares a **Spirit-anointed messianic mission**. The title **Son of Man** evokes Daniel 7, 13 to 14, where the human-like figure receives dominion, thus joining humility with exalted authority in Jesus’ self-reference.

The parables portray the **Kingdom’s hidden yet powerful advent**, as in the mustard seed and leaven, Matthew 13, which underwrites the **already and not yet**. Exorcisms disclose the reign’s arrival, If I cast out demons by the Spirit of God, then the Kingdom of God has come upon you, Matthew 12, 28, matching Luke’s version that speaks of the **finger of God** and thereby aligning Jesus’ ministry with **new-creation power**. The royal entry in Zecharian mode, Matthew 21, 1 to 11, and the temple action proclaim **messianic kingship**, while the passion predictions, Mark 8 to 10, internalize royal victory as suffering service.

Jesus accepts Peter’s confession, You are the Christ, the Son of the living God, Matthew 16, 16, then reframes messiahship through the cross, which binds his **messianic consciousness** to **servant obedience**. Before the Sanhedrin he identifies as the **Coming Son of Man**, Mark 14, 61 to 62, fusing Psalm 110 with Daniel 7 and sealing the claim that his identity involves enthronement. John 18, 36 deepens the theme by declaring, My kingdom is not from this world, which does not mean otherworldly withdrawal but a **kingdom-from-God source** that confronts Pilate’s power. The Fourth Gospel’s *ego eimi* sayings and the Abba prayer in Mark 14, 36 disclose filial intimacy that grounds **Sonship and agency** without dissolving his genuine human learning.

Texts about Jesus’ knowledge are pivotal. Luke 2, 49 signals early consciousness of divine sonship, while Luke 2, 52 affirms growth in wisdom, and Mark 13, 32 acknowledges ignorance of the day and hour. Together they demand a doctrine of **asymmetrical knowing** in the incarnate life, where the Son knows the Father uniquely, Matthew 11, 27, yet lives a genuinely human cognitive pilgrimage by the **Spirit’s anointing**. Paul’s Kingdom language, Colossians 1, 13 and 1 Corinthians 15, 20 to 28, locates the Kingdom in **resurrection victory**, while Revelation 11, 15 culminates the narrative in the Lamb’s reign.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic theology safeguarded the **unity of person and distinction of natures**, so the question of Jesus’ knowing was framed by **Chalcedon 451** and the **two-wills confession** at Constantinople III 680 to 681. Cyril stressed the one acting subject, while Leontius and Maximus enabled language for **theandric operations**, preserving genuine human cognitive activity in the Lord. The Kingdom motif in the Fathers often appeared as **Christus Victor**, as in Irenaeus’ recapitulation, where the King undoes Adam’s exile.

Medieval treatments matured the account of Christ’s knowledge. **Aquinas, Summa Theologiae III, questions 9 to 12**, taught three modes of knowing in Christ’s human soul, beatific, infused, acquired, and argued that the beatific vision coexisted with authentic human learning. This safeguarded **sinless yet creaturely cognition**, shaping later Catholic claims about the Lord’s consciousness. Medieval eschatology positioned the Kingdom in providential history, though Joachimite speculations pressed toward over-realized **historicism** that the magisterium resisted.

The Reformation retained the **munus triplex** and accented the preached Word as royal instrument. Luther interpreted the reign through the **two kingdoms** distinction, while Calvin elaborated **Christ’s kingship** as present governance and future consummation. Post-Reformation orthodoxy, including the **Westminster Confession 1646 to 1648**, codified Christ’s royal office and preserved the **extra Calvinisticum** to secure the Logos’ unconfined deity.

In modernity, **Wrede 1901** explained Mark’s secrecy motif as editorial strategy, leading to doubts about Jesus’ explicit messianic claims. **Schweitzer 1906** framed Jesus as apocalyptic prophet whose expectation failed but inspired ethical heroism. **Bultmann 1953** demythologized apocalyptic and relocated the kingdom to existential decision. In response, **Dodd 1935 and 1936** championed realized eschatology, while **Cullmann 1946** and **Ladd 1959** articulated **inaugurated eschatology** that decisively shaped evangelical and Reformed discourse. **Vos 1948** and **Ridderbos 1950** integrated redemptive history with Pauline and Synoptic theology, arguing that the Kingdom is the **reign of the risen Christ** breaking into the present.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine Old Princeton Dutch Neo-Calvinist Westminster**
Reformed teaching centers on the **inaugurated reign of the crucified and risen King** and the integrity of the **mediatorial consciousness**. Old Princeton emphasized historical exegesis and catholic dogma. **Warfield** argued for a robust **self-witness of Jesus** to divine Sonship and messianic mission, and explored the **emotional life of our Lord** to underscore genuine humanity. **Machen** defended the historical reliability of Gospel Christology against reduction of Jesus to mere prophet, affirming a **royal self-consciousness** congruent with apostolic witness.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism developed the public scope of Christ’s kingship. **Kuyper** insisted that not one square inch lies outside Christ’s lordship, framing culture under **sphere sovereignty** without conflating church and kingdom. **Bavinck** grounded the **munus triplex** in covenantal history and argued that the incarnate Son’s knowledge is perfect as to his person yet exercised **according to the mode of the assumed nature**. **Ridderbos** described the Kingdom as **God’s saving dominion**, tied to the cross and resurrection, while refusing political reductionism or spiritual privatization.

The Westminster standards codify Christ’s kingship. **Westminster Confession 8** declares that the Mediator executes the office of a King, calling, governing, defending, restraining and conquering all enemies, which presupposes a **present royal administration** through Word and Spirit. Reformed orthodoxy insists on **extra Calvinisticum**, thereby denying kenotic surrender of divine attributes, and on **communicatio idiomatum** filtered through person-to-nature asymmetry. Hence Christ’s **messianic consciousness** is personal and filial, truly human in growth and dependence, yet never severed from the personal identity of the eternal Son.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts Roman Catholic Lutheran Modern Analytic**
Roman Catholic theology has long affirmed **Christ’s human knowledge** as inclusive of beatific vision while respecting genuine growth. **Pius XII, Mystici Corporis, 1943**, and **Catechism 472 to 474** teach that Christ knew all things that pertain to God so far as he willed to reveal them, while also learning in a truly human way. The **munus triplex** is integrated with ecclesiology so that the **Kingdom and Church** relate as soul and body, without simple identity yet with intrinsic bond through sacramental mediation.

Lutheran theology accents the **two kingdoms doctrine** and the ubiquity of Christ’s glorified body through **communicatio idiomatum** interpreted with greater extensiveness. Nineteenth-century **kenotic Christologies** by Thomasius and Gess proposed a self-emptying of divine prerogatives, a move largely rejected by confessional Lutheranism and by Reformed theology as compromising **immutability and divine simplicity**. The Kingdom for classic Lutherans remains primarily **God’s gracious rule in the forgiveness of sins**, operative through Word and Sacrament.

Modern critical accounts diverge. **Wrede** and **Bultmann** diminish explicit messianic self-claims, while **Schweitzer** absolutizes apocalyptic expectation. Twentieth-century revisions by **Moltmann** and **liberation theologians** construe the Kingdom as **messianic politics of the crucified**, correlating eschatology with historical praxis. **N T Wright** recontextualizes Jesus within Second Temple Judaism, arguing that Jesus’ **royal vocation** centered on Israel’s restoration and the return of YHWH to Zion through Jesus’ actions. Analytic Christology, exemplified by **Thomas V Morris 1986** and **Oliver D Crisp 2007**, proposes **two minds models** to reconcile omniscience with human cognitive development, offering a metaphysical account of **asymmetric consciousness** that preserves Chalcedon while fitting Mark 13, 32.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A persistent objection alleges that the Gospels project a **post-Easter christology** back into Jesus’ life, so that messianic consciousness is ecclesial fiction. Reformed and catholic responses stress **apostolic eyewitness and inspired witness**, appeal to multiple attestation of **Son of Man** claims, and note the coherence of Jesus’ symbolic actions with messianic identity. A second objection invokes Mark 13, 32 to deny any divine self-knowledge in Jesus. Classical replies employ **dyothelitism and two minds** to argue that the Son knows all things *ad intra* while authentically not accessing certain information in the mode of his **human cognitive economy**.

Debates over **Kingdom and church** continue. Some equate the church with the kingdom and risk **ecclesiocentrism**, while others separate them and risk **activist secularism**. Following **Ridderbos and Ladd**, many hold that the church is the **firstfruits and embassy** of the Kingdom, not its fullness. Political theologians dispute the public scope of Christ’s reign, with **Kuyperian transformation** and **two kingdoms retrievals** representing differing prudential judgments under the shared confession of **Christ’s universal lordship**.

A final debate concerns the category of **apocalyptic**. **Dale Allison** and others emphasize pervasive eschatological expectation, while **Wright** insists on symbolic fulfillment in Israel’s story climaxing in Jesus. A balanced account retains **apocalyptic imagery and inaugurated fulfillment**, reading the parables, exorcisms, and resurrection as the decisive inbreaking of the age to come without collapsing final consummation into present process.

#### 7.**System Links Trinity Christology Anthropology etc.**
With respect to the Trinity, **Rahners Rule** ties Jesus’ messianic mission to **the eternal Son’s procession**, so that filial consciousness in the economy reflects and reveals **eternal Sonship** without unveiling the divine essence exhaustively. In Christology, **anhypostatic and enhypostatic union** clarifies that the human consciousness has no independent subsistence yet is fully enhypostasized in the person of the Son, which grounds a **personal subject of knowledge** who lives and knows humanly.

Anthropologically, Jesus’ Spirit-anointed wisdom becomes the **archetype of redeemed cognition**, where dependence on Scripture, prayer, and Spirit informs Christian knowing. In Soteriology, the **Kingdom is the form of salvation**, the power of the age to come erupting through the cross and resurrection, so that justification, adoption, and sanctification are **royal gifts** of the reigning Christ. In Ecclesiology, Word and Sacrament are **royal instruments**, and the church bears witness as a **kingdom colony**. In Eschatology, inaugurated reality means living between **firstfruits and harvest**, with mission framed as announcing the **king’s victory** to the nations by the Spirit.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the **messianic consciousness** secures confidence that the one who calls disciples knows his path and ours, having embraced the cross as the **royal road** to life. Liturgically, the church prays Thy kingdom come with **eschatological realism**, neither retreating into privatism nor collapsing hope into utopianism. Preaching the parables and miracles as **kingdom signs** nourishes resilient faith amid affliction, since the King still conquers through weakness and love.

Ethically, the **lordship of Christ** relativizes partisan loyalties and empowers vocation under **sphere sovereignty** or legitimate two kingdoms insights. Catechesis can teach the **munus triplex** so believers grasp Christ as Prophet who reveals, Priest who reconciles, and King who rules, while holding together the **already and not yet** in prayer, mission, and patience. Publicly, the church bears embodied witness that the crucified and risen **Son of Man** now reigns, summoning all powers to justice and mercy that mirror the **kingdom’s righteousness**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Jesus’ own filial and royal self-knowledge, enacted in cross and resurrection, grounds the church’s confession that in him the **Kingdom of God** has already dawned and will surely be consummated.
