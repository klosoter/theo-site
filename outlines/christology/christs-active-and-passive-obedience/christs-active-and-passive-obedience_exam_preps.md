### TITLE
One obedience in two states: defending Christ’s active and passive obedience as the covenantal ground of justification

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: Whether and how Christ’s **active and passive obedience** together constitute the imputed, covenantal righteousness that grounds the believer’s justification without collapsing into redundancy, legal fiction, or antinomianism.  
- Decisive figures: Anselm of Canterbury, John Calvin, Francis Turretin, John Owen, Herman Bavinck.  
- Traditions: Medieval Catholic atonement theology, Reformation and post-Reformation **Reformed orthodoxy**, classic Lutheran orthodoxy, modern Reformed revisions and critiques within broader Protestantism.  
- Debates:  
  - Internal: distinctions between active/passive within Reformed theology, relation to the covenant of works, scope of imputation, relation to union with Christ and sanctification.  
  - External: Roman Catholic and New Perspective critiques of imputed righteousness, moral exemplar and governmental theories of atonement, Barthian reservations.  
  - Contemporary: attacks on the covenant of works and active obedience in some Reformed circles, pressures from ecumenism and biblical-theological retrieval, ethical concerns about merit and punishment.  
- Defended position: Christ, as the last Adam and federal head, fulfills the covenant of works through both **active obedience** to the law and **passive obedience** in cursed death; this one, indivisible obedience is imputed to believers in union with him and is the necessary and sufficient ground of their justification, while also securing sanctification and eschatological life.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of Christ’s **active and passive obedience** answers a focused but far-reaching question: what precisely is the righteousness by which a sinner stands justified before the holy God, and how does Christ provide it? At stake is not a peripheral scholastic subtlety but the structural heart of **federal theology**, the coherence of justification by faith alone, and the integrity of the Reformed account of Christ’s priestly work. The classic Reformed answer is that the incarnate Son, as mediator and second Adam, renders to the Father a total obedience corresponding to the demands of the original covenant of works, and that this obedience has two inseparable aspects. His **active obedience** is his lifelong, comprehensive conformity to God’s law in heart and deed; his **passive obedience** is his suffering and death under the curse of the law in the place of his people. Yet this is one obedience of one person, in two states and modes, and the entirety of this righteousness is imputed to believers as the ground of their justification in union with Christ.

To see why this distinction matters and why it must not be discarded, we must first place it within the broader trajectory from medieval atonement theology to Reformed orthodoxy. Anselm of Canterbury (1033–1109) did not articulate the active/passive obedience distinction in its later terminology, yet his Cur Deus Homo decisively reshaped how Western theology conceived the logic of the atonement. Against a purely ransom or defeat-of-the-devil model, Anselm frames the problem of sin as an offense against the divine honor that creates an objective debt of satisfaction. Humanity owes God not only the cessation of sin but a positive recompense restoring the ordered honor disturbed by sin. Because this satisfaction must be something from humanity yet of infinite worth, it can be rendered only by a God-man. Anselm thus moves the discussion decisively toward satisfaction as a work of obedience offered to God.

However, Anselm’s picture of satisfaction is primarily focused on the passion as an overwhelming surplus of honor offered to God. The dimension of a lifelong positive **law-keeping righteousness** is present only implicitly. Nor does Anselm construe satisfaction within a developed **covenant of works** framework or in terms of federal representation in Adam and Christ. Still, he sets crucial parameters: the problem is an objective moral debt before God, the solution is satisfaction rendered by the incarnate Son, and this satisfaction has the character of a work of obedience that can be reckoned to others. Reformed orthodoxy will radicalize and reorganize this Anselmian insight by integrating it with Pauline forensic categories and the Adam–Christ typology.

John Calvin (1509–1564) stands at this turning point. He does not rigidly institutionalize the later terminological pair of “active” and “passive” obedience, but he provides the conceptual seeds that make their mature formulation almost inevitable. In the Institutes, especially book 2 on Christ the mediator and book 3 on justification, Calvin insists that Christ is given to us as “righteousness,” and that this righteousness consists in his perfect fulfillment of the law and in the penal satisfaction of his death. For Calvin, God’s law demands both penalty for sin and perfect obedience as the condition of life. Adam, under what later Reformed theology will call the **covenant of works**, owed not only to avoid transgression but to positively conform to God’s command as the path to confirmed communion and eschatological life. By Adam’s fall, we incur both guilt and corruption, and thus the law demands both punishment and obedience. 

Calvin accordingly speaks of Christ’s “whole course of obedience” as our righteousness. In his exegesis of Romans 5 and Philippians 2, he treats Christ’s becoming obedient “unto death” as encompassing his entire incarnate life as a path of perfect obedience culminating in the cross. In commenting on Romans 5, Calvin can say that “the obedience of Christ is reckoned to us as if it were ours.” This obedience is not exhausted by enduring the curse; it includes what Calvin calls Christ’s “holy and undefiled life.” Near the heart of his view of justification is the conviction that believers are clothed with Christ’s “robe of righteousness,” a phrase that, in context, does not refer only to a cancellation of penalty but to a positive status of acceptance conferred through imputation of Christ’s righteousness.

An examiner may press at this point: does Calvin himself really teach the later “active obedience” doctrine, or is that a later Reformed scholastic inflation? The best answer is to acknowledge a development in terminology while insisting on continuity of substance. Calvin does not sharply bifurcate active and passive obedience; he is more concerned to emphasize the unity of Christ’s mediatorial work and the gratuitousness of justification. Yet his structuring commitments already require precisely what later orthodox Reformed theologians will call “imputed active obedience.” He affirms that the law demands perfect obedience as the condition of life, that Christ fulfills the law for us, and that believers are justified by the imputation of that fulfilled-righteousness. The later terminology simply clarifies, not distorts, this account by distinguishing between Christ’s obedience unto death as curse-bearing and his lifelong law-keeping as positive righteousness.

Francis Turretin (1623–1687), representing high Reformed orthodoxy, is where the doctrine of **active and passive obedience** receives its classic, finely grained articulation. In his Institutes of Elenctic Theology, specifically in his questions on the satisfaction of Christ and the nature of justification, Turretin rigorously defends the imputation of Christ’s active obedience as essential to the Reformed doctrine of justification. Turretin’s method is polemical-elentic: he identifies questions under dispute with Roman Catholics, Socinians, and some Lutherans, and then proceeds with careful distinctions. Against Rome’s denial of a purely forensic justification, he insists on a clear distinction between the righteousness of justification and the inherent righteousness of sanctification. Against the Socinians, he defends the necessity of satisfaction grounded in the moral nature of God and the demands of his law.

Regarding Christ’s obedience, Turretin deploys the covenantal structure more explicitly than Calvin. The law given to Adam in the **covenant of works** annexed a promise of life to perfect obedience. In Adam we all sinned and fell under curse and liability to punishment. Therefore, as Turretin argues, the mediator as second Adam must do two things: take upon himself the curse of the law to remove our guilt, and fulfill the preceptive demands of the law to secure for us the right to life. Turretin thus distinguishes two parts of Christ’s mediatorial righteousness: his satisfaction of the penal sanction through death, and his fulfillment of the precept by obedience. Both are necessary, because mere remission of sins would leave us in a state of innocence only, not entitled to eschatological life. To the objection that such a double obedience makes Christ’s work redundant or speculative, Turretin responds that we must not pit Scripture against itself: the law both threatens and promises, and Christ must fulfill both aspects as our federal head.

Turretin therefore defends the imputation of both the satisfaction and the obedience. He criticizes those Lutherans who, influenced by Osiander, want to ground justification simply in the indwelling divine righteousness of Christ or in his suffering. For Turretin, Christ’s **active obedience** is not an optional add-on but the very means by which he, as incarnate surety, obtains for us a title to life. His **passive obedience** removes the obstacle of guilt; his **active obedience** supplies the positive righteousness required by the covenant’s promise. Crucially, however, Turretin refuses to separate these as two different works. They are rather two modes of the one obedience of the one mediator, distinguished only for the sake of clarity and for the integrity of a covenantal and forensic gospel.

John Owen (1616–1683) in England provides a parallel and, in some respects, deeper integration of active and passive obedience with the broader loci of union with Christ and the priestly office. Owen’s work on the priesthood of Christ, especially in his exposition of Hebrews, binds together sacrifice, offering, and obedience. Christ, as priest, not only offers himself in death but does so as the climax of a will perfectly conformed to the Father. Owen thus emphasizes that the sacrifice is valuable not merely as a physical act of dying but as the obedient self-offering of the incarnate Son. He refuses any sharp dichotomy between what Christ suffers and what he wills and does under the law.

Owen is also acutely aware of the pastoral and antinomian questions that can arise. If Christ’s active obedience is fully imputed to believers, does that not render their own obedience irrelevant and foster antinomianism? Owen responds by tightly relating justification and sanctification through **union with Christ**. The righteousness that is imputed is Christ himself as mediator in the integrity of his obedient person and work, received through faith. That same union necessarily entails participation in his death and resurrection so that believers are renewed and enabled to walk in new obedience. Thus the doctrine of active and passive obedience, properly located within Christology and union, actually secures the ground for sanctification rather than undermining it. The believer’s good works are not the matter of his justification, but they are the necessary fruit of the same Christ whose whole obedient righteousness is counted to him.

Herman Bavinck (1854–1921) helps us see how this doctrine fares under the pressure of modernity and ecumenical critique. In Reformed Dogmatics, Bavinck affirms without embarrassment the classic Reformed view: Christ’s obedience is one and indivisible, yet it can be considered under the twofold aspect of active and passive. He connects this to the covenantal fabric of Scripture: Adam under law, Israel under Torah, Christ born under the law, all culminating in the Son who fulfills the law’s demands and bears its curse. At the same time, Bavinck is sensitive to charges of artificiality. He warns against treating active and passive obedience as two separate “masses” of merit, as if Christ first accumulates a treasury of merits and then separately endures penalty. Such a framing risks reimporting a quasi-Roman economy of merit into Reformed theology. For Bavinck, the key is to see that Christ’s entire life is suffering obedience and obedient suffering in history. From his birth to his cross, he is the obedient servant, and his death is not sheer passivity but the apex of his obedience.

Bavinck also interacts with emerging modern objections that will later be developed by Barth and others. One objection is that the notion of imputation of Christ’s righteousness is a “legal fiction” and that the division into active and passive only heightens the sense of artificial construct. Bavinck responds by tying imputation to **federal representation** and union with Christ. If Christ truly is the last Adam, appointed by God as covenant head, then his obedience belongs to those whom he represents by divine appointment. Imputation is therefore not an arbitrary transfer but the recognition of a real covenantal order. Moreover, because believers are united to Christ by the Spirit, imputation is accompanied by real renewal; God never treats as righteous those whom he does not also sanctify. Again, the doctrine of active and passive obedience, when framed covenantally and christologically, is not an abstract ledger entry but the juridical dimension of a real ontological and relational union.

These figures and trajectories bring into view the internal debates within Reformed theology itself. One internal debate concerns the precise role of the **covenant of works** and whether the promise of eschatological life truly required a distinct positive obedience beyond mere non-violation. Those who downplay the covenant of works often tend to downplay or deny the need for Christ’s active obedience as imputed righteousness. They may affirm that Christ obeyed the law so that he could be a spotless sacrifice but deny that this obedience, as such, is reckoned to believers. They will speak of justification only in terms of forgiveness grounded in Christ’s death.

Another internal debate concerns whether speaking of active and passive obedience inevitably splits the unity of Christ’s work. Some contemporary Reformed theologians urge dropping the language as unhelpful or misleading, arguing that Scripture itself simply speaks of Christ’s obedience culminating in the cross. They fear that the distinction invites a mechanistic and speculative system in which Christ earns a certain quantum of righteousness that can then be imputed apart from his person. The best response here is to agree with the insistence on the unity of Christ’s obedience while refusing the conclusion that the distinction is dispensable. The distinction is a dogmatic judgment built from exegesis of the many-sided demands of God’s law: its preceptive demand for perfect love and its sanction of curse upon disobedience, its promise of life and its threat of death. Precisely because Christ’s obedience is one, we are free to distinguish its aspects without bifurcating his person or work, in order to do justice to the full scriptural witness.

External critiques sharpen the issue. Roman Catholic theology, especially as systematized at Trent, rejects the notion of a purely forensic justification grounded solely in the imputed righteousness of Christ. For Trent, justification involves the infusion of grace and charity whereby the believer actually becomes inherently righteous. The righteousness of Christ is the meritorious cause of this transformation but is not itself the formal cause of our justification as an alien righteousness reckoned to us. From this angle, the Reformed emphasis on Christ’s active and passive obedience as imputed righteousness appears to downplay inherent renewal and to over-objectify the relation between God and the sinner.

In response, Reformed theology insists on both the necessity of inherent renewal and the impossibility of locating the ground of acceptance there. The doctrine of Christ’s active and passive obedience allows us to say that every demand of the law for both obedience and penalty has been fully, objectively met in Christ our representative, so that God may justify the ungodly while remaining righteous. At the same time, union with this obedient Christ necessarily entails regeneration and sanctification. The difference from Rome is not whether the justified are being sanctified but whether their standing before God rests on Christ’s **imputed righteousness** alone or on Christ’s merit plus their inherent grace and works.

New Perspective and related Pauline scholarship brings a different external challenge. If justification is primarily about declaring who is in the covenant community and about badge-of-membership issues like circumcision, then the classic Reformed picture of law as a universal demand for perfect obedience and of Christ as fulfilling a pre-fall covenant of works can look anachronistic. The demand for a distinct active obedience might seem like a scholastic overlay on Paul’s doctrine. Here, the biblical-theological dimension of Reformed **federal theology** is crucial. Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15 present Adam and Christ as representative heads whose one act brings condemnation or justification to the many. Galatians and Romans portray the law as both covenant charter and moral demand whose curse Christ bears and whose fulfillment he accomplishes. The doctrine of active and passive obedience is not a foreign import but a way of honoring both the cosmic-Adamic and the covenantal-legal dimensions of Paul’s thought. Christ is faithful Israel, yes, but Israel itself recapitulates Adam under law. The last Adam’s obedience to the law, climaxing in the cross, provides both the resolution of Israel’s exile and the fulfillment of human vocation before God.

Contemporary debates also apply ethical and pastoral pressure. Some worry that teaching Christ’s active obedience fosters quietism: if Christ has already fulfilled the law for us, does our own obedience matter? Owen and Bavinck already anticipate this objection. The proper response is to anchor the doctrine firmly in **union with Christ** and to insist on the duplex grace of justification and sanctification. Because the same Christ whose obedience is imputed is also the resurrected Lord who indwells believers by the Spirit, there can be no justified person who is not also being sanctified. The distinction lies not in whether obedience matters but in what role it plays. Christ’s active and passive obedience furnish the entire ground of our right standing and title to life, while our Spirit-wrought obedience is the fruit and evidence of that standing, not its supplement.

Others critique the doctrine on moral grounds, charging that imputing righteousness is dishonest or that punishing Christ in our place is unjust. Here, federal representation again is key. The incarnate Son voluntarily assumes our nature and place under the law, by covenantal appointment of the Father, in order to stand as our second Adam. The law deals with him not as a random individual but as the ordained head of his people. In that sense, God is not transferring righteousness and guilt across unrelated persons but recognizing the real unity of Christ and his body established by his incarnation and covenant. Once that unity is in view, both the imputation of guilt to Christ and the imputation of righteousness to believers are morally coherent. Moreover, Christ’s **active obedience** shows that his suffering is not arbitrary violence but the culmination of a freely willed, loving obedience to the Father’s redemptive will.

In defending the doctrine before examiners, it is important to state the position with precision. Christ, as the last Adam and federal head, was born under the law, both as covenant and as moral norm. Throughout his incarnate life, he rendered perfect, personal, exact, and entire obedience to all of God’s commandments. This obedience was rendered as mediator, in our stead, so that he might fulfill the original condition of life held out in the covenant of works and embodied in Israel’s Torah. This is his **active obedience**. At the end of his life, he willingly submitted to suffering and death under the curse of the law, bearing in our place the penalty due to our sins as our surety. This is his **passive obedience**. Yet both are simply aspects of his one mediatorial obedience. By God’s appointment, this total obedience, considered in both aspects, is reckoned to those who are united to him by faith, so that God declares them righteous and gives them title to eternal life. Simultaneously, through union with Christ, believers are renewed and enabled to live in growing, though imperfect, obedience as the fruit of that justification.

To objections that this bifurcation is speculative or exegetically thin, one may summarize the core exegetical lines: Paul’s contrast in Romans 5 between the one trespass of Adam and the one obedience of Christ leading to justification of many; Galatians 3’s depiction of Christ redeeming us from the curse of the law by becoming a curse for us while the promise of the Spirit comes through faith; the insistence in Galatians 4 and Romans 8 that Christ was born under the law and that the righteous requirement of the law is fulfilled in those who walk according to the Spirit in union with him; Philippians 2’s portrayal of Christ becoming obedient unto death. These strands together present Christ not merely as a penalty-bearer but as the law-fulfilling Son whose obedience is the ground of our life. The active/passive distinction is a systematic means of doing justice to that comprehensive biblical portrayal.

In the end, the doctrine of Christ’s **active and passive obedience** is best seen as a crystallization of several Reformed commitments: a robust **covenant of works** framework, a forensic and federal account of justification, a high view of the unity of Christ’s saving work, and a rich doctrine of union with Christ. Anselm supplies the satisfaction structure, Calvin supplies the integrative mediatorial and forensic framework, Turretin and Owen supply the covenantal and scholastic precision, and Bavinck shows the doctrine’s resilience under modern critique. Properly understood, the distinction enhances rather than diminishes Christ’s glory. It asserts that nothing is left for us to earn in order to stand righteous before God. Christ has both fulfilled the law’s positive demands and borne its negative sanctions in our place. In him, the law’s voice is simultaneously silenced in its condemning power and fulfilled in its righteous requirement. That robustly biblical harmony is what the doctrine of Christ’s active and passive obedience aims to safeguard.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: Christ’s **active obedience** is his lifelong, perfect fulfillment of God’s law as our federal head; his **passive obedience** is his willing suffering and cursed death under the law’s penalty; together, as one mediatorial obedience, they are imputed to believers as the sole ground of justification.  
- Decisive figures:  
  - Anselm: framed atonement as objective satisfaction by an obedient God-man, preparing the ground for later focus on law and merit.  
  - Calvin: emphasized Christ’s whole course of obedience and imputed righteousness within a developing **federal theology**.  
  - Turretin: systematically distinguished and defended imputed active and passive obedience to fulfill both precept and penalty of the **covenant of works**.  
  - Owen: integrated the doctrine with Christ’s priesthood and **union with Christ**, and answered antinomian concerns.  
  - Bavinck: affirmed the classic view, insisted on one obedience in two aspects, and answered modern “legal fiction” critiques via **federal representation**.  
- Debates:  
  - Internal: necessity of the covenant of works; whether active obedience is imputed or only passive; whether the distinction unduly splits Christ’s work.  
  - External: Roman Catholic and New Perspective rejection of a purely forensic justification and imputed righteousness; moral exemplar and governmental theories minimizing satisfaction; Barthian reservations about “merit” and imputation.  
  - Contemporary: moves within Reformed circles to drop active obedience and covenant of works; ethical worries about justice and legal fiction; pastoral fears of antinomianism.  
- Defended position: Christ, as last Adam, fulfills both the precept and penalty of God’s law; his entire **active and passive obedience** is imputed to believers in union with him as the necessary and sufficient ground of justification, while that same union ensures real sanctification.  
- Likely examiner questions and one-line answers:  
  - Is the active/passive distinction biblical or speculative?  
    → It is a dogmatic distinction grounded in the law’s twofold demand in Scripture and in texts like Romans 5, Galatians 3–4, and Philippians 2.  
  - Does Calvin actually teach imputed active obedience?  
    → While lacking later terminology, he clearly grounds justification in Christ’s whole obedience to the law imputed as our righteousness.  
  - Why is active obedience needed if sins are forgiven by Christ’s death?  
    → Forgiveness removes guilt, but the covenant promise of life requires positive righteousness; active obedience supplies this title to life.  
  - Does this doctrine promote antinomianism?  
    → No, because the righteousness imputed is that of a Christ with whom we are united, and that union necessarily produces sanctifying obedience.  
  - How is imputation not a legal fiction?  
    → Because it rests on real **federal representation** and Spirit-wrought union, in which Christ truly stands as our covenant head and we truly are in him.
