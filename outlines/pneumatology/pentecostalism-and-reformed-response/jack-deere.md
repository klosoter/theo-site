# Jack Deere (1952– )

Jack Deere is a former cessationist who became a leading advocate for the **continuation of the miraculous gifts of the Spirit** within the church today. Engaging both **Pentecostalism** and the **Reformed tradition**, Deere challenges the traditional Reformed stance of cessationism, arguing that **prophecy, healing, and tongues** remain operative and biblically warranted for the contemporary church.

- "The Bible does not teach that the miraculous gifts of the Spirit have ceased." (Surprised by the Power of the Spirit)

## Key Works

- Surprised by the Power of the Spirit (1993)
- Surprised by the Voice of God (1996)
- The Beginner’s Guide to the Gift of Prophecy (2001)
- "Why I Am No Longer a Cessationist" (various lectures and articles)
- Contributions to Vineyard movement literature

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Continuation of Spiritual Gifts**
   - Argues that **miraculous gifts** such as prophecy, healing, and tongues are for today.
   - Rejects the traditional **cessationist** interpretation of 1 Corinthians 13 and other passages.
   - Emphasizes the ongoing work of the **Holy Spirit** in empowering believers.
   - Sees the gifts as essential for the **edification** and mission of the church.
   - "There is no biblical evidence that the gifts of the Spirit were meant to cease before the return of Christ." (Surprised by the Power of the Spirit)

2. **Biblical Authority and Experience**
   - Maintains a high view of **Scripture** as the final authority.
   - Argues that authentic spiritual experiences must be tested by the **Word of God**.
   - Warns against both uncritical acceptance and blanket rejection of charismatic phenomena.
   - Seeks a balance between **biblical fidelity** and openness to the Spirit’s work.
   - "All genuine spiritual experiences will be consistent with the teaching of Scripture." (Surprised by the Voice of God)

3. **Critique of Reformed Cessationism**
   - Challenges the exegetical basis for **cessationism** in Reformed theology.
   - Points out the lack of explicit biblical support for the cessation of gifts.
   - Argues that historical Reformed figures sometimes experienced or affirmed the miraculous.
   - Calls for humility and openness in theological dialogue with **Pentecostal** and charismatic Christians.
   - "Cessationism is a tradition of men, not a doctrine of Scripture." (Surprised by the Power of the Spirit)

4. **Role of Prophecy in the Church**
   - Affirms that **prophecy** continues as a New Testament gift for the church.
   - Distinguishes between canonical revelation and non-canonical prophetic utterances.
   - Encourages discernment and accountability in the exercise of prophecy.
   - Views prophecy as a means of **edification, encouragement, and comfort** for believers.
   - "New Testament prophecy does not threaten the sufficiency of Scripture." (Surprised by the Voice of God)

5. **Integration of Reformed and Charismatic Theology**
   - Seeks to unite **Reformed soteriology** with charismatic practice.
   - Emphasizes the sovereignty of God in distributing gifts as He wills.
   - Advocates for theological depth alongside spiritual vitality.
   - Promotes a church life marked by both **doctrinal soundness** and supernatural ministry.
   - "We do not have to choose between sound doctrine and the power of the Spirit." (Surprised by the Power of the Spirit)

## Legacy and Impact

Jack Deere has played a significant role in bridging the gap between **Reformed theology** and **Pentecostal/charismatic** practice, especially among evangelicals reconsidering cessationism. His writings have encouraged many to re-examine the biblical basis for the continuation of spiritual gifts, fostering greater openness to the work of the **Holy Spirit** within traditionally non-charismatic circles. Deere’s influence is evident in the ongoing dialogue between Reformed and charismatic communities, challenging both to pursue biblical faithfulness and spiritual vitality.
