# Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834)

Friedrich Schleiermacher, often called the father of modern theology, approached **Pentecostalism** and charismatic movements from the standpoint of **religious experience** and the **feeling of absolute dependence**. While he predates the rise of Pentecostalism, his emphasis on the **subjective dimension of faith** and the **immediacy of the Spirit** influenced later theological responses, including both critiques and appropriations within the **Reformed tradition**.

- "Religion’s essence is neither thinking nor acting, but intuition and feeling." (On Religion: Speeches to Its Cultured Despisers)

## Key Works

- On Religion: Speeches to Its Cultured Despisers (1799)
- The Christian Faith (1821–1822)
- Brief Outline of the Study of Theology (1811)
- Christian Ethics (published posthumously)
- Friedrich Schleiermacher: Pioneer of Modern Theology (ed. Keith W. Clements)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Primacy of Religious Experience**
    - Faith is rooted in the **feeling of absolute dependence** rather than doctrinal assent.
    - Emphasizes the immediacy of the **Spirit’s presence** in the believer’s consciousness.
    - Views charismatic phenomena as expressions of authentic religious life, but subject to critical reflection.
    - Distinguishes between genuine spiritual experience and mere emotionalism.
    - "The essence of religion consists in the feeling of an absolute dependence." (The Christian Faith)

2. **Doctrine as Reflection on Experience**
    - Doctrines are secondary, articulating the community’s shared experience of the divine.
    - Theological formulations are historically conditioned and open to development.
    - Cautions against rigid dogmatism in evaluating new movements like Pentecostalism.
    - Encourages openness to the evolving work of the **Holy Spirit** in the church.
    - "Dogmatics is the science which presents the Christian religious affections in their dependence on the Christian faith." (The Christian Faith)

3. **Universal Accessibility of the Spirit**
    - The **Holy Spirit** is universally operative, not restricted to particular traditions or manifestations.
    - Recognizes the diversity of spiritual gifts and experiences across Christian communities.
    - Sees Pentecostal phenomena as possible signs of the Spirit’s ongoing work.
    - Advocates discernment to distinguish authentic spiritual movements from excesses.
    - "The Spirit is the common element in the life of the Christian community." (The Christian Faith)

4. **Critical Engagement with Tradition**
    - Tradition is valuable but must be critically assessed in light of contemporary experience.
    - Encourages dialogue between historic **Reformed theology** and emerging spiritual movements.
    - Warns against both uncritical acceptance and outright rejection of charismatic expressions.
    - Sees the church as a living, dynamic community shaped by the Spirit.
    - "Every age must seek its own understanding of the faith." (Brief Outline of the Study of Theology)

5. **Ethical Implications of Spirituality**
    - True religious experience leads to ethical transformation and communal responsibility.
    - Evaluates charismatic movements by their fruits in love, justice, and unity.
    - Stresses that spiritual gifts must serve the edification of the church.
    - Connects the work of the **Spirit** to the moral renewal of individuals and society.
    - "The test of the Spirit is the ethical fruit it bears." (Christian Ethics)

## Legacy and Impact

Schleiermacher’s focus on **religious experience** and the **immediacy of the Spirit** laid groundwork for later theological engagement with Pentecostalism, both within and beyond the **Reformed tradition**. His thought challenged rigid confessionalism and encouraged openness to new movements of the Spirit, while also insisting on critical discernment and ethical accountability. Modern Reformed responses to Pentecostalism often reflect his balance of experiential openness and doctrinal reflection, making his legacy significant in ongoing debates about the role of the **Holy Spirit** in the church.
