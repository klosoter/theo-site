### TITLE
Gathered Presence in a Networked Age: Digital Liturgies and the Shape of the Church

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: Twenty-first century communications revolution reframes **ecclesiology and sacramentology** within the locus of **worship and the means of grace**.  
- Core question: How do platform-shaped practices form or deform the church's **visible communion** and the administration of **Word and sacrament**.  
- Principal figures or schools: **Marshall McLuhan** on media ecology, **James K. A. Smith** on cultural liturgies, **Abraham Kuyper and Herman Bavinck** on common grace and culture, **Old Princeton** on divine institution.  
- Key concepts or loci connections: **regulative principle of worship**, **communion of saints**, **cultural liturgies and attention**, **extra Calvinisticum** and ascension.  
- Major texts, events, works: Acts 2 and 1 Corinthians 10 to 14 on assembly, **Directory for Public Worship 1645**, **Westminster Confession 1646 to 1648**, **McLuhan 1964**, **Postman 1985**, pandemic disruptions in 2020.  
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Westminster-regulative guardianship** versus **Roman Catholic sacramental ontology** and **Lutheran sacramental union**, with **evangelical multisite and online church models**.  
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Ongoing discernment about **digital sacraments**, **authority and discipline online**, and the shaping power of **algorithmic liturgies**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Digital liturgies are patterned practices mediated by networked technologies that habituate desire, attention, and action, and so catechize the people of God for good or ill within the sphere of **worship and the means of grace**. As James K. A. Smith argues, cultural practices function as secular liturgies, and when those practices are digitally mediated they compose **formative liturgical ecosystems** that compete with or can be harnessed for **ecclesial formation**. The dogmatic question is ecclesiological and sacramental, touching the marks of the church, the nature of gathered presence, and the ordinary conduits of grace, all within the horizon of **media ecology**.

Ecclesiology confesses one holy catholic and apostolic church, both visible and invisible, with the visible church marked by the pure preaching of the Word, the right administration of the sacraments, and the exercise of discipline, which Reformed standards summarize as **Word, sacrament, and discipline**. Digital liturgies intersect these marks by reshaping how congregations hear, see, speak, and assemble, thereby challenging or extending **the communion of saints**. The issue is not merely instrumental but concerns the way medium co-determines message, a claim captured by **McLuhan's the medium is the message**.

Dogmatically, the locus engages not only ecclesiology but also Christology and Pneumatology, since any account of mediated presence must reckon with the **ascension of Christ** and the Spirit's gathering work. Here **extra Calvinisticum** sustains Christ's heavenly session while affirming real communion with him by the Spirit, a crucial anchor for evaluating claims that digital mediation either suffices for communion or necessarily evacuates it. The **regulative principle of worship** provides a Reformed normative frame for discerning which media serve divinely instituted means and which invent practices alien to Christ's commands.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture describes the church as a gathered assembly, using the term *ekklesia* to denote those called out to gather under the Word, as in Acts 2. The church devoted itself to the apostles teaching, the fellowship, the breaking of bread, and the prayers, a fourfold pattern that nails down **apostolic instruction and sacramental communion**. Hebrews 10 verse 25 exhorts believers not to neglect meeting together, grounding presence in mutual exhortation and the Day drawing near, while 1 Corinthians 11 to 14 ties the Lord's Supper and charismatic edification to when you come together, thus attaching **sacramental action to visible assembly**.

At the same time, the New Testament models mediated presence. Paul writes letters as authoritative apostolic communication, and he can say he is absent in body yet present in spirit in 1 Corinthians 5, a striking claim of **spiritual presence without bodily co-location**. He longs to see the churches face to face in 2 John and 3 John, revealing a hierarchy of presence that prizes bodily fellowship without disparaging mediated speech. Romans 10 underscores that faith comes by hearing, and hearing through the Word of Christ, which welcomes technological extension of proclamation under **the primacy of preaching**.

The sacraments press the question most sharply. In 1 Corinthians 10 to 11 the Eucharist entails one bread and one body, discerning the body, and a communal meal that requires examination and loving regard, not self-exalting individualism. The actions of taking, blessing, breaking, and giving happen in a shared space with shared elements and a shared table, signaling that **visible unity and tactile participation** belong to the rite's sign and seal. Baptism likewise is an immersion into Christ and his body, marked by water and the triune Name, an action that presupposes a minister and candidate within a gathered community under **apostolic order**.

John 4 relativizes sacred place by declaring worship in spirit and truth, a text sometimes conscripted to bypass bodily assembly. Yet Jesus does not erase embodiment, since spirit and truth come through the incarnate Son who gives his Spirit, and his worship reforms temple life rather than negating created media altogether. Eschatologically, 1 Corinthians 13 projects a face to face knowing that surpasses mediated knowledge, implying that present forms of mediation are provisional and ordered to **future embodiment and communion**. Scripture thus authorizes mediated communication while reserving sacramental acts for the gathered, thereby affirming **both mediation and assembly**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The church has always adopted and reformed media. The early codex in the second century accelerated scriptural circulation, and Jerome's Vulgate in 382 standardized Western textuality, a move that formed **biblical catholicity**. The printing press after the 1450s turbocharged the Reformation's vernacular preaching and catechesis, with 1517 to 1522 seeing a pamphlet explosion that crafted **Reformation publicity**. The Westminster Assembly 1643 to 1649 produced the Confession and the Directory for Public Worship 1645, which disciplined worship through **ordinary means and pastoral governance**.

Mass broadcast introduced new challenges. Radio services in the 1920s extended preaching, and television after the 1950s created televangelism, which provoked concerns about **spectator religion**. Roman Catholic documents like Inter Mirifica in 1963 and Communio et Progressio in 1971 offered positive yet cautious accounts of media as gifts to be used for evangelization under **magisterial prudence**. Evangelicals experimented with seeker services and broadcast church, often decentering sacramentality in favor of **media-driven proclamation**.

Digital networks after 1994 and social media after 2003 altered the field by personalizing and platformizing attention. McLuhan's 1964 thesis and Neil Postman's 1985 warning became newly pertinent as smartphones after 2007 created always-on liturgies of swiping, liking, and scrolling, shaping **habituated attention and desire**. The COVID 19 pandemic in 2020 forced emergency dispersal of assemblies, with livestreams and Zoom proliferating, and debates about online communion flaring across traditions as bishops and presbyteries sought **ad hoc pastoral theologies** under constraint.

Post pandemic, churches have settled into hybrid patterns, with multisite video sermons normalized since the 2000s and emerging experiments in virtual reality worship such as VR Church founded in 2016. The ongoing legacy is an unsettled negotiation between the affordances of platforms and the non negotiable goods of **gathered sacramental life**. The history teaches that the church can leverage media for catechesis and mission while resisting the reduction of worship to **consumption of content**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton exemplified by Charles Hodge in 1872 to 1873 and B. B. Warfield after 1880 emphasized that the church is a divine institution whose worship and sacraments are specified by Scripture, not by ecclesial creativity, a stance that protects **the instituted means of grace**. Hodge treated sacraments as signs and seals annexed to the Word, requiring proper administration by lawfully called ministers within the visible church, which resists understandings that privatize baptism or sever the Supper from **ecclesial oversight and discipline**. Warfield's stress on revelation's rational content supports robust preaching while warning against **enthusiastic novelties** masquerading as spiritual immediacy.

The Westminster Standards 1646 to 1648 give decisive contours. Westminster Confession 21 sets the **regulative principle of worship**, limiting corporate worship to what God commands, which entails a cautious adoption of media so that medium serves commanded elements rather than invents new ones. Confession 27 to 29 and the Larger Catechism describe sacraments as effectual to true believers yet bound to their proper use within the church, which implies that the Lord's Supper requires a gathered congregation, shared elements, fencing by elders, and discernment of the body as **visible unity and discipline**. The Directory for Public Worship instructs public reading, preaching, prayer, singing, and sacraments within concrete assemblies, affirming accommodation for the infirm while refusing to collapse **assembly into dissemination**.

Dutch Neo Calvinism contributes a theology of culture under **common grace and the cultural mandate**. Abraham Kuyper in the 1898 Stone Lectures and Herman Bavinck in Philosophy of Revelation 1908 argue that technological development unfolds creational possibilities under God's providence, which legitimates the positive use of media for education, mission, and even liturgical support under **sphere sovereignty**. Bavinck also insists on the organic nature of the church and the importance of the visible institution, reinforcing that whatever digital instruments are employed must sustain rather than supplant **the gathered body and sacramental acts**. Reformed Christology through **extra Calvinisticum** underscores that Christ is bodily in heaven and present by the Spirit in the Supper, which frees the church from a spatial ubiquity claim while directing attention to the Spirit's use of outward means in assembled action.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology grounds worship in **sacramental ontology and ex opere operato**, with the Eucharist as the source and summit of ecclesial life. Magisterial guidance has affirmed the value of televised and streamed Mass for the homebound while clarifying that remote participation does not fulfill the Sunday obligation when public Mass is available, a prudence seen in diocesan instructions in 2020 and rooted in **embodied reception and parochial belonging**. The catechesis on spiritual communion provides a constructive resource for seasons when the faithful cannot sacramentally commune, distinguishing **desire for the sacrament** from its reception.

Lutheran theology of **sacramental union** affirms Christ's real presence in, with, and under the elements through the Word. Many Lutheran bodies concluded in 2020 that consecration cannot occur at a distance for elements scattered across homes, since the rite orders a gathered people to receive from the hand of a pastor within **the local altar fellowship**. Some experiments like drive through communion sought to preserve physical immediacy while adapting to crisis, reflecting a strong emphasis on the integrity of **the rite and the assembly**.

Modern evangelical practice has often centered preaching and music as the functional marks, producing **multisite and online church models** where a central teacher appears by video across campuses or homes. This has multiplied reach but raised questions about pastoral presence, discipline, and the flattening of the Supper into an optional appendix, exposing tensions between **platform charisma and local shepherding**. Advocates argue for mission pragmatism and access, while critics warn of a consumerist ecclesiology that mistakes viewership for **mutual indwelling and accountability**.

Analytic theology brings clarity by parsing action, presence, and speech acts. On a speech act account, the ministerial words of institution are performatives whose success conditions include authority, context, and uptake, which implies that **ritual context and shared action** are not accidental. Analytic metaphysics of location and presence presses whether virtual co presence suffices for shared action or whether sacraments require **coordinated bodily agency**. These analyses sharpen rather than settle the debate, inviting confessional traditions to test their claims about **presence, sign, and ecclesial authority**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Digital liturgies reconfigure attention, and attention is the organ of love. Sherry Turkle in 2011 argued that we are alone together, and Nicholas Carr in 2010 warned of the shallows, diagnoses that expose how feeds and notifications form **restless hearts and fragmented attention**. Theological voices from Augustine on curiositas to Simone Weil on attention frame this as a contest of liturgies, where algorithmic systems by design capture and monetize attention, creating a need for **ascetical resistance and pastoral counter formation**.

Authority and discipline face novel pressures. Matthew 18 presumes processes within a community where face to face confrontation and church involvement occur, raising questions about **church membership online** and the possibility of exercising the keys in spaces that lack a stable, named body. Parasocial relationships between viewers and pastors mediated by screens threaten **mutual knowledge and shared burdens**, while anonymity and disembodiment complicate accountability and care. Churches must develop polity that binds digital attenders to tangible **pastoral oversight and vows**.

Sacramental practice remains contested. Can ministers consecrate at a distance, and can congregants self serve the Supper apart from the gathered table. Reformed, Roman Catholic, and Lutheran cautions converge at a saying no or temporary abstention, with various accommodations for the homebound such as distribution by elders or spiritual communion, all ordered to **restored assembly as the norm**. Proponents of online communion argue for inclusion and mission, pointing to disabled believers and diaspora communities, while critics argue that the sacramental sign demands **co presence and shared elements**.

Emerging concerns include data privacy, surveillance capitalism, and pastoral confidentiality, since platforms commodify user data, including prayer requests and counseling sessions, which threatens **trust and holiness of speech**. Artificial intelligence now drafts sermons and prayers, sometimes used in 2023 experiments, raising issues of authorship, truthfulness, and the pastoral office, and pushing churches to clarify **the distinction between tools and pastoral voice**. Virtual reality churches administer baptisms in virtual environments, which forces traditions to articulate how matter, water, and bodies signify **new creation and incorporation**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinity and Christology anchor presence. The church is the body of Christ constituted by the Spirit, which means that **pneumatological gathering** is real and effectual while ordered to the Son's ascended lordship and the Father's glory. The **extra Calvinisticum** safeguards Christ's bodily absence and heavenly presence, preventing conflation of spiritual communion with technological ubiquity, while **Rahners Rule** reminds us that God reveals himself truly in history, which includes the economy of mediated proclamation yet binds us to **instituted signs and saving acts**.

Anthropology and hamartiology shape our prudence. Humans are embodied, social, and extended creatures who use tools, which licenses a positive account of technologies under **the cultural mandate and common grace**. Yet sin bends tools toward idolatry, vainglory, and acedia, which digital liturgies exacerbate through constant comparison, outrage, and distraction, demanding **sanctified habits and communal rule**. Soteriology clarifies that the Spirit uses outward and ordinary means, principally the Word, sacraments, and prayer, which checks **technological messianism** and relativizes reach as a metric of fruitfulness.

Bibliology is implicated through the authority of preached Scripture. Technologies can amplify hearing, translation, and catechesis, but the church must hold that **preaching is an event of divine address** that occurs in and to a congregation under pastoral charge. Eschatology frames our hope, since the church is a pilgrim people moving toward the marriage supper of the Lamb, where faith gives way to sight and mediation to **face to face communion**. Ecclesiology finally adjudicates forms by the marks of the church, subordinating platforms to **Word, sacrament, and discipline** within a concrete people and place.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastors should catechize congregations in media wisdom that treats platforms as liturgical environments, teaching **discernment of digital habits** and crafting a communal rule of life that guards attention and fosters silence. Practices might include device sabbaths, notification fasting, and liturgies of presence such as greeting, confession, and the peace that enact **mutual recognition and charity**. Leaders can model attentiveness and resist spectacle by shaping services where the elements commanded by Christ are central and unhurried, prioritizing **ordinary means of grace** over production value.

Hybrid patterns require principled design. Livestreaming can serve the homebound, travelers, and seekers, but churches should frame it as window not replacement, repeatedly inviting viewers into embodied fellowship and the table, a pastoral posture of **digital hospitality ordered to presence**. For scattered members, elders can deliver communion with the Word to the sick, maintaining continuity with diaconal traditions and the fencing of the table as **pastoral care and discipline**. Policies should state that communion is administered in the assembly or through delegated visitation, not via self service at home.

Governance should address membership and oversight. Digital attendees who profess faith should be welcomed into a process that gathers names, assigns shepherds, and invites vows, refusing the fiction of a church without a flock, thereby reinforcing **the communion of saints as covenantal**. Safeguards should protect privacy, including prohibiting the use of prayer data for marketing and ensuring that counseling occurs on secure channels, thus honoring **truth telling and pastoral trust**. Churches should also invest in accessible design for disabled members, not as an afterthought but as an expression of **catholicity and justice**.

Finally, homiletics and worship planning should treat technologies as servants. Slides, projection, and microphones can carry the Word and song further, but the sermon remains an event of **Christ addressing his people** through a called minister, not a commodity to be optimized by algorithms. If AI is used as a drafting tool, pastors should disclose and the final proclamation should be authored and owned by the preacher under **the yoke of accountability**. Music teams can resist click track tyranny in favor of congregational song that foregrounds the priesthood of all believers and **shared vocal participation**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Let the church harness technologies as servants of **Word, sacrament, and discipline**, while resisting algorithmic liturgies by gathering in the Spirit around Christ's table for the life of the world.
