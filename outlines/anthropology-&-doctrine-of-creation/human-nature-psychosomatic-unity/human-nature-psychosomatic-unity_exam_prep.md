### TITLE
Embodied souls: defending psychosomatic unity through hylomorphic dualism against Cartesianism and physicalism

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: how to affirm **psychosomatic unity** while explaining body–soul distinction, personal identity, sin’s transmission, the **intermediate state**, and resurrection without collapsing into **substance dualism** or **physicalism**.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, René Descartes, John Calvin, Herman Bavinck, Nancey Murphy.
- Traditions engaged: Patristic and medieval anthropology, Reformation and Reformed dogmatics, modern philosophy of mind and neuroscience.
- Debates:
  - Internal: **hylomorphic dualism** versus Cartesian dualism, dichotomy versus **trichotomy**, **creationism** versus **traducianism**, status of the **intermediate state**.
  - External: Platonic dualism, reductive and **nonreductive physicalism**, emergentism.
  - Contemporary: neuroscience and mental causation, personal identity through death, AI and personhood, disability and embodiment, body integrity in sanctification.
- Defended position: a classical Reformed **hylomorphic dualism** that affirms one human nature constituted by an immaterial, subsistent rational soul as the form of the living body, denies trichotomy, rejects reductive physicalism, affirms a conscious **intermediate state** without diminishing the primacy of embodied resurrection, and favors a Reformed **traducianism** ordered by **federal theology**.

### ESSAY
The doctrine of **psychosomatic unity** claims that a human being is neither a mere body nor a ghostly mind inhabiting a machine, but a single substantial person constituted by body and soul. It is not an optional addendum to anthropology but a load-bearing beam under Christology, soteriology, and eschatology. To miss it is to distort the meaning of the incarnation and the resurrection, the nature of sanctification in the body, and the hope that anchors Christian burial. The core assertion is ontological, not merely moral or social: the soul and body are distinct principles of one living human nature, ordered to God under the **image of God** in knowledge, righteousness, and holiness. The unity is neither a pragmatic cooperation nor a reversible choice; it is the creaturely mode of human existence willed by God. Scripture presents man as dust inbreathed by God’s breath in Genesis 2 and insists on bodily resurrection in 1 Corinthians 15. That double witness establishes a dual-aspect unity that must be guarded both from Platonic contempt of the body and from reductive physicalism.

Augustine stands at the watershed where Christian faith confronts late antique Platonism. Educated in rhetoric and formed by Manichean dualism before his conversion, he forged a biblical psychology in conversation with neo-Platonism. In Confessions and De Trinitate he seeks analogies of the Trinity in the human mind as memory, understanding, and will, which elevates the interior life yet never unfastens it from embodiment and history. In De Genesi ad litteram he rejects the demotion of the body, contending for the goodness of the material creation and the destiny of the body’s resurrection. Augustine is often read as a **substance dualist**, and he does speak of the soul as superior and ruling. But the point is moral and teleological, not an ontological severing. He denies **trichotomy**, critiques Apollinarian flattening of Christ’s rational soul, and resists the body-soul divorce of popular Platonism by anchoring desire in love’s ordering toward God. On the origin of souls Augustine vacillates between **traducianism** and **creationism**, sensing that traducianism better explains the solidarity of sin while creationism better protects the soul’s spirituality. On the **intermediate state**, he affirms a conscious presence with Christ for believers and anticipatory judgment for the wicked, while insisting that this state is not the telos. Augustine’s decisive contribution is a theological psychology in which the soul is not a free-floating self but the life-principle of a creature made for God and destined for bodily glory. He reframes the field by integrating biblical holism with a careful soul-body distinction so that neither collapses into the other.

Thomas Aquinas advances that Augustinian trajectory into metaphysical clarity by reclaiming Aristotelian **hylomorphism**. In Summa Theologiae and De Anima commentaries, he argues that the human soul is the substantial form of the living human body. The form is not a spatial part but the principle that makes a body to be a living human body. This provides conceptual ballast for **psychosomatic unity**. The soul is not a complete substance alongside the body; together soul and body constitute one substance. Yet the rational soul, unlike sensitive and vegetative forms, is immaterial and subsistent; it can operate in acts of intellection that do not use a bodily organ and therefore can survive bodily death. This allows Aquinas to affirm a real **intermediate state** without exalting it. The separated soul is not a complete human being. Its state is ontologically deficient. That deficiency explains the scriptural longing to be clothed with immortality rather than unclothed. Aquinas’s **hylomorphic dualism** also avoids the interaction problem that later haunts Cartesianism. The soul does not push on matter; it informs it. Mental causation is formal and final before it is efficient. His account grounds personal identity in the subsisting intellectual soul that is the form of this body. Resurrection continuity is thus secured by the persistence of the soul and the reconstitution of a body proportioned to it by divine action. Aquinas defends creationism of the soul to protect its spirituality, a position that Reformed theologians later debated. Yet his framework strengthens the unity of human nature, maintains the integrity of Christ’s true humanity with a rational soul, and gives a principled account of the sacraments as fitting means for embodied creatures. In contemporary debates over neuroscience, Aquinas equips us to say that brain injury impairs the soul’s operations by disorganizing its instruments without reducing the soul to those instruments.

René Descartes decisively reshaped the field by redefining mind and body as two complete substances, res cogitans and res extensa. Methodologically, he sought indubitable foundations and found them in the self’s thinking. The clarity and distinctness of mental awareness, on his account, entails that mind is essentially thinking and non extended, while body is extended and non thinking. The human person is a conjunction of two substances. This move sharpens the distinction at the cost of **psychosomatic unity**. The mind becomes a spectator lodged in a machine, and the body becomes a mechanism lacking intrinsic teleology. The infamous interaction problem arises because the two substances, defined by opposed attributes, lack an intelligible nexus. Theologically, Cartesianism tempts toward either angelism, where salvation is escape from embodiment, or toward an embarrassed defense of resurrection bolted onto a self-sufficient spirituality. Historically, Descartes provoked both an ecclesial defense of unity and a secular reaction toward materialism. The modern mind-body problem is in large measure a post-Cartesian problem. The downstream pastoral effects include a privatized spirituality and the evacuation of bodily practices from sanctification. In responding, one should not caricature Descartes, who affirmed resurrection and the goodness of creation, but the structural instability of his two-substance scheme drove the debate toward extremes that we must resist.

In the Reformation, John Calvin resituates anthropology under Scripture and union with Christ. In the Institutes and his commentaries he insists that the person is a unity, rejects **trichotomy** as a lexical misunderstanding that confuses biblical synonymy of soul and spirit, and emphasizes the dignity and goodness of the body under providence and redemption. Calvin defends a robust **intermediate state**. He argues from Luke 23, Philippians 1, Hebrews 12, and Revelation 6 that believers are consciously with Christ upon death while they await the resurrection of the body. He rejects both the rationalist divorce of soul from body and the soul-sleep positions that deny any post mortem consciousness. His anthropology is ordered by the gospel. The image of God is not lost but marred, renewed in Christ by the Spirit, and consummated in the resurrection. On the origin of the soul, Calvin leans toward creationism but refuses dogmatic closure, grounding the transmission of sin chiefly in **federal theology** through Adam’s headship in Romans 5. Calvin’s decisive contribution is not a new metaphysical scheme but a biblical and covenantal reordering that protects **psychosomatic unity** through the doctrines of creation, fall, redemption, and consummation. The body is the theater of obedience; sanctification is bodily; sacraments are not mere signs but Spirit-wrought means that engage the senses of embodied image bearers.

Herman Bavinck, standing at the intersection of modern science and classic dogma, retrieves and refines this outlook. In Reformed Dogmatics he insists that humanity is an organic unity, an ensouled body and embodied soul, created as a microcosm mediating between spiritual and material orders. He rejects **trichotomy** and laments both Cartesianism and materialism for sundering what God joined. Bavinck gives sustained attention to biological development, culture, and history, arguing that grace restores nature rather than replacing it. He favors a chastened **traducianism** as a better account of the organic propagation of human nature and a more natural fit with **federal theology**, while maintaining that God’s providence is the immediate cause of each human life. Bavinck stresses the eschatological destiny of the body in the resurrection and the continuity of personal identity across death by the preserving power of God. He integrates theology and psychology, explaining how the Spirit renews the whole person such that intellectual, affective, and volitional powers are restored without denying their embodied operation. His modern contribution is to fold neuroscience and developmental psychology into a wider teleological frame, securing **psychosomatic unity** while rebutting reductive accounts of mind.

Nancey Murphy represents a leading contemporary alternative known as **nonreductive physicalism**. She proposes that the best biblical reading and the findings of neuroscience counsel abandoning any immaterial soul while preserving personhood through higher-level properties supervening on the brain. On this view, mental states are realized in neural states yet are not reducible to them. She argues that Hebrew notions of nephesh and ruach are holistic, that Greek body-soul dichotomies misled Christian tradition, and that divine action can be understood as top-down causation within complex systems. Her account keeps strong unity and invites serious engagement with empirical science. However, it strains at several points. First, the biblical data do not merely say that humans are unified but that some human operations transcend bodily organs. Jesus commands love of God with heart, soul, and mind in ways that resist strict supervenience on the brain. The texts on the **intermediate state** register conscious existence with Christ between death and resurrection that cannot be reduced to a worldline in the space-time of a decomposed body. Second, nonreductive physicalism faces the dilemma of mental causation under the causal closure of the physical. If all physical effects have sufficient physical causes, then higher-level mental causation becomes epiphenomenal or collapses into reduction. Third, the christological tradition requires that the Son assumed a complete human nature, including a rational soul. Denying an immaterial principle imperils the historic anti-Apollinarian consensus and reverberates into the Logos’ assumption of a mere functional organization. Murphy’s challenge is salutary in forcing us to articulate a unity that is not simply a pact between two substances. But her proposal ultimately undermines features of Scripture, creed, and experience that demand more than supervenience.

A defensible position therefore is a classical Reformed **hylomorphic dualism**: the human being is one substance constituted by the union of an immaterial, subsistent soul and a living body, the soul as the form that organizes the body’s matter into a human organism. This view safeguards **psychosomatic unity** and secures the immortality of the soul not as a natural right but as a gift that God sustains. It denies **trichotomy** by reading biblical language as overlapping ways to speak of the one human life before God rather than discrete parts. It affirms a conscious **intermediate state**, with all the constraints Aquinas recognized and all the christological ordering Calvin stressed. It rejects **substance dualism** in the Cartesian sense and resists both reductive and **nonreductive physicalism**. On the origin of the soul, it favors a Reformed **traducianism**. The reason is not that souls are carved from parental souls like pieces from a loaf. Rather, under divine concurrence, the procreative act brings into existence a new human nature with its own form emerging from the human stock God ordained. This better accounts for the deep familial inheritance of temperament and for the solidarity of sin in Adam under **federal theology**, without diminishing God’s immediate causality nor the spirituality of the soul. It also coheres with the organismal character of **hylomorphic dualism**. Creationism remains a permissible view, but traducianism, chastened and theologically supervised, fits the organic unity of human life and avoids implying that God creates a soul morally corrupt ex nihilo.

Examiners will press the biblical case. The Old Testament’s holistic language does not erase duality. Nephesh and ruach can denote the person or the life principle. Ecclesiastes speaks phenomenologically from the vantage of life under the sun. The New Testament clarifies, not contradicts, by distinguishing sōma, psychē, and pneuma without partitioning the person. Jesus’ promise to the thief today you will be with me in paradise, Paul’s desire to depart and be with Christ, the vision of the spirits of the righteous made perfect, and the souls under the altar crying out together frame a real **intermediate state** that is not annihilation or mere divine remembering. Yet the same texts subordinate that state to the resurrection of the body. The Christian hope is not a floating immortality but the restoration of full **psychosomatic unity** in glory. To the soul-sleep objection, one replies that figurative descriptions of death as sleep refer to the body and the hope of awakening, while the narrative of the transfiguration and the apocalyptic scenes imply consciousness of saints.

Every layer of doctrine leans on this anthropology. In Christology, the humanity assumed by the Son must include a true rational soul. **Apollinarianism** is excluded precisely because a human body without a rational soul is not a complete human nature. The soul of Christ is not replaced by the Logos but united to him in the one person. Only thus can Christ represent us in **covenant headship**, obey for us with a human intellect and will, and heal what he assumed. In soteriology, sanctification renews the whole person. The body is not a disposable shell but the field of the Spirit’s work. Romans 12 calls for bodily sacrifice as spiritual worship. Sexual ethics are not peripheral moralities but integral to honoring God with our bodies as temples of the Spirit. The sacraments are not optional symbols but divinely instituted means that address us as embodied image bearers, engaging sight, taste, and touch with promises and signs that the Spirit uses to communicate grace. In eschatology, burial liturgies confess the good creation and the resurrection. The **resurrection of the body** secures cosmic redemption, not mere survival.

Contemporary challenges make these commitments more urgent. Neuroscience richly documents correlations between brain states and mental states and shows that injuries alter cognition and personality. Yet correlation is not identity. Intentionality, first-person subjectivity, and the unity of apperception resist capture by neural description. If neural firing patterns were sufficient for thought, the semantic aboutness and normativity of reasoning would be inexplicable. The formal causality of the soul in **hylomorphic dualism** explains why reorganizing neural networks changes experience without forcing us to say that the person is nothing but a brain. Brain death protocols and criteria for personhood must be assessed in light of the soul’s organizing role: when the organism as a whole irreversibly ceases to function, the soul has departed and the person has died, even if some cellular activity remains. Artificial intelligence raises further questions. A software stack executing pattern recognition lacks the intrinsic teleology and rational appetite of a human soul. No amount of computational complexity generates the powers that belong to a rational animal ordered to God. This is not dismissive of technological goods; it is precision about **analogical knowledge** of our status as embodied creatures under divine law.

Possible examiner objections can be anticipated. Does the **intermediate state** compromise unity? Answer: the deficiency of the disembodied soul does not negate the creaturely order but confirms it. Death is an enemy that rips apart God’s intended unity. The temporary survival of the soul is not a second-best anthropology but the penultimate mercy of God until the resurrection reunites what belongs together. Does **hylomorphic dualism** entail that the resurrected body must reuse the same atoms for identity? No. Identity rests in the persistence of the form and the continuity of personal history preserved in the subsistent soul and by divine fidelity. Paul’s seed analogy insists on continuity without material sameness. Does **traducianism** demean the spirituality of the soul? No. The mode of origination is not material division but the organic mediation of a new form through parental generation under God’s concursus, just as in all natural processes. Does the denial of **trichotomy** flatten spiritual experience? No. The Spirit’s work energizes the whole person in all faculties. Spirit in Scripture often names God’s breath of life or the renewed disposition in union with Christ; it does not posit a third substance. Does **nonreductive physicalism** better honor biblical holism? It honors holism rhetorically, but its metaphysic ultimately cannot account for consciousness, moral agency, or the **intermediate state** without borrowing from dualism covertly.

The pastoral stakes are concrete. The church must resist both disembodied piety and practical materialism. Bodies matter in worship, catechesis, and care. The dying must be taught to hope for the resurrection even as they are comforted with the truth that to be away from the body is to be at home with the Lord. The grieving must learn to honor the body in burial, confessing the seedlike destiny of the corpse sown in hope. Discipleship must embrace diet, sleep, sexuality, and work as arenas for sanctification, for grace renews embodied nature. Those with dementia or disability remain fully persons under God because the soul’s dignity is not measured by cognitive performance. The church must also catechize against cultural distortions of identity that sever personhood from bodily givenness. **Psychosomatic unity** anchors Christian resistance to self-creation projects that deny the order of nature.

This doctrinal locus is not isolated. It presupposes **divine simplicity** and immutability in God, ensuring that the Creator-creature distinction is not blurred when we speak of souls. It presupposes creation out of nothing, so that body and soul are contingent through and through. It presupposes providence, so that all creaturely operations are under God’s governance. It presupposes Christology and pneumatology, so that salvation heals nature by grace. The doctrine of **psychosomatic unity** is thus an intersection where metaphysics, exegesis, and pastoral theology meet. Augustine framed the conversation by subordinating soul and body to God. Aquinas rendered the unity intelligible by a metaphysic of form and matter. Descartes forced the issue by dividing the human across two substances. Calvin rethreaded the tapestry with Scripture and covenant. Bavinck rearticulated it under modern pressures. Murphy challenges it with scientific holism. The best account takes the truth in each, rejects their distortions, and binds the result to the gospel. Human beings are embodied souls, called to God in Christ, destined for bodily resurrection. To defend that claim is to secure not only an anthropology but the grammar of Christian hope.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: **psychosomatic unity** is the one human nature constituted by an immaterial, subsistent rational soul as the form of a living body, ordered to God under the **image of God**, temporarily separable at death in a deficient **intermediate state**, and perfected in the **resurrection of the body**.
- Decisive figures:
  - Augustine: biblical duality within unity, anti-trichotomy, guarded affirmation of intermediate state, tension between **traducianism** and **creationism**.
  - Thomas Aquinas: **hylomorphic dualism**, soul as subsistent form, unity without Cartesian interaction, creationism of the soul, intermediate state as deficient.
  - René Descartes: two-substance **substance dualism**, sharpened distinction at cost of unity, set stage for materialist reactions.
  - John Calvin: covenantal, scriptural holism, dichotomy, robust intermediate state, body’s dignity, sin’s transmission under **federal theology**.
  - Herman Bavinck: organic unity, anti-trichotomy, chastened **traducianism**, integration of science with teleology, eschatological emphasis.
  - Nancey Murphy: **nonreductive physicalism**, biblical holism and neuroscience, rejects immaterial soul, challenges mental causation and intermediate state.
- Debates:
  - Internal: **hylomorphic dualism** vs Cartesianism, dichotomy vs **trichotomy**, **creationism** vs **traducianism**, scope of the **intermediate state**.
  - External: Platonic disdain of body, reductive and nonreductive physicalism, emergentist accounts.
  - Contemporary: neuroscience correlations vs identity, mental causation and causal closure, AI personhood, disability and embodiment.
- Defended position: classical Reformed **hylomorphic dualism**, dichotomy not trichotomy, conscious intermediate state subordinated to resurrection, rejection of physicalism, and preference for Reformed **traducianism** under **federal theology**.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - Does the intermediate state violate unity? It is a temporary deficiency due to death, not the telos; unity is restored in resurrection.
  - Why not trichotomy given 1 Thessalonians 5? Scripture uses soul and spirit overlap; no ontological third part is taught.
  - How does hylomorphism handle mental causation? The soul causes formally and finally, informing the body’s operations without pushing matter.
  - Does neuroscience disprove the soul? Correlation is not identity; intentionality and normativity outrun neural description.
  - How is identity preserved in resurrection? By the persistence of the subsistent soul and divine reconstitution of a body proportioned to it.
