### TITLE
Soul by Propagation or by Immediate Creation: Origin of the Soul, Original Sin, and Divine Agency

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How the human soul originates—by parental propagation or by God’s immediate act—and what this implies for **original sin**, **federal theology**, **secondary causality**, and Christology.
- Decisive figures: Tertullian, Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, Martin Luther, Francis Turretin, W. G. T. Shedd.
- Relevant traditions: Latin and Greek patristics, medieval scholastic **hylomorphism**, Reformation Lutheran and Reformed scholastic debates, nineteenth-century Reformed developments.
- Debates addressed:
  - Internal: Augustinian tensions on traducianism, Thomist creationism versus Lutheran traducianism, Reformed creationism versus traducianism under **federal theology**.
  - External: Origenist preexistence, modern physicalism and emergentism, monogenism and **creation ex nihilo**.
  - Contemporary: IVF and cloning, timing of ensoulment, bioethics, and brain death.
- Position: Defend classical creationism of the soul with robust **federal theology** and mediate transmission of corruption, resolving traducianist worries without compromising **divine simplicity** or Christology.

### ESSAY
The classical dispute over traducianism and creationism concerns a precise question in theological anthropology: does each human soul originate by propagation from the parents along with the body, or is each soul immediately created by God and united to the human organism at its inception. The answer controls how one construes the relation between **creation ex nihilo**, **secondary causality**, the metaphysical status of the soul, the transmission of **original sin**, and the integrity of Christology. It is not a speculative curiosity but a load-bearing joint across loci, where an examiner will probe whether one’s position coheres with **divine simplicity** and avoids making God the author of sin, sustains the **federal theology** of Romans 5, and accounts for human solidarity with Adam without diminishing the soul’s spirituality.

Traducianism received its earliest systematic voice in Tertullian’s De Anima. Writing amid Carthaginian polemics and employing a Stoic-tinged psychology, Tertullian argued that soul is a subtle, material entity propagated through generation. The conceptual lever was organic unity. If the soul is the life-principle of the body and if human nature is one living stream from Adam through procreation, then soul must be transmitted with the body. Tertullian believed this provided a straightforward account of how Adam’s sin becomes ours. But the price was steep. His materialist construal pressed against the soul’s immateriality and immortality, misaligning the anthropology that later councils and theologians would standardize. The examination to apply here is simple: if the soul is divisible or extended, how does it exercise universal intellectual acts or survive bodily dissolution. Tertullian’s model falters at precisely this point. Later traducianists jettison his materialism but retain the propagation thesis, reconfiguring the metaphysics. Yet this historical origin warns that the very intuitions driving traducianism tend toward a realism about human nature that can undercut the metaphysical independence of the rational soul.

Augustine changed the center of the debate by setting original sin, not the soul’s materiality, at the heart of the problem. Two pressures shape Augustine. First, he is committed to the soul’s immateriality, intellective powers, and the primacy of God’s agency in all being. Second, his battle with Pelagianism convinced him that the universal guilt and corruption of the human race require a mode of transmission that is not merely moral imitation. Early on Augustine vacillated. He sometimes spoke as if traducianism better explained how sin is truly ours, yet his mature judgment leans to creationism, though with confessed difficulty about the mechanics of original sin. The Augustinian reframing is decisive: a proper doctrine must cohere with the soul’s spiritual simplicity and with a non-Pelagian account of the human condition. Augustine’s strategy anticipates the later move of **federal theology**. Adam is not merely the root of our biological line but the public person whose act is legally constitutive for his posterity. Guilt is not a quantity physically transmitted but a judicial reality grounded in divinely ordered representation. Corruption then follows through nature by way of **secondary causality**, the disordered concupiscence of the parents communicated to their offspring in generation. When an examiner asks whether creationism saddles God with creating sinful souls, Augustine’s grammar answers in two steps. Sin, as such, is a **privation** of rectitude, not a positive entity requiring a cause. God creates the soul good and without defect, yet in justice withholds the original righteousness forfeited in Adam, and he concurses with the natural act of generation which, in a fallen order, conveys concupiscence. The corruption is not authored by God but arises from the defective condition of the human line, even as he justly ordains its consequence. Augustine never solves every riddle but sets the parameters: the soul is not materially divisible, God is the direct source of spiritual being, and Adam’s sin is truly and justly ours.

Thomas Aquinas converts these Augustinian parameters into metaphysical clarity by deploying **hylomorphism**. For Aquinas the human soul is the subsistent form of the body, a spiritual substance that naturally informs matter to constitute one human nature. Because the rational soul is subsistent, not dependent on matter for its existence as such, it cannot be generated by material causes. Generation requires the action of a like cause bringing forth a like form from potency to act. But no finite form can produce a subsistent intellectual soul; a cause must be proportionate to its effect. Hence Aquinas argues that the efficient cause of each rational soul is God alone. The parents cause the body through natural generation, disposing matter and thereby occasioning the infusion of the soul. God immediately creates the soul and unites it to the body, which obtains its life and operations through that union. This is not an episodic miracle but the ordinary manner of **divine concurrence** with secondary causes. The concursus is double: natural agents generate the bodily organism, God creates the soul and conjoins it. The appeal to **divine simplicity** underwrites this. If spiritual substances exist, their being must be from the First Being in a way irreducible to material processes. The Thomist insists that God’s rest from creating new kinds does not preclude his continual immediate causation of each soul. The examiner’s challenge that Genesis speaks of God resting is answered by distinguishing works of the six days from the ongoing divine act by which all things are governed and particularized. Aquinas further addresses original sin with precision. Sin is formally the privation of original justice. The privation is transmitted not by efficient causation of defect in the soul by God, but by lack of rectitude in the human nature communicated through generation. The soul is created good; by its union with a nature lacking original justice, it is constituted in a state of disorder relative to God. There is, then, no contradiction between creationism and the universality of sin, once one locates guilt in the covenantal order and corruption in the ontological order of a fallen nature.

The Reformation replays and intensifies these questions because justification by faith presupposes a solid account of Adamic sin. Martin Luther tended toward traducianism, moved by the organic solidarity of the human race and a realist sense that our sinfulness is not extrinsic. He speaks as if both body and soul are propagated, with the virgin birth marked off by a sanctifying miracle of the Spirit. Yet his position is not methodically developed. It carries tension with his commitment to the soul’s spirituality and highlights a Christological pressure point: if souls are propagated, how is Christ’s human soul exempt from depravity without denying his consubstantiality with us. Lutheran orthodoxy commonly opted for traducianism but secured Christ’s sinlessness by special sanctification in the virgin conception. Here the examiner will push: does this not adopt a two-tiered anthropology, where the ordinary case generates sinful souls, and the extraordinary case suspends the mechanism. The traducianist must answer by distinction between ordinary providence under Adamic headship and the extraordinary generation of a new Adam, yet the metaphysical tensions remain.

Reformed scholasticism, by contrast, largely embraced creationism, not because it softened original sin, but because it anchored original sin in **federal theology**. Francis Turretin is the master here. Working within a robust classical theism, Turretin argues that the rational soul, being simple and spiritual, cannot be divided or propagated. He marshals scriptural testimonies like Hebrews 12, God the Father of spirits, and Zechariah 12, God who forms the spirit of man within him, as positive indicators that God’s immediate agency is requisite for the soul’s origin. He contends that traducianism threatens the soul’s simplicity and confuses the causal orders. Parents are true causes of the body according to nature. God alone is the cause of spiritual subsistence. Regarding original sin, Turretin makes explicit the legal-moral architecture hinted in Augustine. Adam’s sin is imputed to his posterity on covenantal grounds. The privation of original righteousness and the positive habitus of concupiscence are conveyed mediately through generation as God concurs with nature. The potential charge that this makes God create souls already culpable is met by distinguishing between the soul’s ontic goodness and its relational state within a fallen order. Guilt is juridical before God, not ontic impurity bestowed by divine act. Corruption is a disorder arising from a deficient principle in human nature, not a substance God inserts into the soul. Thus **imputation** and mediate transmission cohere. Turretin also shows that creationism better preserves Christology. The human soul of Christ is immediately created by God and united to a body from the Virgin, receiving human nature truly while being preserved from Adamic guilt by the absence of federal relation to Adam and the positive sanctifying act of the Spirit. Christ remains consubstantial with us in humanity without participating in Adamic headship.

Against this, W. G. T. Shedd revives traducianism among nineteenth-century Reformed. Shedd’s motive is theological rather than metaphysical. He fears a gap between imputed guilt and inherent corruption if creationism is adopted. He presses a realist line: the human nature numerically one in Adam is propagated to us, carrying guilt with it in a way deeper than legal relation. He argues that Scripture speaks in realistic terms of the race sinning in Adam and of Levi being in the loins of Abraham. Moreover, Shedd worries that creationism places God too close to the origin of a soul that is immediately a sinner. His solution is to secure both guilt and corruption through natural propagation. Yet Shedd’s own system concedes that traducianism cannot be mechanical division. He must treat the soul as a simple substance replicated through generation, a metaphysics that is difficult to render without positing either preexistent souls or a kind of finite spiritual generation. The examiner should press here: if the soul is simple, how is its replication by finite causes possible. Shedd appeals to mystery at this point, arguing by analogy with the generation of life. But the analogy fails because vegetative and sensitive life in animals are not subsistent spiritual substances. The metaphysical disproportion remains. Further, Shedd’s realism risks displacing **federal theology** by making our union with Adam primarily a numerical identity of nature. Reformed orthodoxy has generally refused this, lest justification be similarly collapsed into realism rather than imputation.

The internal debates thus crystallize. Among the Fathers, Tertullian’s traducianism pioneers but introduces materialist encumbrances; Augustine hesitates yet orients decisively toward creationism governed by grace and justice. In the medieval tradition, Aquinas demonstrates that **hylomorphism** aligns naturally with creationism, since the intellective soul is a subsistent form whose efficient cause cannot be material. In the Reformation era, Luther’s traducianism underscores solidarity but strains metaphysically and Christologically, while Turretin anchors Reformed creationism in **divine simplicity**, **secondary causality**, and **federal theology**. In the modern Reformed context, Shedd’s traducianism valorizes realism yet struggles to maintain the soul’s simplicity and to avoid implicating Christ’s human soul in Adamic depravity.

External debates clarify the framework. Origen’s preexistence of souls is a rejected third option. It violates scriptural chronology and confuses individual moral history by positing an ante-mundane fall. Modern physicalism, by contrast, collapses the problem by denying the soul altogether. On such accounts the mind is either identical to or emergent from brain activity. But classical Christian anthropology affirms a rational, immaterial soul capable of **analogical knowledge** of God, and of surviving bodily death. With physicalism excluded, the question of origin reasserts itself. Emergent dualists attempt a middle path, claiming that God produces a soul when the organism reaches sufficient complexity. This rephrases creationism in biological terms, and fits poorly with traducianism because it depends on the threshold of biological organization rather than the replication of spiritual substance. Finally, monogenism and the history of the race raise apocalyptic questions in the age of evolution. Wherever one stands on the biological mechanisms, the theological anthropology must insist that each rational soul has its origin in God’s immediate causality if it is a spiritual substance. Traducianism is not theologically mandated by monogenism, and creationism is compatible with it because it affirms the single federal root in Adam.

Scripture, rightly handled, does not resolve the dispute by proof text but supports the creationist architecture. Genesis 2 describes God’s unique animation of Adam. Creationists point to texts like Ecclesiastes 12, the spirit returns to God who gave it, and Hebrews 12, God the Father of spirits, and Zechariah 12, he forms the spirit of man within him, as loci where God’s direct forming of human spirit is underlined. Traducianists appeal to Psalm 51, I was brought forth in iniquity, and Hebrews 7, Levi in the loins of Abraham, to ground solidarity. The decisive move is not to reduce either set to metaphysical proof, but to see how the broader doctrinal system gives them their place. If one accepts that guilt is covenantal and that the soul is simple and spiritual, the creationist reading draws the lines cleanly: God is the origin of spiritual existence, parents are the origin of embodied nature, and the juridical and ontological orders intersect without confusion.

A series of examiner objections can be anticipated and answered concisely. Does creationism make God the author of sin. No, because sin is a **privation** in a nature that by divine judgment lacks original righteousness after Adam. God creates the soul good, concurs with the natural union in a fallen order, and imputes Adam’s guilt according to public justice. Does traducianism better secure Romans 5. No, because Romans 5 ties condemnation to the one man’s act under covenantal arrangement, the backbone of **federal theology**, not to metaphysical identity. Does creationism disrupt human solidarity. No, solidarity is covenantal and natural by common kind and descent, not by numerical identity of spiritual substance. What of Christ. Under creationism, Christ’s human soul is immediately created and united to the body assumed from Mary; he is like us in all things yet without sin, not a son of Adam by covenantal headship but the new head. Under traducianism one must add a sanctifying exception to prevent Christ from inheriting depravity, threatening the universality of the mechanism. How does creationism cohere with **divine simplicity**. It follows from it, since only the First Cause can be the efficient cause of a subsistent spiritual substance; finite causes cannot give being of this order. What of twinning or chimeras. Creationism holds that whenever a new human organism arises with the natural potency for rational life, God immediately creates a new rational soul and unites it. Twinning occasions the creation of a second soul when the organism divides. This is ordinary providence, not after-the-fact insertion. Does traducianism better handle the empirical resemblance of children to parents. No, those resemblements are bodily and psychophysical dispositions communicated genetically, which under **hylomorphism** are formalized by the soul’s powers. The soul’s origin need not explain inherited temperament directly.

Contemporary scenarios pressure-test the doctrine. IVF and cloning raise the question of whether souls attach to laboratory-produced embryos. Creationism answers cleanly. Where an organism of human nature comes to be, God alone is competent to be the immediate efficient cause of a rational soul, and he does so according to his ordinary governance. Traducianism, which relies on parental propagation, must either extend propagation to somatic cell nuclear transfer or concede that a nonsexual path can generate a soul, undermining its central appeal to familial descent. Brain death and end-of-life definitions likewise illuminate the soul-body relation. If the rational soul is the form of the human body, death is the separation of soul and body. Creationism does not settle medical criteria but supplies the metaphysical contour: the loss of integrative functioning indicates the departure or nonfunction of the informing principle. Bioethical debates on personhood at conception find in creationism a transparent ground for the dignity of the embryo: a human organism is ensouled by God’s immediate act from its earliest formation.

The position I defend is classical creationism of the human soul, framed by **divine simplicity**, **hylomorphism**, and **federal theology**. This position not only aligns with Scripture read in the full economy of doctrine but also secures the soul’s spirituality and God’s unique efficient causality of rational being. It handles original sin by distinguishing guilt and corruption in the covenantal and ontological orders respectively. It avoids implicating God in sin by locating evil as **privation** and the propagation of concupiscence in secondary, fallen nature. It preserves Christology by allowing the immediate creation of the human soul of Christ in the virgin conception while maintaining his **consubstantiality** with us through a shared human nature and a real maternal descent. It is compatible with the full range of human generation scenarios under ordinary providence and treats the continuation of God’s immediate causality not as a post-creation intrusion but as the very mode by which the First Cause sustains and specifies being at every moment.

Traducianism’s central strength is the felt congruity of moral and natural solidarity. But this strength is better met by theological precision than by metaphysical overreach. The Reformed insight that Adam is a public person under oath before God, and that his act renders the many sinners through **imputation**, is not a forensic fiction but the deep structure of the covenant. That structure grounds the ordinary transmission of corruption through nature and supplies the explanatory power that traducianism seeks without demanding that spiritual substances propagate through finite causes. Augustine’s unease, Aquinas’s metaphysical demonstration, Turretin’s covenantal rigor, and even Luther’s and Shedd’s pressures all converge to show that the origin of the soul is a theorem of theology proper as much as of anthropology. God is the Father of spirits. He alone gives the rational soul. He does so in concert with the created order, in judgment and mercy, so that Adam’s fall and Christ’s obedience may be accounted to many without confusion of causes. That is the grammar that will withstand an examiner’s cross-examination and sustain doctrinal coherence across the loci.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: Traducianism claims the human soul is propagated from parents with the body; creationism claims each rational soul is immediately created by God and united to the body at its inception.
- Decisive figures:
  - Tertullian: earliest traducianist, materialized the soul to secure propagation.
  - Augustine: vacillated but leaned to creationism, anchored transmission of sin in justice and nature, not soul division.
  - Thomas Aquinas: argued from **hylomorphism** and **divine simplicity** that God alone is the efficient cause of each rational soul.
  - Martin Luther: favored traducianism for solidarity, invoked sanctification in Christ’s conception.
  - Francis Turretin: Reformed creationist, fused **federal theology**, **imputation**, and mediate corruption with immediate creation of the soul.
  - W. G. T. Shedd: Reformed traducianist, realist account of original sin, metaphysical difficulties about spiritual propagation.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Augustine’s tensions; Thomist creationism versus Lutheran traducianism; Reformed creationism versus Shedd’s traducianism.
  - External: Origenist preexistence rejected; modern physicalism and emergentism; monogenism under **creation ex nihilo**.
  - Contemporary: IVF and cloning, twinning and ensoulment timing, personhood and brain death.
- Defended position: Classical creationism of the soul with **federal theology** explaining guilt by imputation and corruption by mediate transmission through nature; safeguards **divine simplicity**, **secondary causality**, and Christology.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - Does creationism make God the author of sin? No; sin is **privation** and guilt is covenantal; God creates good souls and concurs with fallen nature without causing evil.
  - Does traducianism better fit Romans 5? No; Romans 5 is covenantal headship, not metaphysical identity.
  - How is Christ sinless under your view? His soul is immediately created and united to a body from Mary; he is not under Adamic headship and is sanctified by the Spirit.
  - What about twinning and cloning? God creates a rational soul whenever a new human organism arises; this is ordinary providence.
  - Why can parents not generate souls? A subsistent rational soul requires an efficient cause proportionate to its spiritual being; only God, under **divine simplicity**, suffices.
