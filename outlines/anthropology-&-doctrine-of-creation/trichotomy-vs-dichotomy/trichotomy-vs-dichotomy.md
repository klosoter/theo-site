### TITLE
Whole person under God, two or three parts

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Christian anthropology** within the **doctrine of creation**, debated from the second century through **Reformation confessions** and renewed in **modern analytic theology**.
- Core biblical or theological question: Does Scripture teach a **tripartite human constitution** body-soul-spirit or a **dichotomous constitution** body and soul or spirit, and how does this bear on **imago Dei** and **eschatology**.
- Principal figures or schools with short roles: **Irenaeus** and some Alexandrians tilted trichotomist, **Augustine** and **Aquinas** favored dichotomy or **hylomorphism**, **Calvin** and **Old Princeton** argued dichotomy, **Bavinck** offered **Reformed holism**, **Watchman Nee** popularized modern trichotomy, analytic philosophers like **Swinburne** and **Hasker** defend forms of dualism.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **image of God**, **substance dualism** and **hylomorphism**, **sanctification by the Spirit**, **intermediate state**, **Christological integrity** against **Apollinarianism**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **1 Thessalonians 5:23** and **Hebrews 4:12**, **Council of Constantinople 381** rejecting **Apollinarianism**, **Summa Theologiae 1265–1274**, **Institutes 1559**, **Westminster Standards 1646–1648**, **Warfield on Trichotomy 1911**, **Watchman Nee The Spiritual Man 1928**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Westminster confessional dichotomy**, **Old Princeton anti-trichotomy**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist holism**, contrasted with **Wesleyan–Keswick trichotomy** and **Thomistic hylomorphism**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Ongoing questions about **neuroscience and the soul**, **nonreductive physicalism**, and whether **trichotomy vs dichotomy** clarifies or distorts **biblical holism**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The trichotomy versus dichotomy debate asks whether human beings are constituted by three distinct parts, **body, soul, and spirit**, or by two, **body and soul or spirit** understood as a single immaterial principle. The issue sits in Christian anthropology within the **doctrine of creation**, and it radiates into **imago Dei**, **Christology**, **soteriology**, and **eschatology**. A trichotomist ontology treats soul and spirit as distinct substances or at least as distinct essential principles, often assigning to spirit Godward capacities like communion and intuition and to soul psychological capacities like mind, will, and emotion. A dichotomist ontology affirms a real body and a single immaterial self, called soul or spirit in Scripture, resisting any third constituent. The Reformed tradition has typically defended a **holistic dualism** body and soul against both trichotomist partition and reductive monism, appealing to **biblical semantics** and the **unity of the person**. The question is not pedantic, since it pressures how we speak about sin and grace in the faculties, the **intermediate state**, and the integrity of Christ's humanity.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Old Testament usage regularly deploys multiple overlapping terms for the human being that function more as perspectival descriptors than as ontological compartments. In Genesis 2:7 the man formed from dust receives the *nishmat chayyim* and becomes a *nephesh chayyah*, a living being, so **nephesh** is not a separable part but the whole person in a vital mode. Animals are also called *nephesh* in Genesis 1:20, which undermines any automatic elevation of **nephesh** to a unique rational soul. The Spirit words are similarly fluid. **Ruach** denotes wind, breath, and the animating principle of life, as in Ecclesiastes 12:7 where the **ruach returns to God**, but the language does not require a third ontological constituent in addition to **basar**, the fleshly or bodily aspect. Psalms and Wisdom literature coordinate **lev or heart**, **nefesh**, and **basar** in symbolic parallelism, for example Psalm 84 where the **heart and flesh sing for joy**, indicating holistic prayer rather than dividing parts into discrete substances.

New Testament texts commonly cited for trichotomy do not require it when read with attention to *psyche* and *pneuma* as overlapping terms. In 1 Thessalonians 5:23 Paul blesses the church that their **spirit and soul and body** be kept blameless at the parousia, which many interpreters read as a **merism** that accumulates terms for totality rather than a metaphysical map. Hebrews 4:12 speaks of the word dividing **soul and spirit, joints and marrow**, which reads naturally as a rhetorical penetration rather than a literal slicing of distinct substances, using parallel pairs to stress the **discernment of the heart**. Luke 1:46–47 offers a strong witness, where Mary says my **soul magnifies the Lord and my spirit rejoices**, employing **Hebraic parallelism** to treat soul and spirit as near synonyms. Other texts complicate neat tripartition. In Matthew 10:28 Jesus distinguishes **body that can be killed** from **soul that cannot be killed by men**, a clear dualist text that does not add a third part. In 1 Corinthians 2:14 through 3:4 Paul contrasts the **psychikos human** and the **pneumatikos human**, which describes life-stage under the Holy Spirit rather than innate ontological strata. James 2:26 says the **body without spirit is dead**, again pairing two rather than three. The New Testament range of terms includes *kardia*, *nous*, *soma*, *sarx*, *psyche*, and *pneuma*, and the most plausible synthesis is that Scripture speaks **holistically with multiple angles** rather than teaching a strict **tripartite anthropology**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic sources display variety linked to exegetical and philosophical currents. **Irenaeus**, writing around 180 in Against Heresies, used body, soul, and spirit language to counter **Gnostic dualisms**, sometimes sounding **trichotomist**, yet his goal was the unity of the redeemed person under the Holy Spirit. Alexandrian theologians like **Origen** and **Clement** sometimes parsed soul and spirit distinctly, influenced by **Platonic tripartition**, but later concerns about Origenist speculation tempered such schemes. The **Apollinarian controversy** proved decisive for later caution. **Apollinaris of Laodicea** denied a rational human soul in Christ, collapsing the human mind into the Logos, and was condemned at **Constantinople 381**, with **Gregory of Nazianzus's maxim**, what is not assumed is not healed, safeguarding a full range of human faculties in the **one person of Christ**. **Augustine** emphasized **soul-body unity** and often treated **spirit** as the soul oriented Godward rather than a third substance, setting a trajectory toward dichotomy.

In the medieval synthesis, **Thomas Aquinas** articulated **Thomistic hylomorphism** in the Summa Theologiae 1265–1274. The human soul is the single **substantial form of the body**, with vegetative, sensitive, and rational powers, which distinguishes faculties without multiplying substances. This became the dominant Catholic account. During the Reformation, **Luther** occasionally used **trichotomic rhetoric** for pastoral or homiletical purposes, distinguishing spirit before God from soul in relation to the world, yet he did not advance a metaphysical tripartition in his catechisms. **Calvin** in the Institutes 1559 rejected speculative **trichotomy**, treating soul and spirit as near synonyms and warning against turning rhetorical parallels into ontology, while insisting on the **immortality of the soul** and the integrity of the body. Post-Reformation **Protestant Orthodoxy** largely codified dichotomy, and the **Westminster Standards 1646–1648** spoke of a **reasonable and immortal soul** without positing a third part. Nineteenth-century revivalism and Keswick holiness renewed **trichotomy** as a practical psychology, culminating in **Watchman Nee's The Spiritual Man 1928**, which divided spirit into **conscience, intuition, and communion** and soul into **mind, will, and emotion**, a schema influential in some evangelical circles but sharply criticized by **Old Princeton**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton theologians defended a robust **dichotomist and holistic dualism**. **Charles Hodge**, in his Systematic Theology 1871–1873, argued that Scripture uses **soul and spirit interchangeably** and that the tripartite reading risks importing foreign psychology. **B. B. Warfield**, in essays around 1911 gathered later in his Selected Shorter Writings, critiqued **trichotomy** as exegetically thin and theologically perilous, warning that it can slide toward **Apollinarian replacements** in Christology and toward **semi-Pelagian anthropology** by allocating the higher spirit as unfallen. The **Westminster Confession** and Catechisms, 1646–1648, embed this instinct by confessing that man consists of **body and a reasonable, immortal soul**, affirming **intermediate state consciousness** and bodily resurrection without third-part metaphysics. 

Dutch Neo-Calvinism deepened the **Reformed holism** rather than altering its dualism. **Abraham Kuyper** in his Encyclopaedie 1894 and Stone Lectures 1898 stressed the **organism of the person** and the sovereign grace that renews the heart rather than a special unfallen spirit. **Herman Bavinck**, in Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901, rejected strict **trichotomy** while also resisting crude **substance dualism**, preferring to speak of one human nature with **many faculties** under the unity of the **self before God**. He tied the Spirit's work to the **renewal of the whole person** rather than awakening a separate spirit-substance. Contemporary Reformed voices such as **John Cooper**, Body, Soul, and Life Everlasting 1989, defend **holistic dualism** against reductive physicalism without embracing trichotomy, arguing that the **biblical semantics** and **resurrection hope** sit best with a two-term ontology and a strongly integrated psychology.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology, shaped by **Thomistic hylomorphism**, affirms a **soul-body unity** with a single intellectual soul as the substantial form of the living human. Within that framework, talk of **spirit** is often a description of the **intellectus or mens** oriented to God rather than a third ontic item. **Thomas Aquinas** distinguishes intellectual powers without multiplying substances, which protects both **embodiment** and **survival after death** through the subsistence of the rational soul by God's gift. Eastern Orthodox spirituality often emphasizes the **nous**, the intellective faculty, and some Fathers and later writers like **Gregory Palamas** in the Triads of the 1340s spoke of **deification** as the healing of the **noetic faculty**, yet most Orthodox dogmatics resist hard **trichotomy** in favor of holistic personalism.

Lutheran theology contains a strand of **trichotomic language** in devotional and homiletical settings, distinguishing **spirit coram Deo** from **soul coram mundo**, yet the **Book of Concord 1580** and later dogmatics do not require a tripartite ontology. Modern evangelical piety sometimes adopted **trichotomy** through **Wesleyan–Keswick** streams and through **Watchman Nee**, leading to pastoral schemas that map **sanctification** onto a spirit-soul-body ladder. In analytic philosophy of religion, the scene is mixed. **Richard Swinburne**, The Evolution of the Soul 1986 and 1997, defends a **substance dualism** that aligns naturally with dichotomy. **William Hasker**, The Emergent Self 1999, argues for **emergent dualism**, where a new immaterial mind emerges from complex brains and then persists, again two-part rather than three-part. **Eleonore Stump** and **David Oderberg** defend **Thomistic hylomorphism**, while other Christian philosophers such as **Nancey Murphy** and **Kevin Corcoran** argue **nonreductive physicalism**, challenging both **trichotomy** and **dichotomy** in favor of strong **biblical holism** that stresses resurrection over intermediate survival. These alternatives pressure theologians to clarify how **Scripture's language**, **Christ's humanity**, and **resurrection hope** govern anthropological models.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Several objections sustain the debate. Exegetically, critics of trichotomy argue that **1 Thessalonians 5:23** and **Hebrews 4:12** are rhetorical accumulations, not ontologies, and that **Luke 1:46–47** treats **soul and spirit as synonyms**, placing the burden of proof on trichotomists. Theologically, **Warfield's warning** remains apt: dividing **spirit** as a higher, untainted faculty risks **Apollinarian tendencies** in Christology and **Pelagian drift** in anthropology by creating a sanctified summit within fallen man. Conversely, critics of dichotomy warn that some dualisms become **Cartesian isolationisms**, poorly integrating embodiment and giving comfort to **gnostic impulses**.

Neuroscience and philosophy force new questions. Data about brain injury and personality change challenge simplistic faculty maps and call for a **holistic anthropology** that honors **embodiment**. Christian physicalists press that Scripture's focus is on **bodily resurrection** and **new creation**, not the extraction of a separable soul, while Reformed dualists counter with **Matthew 10:28**, **Philippians 1:23**, **2 Corinthians 5:8**, and **Revelation 6:9–11** which affirm **intermediate state consciousness** without denying the centrality of resurrection. Pastoral misuse of **trichotomy diagrams** in deliverance or counseling, where sins are relegated to soul while spirit is deemed pure, can blunt **repentance** and fragment sanctification. The best recent work pursues **biblical holism with dualist modesty**, resisting both reductive monism and speculative tripartition.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Anthropology must mirror and not distort other loci. The **Trinity** is one simple God in three persons, not a warrant for **tripartite human ontology**. **Christology** requires that the **Son assumed a complete human nature**, including a rational human soul or spirit, which is safer in a **dichotomist or hylomorphic frame** that names one immaterial principle rather than two competing inner substances. **Hamartiology** concerns corruption of the whole person, so dividing clean spirit from dirty soul risks **semi-Pelagianism**. **Soteriology** teaches that the **Holy Spirit regenerates the heart**, not just a higher slice, and that **sanctification** addresses mind, will, affections, and bodily practice. **Eschatology** requires both **intermediate state communion with Christ** commonly called the soul's survival and the **resurrection of the body**, which a **Reformed holistic dualism** honors. **Ecclesiology** distributes grace through **Word and sacrament** to whole persons in the body of Christ, shaping practices that resist **anthropological fragmentation**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, a wise **dichotomist holism** avoids both speculative partition and reductive monism. It teaches congregants that they are a **unified self in a body**, called to **love God with heart, soul, mind, and strength**, a fourfold merism for total devotion. In counseling, it resists the neat boxes of **Watchman Nee's spirit–soul charts**, instead treating **conscience, intuition, and emotion** as interwoven operations of one person under the **Holy Spirit**. In catechesis, it grounds hope in being **with Christ at death**, then **raised bodily** at the last day, joining funeral liturgies to **Christian burial** that honor the body as destined for glory. In prayer and sacrament, it emphasizes the **whole-person sanctification** promised in **1 Thessalonians 5:23**, not as a metaphysical map but as a covenantal blessing. This stance equips the church to engage **neuroscience** with gratitude, valuing medicine that heals brains while confessing the **soul's accountability** before God.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Scripture and the best of the Christian tradition point to a **holistic dualism** that resists speculative **trichotomy** while safeguarding the **unity of the person** before God in creation, redemption, and resurrection.
