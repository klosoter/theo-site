### TITLE
The Spirit’s Sovereign Summons: Monergistic Regeneration through the Word’s Effectual Call

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: Soteriology within Protestant systems ca. 1517–present; anchored in Augustinian grace debates since 412–418 and codified at Dort 1618–1619 and Westminster 1646–1648.
- Core biblical or theological question: How do **effectual calling** and **regeneration** relate such that dead sinners truly and freely believe?
- Principal figures or schools (with short roles): **Augustine** versus Pelagius on grace; **Calvin** on **irresistible grace**; **Synod of Dort** on **inward calling**; **Westminster divines** on Word and Spirit; **Old Princeton** (Hodge, Warfield) on instantaneous regeneration; **Kuyper** and **Bavinck** on covenantal and organic accounts; **Wesley** and **Arminians** on **prevenient grace**; **Trent** and Thomists on **efficacious grace**; modern analytic Augustinians (Crisp).
- Key concepts or loci connections: **ordo salutis**, **union with Christ**, **total inability**, **means of grace**, **compatibilist freedom**, **baptismal regeneration**, **prevenient grace**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: Romans 8:30; John 3; Ezekiel 36; **Dort Canons** 1618–1619; **Westminster Confession X** 1646; **Edwards Religious Affections** 1746; **Finney Lectures** 1835; **Kuyper Stone Lectures** 1898; **Bavinck Reformed Dogmatics** 1895–1901; **Barth CD II/2** 1942 and IV/1 1956; **Plantinga Warranted Christian Belief** 2000.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: Classic **monergism** with **effectual calling** versus **synergism** with **resistible grace**; sacramental **baptismal regeneration** versus evangelical **word-centered calling**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Ongoing disputes about **regeneration-before-faith**, **union with Christ and ordo salutis**, the status of **infant regeneration**, and the nature of **freedom** in conversion.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The Reformed tradition defines **effectual calling** as the Triune God’s sovereign, internal summons by Word and Spirit that unfailingly brings sinners to Christ, and defines **regeneration** as the Spirit’s monergistic impartation of new life, creating a new heart inclined to God. In **Westminster Confession X.1** (1646), effectual calling is by the Spirit working through the Word, enlightening minds, renewing wills, and drawing to Christ so as to make them willing. On this view, **regeneration and effectual calling** are intimately joined: regeneration is the Spirit’s life-giving act within the broader complex of the call, issuing in conversion and faith.

Dogmatically, the doctrine lives within **soteriology** but touches **anthropology** and **Trinity**. It presupposes **total inability** from Adam (Ephesians 2:1) and grounds salvation in the Father’s purpose, the Son’s mediation, and the Spirit’s personal application. The classic **ordo salutis** distinguishes calling, regeneration, conversion, justification, adoption, and sanctification while insisting all are encompassed by **union with Christ**. Theological precision aims to preserve **monergism in regeneration** alongside the real human act of faith as the Spirit’s gift.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Biblically, effectual calling is anchored in the divine summons that creates what it commands. Romans 8:30 sequences those whom God foreknew, predestined, called, justified, and glorified, with the **golden chain** establishing calling as efficacious. Paul routinely uses **kaleo/klesis** to identify believers as the called of Jesus Christ (Romans 1:6–7), and explains in 2 Thessalonians 2:14 that God called us through our gospel, showing the joint operation of **Word and Spirit**. The distinction between outward call and inward call is implied where the gospel preached is accompanied by power and the Holy Spirit (1 Thessalonians 1:5), underscoring **effectual calling** as more than bare invitation.

Regeneration as new birth is richly portrayed in John 3, where Jesus insists one must be born *anothen*, from above, to see the kingdom. The allusion to Ezekiel 36:25–27 frames the promise of a clean heart and the Spirit’s indwelling as the matrix of **new creation**. Titus 3:5 explicitly speaks of the washing of **palingenesia** and renewal by the Holy Spirit, while 1 Peter 1:3 attributes new birth to God’s mercy through Christ’s resurrection. James 1:18 unites **Word and regeneration**: of his own will he brought us forth by the word of truth, indicating the instrumentality of preaching in the Spirit’s vivifying work.

The dynamic interplay of **sovereign grace** and human response is classically patterned in narratives like Acts 16:14, where the Lord opens Lydia’s heart to heed Paul’s message. John 6:44 and 6:65 stress that none can come unless drawn and granted by the Father, while John 6:37 assures that all given to the Son will come, capturing **irresistible grace** alongside genuine coming. The Spirit’s vivification in 2 Corinthians 4:6, God’s shining in our hearts to give the light of the knowledge of God in Christ, mirrors the creative fiat, situating **effectual calling** within God’s power to call light out of darkness.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The doctrinal lineage runs from **Augustine**’s anti-Pelagian theology to the Reformation’s precise treatments of grace. In 412–418 Augustine contended that fallen humanity is incurvatus in se and unable to choose God without **prevenient and efficacious grace**, against Pelagius’s moralism. Augustine’s later anti-Semi-Pelagian controversies solidified the claim that **grace is operative and cooperative**, yet the operative stage is decisive in begetting faith itself. Medieval scholasticism, particularly **Thomas Aquinas** (d. 1274), developed **habitus of grace** and instrumental causality of sacraments, tending to identify **baptismal regeneration** with the infusion of charity in the justified.

The Reformation reasserted **sola gratia**. **Luther**’s 1525 Bondage of the Will insisted that the human will is captive apart from the Spirit’s liberating word, and the **Augsburg Confession** (1530) confessed that faith is wrought by the Holy Spirit through the gospel and sacraments. **Calvin** in Institutes III.1–3 integrated **effectual calling**, the internal witness of the Spirit, and the new faculties bestowed in **regeneration**, emphasizing **Calvin’s autotheos** and the Spirit’s freedom while keeping Word as the ordinary means.

Post-Reformation confessionalization sharpened debates. The **Synod of Dort** (1618–1619) rejected Remonstrant synergism, stating in Heads III/IV that **irresistible grace** renews the will, sweetly and powerfully bending it to faith without violating human freedom. The **Westminster Standards** (1646–1648) articulated effectual calling in WCF X and added in X.3 that elect infants and those incapable of outward call may be regenerated and saved, introducing the category of **extraordinary regeneration**. Puritan and post-Puritan writers like **John Owen** (1657) and **Jonathan Edwards** (1734–1746) described the Spirit’s implantation of a new taste for divine beauty, a phenomenology of **new affections** consonant with **regeneration**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster** strand emphasizes that the Spirit ordinarily works through the Word, with **effectual calling** encompassing illumination, heart renewal, and persuasion enabling faith. The **ordo salutis** places effectual calling at the head of application, but literature debates the micro-order: in WLC Q.67, **regeneration** is tucked within effectual calling as the grace renewing the will. This maintains **monergism** while affirming that faith is genuinely human, Spirit-enabled agency responding to Christ.

**Old Princeton** systematized these claims with analytic clarity. **Charles Hodge** (Systematic Theology, 1871–1873) defined regeneration as an instantaneous change of the governing disposition, distinct from conversion as the first exercise of the new nature in faith. **B. B. Warfield** argued that **regeneration is monergistic and sovereign**, rejecting any theory of mere moral suasion. For Princeton, the Spirit’s work is immediate in causality yet mediate in instrumentality, ordinarily accompanying the preached Word, a position holding together **Word-and-Spirit** and the necessity of **effectual calling**.

**Dutch Neo-Calvinism** nuanced the timing and covenantal setting. **Abraham Kuyper** proposed **immediate regeneration** in some cases, including presumptive regeneration of covenant infants, with public confession later as the dawning of consciousness. **Herman Bavinck** adopted a more organic and christological account: regeneration is the principial beginning of new life planted by the Spirit, ordinarily by the Word, flowering into faith and union with Christ. This stream stresses **covenant and election**, allowing for **infant regeneration** without collapsing into sacramentalism, while insisting that the **means of grace** remain normative for conscious faith.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology, especially at **Trent** (1545–1563), identifies initial justification with the infusion of grace and charity, often linked with **baptismal regeneration** as an instrumental cause. The question of why some consent while others do not divides **Thomists** and **Molinists**. Thomists speak of **efficacious grace** by virtue of God’s predetermining motion, while Molinists appeal to **middle knowledge** and congruism, where grace becomes efficacious in foreknown circumstances of free consent. The whole is synergistic, preserving **free cooperation**, yet it affirms the necessity of **prevenient grace** against Pelagianism.

Lutheran confessional theology is monergistic yet resists the Reformed idiom of **irresistible grace**. The **Formula of Concord** (1577) insists conversion is wholly God’s work through the **Word and Sacraments**, but it affirms that grace is resistible in the non-elect, making the divide between elect and non-elect less decretal and more mystery-laden. Lutherans often equate **regeneration** with the bestowal of faith itself and locate its instrumental cause in baptism, whereas Reformed distinguish **regeneration and faith** conceptually while retaining the primacy of preaching in **effectual calling**.

Modern Protestant alternatives include **Wesleyan Arminian** accounts of **prevenient grace** that universally restores libertarian ability, making faith resistible though enabled. Revivalist **Finney** (Lectures, 1835) rejected **original sin** and conceived regeneration as a moral change by persuasive truth, a view criticized by Reformed and Lutheran theologians as reducing **grace** to psychology. **Pentecostal** and charismatic traditions distinguish **new birth** from subsequent **Spirit baptism**, sometimes evidencing subsequent fullness by charisms, while maintaining the centrality of confession that Jesus is Lord by the Spirit.

Analytic and contemporary dogmatics revisit metaphysics and epistemology. **Plantinga’s internal instigation of the Holy Spirit** frames faith as produced by the Spirit and warranted apart from argument, dovetailing with **effectual calling** as an epistemic grace. **Oliver Crisp** defends Augustinian **monergism** while exploring models of **compatibilist freedom** and the nature of grace’s causal sufficiency. **Barth** reframes the locus within **union with Christ**, emphasizing the once-for-allness of Christ’s history as the concrete form of election and calling, with the Spirit actualizing **participation** such that **irresistible grace** is reinterpreted christologically rather than decretally.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
One debate turns on the micro-order: does **regeneration precede faith** logically, or is faith the instrument through which new life is received in union with Christ? Some recent Reformed dogmatics, emphasizing **union with Christ**, resist sequencing and speak of a duplex simultaneity: the Spirit unites to Christ and grants faith together. Others retain the **ordo salutis** for clarity, asserting that **regeneration** is the root producing the act of trust. The risk of collapsing distinctions is weighed against the biblical idiom of causality in texts like **John 1:13**.

Another debate concerns **means of grace** and sacrament. Does asserting **immediate regeneration** threaten the primacy of preaching and baptism? Reformed orthodoxy replies with Westminster’s “ordinarily by the Word” and its clause on **extraordinary regeneration** for infants and the disabled, guarding pastoral comfort while preserving **Word-and-Spirit** normativity. The Federal Vision controversy (2002–2008) raised questions about **baptismal union** and **covenantal objectivity**; classic Reformed responses insisted on distinguishing **sign and thing signified** while affirming the Spirit’s freedom.

Philosophical objections target **compatibilist freedom** as insufficient for responsibility, and moral objections question selective **irresistible grace**. Augustinian replies stress **divine simplicity and aseity** grounding grace as gratuitous, and argue that moral responsibility requires reasons-responsiveness, not alternate possibilities, a line developed within **analytic theology**. The problem of **divine hiddenness** is addressed by the doctrine of the **internal call**, which posits that unbelief stems from culpable suppression, while the Spirit’s summons is effectual in the elect and morally serious in the non-elect.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinitarianly, the Father elects, the Son redeems, and the Spirit applies; **effectual calling** is the Spirit’s **missio** ordered to the Father’s purpose in Christ, displaying **Rahner’s Rule** that economic missions reveal immanent processions. Christologically, union with the crucified and risen Lord provides the ontological ground for **regeneration**, as believers participate in his death and life, so that calling is not mere summons but incorporation into **Christ’s pneumatized humanity**.

Anthropologically, the necessity of **regeneration** presupposes **original sin** and **total inability**, yet it also affirms that the image of God is restored and elevated in new creation. Ecclesiologically, the church is the sphere of **means of grace**, where preaching, sacraments, and catechesis serve as instruments of the **inward call**. Bibliologically, the Spirit’s **illumination** is the subjective correlate to the Word’s inspiration, so that **effectual calling** is the personal application of the same truth that the church proclaims.

Eschatologically, regeneration is the proleptic participation in the **first resurrection**, the down payment of the Spirit who will raise mortal bodies. Ethically, the new heart issues in **sanctification** as the necessary fruit, while maintaining the forensic distinctness of **justification by faith alone**. Missiologically, the doctrine underwrites confidence in evangelism: God calls through the gospel and raises the dead by the Spirit, aligning missionary practice with **divine sovereignty** and human responsibility.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, **effectual calling** grounds bold, patient preaching: ministers herald the external call trusting the Spirit to grant the internal call in God’s time. Assurance gains from **monergistic regeneration**: faith’s weak hand rests on a strong Savior, and the presence of **new affections** and repentance are evidences, not bases, of acceptance. In catechesis, clearly distinguishing **regeneration, conversion, and justification** protects consciences from confusing fruit with root.

Liturgically, prayers for **illumination** enact dependence on the **Word-and-Spirit** economy, and baptism is administered with confidence in God’s promises while avoiding **sacramental automatism**. In pastoral care for parents grieving miscarried or infant deaths, Westminster’s clause on **elect infants** and God’s goodness authorizes hope grounded in **extraordinary regeneration**. Evangelistically, the doctrine opposes manipulative **decisionalism**, encouraging patience with process and seriousness about the **ordinary means**.

Practically, counseling acknowledges the transformative power of **regeneration** while expecting conflict with remaining sin; spiritual disciplines are framed as **means of grace** rather than self-salvation. For those wrestling with doubt, teaching on the **internal instigation of the Spirit** offers an epistemic anchor. For those anxious about the unevangelized or the cognitively disabled, pastors stress the Spirit’s freedom in **extraordinary cases** and the imperative of mission.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Effectual calling is the Triune God’s sovereign, Word-wrought summons that, in the Spirit’s monergistic regeneration, creates responsive faith and unites sinners to Christ unto life.
