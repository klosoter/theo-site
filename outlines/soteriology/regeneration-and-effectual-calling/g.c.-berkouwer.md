# G.C. Berkouwer (1903–1996)

G.C. Berkouwer approached **regeneration** and **effectual calling** within the broader context of **Reformed soteriology**, emphasizing the dynamic relationship between **divine sovereignty** and **human responsibility**. He rejected deterministic or mechanistic views, instead highlighting the personal and mysterious work of the **Holy Spirit** in uniting the sinner to Christ and effecting new life. Berkouwer’s nuanced treatment sought to avoid both Pelagianism and fatalism, stressing the existential reality of the believer’s transformation.

- "Regeneration is not a magical change but the beginning of a new life in communion with Christ." (Faith and Justification)

## Key Works

- Faith and Sanctification
- Faith and Justification
- The Work of Christ
- Divine Election
- Studies in Dogmatics (series)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Regeneration as a Personal and Mysterious Work**
    - Regeneration is not a mechanical process but a personal act of God in the life of the believer.
    - The Holy Spirit’s agency is central, transcending human comprehension and manipulation.
    - Berkouwer resists attempts to psychologize or rationalize the new birth.
    - The mystery of regeneration safeguards the freedom and sovereignty of God in salvation.
    - "The mystery of regeneration is not to be unraveled by human analysis." (Faith and Sanctification)

2. **Effectual Calling and the Word**
    - Effectual calling is inseparable from the proclamation of the **Word of God**.
    - The call is not merely external but is made effective by the Spirit’s inward work.
    - Berkouwer emphasizes the unity of Word and Spirit in bringing about faith.
    - The response of faith is evoked, not coerced, by God’s gracious initiative.
    - "The Word and Spirit are never separated in the effectual calling of the sinner." (Faith and Justification)

3. **Rejection of Determinism and Automatism**
    - Berkouwer critiques views that treat regeneration as an automatic or irresistible force.
    - He insists on the genuine engagement of the human person in response to God’s call.
    - The relationship between divine action and human response is dynamic, not static.
    - Regeneration is not a metaphysical change imposed apart from the person’s will.
    - "The Reformed confession does not teach a mechanical or automatic regeneration." (Divine Election)

4. **Union with Christ as the Context for Regeneration**
    - Regeneration is always connected to union with Christ, not an isolated event.
    - The new life is participation in Christ’s resurrection and ongoing fellowship with Him.
    - Berkouwer resists abstracting regeneration from the broader reality of salvation in Christ.
    - The believer’s new identity is rooted in Christ’s person and work.
    - "Regeneration is the beginning of the new life in Christ, not a separate entity." (Faith and Sanctification)

5. **Pastoral and Existential Emphasis**
    - Berkouwer stresses the existential reality of regeneration in the believer’s life.
    - He is concerned with the assurance and comfort that flow from the Spirit’s work.
    - The doctrine is not speculative but intended to foster faith and gratitude.
    - Regeneration is experienced in the context of the church and the means of grace.
    - "The reality of regeneration is known in the life of faith and gratitude." (Faith and Sanctification)

## Legacy and Impact

Berkouwer’s treatment of **regeneration** and **effectual calling** has shaped modern Reformed theology by emphasizing the personal, mysterious, and Christ-centered nature of salvation. His careful avoidance of both determinism and subjectivism has influenced subsequent discussions on the relationship between divine grace and human response. Berkouwer’s work remains a significant resource for those seeking a balanced and pastoral approach to the doctrines of grace within the Reformed tradition.
