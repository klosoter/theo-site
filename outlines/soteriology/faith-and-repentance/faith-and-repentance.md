### TITLE
Turning to God: The ordered unity of **faith and repentance** in conversion

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **soteriology within the ordo salutis** from Augustine through the Reformation to contemporary evangelical debates.
- Core question: Are **faith and repentance** two graces or one act, what is their order, and how do they relate to **justification and sanctification**.
- Principal figures: **Augustine** on grace and turning, **Luther** on repentance as the whole of life, **Calvin** on **duplex gratia**, **Westminster Divines** on instruments and fruits, **Trent** on **fides caritate formata**, **Edwards** on **religious affections**.
- Key concepts: **saving faith** as knowledge, assent, and trust; **evangelical repentance** as turning from sin to God; link to **union with Christ** and **effectual calling**.
- Major texts and events: Mark 1.15; Acts 2.38; Acts 20.21; **Council of Orange 529** on grace; **Ninety-Five Theses 1517** on repentance; **Trent 1547** on justification; **Westminster Standards 1646–1648** on faith and repentance.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **sola fide** with necessary repentance in Reformed confessions versus **Tridentine infused righteousness** and **Lutheran simul iustus et peccator** accenting law and gospel.
- Legacy cue: Ongoing debates on **lordship salvation**, **Free Grace soteriology**, and the **pistis Christou** question continue to shape preaching, catechesis, and pastoral care.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
At the heart of conversion stand two evangelical graces: **saving faith** and **evangelical repentance**. Faith is that grace by which we receive and rest upon Christ alone for salvation as he is offered to us in the gospel. Repentance is that grace by which a sinner, out of a true sense of sin and apprehension of the mercy of God in Christ, turns from sin to God with full purpose of and endeavor after new obedience. Dogmatically, these belong to the **ordo salutis** following **effectual calling** and regeneration and preceding justification, adoption, and sanctification, while being inseparable from **union with Christ**.

The conceptual tension lies in distinguishing while inseparably conjoining **faith and repentance**. They are not two disconnected steps but one twofold movement of turning, where **faith** is the receptive instrument of **justification** and **repentance** is the necessary concomitant that issues in **sanctification**. The Reformed insistence that faith alone justifies protects **sola fide**, while also asserting that the faith that justifies is never alone, being always accompanied by **repentance unto life**.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Jesus inaugurates his preaching with a single imperative that is twofold in content: Repent and believe the gospel. The Greek imperatives *metanoeite* and *pisteuete* in Mark 1.15 signal a decisive, continuous reorientation toward the kingdom. Luke-Acts binds **faith and repentance** programmatically. In Acts 2.38 Peter commands repentance and baptism in Jesus name for the forgiveness of sins, while Acts 16.31 bids the jailer to believe in the Lord Jesus and be saved, and Acts 20.21 summarizes apostolic ministry as **repentance toward God and faith in our Lord Jesus Christ**. The apostolic kerygma intertwines the **gift character of faith** with **repentance unto life**, attributing both to divine grant in texts like 2 Timothy 2.25 and Philippians 1.29.

Paul grounds **saving faith** in the righteousness of God revealed in the gospel. Romans 3.21–26 places faith as the instrument that receives the propitiatory work of Christ. The *pistis* language spans knowledge and trust, not mere assent, in line with the **personal-relational nature of faith**. Galatians 2.16 insists that justification is by faith in Jesus Christ and not by works of law, thus safeguarding **sola fide** while Romans 6 links deliverance from sin’s dominion with the baptismal union that issues in a life of **repentant obedience**. James 2 clarifies that a living faith is evidenced by works, refusing **antinomian faith** that lacks the fruit of **repentance**.

The Old Testament provides the grammar of turning. The verbs *shuv* and *nacham* depict covenantal return and heartfelt sorrow, as in Psalm 51 and Joel 2 where rending hearts, not garments, aligns with **evangelical repentance**. Prophetic calls to return anticipate the New Covenant promise of a new heart in Ezekiel 36, where **the Spirit’s gift** empowers the turn. In the Gospels, the prodigal’s return in Luke 15 dramatizes **repentance unto the Father**, while Luke 7 portrays the forgiven woman whose love enters as evidence of **faith working through love**.

Biblically, **law-gospel dialectic** illumines the dynamics. The law exposes sin, producing godly grief that leads to repentance without regret in 2 Corinthians 7.10, while the gospel announces mercy in Christ that evokes **trust and turning**. John’s Gospel, with its insistence on believing so as to have life in his name, assumes **repentant reorientation** from darkness to light, and Hebrews 6.1 speaks of the foundation of **repentance from dead works and faith toward God**. Together, Scripture presents a unified act of conversion with distinguishable aspects of **faith and repentance**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The patristic tradition frames **faith and repentance** within catechesis and penance. **Augustine** develops a robust doctrine of grace in the early fifth century, insisting that even the will to repent is wrought by God through **prevenient grace**. The **Council of Orange 529** codifies anti-Pelagian convictions, asserting that initial turning is not self-initiated. The post-Nicene catechumenate integrated confession with baptismal **faith** as a turning from idols to serve the living God, prefiguring later sacramental debates about **penance and absolution**.

Medieval theology systematizes repentance within the **sacrament of penance**. **Aquinas** in the Summa Theologiae II-II treats faith as the foundation of the Christian life and penance as a virtue perfected through the sacrament, distinguishing contrition, confession, and satisfaction. The coherence of **fides caritate formata** emerges, where faith is formed by charity and thus justifying in a way joined to infused virtues. Late medieval practice, with canonical tariffs and indulgences, risked decentering **faith in Christ** by accenting satisfactions, which set the stage for reform.

The Reformation reconfigures the field. **Luther** in 1517 begins with the thesis that the whole of the Christian life is **repentance**, while his 1520 treatises emphasize **justification by faith alone**. For Luther, law and gospel structure **repentance and faith**, the law killing and the gospel making alive, so that repentance is the continual return to baptism and faith grasps the alien righteousness of Christ. **Calvin** in the Institutes 1559 articulates **duplex gratia**, the twofold grace of justification and sanctification bestowed in **union with Christ**, with faith as instrument and repentance as inevitable fruit, yet so inseparable that repentance is born of faith in **Christ’s mercy**.

Post-Reformation confessional codification is decisive. The **Heidelberg Catechism 1563** frames the Christian life as guilt, grace, and gratitude, situating **repentance** in the sanctification of gratitude. The **Westminster Confession 1646–1647** clarifies that faith alone justifies while it is never alone, and adds a distinct chapter on **repentance unto life**, calling it of such necessity that none may expect pardon without it, while insisting it is not the cause of pardon. Puritan divines nuance **assurance and preparatory works**, sometimes shading toward introspective concerns but ordinarily anchoring repentance in the sweetness of **Christ’s promises**.

Modernity introduces experimental and revivalist textures. **Jonathan Edwards** in Religious Affections 1746 distinguishes legal and evangelical repentance, grounding true turning in the Holy Spirit’s impartation of a new sense of the beauty of divine things, and tying **faith and repentance** to transformed affections. **Wesleyan Methodism** accents synergistic cooperation without denying **prevenient grace**, producing vigorous calls for immediate repentance. Nineteenth-century revivalism, from **Charles Finney 1835** onward, sometimes psychologizes **repentance** as a resolute decision, risking moralism detached from **monergistic grace**. Twentieth-century voices like **Karl Barth** interpret faith as obedient response to the Word’s summons, while existential exegesis under **Bultmann** emphasizes decision, redirecting **repentance** toward authenticity, which invites critical retrieval by recent **evangelical and Reformed** scholarship.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Reformed tradition speaks with a unified voice on **sola fide** while giving rich dogmatic place to **repentance unto life**. The **Westminster Confession** teaches that faith is the alone instrument of justification, yet it is always accompanied by all other saving graces and is no dead faith but works by love. Westminster’s chapter on repentance insists that no one may expect pardon without **repentance**, though it is not the cause of pardon, thus distinguishing **instrumental faith** from the necessary fruit of **repentant obedience**. The **Larger Catechism 1648** elaborates repentance as grief and hatred of sin with purpose to walk in new obedience.

**Old Princeton** theologians like **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield** defend **justification by faith alone** and treat repentance as inseparable from faith because the object of faith is Christ for us who also becomes Christ in us. They reject any view that makes repentance contributory to the ground of pardon, preserving the forensic character of **justification** while rooting holiness in **union with Christ**. **Dutch Neo-Calvinism**, through **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck**, integrates **regeneration**, **faith**, and **repentance** within the organism of the new creation, emphasizing the Spirit’s renewing work and the cultural breadth of **conversion** as a turn from idols to serve Christ in every sphere. **John Murray**, within the Westminster tradition, adds a pivotal note on **definitive sanctification**, describing a once-for-all breach with the dominion of sin that grounds ongoing **repentance** and faith as daily mortification and vivification.

Across these strands, Reformed theologians articulate **saving faith** in three acts of knowledge, assent, and trust, correlating repentance’s elements of sight of sin, sorrow for sin, confession of sin, and a turning from sin to God. All locate **faith and repentance** under **effectual calling**, wrought by the Spirit through the Word and sacraments as **means of grace**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology after **Trent 1547** maintains that initial justification involves **faith formed by love** in the infusion of righteousness, and that the **sacrament of penance** restores the justified who have fallen through contrition, confession, and satisfaction. Here, **repentance** is sacramentally mediated and synergistic, though empowered by grace. Lutheran theology accents the **law-gospel** structure, defining **repentance** as terror of conscience produced by the law followed by faith in the promise, with the Christian life lived as **simul iustus et peccator**. Faith alone justifies while repentance is constant return to the baptismal promise.

Modern Protestant alternatives include **Wesleyan Arminianism**, which emphasizes **prevenient grace** and the capacity to cooperate in **repentance and faith**, and **Pietist** trajectories that center heartfelt contrition and transformed life. Eastern Orthodox theology frames **metanoia** within **theosis**, understanding repentance as humble reorientation toward participation in divine life, less juridical and more medicinal. Contemporary biblical studies debates on the **pistis Christou** genitive seek to nuance whether faith is primarily human trust in Christ or the **faithfulness of Christ**, with implications for the grammar of **repentance and faith** in preaching.

Analytic theology recasts definitions. Some, drawing on **Plantinga’s** warrant and **Alston’s** doxastic practices, defend **rational trust** as properly basic in the context of the Spirit’s testimony. Others, like **Richard Swinburne**, construe **repentance** as a moral transformation that is evidentially expressible. Recent analytic soteriology under **Oliver Crisp** retrieves **union with Christ** to integrate **faith and repentance** as distinct yet simultaneous aspects of participation, voicing anew the classical Reformed pattern.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The late twentieth century saw the rise of the **lordship salvation** controversy. **John MacArthur** argues that saving faith necessarily includes submissive trust in Christ’s lordship and thus entails **repentance**, while **Zane Hodges** and **Free Grace soteriology** define faith as simple assent to the truth of the promise apart from commitment, warning against adding works to **sola fide**. The Reformed response maintains that **repentance** is not a meritorious precondition but an inseparable effect of **union with Christ**, preserving the gratuitous ground of **justification** while refusing a reduction of **saving faith** to bare assent.

Missiological objections worry that calls to **repentance** can become culturally coded guilt appeals. Scripture, however, presents **repentance** as turning from idols to serve the living God in 1 Thessalonians 1.9, a trans-cultural transformation. Pastoral psychology adds questions about shame, trauma, and scrupulosity, requiring careful distinction between **godly grief** that leads to life and morbid regret that leads to death. Academic debates about the **pistis Christou** construction raise whether focus should fall on Christ’s faithfulness as the ground of salvation, which Reformed theology gladly affirms, yet without collapsing the call to **human faith** and **repentance** commanded by the gospel.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
In theology proper, **faith and repentance** are operations of grace from the triune God. The Father elects, the Son accomplishes redemption, and the Spirit applies the work through **effectual calling**, producing **saving faith** and **repentance unto life**. Christologically, **prophet priest king** offices meet these graces: as Prophet he elicits faith by his Word, as Priest he secures pardon that makes **repentance** hopeful, as King he subdues hearts to new obedience. Pneumatologically, the Spirit grants **regeneration** that frees the will to believe and to turn.

Anthropology insists on **total depravity** and inability, rendering **monergistic grace** necessary for any true **repentance and faith**. Ecclesiology locates these graces within the church’s **means of grace**, especially the preaching of the Word and the sacraments, where baptism signifies the initial turning and the Supper nourishes **ongoing repentance**. Eschatology frames **repentance** as the present judgment of sin in the self that anticipates the final judgment, while **faith** now apprehends the future verdict of justification announced ahead of time. Bibliology shapes the hermeneutic of **law and gospel**, a distinction that guards the indicatives that evoke **faith** and the imperatives that guide **repentance** without confusion.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the gospel summons to **repentance and faith** must be preached clearly, neither front-loading repentance as contributory merit nor omitting it as optional. A faithful homiletic orders **law and gospel**, exposing sin to produce **godly grief** and announcing Christ’s mercy to engender **saving faith**. Catechesis should define **faith** as knowledge, assent, and trust, and **repentance** as turning from sin to God, situating both within **union with Christ** and the rhythms of daily discipleship.

Liturgically, weekly services should include **confession and assurance**, teaching **daily repentance** under the kindness of God that leads to turning. Baptismal theology should encourage a life of **repentant faith**, returning to the promises in times of temptation. The Lord’s Supper serves as a school of **assurance through Christ** where penitents feed on him by **faith** for strength to walk in new obedience. In counseling, pastors must discern between **legal repentance** driven by fear and **evangelical repentance** animated by the Spirit’s witness, applying **means of grace** to foster hope and change.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
God grants in the gospel a single turning with two faces, **faith and repentance**, whereby sinners receive Christ and return to God unto life.
