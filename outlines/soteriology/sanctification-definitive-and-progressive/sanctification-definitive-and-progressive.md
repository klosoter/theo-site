### TITLE
Holy Becoming: the once-for-all and life-long sanctification of the saints in union with Christ

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **post-Reformation Soteriology** within the **ordo salutis** and **union with Christ**, intensified in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries.
- Core biblical or theological question: how do **definitive sanctification** and **progressive sanctification** relate without collapsing into **justification** or moralism.
- Principal figures or schools: **Augustine against Pelagius** on grace, **Luther and Calvin** on sanctity by union, **Trent** on infused habitus, **John Owen** on mortification, **John Murray** on the definitive act, **Wesley** on entire sanctification, **Bavinck** on organic growth.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **mortification and vivification**, **Spirit-wrought union**, **means of grace**, **theosis and participation**, **virtue formation and habitus**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **Council of Carthage 418**, **Council of Trent 1547**, **Westminster Confession ch. 13, 1646–1648**, **Owen, Mortification of Sin, 1656**, **Wesley, Plain Account, 1766**, **Keswick Conventions, 1875 onward**, **Murray, Definitive Sanctification, 1955**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Westminster and Old Princeton** emphasize gradual grace, **Dutch Neo-Calvinism** integrates culture, **Roman Catholic** infused grace and sacraments, **Lutheran** law-gospel clarity, **Wesleyan-Holiness** crisis and process, **Orthodox theosis** as sanctification.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: tensions over **law and gospel**, **participation and virtue ethics**, and the **assurance and effort** dynamic in contemporary discipleship.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Sanctification names the sanctifying work of the Triune God whereby sinners are consecrated to God and progressively conformed to Jesus Christ. The doctrine has a double aspect. **Definitive sanctification** refers to the once-for-all act of God by which believers are separated from the dominion of sin, transferred into the realm of holiness, and given a new identity as saints in union with Christ. **Progressive sanctification** refers to the lifelong, Spirit-enabled growth in holiness, the renewal of the whole person after the image of God, and the weakening of sin’s presence. Located within **Soteriology and the ordo salutis**, sanctification stands downstream from effectual calling and regeneration, inseparably joined to **union with Christ**, and distinguished yet never divided from **justification and adoption**.

This double aspect guards two errors. Against quietism, **progressive sanctification** insists on active obedience, mortification of sin, and vivification unto righteousness. Against moralism, **definitive sanctification** grounds all growth in the accomplished victory of Christ’s cross and resurrection. Reformed divinity stresses that the same Christ who justifies also sanctifies, the **duplex gratia** of grace’s twofold gift. The ruling concern is to keep **sanctification** neither absorbed into **justification** as a mere declaration, nor detached as a humanly achievable virtue ethics apart from **the Holy Spirit**.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The Old Testament frames sanctification as consecration to the Holy God. Israel is summoned, You shall be holy, for I am holy, an identity conferred by covenant election and enacted through rituals of consecration. Exodus 19 declares a holy nation at Sinai, and Leviticus 11.44 and 20.7–8 join identity and obedience. The pattern is grace then law, a primitive shape of **definitive sanctification** followed by **progressive sanctification**, with the Lord’s presence in the tabernacle as the source and measure of holiness. Prophetic promises of a new heart and Spirit in Ezekiel 36 bind holiness to future **pneumatological renewal**, anticipating a sanctification both positional and transformative.

The New Testament sharpens the distinction with decisive grammar. In 1 Corinthians 1.2 believers are saints by calling, and in 1 Corinthians 6.11 Paul declares, you were washed, you were sanctified, you were justified, using aorist verbs for a past, accomplished reality. This is **definitive sanctification in union with Christ**, a once-for-all consecration. Romans 6 teaches that those baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into his death, and therefore reckon themselves dead to sin and alive to God. The aorist co-crucifixion and co-resurrection, rendered in the language of *syn* compounds, grounds the imperative in the indicative, an essential pattern for **progressive sanctification**.

Progressive transformation is equally prominent. Romans 12.1–2 calls for ongoing renewal of the mind, and 2 Corinthians 3.18 speaks of being transformed from glory to glory as by the Lord who is the Spirit. Galatians 5.16–25 contrasts the works of the flesh with the fruit of the Spirit, an organic metaphor that sustains **growth in holiness** through the Spirit’s agency. John 17.17 prays, Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth, tying sanctification to **Scripture as means of grace**. Hebrews integrates both aspects. Hebrews 10.10 affirms that by a single offering we have been sanctified, while Hebrews 12.14 commands the pursuit of holiness without which no one will see the Lord. The language of *hagiasmos* and *hagiazō* thus covers both definitive act and progressive pursuit, all within **Christ’s priestly mediation**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic writers framed sanctification as participation in divine life. **Athanasius** tied sanctification to the Incarnation, for the Son assumed our humanity to deify it, a seed of **theosis as sanctification**. **Augustine**, opposing Pelagius in 418 at Carthage, emphasized grace’s primacy. For Augustine, sanctification is the Spirit’s infusion of love that heals the will’s bondage, preventing both perfectionism and despair. The monastic tradition elaborated practices of prayer and asceticism as means of growth, while maintaining that the root of holiness is **grace, not human effort**.

The medieval scholastics developed a **habitus framework**. **Thomas Aquinas** described sanctifying grace as a created habitus that indwells the soul, ordering it to God. The theological virtues of faith, hope, and love, infused by grace, nurture conformity to Christ. The **Council of Trent in 1547** aligned justification and sanctification more closely, treating justification as the infusion of grace that renews the person. This codified a **Roman Catholic synergy**, sustained by sacraments as instrumental causes of sanctifying grace.

The Reformers recast sanctification through **union with Christ**. **Luther** asserted that the Christian is simul iustus et peccator, righteous by imputation and still a sinner in fact, which guards the progressive character of sanctification while protecting assurance. **Calvin** taught the duplex gratia, justification and sanctification inseparable in the one Christ. The **Heidelberg Catechism in 1563** arranged the Christian life as guilt, grace, and gratitude, with the Decalogue as guide for the thankful life, the classic **third use of the law**. The **Westminster Confession of Faith 1646–1648**, chapter 13, speaks of sanctification as definitive dominion-breaking and as imperfect yet real growth, while **John Owen in 1656** expounded **mortification of sin** as Spirit-enabled putting to death of indwelling sin.

In the modern period the doctrine diversified. **John Wesley 1739–1791** proposed entire sanctification as perfect love, sometimes framed as a crisis of full consecration. The **Keswick movement from 1875** popularized the slogan let go and let God, often producing a quietist alternative to Reformed gradualism. **Holiness and Pentecostal streams after 1906** linked sanctification to Spirit-baptism and empowerment. Within Reformed theology, **John Murray in 1955** coined **definitive sanctification** to name the once-for-all breach with sin’s dominion, refreshing Pauline exegesis and stabilizing assurance. **Herman Bavinck 1895–1901** framed sanctification as organic renewal within creation, a **Neo-Calvinist integration of nature and grace**. In the twentieth century **Karl Barth** positioned sanctification within divine command theology, highlighting participation in Christ’s sanctity and resisting both legalism and subjectivism.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton exemplified careful analytic clarity. **Charles Hodge in 1877** distinguished **justification by imputation** from **sanctification by impartation**, yet insisted both flow from union with Christ. He rejected Wesleyan perfectionism as confusing degrees with kind, upholding the necessity and inevitability of growth under the ordinary **means of grace**. **B. B. Warfield in 1918** criticized perfectionism as underestimating sin’s persistence and misreading scriptural teleology. The Princeton emphasis reinforced **progressive sanctification** as gradual, Spirit-wrought, and churchly.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism added a cultural horizon. **Abraham Kuyper in 1898** and **Herman Bavinck** argued that sanctification concerns the whole of life, family, arts, and politics, under **Christ’s lordship**. Their doctrine of **common grace** restraining sin and **particular grace** renewing nature underwrote a vocation-shaped holiness. For Bavinck, sanctification is the restoration of the imago Dei’s ethical likeness, an organic process rooted in **union with Christ** and mediated through Word, sacrament, and communal practices.

The **Westminster Standards 1646–1648** give precise dogmatic formulations. WCF 13 asserts that in sanctification the dominion of the whole body of sin is destroyed and the several lusts thereof are more and more weakened and mortified. The divines bind **mortification and vivification** to the Spirit’s use of means, especially Scripture, sacraments, and prayer. They also insist on imperfection in this life and conflict between flesh and Spirit, while promising real growth and final victory. This confessional balance secures **definitive sanctification** through effectual union and **progressive sanctification** through persevering obedience.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology conceptualizes sanctification as **infused grace and habitus** imparted through the sacramental economy. **Trent 1547** portrays justification as renewal of the person, so sanctification is intrinsic to justifying grace. The interior virtues of charity, formed faith, and hope grow through cooperation, though always enabled by prevenient grace. The modern Catechism reiterates **sacramental realism**, with baptism conferring sanctifying grace definitively and Eucharist nourishing growth progressively, an integration of **definitive and progressive sanctification** within ecclesial mediation.

Lutheran theology keeps **law and gospel** distinct while affirming sanctification as the necessary fruit of faith. The **Formula of Concord 1577** rejects both antinomianism and legalism, speaking of the **third use of the law** as a guide for the regenerate. Lutheran divines guard **simul iustus et peccator**, resisting any notion of intrinsic perfection prior to the eschaton. The accent falls on the Word’s external promise, with sanctification understood as the Spirit’s work through the preached gospel, catechesis, and prayer.

Modern holiness traditions offer diverse proposals. **Wesleyan theology** speaks of entire sanctification as perfect love, a heart purified from contrary tempers, often as a crisis subsequent to justification yet sustained by means of grace. The **Keswick movement** construed sanctification as consecration and Spirit-filling, though critics faulted quietist rhetoric that neglected ongoing **mortification of sin**. **Pentecostal theologies after 1906** variably sequence Spirit-baptism and sanctification, some as second blessing entire sanctification, others as empowerment for witness. **Karl Barth mid twentieth century** recast sanctification as participation in Christ’s sanctity, an act of divine command and human correspondence, rejecting anthropological capacities while affirming **obedience as grace**.

Analytic and virtue-oriented accounts retrieve moral psychology. **Eleonore Stump in 2010** depicts sanctification as healing of the will through love’s second-personal union with God, while **Robert Adams in 1987** frames the moral life as imitation of God’s excellence, a form of **participation ontology**. **Oliver Crisp** has argued that deification and sanctification are correlative without loss of **Reformed distinctives**, integrating patristic theosis with forensic and renovative grace. These approaches illuminate **habit formation**, attention, and practices as Spirit-animated but psychologically thick processes.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A central debate asks how **definitive sanctification** relates to **progressive sanctification** without flattening the latter into mere recognition of status. Some contemporary preaching risks treating definitive language as a substitute for active repentance, while others fall into activism that ignores the indicative. The Reformed emphasis anchors the imperative in **union with Christ**, resolving the tension by insisting that the Spirit who unites also conforms.

The relation of sanctification to justification remains contested. Those worried about legalism stress **justification by faith alone**, fearing that strong sanctification language undermines assurance. Others worry that neglect of **good works** disfigures discipleship. The classic resolution is duplex gratia, one Christ giving both gifts distinctly. The **law and gospel** relation remains an arena of dispute, with some evangelical circles questioning the **third use of the law**, while confessional traditions affirm law as a gracious guide for the regenerate.

There is fresh attention to embodiment and social dimensions. Questions about **virtue ethics and habitus** explore how practices like liturgy, fasting, and almsgiving form the self under grace. Trauma studies raise concerns about exhortations that ignore wounded agency, leading to renewed emphasis on **pastoral prudence**. Cultural debates over sexuality and justice test whether sanctification is merely private or also communal, pressing the church to consider **corporate sanctification** in its institutional life and public witness.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Sanctification depends on **Trinitarian missions**. The Father elects and sanctifies a people, the Son is our sanctification, and the Spirit indwells to consecrate. The economy reflects **Trinitarian taxis**, with the Spirit applying what the Son achieves. Christologically, believers are sanctified through **union with Christ’s death and resurrection**, sharing the pattern of cross and new life. John 17 portrays Jesus as the sanctified sanctifier, who consecrates himself that his people may be consecrated in truth.

Anthropology is equally central. **Imago Dei renewal** means the restoration of intellect, will, and affections to God, involving **habitus and virtue** yet always as gifts of grace. The body is not a passive stage but an active site for **embodied holiness**, thus the practices of fasting, sexuality, and speech belong to sanctification. Ecclesiologically, sanctification is communal under **Word and sacrament**, with baptism marking **definitive sanctification** into Christ’s body and the Lord’s Supper nourishing **progressive sanctification** through communion with Christ. Eschatology frames the already and not yet, for holiness now anticipates **glorification**, where progressive sanctification terminates in the beatific fullness at the resurrection.

Bibliology supplies the means. Jesus prays, Sanctify them in the truth, and Scripture functions as **instrumental means of grace**, read in the Spirit within the church. The doctrine of God anchors sanctification in **divine holiness**, so that conformity to God is likeness to the God who is love and light. Creation and providence provide the arena for **vocation**, where sanctification extends to labor and culture under Christ’s reign.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, **definitive sanctification** grounds assurance. Believers fight sin as those already transferred from darkness to light, not to earn standing but to live it. Preaching should follow the apostolic order, declaring **union with Christ** and then issuing commands. Counseling should unite **mortification and vivification**, naming sin honestly while directing faith to Christ’s sufficiency. Spiritual disciplines are means, not merit, received as **means of grace** under the Spirit’s agency.

Liturgically, the church performs sanctification. Baptism declares an exodus from sin’s dominion into **holy identity**, while weekly Eucharist reorients desire to Christ’s body and blood, fostering **progressive sanctification** through communion. Confession and absolution rehearse **law and gospel**, forming a people who repent and rejoice. Catechesis, the Decalogue, and the Sermon on the Mount function as guides within grace, shaping habits over time in the community of saints.

Communally, sanctification bears public fruit. The peaceable, patient, self-controlled life of **Galatians 5** becomes visible in justice, mercy, and peacemaking. Vocation matters. Workers pursue excellence as service to God, the poor are remembered as neighbors, and families practice forgiveness. The church disciplines gently, restores the fallen, and resists both **perfectionist pride** and **permissive antinomianism**. Under the Spirit, the long obedience in the same direction becomes plausible and joyful.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Sanctification is the Spirit’s holy making of a people already consecrated in Christ, a definitive liberation flowering into progressive obedience until glory.
