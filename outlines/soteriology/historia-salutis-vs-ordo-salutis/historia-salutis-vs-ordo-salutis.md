### TITLE
One Christ, Two Axes: Coordinating historia salutis and ordo salutis without Collapse

### PREVIEW NOTES
- 2nd–21st centuries; locus in Soteriology with strong ties to Biblical Theology and Dogmatic Theology.
- Core question: How do the once-for-all events of redemption relate to the Spirit’s ordered application of salvation to persons?
- Principal figures: Irenaeus (recapitulation), Augustine (ordo of grace), Calvin (unio), Westminster divines (ordo), Vos and Ridderbos (redemptive-historical), Murray (accomplished/applied), Barth and Cullmann (salvation-historical), Wright and Martyn (participation/apocalyptic).
- Key concepts: **historia salutis**, **ordo salutis**, **union with Christ**, **already-not yet**, **means of grace**, **golden chain**.
- Major texts and works: Romans 8:29–30; 1 Corinthians 15; Acts 2; Ephesians 1–2; Calvin Institutes 1559; Westminster Confession 1646–1648; Council of Trent 1547; Vos Biblical Theology 1948; Murray Redemption Accomplished and Applied 1955; Ridderbos Paul 1957; Cullmann Christ and Time 1946.
- Reformed trajectory: Westminster and Old Princeton emphasize **ordo salutis**; Dutch Neo-Calvinism integrates **historia salutis** through **union with Christ**.
- Legacy: Ongoing debate over the primacy of participation versus sequence, the place of assurance, and justification’s relation to final judgment.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The distinction between **historia salutis** and **ordo salutis** marks two axes of soteriology: the axis of redemptive events and the axis of their personal application, ordered by the Spirit in the church and in believers. By **historia salutis** we mean the once-for-all economy of God’s saving action in history culminating in Christ’s incarnation, obedient life, atoning death, resurrection, ascension, and Pentecostal outpouring, together with the church’s mission to the nations between Pentecost and Parousia. By **ordo salutis** we mean the Spirit’s application of that accomplished redemption in a structured set of relations such as calling, regeneration, faith, justification, adoption, sanctification, perseverance, and glorification. The nerve that binds these axes is **union with Christ**, so that the work accomplished in Christ is applied through participation in him.

Dogmatically, the distinction sits within Soteriology but touches the whole system because the economy reveals the triune God’s missions and the ordo is the Spirit’s appropriation of the Son’s work from the Father’s decree. A classical maxim guides coordination: the opera of the Trinity are undivided, yet exhibit a **trinitarian taxis**, so that the Father sends, the Son accomplishes, and the Spirit applies; the **historia salutis** therefore grounds and norms the **ordo salutis** without reducing either to the other. This coordination guards the sufficiency of Christ’s once-for-all work and the specificity of the Spirit’s sanctifying work, joined in a robust doctrine of **union with Christ**.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture narrates the **historia salutis** in concentrated form in 1 Corinthians 15:3–8, where Paul recites the gospel as Christ’s death for sins, burial, resurrection on the third day, and appearances, a sequence that establishes objective salvation. The narrative arc of Luke–Acts reaches from Nazareth to Jerusalem to the ends of the earth, climaxing at Pentecost in Acts 2, where Peter connects the exaltation of Jesus to the gift of the Spirit, a key hinge between **accomplished redemption** and **applied redemption**. The Gospels present the two states of Christ’s **humiliation and exaltation**, while Acts and the Epistles present the church’s participation in that exaltation through the Spirit’s presence and power.

Paul articulates an **ordo salutis** in Romans 8:29–30, the so-called golden chain: those foreknown are predestined, called, justified, and glorified. The aorist verbs express the security of God’s purpose, compressing the eschaton into the present in an **already-not yet** mode that integrates individual application into the sweep of the **historia salutis**. Ephesians 1–2 rehearses election in Christ, redemption through his blood, and sealing with the Spirit, then depicts the transition from death to life by grace through faith, a pattern that concretizes **effectual calling** and **regeneration** leading to **faith and justification**. In John 3, new birth from above anticipates the Spirit’s agency in the **ordo salutis**, while John 20 and Acts 2 embed that new birth in the **resurrection–Pentecost complex** of the **historia salutis**.

Exegetically, the priority of **union with Christ** frames personal benefits. Romans 6 grounds sanctification in co-crucifixion and co-resurrection with Christ; Galatians 2:20 identifies the believer’s life as Christ living in me; and 1 Corinthians 1:30 attributes righteousness and sanctification to Christ himself, not as separable gifts but as participation in his person. The lexemes *kaleo* (call), *pisteuo* (believe), and *dikaioo* (justify) exhibit logical relations: calling yields faith, faith receives righteousness, and righteousness inaugurates peace with God, displaying an ordered pattern that remains subordinate to **Christ’s definitive accomplishment** in history.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Early church emphasis tilted toward **historia salutis**, especially in Irenaeus’s doctrine of **recapitulation** in the 180s, where Christ relives and heals Adamic history, and in the paschal focus of the second-century rule of faith. Yet Augustine’s anti-Pelagian writings in the 410s–420s sketch an incipient **ordo gratiae**, with **predestination**, calling, and the gift of perseverance, while retaining sacramental and ecclesial mediation of grace. The patristic synthesis thereby bound liturgical commemoration of the saving events to a personal story of grace.

Medieval theology developed an ecclesially mediated **ordo salutis** through the sacraments as instrumental causes of infused grace, climaxing in the Council of Trent’s 1547 Decree on Justification, which articulates a multi-stage movement from preparation and initial justification to increase and final justification. The Reformers, while reasserting the once-for-all sufficiency of the cross and resurrection as **historia salutis**, articulated a personal **ordo salutis** shaped by sola fide and sola gratia. Luther’s **simul iustus et peccator** protected the priority of justification, while Calvin’s 1559 Institutes centralized **union with Christ** such that duplex gratia, justification and sanctification, flow inseparably from Christ’s person.

Seventeenth-century Reformed scholasticism codified the **ordo salutis** in confessional form. The **Westminster Confession of Faith** (1646–1648) sets effectual calling prior to justification, which leads to adoption and sanctification, with perseverance and glorification assured, all under the eternal decree. Federal theology’s **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** provided a **historia salutis** backbone into which the **ordo salutis** was grafted. Pietism and Puritanism refined experiential sequences of conversion, often tilting toward introspective ordo, even as the Protestant dogmatic tradition kept the **once-for-all work of Christ** central.

The twentieth century renewed the redemptive-historical axis. **Geerhardus Vos** in 1903 and 1948 championed biblical theology as the study of revelation unfolding in history, while **Herman Ridderbos** in 1957 framed Paul’s gospel as participation in the kingdom’s inbreaking. **John Murray** in 1955 held both together in the programmatic title Redemption Accomplished and Applied, integrating the cross-resurrection-ascension with effectual calling and union. Meanwhile, **Oscar Cullmann** in 1946 advanced the **Heilsgeschichte** model, and **Karl Barth** in Church Dogmatics IV (1953–1967) re-centered soteriology in the **christological event**, recasting the **ordo salutis** as the subjective realization of an objectively determined reconciliation.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton solidified an ordered application. **Charles Hodge** (1872–1873) arranged the **ordo salutis** as effectual calling, regeneration, faith, repentance, justification, sanctification, and perseverance, stressing the Spirit’s sovereign causality and the instrumental role of faith. **B. B. Warfield** ratified the golden chain’s unbreakable sequence, making **assurance** a corollary of divine decree, while resisting any disjunction between the justified state and sanctification’s necessity.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism re-weighted toward **historia salutis** while deepening **union with Christ**. **Abraham Kuyper** emphasized the Spirit’s regenerating work as the root of faith, but **Herman Bavinck** (1895–1901; 2nd ed. 1906–1911) saturated the ordo in Christology: all benefits are benefits of **union with Christ**, which is covenantal and organic, not merely forensic. **Geerhardus Vos** and **Herman Ridderbos** championed the kingdom’s arrival as the context for every soteriological benefit, correlating justification and sanctification with the believer’s participation in **Christ’s exaltation**.

The Westminster Standards define a confessional core. The **Westminster Confession** 11–13 sets justification by faith alone, adoption, and sanctification as distinct yet inseparable benefits, each grounded in **union with Christ**. **Witsius’s economy of the covenants** (1677) organizes **historia salutis** under federal headship, anchoring the **ordo salutis** in the Adam–Christ typology. **John Murray** revised sequence language by setting union with Christ as the fountainhead, so that effectual calling and faith cohere in a personal bond with Christ, preserving the logical priority of **regeneration** without collapsing temporal sequences across cases.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology, especially at Trent (1547), presents justification as the **infusion of righteousness**, initiated in baptism and increased by cooperation through the virtues and sacraments, with a final justification according to works done in grace. The **ordo salutis** is sacramentally textured and ecclesially mediated, with the **historia salutis** rendered present in the liturgy as the church participates in Christ’s paschal mystery. Twentieth-century **ressourcement** thinkers like de Lubac and Congar re-located personal salvation within the **mystery of the church**, while Vatican II (1962–1965) deepened the ecclesial and eschatological horizon, sustaining both **grace’s primacy** and synergistic cooperation.

Lutheran theology centers the **means of grace** and the absolving Word, giving pride of place to **justification by faith alone** as the constitutive judgment that creates the new reality of the believer who is **simul iustus et peccator**. The Lutheran **ordo salutis** emphasizes calling, illumination, conversion, and justification, with sanctification flowing from the Word’s efficacy and baptismal identity. The **historia salutis** is liturgically delivered through preaching and sacrament, but ordo-sequencing tends to be held loosely in favor of law–gospel dynamics anchored in Christ’s objective gift.

Modern Protestant theology re-specified the relation. **Karl Barth’s christocentric objectivism** reconceived **historia salutis** as the once-for-all reconciliation in Jesus Christ, with the **ordo salutis** recast as awakening to an already established divine Yes. **T. F. Torrance** emphasized ontological union, while maintaining the Spirit’s personalizing agency. The mid-century **Heilsgeschichte** school, notably **Oscar Cullmann**, coordinated the now and the not yet with D-Day/V-Day imagery, prioritizing eschatological structure over personal sequence. More recently, the **New Perspective on Paul** (E. P. Sanders 1977; J. D. G. Dunn 1983; N. T. Wright 1997) stressed covenant membership and **participationist eschatology**, complicating individual **ordo** charts and lifting the ecclesial story. Apocalyptic readings of Paul (J. Louis Martyn 1997) foreground God’s invasive deliverance, shifting the focus from stages to divine act.

Analytic theology interrogates the **logical relations** within the **ordo salutis**, distinguishing causal from grounding priority and temporal from modal order. **Oliver Crisp** and **Thomas McCall** have examined **regeneration and faith** relations, with some proposing that regeneration is causally prior but temporally coincident with faith, preserving monergism while securing the instrumentality of faith. Such work clarifies that ordo claims are typically about **explanatory priority**, not rigid temporal steps, and protects against reification of a mechanical sequence detached from **union with Christ**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A major objection claims the **ordo salutis** can become a reductionistic checklist that fragments salvation into separable parts and domesticates the gospel’s drama. In response, Reformed theologians have recalibrated around **union with Christ**, insisting that justification and sanctification are distinguishable yet inseparable gifts of the one Christ, received in faith by the Spirit. Critics also allege that the **historia salutis** emphasis can render personal transformation secondary; the counterclaim is that the Spirit’s Pentecostal ministry binds believers to the living Christ in whom the history is made present through **means of grace**.

Another debate concerns justification and final judgment. Some, building on Matthew 25 and Romans 2, contend for a **final judgment according to works** that is congruent with initial justification by faith alone, while others fear this introduces a two-stage justification that compromises the **Reformation solas**. A mediating proposal sees works as the necessary and Spirit-given evidences of living faith, judged as such at the last day, preserving the **already-not yet** of justification’s eschatological horizon. Questions about the order of **regeneration and faith** persist, with Reformed monergism typically asserting the priority of new birth, while Wesleyan-Arminian accounts defend prevenient grace that enables faith without determining it.

The communal scope of salvation is another fault line. The **New Perspective** and apocalyptic Paul have pressed the corporate dimension, arguing that traditional **ordo** charts individualize what Paul narrates ecclesially. Reformed responses highlight that the **ordo salutis** unfolds within the **historia salutis** people of God, through Word and sacrament, guarded by **union with Christ** which is both personal and corporate. Finally, debates over assurance and apostasy test how firmly the golden chain binds; Reformed accounts lean on **perseverance of the saints**, while others elevate the many warning texts as real means God uses to secure perseverance.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinitarianly, the distinction displays **Rahners Rule**: the economic missions reveal the immanent relations, yet we do not collapse the **trinitarian taxis** into a temporal sequence, since the same undivided God acts in all operations. The Father elects and sends, the Son accomplishes and is the content of salvation, and the Spirit applies and indwells; thus **historia salutis** maps the mission of Son and Spirit, and **ordo salutis** maps the Spirit’s bringing us into the Son toward the Father. The unity lies in **union with Christ**, a pneumatologically effected participation in the Son.

Christologically, the **two states of Christ** ground the entire schema: we participate in his death and resurrection, and we live out of his session and intercession. The **extra Calvinisticum** secures the transcendence of the Son even in the incarnation, such that the benefits are not mere qualities but share in the life of the risen Lord. Anthropologically, doctrines of **original sin** and **total depravity** underwrite monergism in regeneration and the gratuity of justification, while affirming that the renewed will cooperates in sanctification as the Spirit recreates agency.

Ecclesiologically, the **means of grace** deliver the benefits of Christ: the preached Word effects calling and faith, baptism signifies and seals incorporation into Christ, and the Supper communicates the crucified and risen Christ for nourishment and growth. Eschatologically, inaugurated **already-not yet** frames both axes: salvation is accomplished and applied proleptically, awaiting resurrection and the new creation. Finally, Scripture’s authority and clarity underwrite the form of doctrine here; **biblical theology** and systematic construction collaborate so that **historia salutis** and **ordo salutis** interpret each other within the canon’s Christ-centered unity.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, holding together **historia salutis** and **ordo salutis** protects both proclamation and cure of souls. Preaching announces the finished work of Christ in history, while catechesis names how that work seizes sinners: **effectual calling**, **regeneration**, **faith and repentance**, **justification**, and **sanctification**. This ordered grammar stabilizes assurance by locating it in Christ’s **once-for-all accomplishment**, ministered by the Spirit through Word and sacrament, while guiding believers into holiness as a fruit inseparable from justification.

Liturgically, the calendar enacts **historia salutis** from Advent through Pentecost, while the weekly ministry enacts the **ordo salutis** as the call to worship, confession of sin, absolution, and communion mirror calling, repentance, **absolution/justification**, and fellowship. Baptism as covenantal incorporation dramatizes **union with Christ**, and the Supper nourishes the same union, thereby resisting privatized ordo accounts. In spiritual counsel, the distinction helps diagnose whether a believer needs renewed sight of Christ’s objective sufficiency or instruction about the Spirit’s sanctifying order, preventing both antinomianism and legalism by centering **union with Christ**.

Missiologically, the church proclaims a cosmic **historia salutis** that fulfills Israel’s story and invites nations into Christ, while patiently instructing converts through the **ordo salutis** so that conversion is not reduced to decisionism but deepened as Spirit-led transformation. Ethically, the Spirit’s ordered application grounds virtue formation and justice as fruits of participation in the risen Christ, while the **already-not yet** guards patience and hope. Across these ministries, the coordination of axes yields a doxological soteriology that points beyond the self to the living Lord who saves.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Salvation is the one work of the triune God in Christ, historically accomplished and personally applied in **union with Christ**, so that **historia salutis** and **ordo salutis** are distinct, mutually interpreting axes of the same grace.
