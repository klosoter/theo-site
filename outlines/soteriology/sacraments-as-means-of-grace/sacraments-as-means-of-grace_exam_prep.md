### TITLE
Visible words and spiritual feeding: the sacramental economy as means of grace in the covenant of grace

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core conceptual issue: Whether and how the sacraments are true **means of grace** that instrumentally communicate Christ to his people without collapsing into either mere symbolism or automatic efficacy.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, Martin Luther, John Calvin, Francis Turretin, Huldrych Zwingli.
- Relevant traditions: Patristic anti-Donatist foundations, medieval scholastic sacramental causality, Reformation Lutheran and Reformed trajectories, Tridentine Roman Catholic sacramentalism, Reformed scholastic and confessional codification.
- Debates:
  - Internal: Reformed disputes over **spiritual presence** versus memorialism, sacramental efficacy and the elect, infant baptism and **federal theology**, frequency and fencing of the Supper.
  - External: Roman Catholic **ex opere operato** and Eucharistic sacrifice, Lutheran **sacramental union** and ubiquity, Baptist credobaptism and ordinance-only accounts.
  - Contemporary: Online communion, sacramental minimalism in evangelicalism, paedocommunion claims, ecumenical pressures.
- Defended position: The Reformed confessional doctrine that Baptism and the Supper are instituted by Christ as **means of grace**, true signs and seals of the **covenant of grace**, instrumentally effective by the Word and Spirit to the elect, conveying Christ’s benefits through **sacramental union** and received by faith, while rejecting **ex opere operato** and maintaining Christological and trinitarian integrity.

### ESSAY
To say the sacraments are means of grace is to claim that the ascended Christ communicates himself and his benefits to his church through instituted visible signs, such that the Word’s promise is made tangible, ecclesial, and covenantal. A sacrament is a divine ordinance consisting of an outward sign and an attached promise, appointed by Christ to signify and seal the benefits of the **covenant of grace** and to nourish communion with him. The sacraments are not bare emblems, nor are they self-acting rites whose efficacy flows from performed ritual. They are **means of grace** because God uses them as instruments to unite us more deeply to Christ and to strengthen faith, where the principal efficient cause is the triune God, the meritorious cause is the finished work of Christ, the instrumental cause is the visible sign with the Word, and the Spirit is the immediate efficient who makes the sign effective in the elect. Their end is participation in Christ, the unity and holiness of the church, and the eschatological foretaste of the kingdom. All serious accounts must therefore articulate both their objectivity and their dependence on the Spirit, both their covenantal identity and their soteriological function, and they must do so with precision about Christology, ecclesiology, and the doctrine of God, including **divine simplicity** and inseparable operations lest sacramental causality drift into quasi-physicalism.

Augustine forged the grammar without which sacramental theology is unintelligible. In the Donatist controversy he secured the objectivity of Baptism against those who tied validity to the minister’s holiness. His axiom was that the sacrament is Christ’s act through the church, and so Baptism performed with the evangelical form is valid regardless of the minister’s moral state, even though its fruitfulness depends on the Spirit’s gift of charity. Augustine clarifies sign and thing signified. A sacrament is a holy sign that both signifies and contains a grace, but the containment is not physical; it is ecclesial and promissory. He speaks of the inseparable link between sign and reality, yet articulates that hypocrites receive the sacrament only to judgment while the godly receive the res, the thing itself. He calls the sacraments visible words, emphasizing their location within the economy of revelation. His exegesis of John 6 and 1 Corinthians 10 underwrites the realism of sacramental participation without compromising the uniqueness of Christ’s sacrifice. What he did not finally fix was the analysis of sacramental efficacy in causal terms; that waits for medieval scholastic clarification.

Thomas Aquinas codified the metaphysics of sacramental causality for the Latin West. He gives a systematic account in the Summa Theologiae, treating the nature, number, and effects of the sacraments. Aquinas describes sacraments as instruments of Christ’s humanity, united to the divine Word, by which the power of the Passion is communicated. The sacraments cause grace **instrumentally**, not as principal causes but as conduits ordained by God. He distinguishes signification and causation: they signify grace and also confer it by divine appointment, not by a natural efficiency but by a supernatural efficient power. His triadic analysis of sacramental reality clarifies confusions. The sacramentum tantum is the outward sign; the res et sacramentum is the sacramental effect that both signifies and contains a further grace, such as the character in Baptism; the res tantum is the ultimate grace, union with God by charity. Aquinas’s careful restriction of **ex opere operato** meant that the sacrament confers grace by the work performed because Christ’s power is attached to his instituted sign, while the subjective disposition pertains to the fruitfulness of the res tantum. This framework would shape Trent’s doctrinal definitions and set the Reformation agenda. Its central contribution is the robust account of **instrumental causality** and the centrality of Christ’s Passion as the wellspring of sacramental virtue.

Luther reorganized the sacramental field around the gospel’s promissory Word. His protest was not against sacraments as such but against the sacramental system that converted them into conditions of merit and multiplied rites without evangelical warrant. He reduced the number to those instituted with a visible sign and a clear promise. His Babylonian Captivity concentrated on Baptism, the Supper, and Absolution, arguing that the sacrament’s essence is Word plus sign. For Luther, Baptism truly gives forgiveness because God’s promise is attached to water, and the Supper truly gives Christ’s body and blood because of Christ’s institution. Efficacy is objective because God’s promise is objective. Faith receives, it does not create, the reality. In order to preserve real presence, Luther articulates **sacramental union**. The body and blood are truly present in, with, and under the bread and wine by virtue of Christ’s word. He rejects transubstantiation as a needless theory yet maintains a robust identification of the sacramental elements with Christ’s body and blood. This is secured by his Christology, especially the genus maiestaticum, according to which Christ’s human nature shares in the divine majesty and thus can be present multivocally. His critique of **ex opere operato** attacked the Roman construal of automatic efficacy divorced from faith, but he never turned the Supper into a memorial. Luther’s polemics against Zwingli at Marburg show his determination that the gospel is something given, not performed by human memory.

Zwingli sharpened the other edge of the Reformation critique. His sacramental minimalism was a protest against magical religion and civic corruption. He argued that a sacrament is primarily a badge of profession and a communal remembrance. In his account, the Supper commemorates Christ’s death and expresses the unity of believers; Baptism professes repentance and faith. Grace does not run through the sign as a conduit; rather, the sign points to the reality and prompts the heart to faith. Zwingli is not purely reductionist, since he places the signs within divine institution and ecclesial practice, yet his stress remains moral and symbolic. This created a lasting fault line within the Reformed world: whether to maintain Luther’s objective gift without his Christological ubiquity or to press a memorial direction that protects transcendence but hazards sacramental vacuity.

Calvin offered the most balanced and enduring Reformed account, binding sacramental theology to **union with Christ** and the ministry of the Spirit. He insists that the signs are not empty but instruments by which God truly offers and conveys what they signify. The Supper is a banquet where the Spirit lifts believers to feed on Christ who remains bodily in heaven. He opposes carnal presence and denies that Christ’s body is locally present in the elements, arguing from the ascension and the finitude of the human nature. Yet he equally opposes mere symbolism by asserting **spiritual presence**. The Spirit is the bond of communion, making the sign an instrument that truly communicates Christ. Faith is the organ that receives this gift, but the efficacy is not generated by faith; it is given by God through the means. He reworks the medieval **instrumental causality** within a biblical register, connecting it to the Word’s promise. The sacraments are annexed to the Word, sealing the promises and ratifying them to the conscience. In Baptism, God engrafts into Christ and seals forgiveness, not because water cleanses, but because the Spirit uses water as a **means of grace**. He defends infant Baptism on **federal theology** grounds: the children of believers belong to the **covenant of grace** and receive the sign of that covenant just as under Abraham. He links the Supper to sanctification and to ecclesial discipline, defending fencing the table to protect against profanation and judgment. Calvin’s argumentative rigor appears in his replies to Westphal and Heshusius, where he refuses both Roman substantialism and Lutheran ubiquity while maintaining objectivity by the Spirit’s action. He is a decisive figure because he relocates sacramental efficacy within the triune economy without metaphysical simplification. The Spirit unites believers to the whole Christ, and the sacraments are the Spirit’s chosen instruments for that union’s deepening.

Francis Turretin consolidated the Reformed position with scholastic precision. He defends two sacraments of the new covenant as instituted by Christ and supplies careful causal analysis. For Turretin the sacraments are signs and seals, moral instruments subordinated to the Spirit who is the principal cause. He denies that they confer grace by inherent power or by the mere doing of the work. He distinguishes the efficacy that belongs to the sacrament as an instrument and the efficacy of the Spirit who applies grace according to election and the divine will. He vigorously rejects the Lutheran doctrine of ubiquity as incompatible with the communication of properties and with the integrity of Christ’s natures. He frames the Reformed appeal to the ascension and to the rule that the finite is not capable of the infinite as a Christological ground for **spiritual presence**. He upholds infant Baptism within **federal theology**, explaining that the sign and seal are objective even when the subjective act of faith is not yet present. He opposes paedocommunion on the ground that the Supper requires active self-examination and discernment. Turretin’s contribution is the hardening of Reformed clarity, aligning sacramental doctrine with the ordo salutis and with Reformed doctrines of election and perseverance. He thereby inoculates the doctrine against both Pelagian drift and sacramental superstition.

With these figures in view, we can articulate the internal, external, and contemporary debates that frame a defensible Reformed position. Internally, the fault line between Calvin and Zwingli remains decisive. A memorialist reduction empties the language of **means of grace** and weakens the ecclesial character of salvation. It tends to privatize faith and moralize the sacraments. Yet a quasi-Lutheran realism that inflates presence away from ascension threatens Christological clarity and gives host to confusion about the Spirit’s role. The Reformed path must insist on a robust **sacramental union** by which the sign and thing signified are truly conjoined by divine appointment such that the sacrament delivers what it promises when and where the Spirit so wills, while denying any local inclusion of Christ in the elements. A second internal debate concerns efficacy and election. If the sacraments are **means of grace**, do they confer grace on all who receive them. The answer is that they confer Christ according to the mode of the recipient and according to the Spirit’s sovereign distribution. The sacrament does not fail when hypocrites eat and drink judgment, because the sacrament’s nature is not altered by abuse. The signs are grace to the elect, judgment to the unbelieving. A third internal debate concerns infant Baptism and the relation of sign and thing signified. The sign signifies and seals covenant inclusion objectively, yet the res is received in due time by faith, and the sacrament has a proleptic efficacy in the elect that the Spirit actualizes. The pastoral danger of identifying Baptism with regeneration simpliciter must be resisted, yet denying any efficacy in Baptism undoes the sign’s promised grace. Finally, internal debates over frequency and fencing must be ordered by the fact that the Supper is a **means of grace** for the life of the church. The New Testament patterns and confessional reasoning favor frequent communion under faithful discipline and catechesis.

Externally, three fronts require concise argument. Against Rome, we affirm with Augustine that the minister’s unworthiness does not invalidate the sacrament, but we reject the Tridentine consolidation of **ex opere operato** according to which the sacrament confers grace by the work performed apart from the recipient’s faith, as well as the multiplication of seven sacraments and the Eucharistic sacrifice understood as propitiatory repetition. The Reformed case stresses Christ’s once-for-all oblation and the unique priesthood of Christ. The Mass is not a new sacrifice but a communion in the one sacrifice by thanksgiving and praise, a real participation by **spiritual presence**. The Roman account is vulnerable to a causal model that risks violating **divine simplicity** by positing created rites as quasi-autonomous vehicles of created habits infallibly attached to performance. Against Lutheran sacramental union we affirm the gravity of the promise and the objectivity of the gift while denying ubiquity on Christological grounds. The finite cannot bear the infinite, and the ascended body is locally present in heaven. The Spirit’s agency secures our real feeding without collapsing the natures. Against Baptists and ordinance-only accounts we insist that the sacraments are more than symbols of obedience; they are divine instruments that seal promises and strengthen faith. The **covenant of grace** requires the sign to be administered to believers and their children, not because faith can be presumed but because God claims families and promises to work through means over time. The New Testament links Baptism to incorporation into Christ and to the promise, which is for you and your children, and the Supper to a real participation in the body and blood of Christ.

Contemporary challenges test the coherence and resilience of this doctrine. The proposal of online communion attempts to sever ecclesial presence from bodily gathering. A Reformed account must answer that the Supper is an ecclesial act under ordained ministry with tangible elements, fencing, and mutual recognition. The sign is bodily and communal, because it is a **visible word** that orders the church’s public worship. Digital mediation can supplement preachment for the homebound, but it cannot constitute sacramental action. Another challenge is evangelical sacramental minimalism that treats the sacraments as optional or sentimental. This deprives congregations of Christ’s appointed **means of grace** and weakens the church’s grasp on **union with Christ** and covenant identity. The remedy is catechesis and disciplined practice that restores weekly or frequent communion and robust preparation, without collapsing into sacerdotalism. Paedocommunion proposals press for children at the table on the ground of covenant inclusion. The Reformed reply affirms covenant inclusion but notes that the Supper requires active self-examination and discerning the body, which presupposes cognitive and spiritual maturity. This is not legalism but protection of the sacrament’s telos. Ecumenical pressures invite compromises on presence and sacrifice; here the Reformed position must be explicit about **sacramental union** and **spiritual presence** and also generous in recognizing the objective validity of sacraments performed with evangelical form.

A Westminster candidate must connect sacramental doctrine to adjacent loci under pressure. First, to Scripture: the sacraments are instituted ordinances and require the Word for their being and meaning. The Word is the primary **means of grace**, and the sacraments are Word visible. The annexation of promise to sign is crucial. Second, to soteriology: the sacraments communicate Christ. They do not add to justification, but they seal it and strengthen assurance. They are vital for sanctification as places where the Spirit conforms believers to Christ through humble reception. Third, to ecclesiology: the sacraments mark the church, distinguish the world from the covenant people, and order discipline. The keys of the kingdom are exercised in admitting to and excluding from the table. Fourth, to Christology and trinitarian doctrine: the mode of presence in the Supper depends on the ascension, the two natures, and the missions of the Son and Spirit. Real presence is the Spirit’s presence uniting us to the personal, heavenly Christ. Fifth, to **federal theology**: the sacraments are signs and seals of the **covenant of grace**, patterned on Abrahamic inclusion and new covenant fulfillment. This constrains our practice of infant Baptism and our rejection of paedocommunion while honoring the organic unity of the people of God.

To anticipate objections and provide crisp responses: Does a Reformed account collapse into memorialism. No, because it maintains a true **sacramental union** where the Spirit makes the signs effective instruments. The Supper communicates Christ spiritually to faith; memorialism denies communication. Does refusing **ex opere operato** make the sacrament a mere occasion for the Spirit’s work rather than an instrument. No, because God has bound himself by promise to work through these means. The sacrament is an instrument by divine institution, not a natural efficient. Does objective covenant inclusion in Baptism teach baptismal regeneration. No, because the sign and the thing signified are joined by promise not by identity. Baptism is a real sealing of promise and incorporation into the visible church; regeneration is the Spirit’s sovereign act, sometimes concurrent with Baptism, often not. What about apostasy after Baptism. Apostasy is a betrayal of covenant signs and a profanation of holy things. It proves that the person never had the res in the manner of the elect. Yet the sacrament was not vain; it stood as a true means of grace resisted by unbelief. Why admit infants to Baptism but not to the table. Because the signs have different structures. Baptism is initiatory, sealing membership and promise; the Supper is nutritive, requiring self-examination and discernment. This difference is biblical and not arbitrary. Are weekly communions required. The apostolic pattern suggests frequent communion, and the fact that the Supper is a **means of grace** strongly favors weekly observance where possible, with proper catechesis and discipline. Can extraordinary circumstances justify adaptations. In times of plague or persecution, elders must prudently apply the elements of sacramental action without abandoning their bodily, ecclesial nature. Necessity does not create new sacraments.

The defended position is thus clear. The sacraments of Baptism and the Lord’s Supper are instituted by Christ as **means of grace** and are true signs and seals of the **covenant of grace**. They are annexed to the Word and are effective means for the elect by the Holy Spirit’s operation, conveying Christ and his benefits, strengthening faith, and building up the church. The efficacy of the sacraments does not flow from their performance or from the holiness of the minister but from the Spirit who uses them as instruments in accordance with the divine will. In the Supper, Christ is really present by the Spirit and is truly communicated to the faithful, who feed on him by faith. In Baptism, God truly seals forgiveness and covenant inclusion, promising the Spirit’s grace in his time. This position avoids Roman **ex opere operato** and Lutheran ubiquity, and equally refuses Zwinglian memorialism. It safeguards Christology by insisting on the ascension and the integrity of the two natures. It safeguards trinitarian doctrine by attributing efficacy to God’s undivided work ad extra. It safeguards ecclesiology by embedding the sacraments within the visible church’s ministry of Word and discipline. It safeguards soteriology by insisting that justification is by faith alone and that the sacraments are instruments of grace, not conditions of merit. Insofar as this account is faithful to Augustine’s objectivity, Aquinas’s measured **instrumental causality**, Luther’s promissory center, Calvin’s **spiritual presence** and union, and Turretin’s confessional clarity, it provides a coherent, historically grounded, and pastorally fruitful doctrine suitable for rigorous defense.

One may press one more challenge. If the Spirit is sovereign, why attach such value to means. Because the God who is simple and free binds himself by promise and condescends to act through means for our good. The sacraments acknowledge our embodied weakness and God’s covenantal faithfulness. To despise the means is to despise the God who appointed them. Alternatively, if the sacraments are true **means of grace**, why are many believers sustained in contexts with weak sacramental practice. God’s mercy is not limited by our failures, yet the ordinary rule of discipleship is that growth occurs under Word, sacrament, and prayer. Our doctrine must set the church to seek the ordinary, not presume upon extraordinary indulgence. For oral examiners, the judicious resolution of these pressures requires crisp commitment: the sacraments are neither empty rituals nor sacramental machines. They are Christ’s own instruments by which the Spirit grants communion with him in the church through **sacramental union**. Such is the substance of a Reformed confession, resilient under patristic scrutiny, medieval metaphysics, Reformation testing, and modern trials. It is an integrated claim about who God is, what Christ accomplished, how the Spirit works, and what the church is for, and it is precisely because of this doctrinal coherence that it can be defended with confidence.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: Sacraments are Christ-instituted signs and seals of the **covenant of grace**, annexed to the Word and used by the Spirit as **means of grace** to communicate Christ and his benefits to the elect through **sacramental union**.
- Decisive figures:
  - Augustine: visible words, sign and thing, anti-Donatist objectivity with Spirit-dependent fruit.
  - Aquinas: **instrumental causality**, triadic res analysis, careful limit to **ex opere operato**.
  - Luther: promissory center, objective efficacy, **sacramental union** with real presence, anti-magical.
  - Calvin: **spiritual presence**, union with Christ, ascension-grounded realism, covenantal infant Baptism.
  - Turretin: Reformed scholastic precision, efficacy to the elect, anti-ubiquity, fencing and paedocommunion rejection.
  - Zwingli: memorialist trajectory, sign as pledge and profession, catalyst for internal Reformed debate.
- Debates:
  - Internal: spiritual presence versus memorialism; efficacy and election; infant Baptism and paedocommunion; frequency and discipline.
  - External: Roman Catholic **ex opere operato** and Eucharistic sacrifice; Lutheran ubiquity and sacramental union; Baptist ordinance-only and credobaptism.
  - Contemporary: online communion, evangelical minimalism, ecumenical pressures.
- Defended position: Reformed confessional view that Baptism and the Supper truly and instrumentally convey Christ by the Spirit to faith, rejecting **ex opere operato**, affirming **spiritual presence**, and grounding practice in **federal theology**.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - If not ex opere operato, how are sacraments efficacious. By divine promise and the Spirit’s **instrumental causality** through the signs, received by faith.
  - Why baptize infants. Covenant inclusion and promise; the sign seals membership as a real **means of grace** proleptically.
  - Why deny Lutheran ubiquity. Ascension and two-natures Christology; the Spirit unites us to the bodily absent yet truly present Christ.
  - Is this memorialism. No, because of real **sacramental union** and Spirit-given feeding on Christ.
  - Weekly communion. Strongly commended as ordinary **means of grace**, with discipline and catechesis.
  - Online communion. Not proper sacramental action, since the signs are bodily and ecclesial acts under ordained ministry.
