### TITLE
Between benevolence and new birth: distinguishing common grace from preparatory grace

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **post-Reformation soteriology** situated within **providence and effectual calling**; intensified in 1618–1619 and 1898–1924 debates.
- Core question: What distinguishes **non-saving divine benevolence** from **grace that disposes or enables conversion**, and can either be called grace in a strict soteriological sense.
- Principal figures or schools: **Augustine and Orange 529** on prevenience; **Calvin** on civic good; **Kuyper and Bavinck** on common grace; **Old Princeton** on restraint; **Hoeksema** against common grace; **Trent 1547** and **Wesley** on prevenient grace.
- Key concepts or loci: **providence versus redemption**, **total depravity and civic righteousness**, **natural law and antithesis**, **means of grace and effectual calling**.
- Major texts and events: **Matt 5:45; Acts 14:17; Rom 2:4; Gen 8:21–22**; **Westminster 1646–1648**; **CRC Three Points 1924**; **Kuyper’s Stone Lectures 1898**; **Trent, Decree on Justification 1547**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Westminster-monergist preparation**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist culture-building**, **Roman Catholic gratia praeveniens**, **Lutheran two kingdoms**, **Wesleyan prevenient grace**.
- Legacy and debate: ongoing dispute about **natural law and public theology**, the scope of **the Spirit’s universal work**, and whether **preparatory grace** compromises monergism.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
At minimum, common grace names God’s **non-saving benevolence** that preserves, orders, and enriches creaturely life for all, while preparatory grace identifies divine actions that **precede conversion** by awakening, convicting, or disposing the sinner toward the gospel. The dogmatic location is where **providence intersects soteriology**: common grace belongs chiefly to God’s governance of the world, while preparatory grace stands at the threshold of **effectual calling and regeneration**.

Within a classic Reformed grammar, **common grace** includes the restraint of sin, bestowal of gifts, cultural achievements, and the stability of nature, without imparting **saving union with Christ**. By contrast, **preparatory grace** is a site of contestation: Rome and Wesleyans use **gratia praeveniens** for an enabling grace that heals the will to cooperate, while Reformed theology typically restricts the term to **non-meritorious preparations** under the Word and Spirit that never guarantee conversion and do not mitigate **total depravity**. The two doctrines together clarify the difference between God’s **general kindness** and God’s **special, efficacious mercy** in salvation.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Jesus describes the Father who makes his sun rise on the evil and on the good and sends rain on the just and on the unjust in Matthew 5:45, a programmatic statement of **indiscriminate divine beneficence**. Paul adds that God did not leave himself without witness, doing good by giving rains from heaven and fruitful seasons in Acts 14:17, echoing **universal providential gifts**. The Noahic covenant in Genesis 8:21–22 promises a stabilized order after the flood, undergirding the doctrine that **creation’s regularities** persist even among the unrighteous.

Yet these benefactions have a moral vector. Romans 2:4 invokes the riches of God’s kindness leading to repentance, showing that **patience and forbearance** can have a preparatory function while not guaranteeing conversion. Psalm 145:9 declares the Lord is good to all,, locating **divine kindness** within worship rather than merely within nature. First Timothy 4:10’s Savior of all people, especially of those who believe, has been read as encompassing **preserving grace for all** and **saving grace for believers**, a distinction consonant with common and special grace.

Scripture also speaks of divine operations that cut nearer to the heart. John 16:8 promises that the Paraclete will convict the world concerning sin and righteousness and judgment, suggesting a **universal convicting work** that may precede faith. Galatians 3:24’s law as our custodian to Christ portrays the law’s pedagogic function as **preparatio evangelii**, while the soils in Mark 4 illuminate how **external means** may prepare or reveal the condition of the heart. Still, total depravity stands: Romans 3:9–20 denies inherent capacity to turn, and 1 Corinthians 2:14 insists the natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit, so any true preparation must itself be **divine and non-meritorious**.

Additional texts qualify the extent and limits. Second Thessalonians 2:7–8 speaks of a restrainer, often associated with **providential restraint of lawlessness**, a staple of common grace. Titus 2:11’s grace appearing bringing salvation to all has been read as the **manifestation of Christ** extended across nations rather than an equalized individual enablement, though Wesleyans see **universal prevenience** here. John 1:9’s true light that enlightens everyone articulates a **Logos doctrine** consistent with general illumination, while maintaining that saving knowledge is given in **new birth**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
In the patristic era, apologists like **Justin Martyr** and **Eusebius** deployed *logoi spermatikoi* and *praeparatio evangelica* to argue that **seeds of the Word** were scattered among the nations. This was not yet a doctrine of common grace proper, but it valorized **creational truth and civic virtue** as providentially ordered toward Christ. Augustine advanced **gratia praeveniens** as God’s gracious initiative that heals the will, a position consolidated against semi-Pelagianism at the **Council of Orange 529**, where the church affirmed that **prevenient grace** is necessary for the beginning of faith.

The medievals distinguished **actual grace** from **habitual grace**, with Thomas Aquinas articulating that **gratuitous divine motions** precede and enable even the first steps toward God. Cultural goods were read under **general providence**, while **preparatory movements** were ascribed to *gratia praeveniens*. The Reformers recast the issues: Luther’s The Bondage of the Will in 1525 denied **synergistic preparation**, yet he retained **civic righteousness** and a **two kingdoms** framework. Calvin emphasized that the Spirit scatters **excellent gifts** even among the reprobate and that civil order is a **divine blessing**, while soteriologically he underscored **monergistic regeneration**.

Post-Reformation Reformed scholastics preserved both themes. The **Synod of Dort 1618–1619** guarded **irresistible grace** and rejected **prevenient enabling** that leaves the decisive cause in human freedom. Yet theologians like **Francis Turretin** allowed for **non-meritorious preparations** through law, conscience, and the ministry of the Word. The **Westminster Standards 1646–1648** located common grace within **providence** and spoke of **common operations of the Spirit** that fall short of salvation, a careful hedge against **preparationism** that would trade on creaturely power.

In the nineteenth century, **Old Princeton** (A. A. Hodge, B. B. Warfield) framed common grace as **restraint of sin, moral order, and intellectual illumination**, crucial for science and culture yet distinct from regeneration. The Dutch Neo-Calvinist project of **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck** developed a rich doctrine of **gemeene gratie** that grounds **culture, science, and the arts** in God’s favor to the world, articulated from the 1898 Stone Lectures and in publications 1901–1905. The **Christian Reformed Church** adopted the **Three Points of 1924** affirming favorable disposition to all, restraint of sin, and civic good, over against **Herman Hoeksema**, who denied common grace as a theological category.

Twentieth-century shifts complicated preparatory grace. **Karl Barth** rejected dualisms of nature and grace, reframing **creational preservation** christologically and preferring to speak of the **one grace of Jesus Christ** with universal reach rather than a separable common grace. In Roman Catholic theology, **Trent 1547** had already codified **prevenient and cooperating grace** in justification; later, **Henri de Lubac** in 1946 critiqued a **pure nature** hypothesis, intensifying debates over **nature-grace** relations. Wesleyan Methodism crystallized **universal prevenient grace**, asserting an **enabled but resistible** human freedom under the Spirit’s prior work.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton construed common grace as an aspect of **providence under divine goodness**. For Charles Hodge, God’s common operations maintain **moral order and rational inquiry**, rendering society livable and permitting **the external call** to be heard. Warfield tied **intellectual advancement** to restraining grace, yet insisted on **Calvinist monergism** for faith. Preparatory grace was affirmed only as the **Spirit’s common operations** accompanying the Word, which never infuse **new spiritual life** apart from regeneration.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism interpreted common grace as the **broad river of God’s favor** that keeps history from collapsing into chaos and opens space for **cultural mandate** fulfillment. **Kuyper’s sphere sovereignty** uses common grace to explain why the antithesis between church and world is not absolute in culture, even as **special grace** gathers the elect. **Bavinck** integrates **creation and redemption** without conflation, arguing that grace restores and perfects nature, so **common grace** is positive while never saving. Later, **Dooyeweerd and Vollenhoven** recast structure and direction, leveraging **common grace and antithesis** to analyze culture.

The Westminster stream binds the doctrine to confessional loci. **WCF 5** places **benevolent providence** and the restraint of sin under God’s governance. **WCF 10.4** acknowledges **common operations of the Spirit** in the non-elect. **WLC 95–161** frames the law and Word as **outward and ordinary means** God uses to prepare some by conviction, yet **WCF 10.1–2** protects **effectual calling** as solely of God’s **sovereign will**. Reformed piety therefore welcomed **awakening and conviction**, but rejected **preparationism** as a causal step toward meriting grace.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology distinguishes **gratia praeveniens** and **gratia cooperans**, teaching that God’s **actual grace** precedes and enables every salutary act. The **Council of Trent 1547** describes justification’s beginning as moved by **prevenient grace** through hearing the gospel, fear of divine judgment, and hope of mercy. This is not Pelagian, since **no preparation merits grace**, yet it retains **synergy** as the will is healed and truly cooperates. The result is a robust account of **preparatory grace**, with common grace folded into **providence and natural law** within a unified nature-grace economy.

Lutheran theology affirms **civic righteousness** and **two kingdoms**, recognizing political order, arts, and learning as **God’s gifts**. However, the **Formula of Concord 1577** repudiates **free-will preparation**, insisting that conversion is the Spirit’s work through the **Word and Sacraments**. Some Lutherans acknowledge **prevenient operations** in a strictly monergistic sense, yet remain wary that talk of **preparatory grace** can lapse into synergism. Common grace as a dogmatic term is less central than **law-gospel** and **creation-preservation** under God’s hidden yet benevolent hand.

Modern proposals vary. **Karl Barth** reframes common grace as the **presence of Christ’s lordship** in creation and history, placing **preparation** under the **mission of the Word** rather than under a nature-grace schema; he worries that common grace can legitimize **autonomous spheres** detached from Christ. Wesleyan and Pentecostal traditions teach **universal prevenient grace** that counters **total depravity’s disablement** and enables a genuine though resistible response. Eastern Orthodoxy articulates **synergy** within the **divine energies**, sometimes reading the **logoi** as a **creational preparation** toward deification, while maintaining that **illumination** becomes salvific only in **participation**.

Analytic theology presses questions about **freedom and grace**. If prevenience is universal, does **resistible grace** avoid **determinism** while preserving **responsibility**. Compatibilists construe preparatory grace as **change of dispositions** effectually bringing faith, while libertarians defend **sufficient but not efficient** grace. Epistemology invokes **sensus divinitatis** and **the noetic effects of sin** to model how **common grace** might ground rational belief while **sin’s suppression** requires **regenerating light**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
One debate centers on **natural law and public theology**: can Christians appeal to shared moral knowledge as a fruit of **common grace**, or does **antithesis** preclude neutral rationality. Neo-Calvinists invoke **common grace** to underwrite collaboration for the **common good**, while some Van Tilian presuppositionalists warn that **autonomous reason** is fiction and that common grace must be tethered to **special revelation**.

Another debate explores **religious pluralism**: are the truths and virtues found in other religions **gifts of common grace**, and can the Spirit’s **prevenient work** operate within them as **preparation for the gospel**. Inclusivists appeal to **Acts 17** and **Romans 2**, while exclusivists caution that **saving knowledge** comes only through **explicit faith in Christ**. The risk is sliding from **preparation** to **soteriological optimism** that erodes the necessity of **mission**.

Pastorally, critics fear that **preparatory grace** can reintroduce **synergism**, nurturing a spirituality of staged self-improvement. Reformed replies distinguish **conviction and awakening** as fruits of **the external call** and **common operations**, while insisting on **monergistic regeneration**. Similarly, some deny **common grace** altogether, arguing it dilutes the **antithesis**; defenders reply that Scripture commends **civic goods** and **shared rational norms** as divine kindness without confusing them with **salvation**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinitarianly, common and preparatory grace are **inseparable operations** of the triune God: the Father orders providence, the Son as **Logos** enlightens, and the Spirit **restrains and convicts**. Christologically, John 1 and Colossians 1 ground common grace in **Christ’s sustaining lordship**, while soteriologically the same Christ grants **new birth** by the Spirit. Pneumatology maps **restraining work** and **common operations** to the Spirit’s agency, while distinguishing **effectual calling** as **Spirit-wrought union**.

Anthropology and hamartiology must balance **imago Dei** and **total depravity**: the image grounds **civic capacities**, while sin’s noetic and moral damage requires **gracious initiative** even for convictions that precede faith. In providence, the **Noahic covenant** maintains **creational stability** for culture and mission. Ecclesiologically, the **means of grace** are the ordinary theater where **preparatory awakenings** occur through law and gospel, while **sacraments** seal grace only to the **regenerate**. Eschatologically, common grace sustains **cultural life** until the **consummation**, yet only **special grace** inherits the kingdom, preserving the tension between **penultimate goods** and **ultimate salvation**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Preaching should honor **common grace** by naming God’s daily kindnesses and calling for gratitude, while pressing **preparatory conviction** through the law that exposes sin and the gospel that announces **free grace**. This guards against **moralism** even as it treats **awakenings** as **gifts, not merits**. Catechesis can teach believers to recognize **civic righteousness** and **vocation** as arenas of obedience, without confusing them with **saving faith**.

Public worship can include intercessions for rulers and rain, acknowledging **providence and common good**, and petitions for the Spirit’s **awakening work** among seekers. Pastoral counseling of inquirers should welcome **signs of conviction**, clarify that such stirrings are **insufficient without Christ**, and direct them to the **means of grace**. In cultural engagement, Christians can enter collaborative projects as beneficiaries of **common grace**, while bearing witness that **new creation** comes only by **union with Christ**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Common grace sustains and summons while preparatory grace awakens and convicts, but only **effectual calling** saves, keeping **providence and redemption** distinct yet harmonized in the **triune economy**.
