### TITLE
Participation in the Second Adam: Union with Christ as the Organizing Center of Reformed Soteriology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core conceptual issue: How **union with Christ** functions as the ontological and federal bond by which all saving benefits are communicated, while preserving the Creator–creature distinction and the integrity of justification by faith alone.  
- Decisive figures: John Calvin, John Owen, Francis Turretin, Herman Bavinck, Richard Gaffin.  
- Relevant traditions: Reformed theology from the sixteenth to twenty-first centuries, with attention to patristic participatory themes as background and to Lutheran and Roman Catholic alternatives as contrasts.  
- Debates addressed:  
  - Internal: ordo salutis versus historia salutis, logical order of union and justification, nature of mystical versus federal union, relation to sanctification and perseverance.  
  - External: Roman Catholic infused righteousness, Lutheran simul and sacramental union, modern existential and liberal accounts of Christ-mysticism.  
  - Contemporary: the place of **union with Christ** in the justification debate, Federal Vision and new perspectives on Paul, and existential or therapeutic reductions of union.  
- Defended position: Union with Christ is the comprehensive, Spirit-wrought, covenantal participation in the crucified and risen Second Adam, logically prior to and the ground of every saving benefit, yet distinct from those benefits and structured by **federal theology**, **divine simplicity**, and **analogical knowledge** so as to safeguard forensic justification and classical theism.

### ESSAY
Union with Christ names the Spirit-wrought bond by which the elect sinner comes to share in the crucified and risen Christ in such a way that all the benefits of redemption are communicated to him, without confusion of natures or collapse of the Creator–creature distinction. It is a participatory relation, but a covenantal, **federal** and forensic participation in the incarnate Mediator as Second Adam, not an absorption into the divine essence. Any defensible Reformed account must keep these two poles in tension: the sheer intimacy of biblical language about being “in Christ” and Christ being “in you,” and the unyielding commitment to **divine aseity** and **divine simplicity**, according to which God remains who he is in himself irrespective of any relation to the creature. The examiners will probe whether you can keep both poles simultaneously: real participation without ontological conflation, robust union without compromising justification by faith alone.

Historically, the decisive Reformed voice on this doctrine is John Calvin. Calvin stands at the moment when the Reformation’s forensic insistence on justification by faith alone had to be integrated with the biblical language of participation. His core contention, crystallized in the Institutes book 3, is that as long as Christ remains outside of us, and we are separated from him, all that he has suffered and done for the salvation of the human race remains useless and of no value to us. Here Calvin gives **union with Christ** a structural role: it is the duplex gratia, the double grace of justification and sanctification, that comes by the Spirit’s uniting us to the whole Christ. The key work of the Spirit is to engraft us into Christ, like branches into a vine. Methodologically, Calvin is profoundly biblical and trinitarian: he proceeds by exegesis of Pauline “in Christ” texts woven into a robust doctrine of the Spirit and of Christ’s mediatorial office.

For Calvin, union is not an optional add-on to a prior legal transaction; rather, it is the matrix within which forensic justification and progressive sanctification are distinct yet inseparable operations of the one Christ. Faith is the Spirit’s instrumentally uniting bond, not a meritorious act. Calvin refuses to locate our righteousness in an inherent habitus, as Roman Catholic theology had done; yet he equally refuses to make justification a naked decree detached from Christ himself. The righteousness by which we are justified is extra nos, in Christ alone, but we possess it by being united to him. The examiner will often press: does this make justification ontologically rather than forensically grounded? Calvin’s answer, which you must reproduce, is that our righteousness is still wholly alien, located in Christ’s person and work, and is imputed to us in a clear forensic sense; union is the relational context that secures that the benefits we receive are never possessed apart from the person of the Mediator. Union does not internalize justifying righteousness as a quality, but personalizes it as a bond to the righteous One.

John Owen deepens and refines Calvin’s insights within a mature **federal theology**. Writing in a later seventeenth century context, Owen must respond both to Rome and to internal English debates about covenant, assurance, and the nature of faith. In works such as “Communion with God” and his treatises on the Holy Spirit, Owen develops a richly trinitarian account in which believers have distinct yet inseparable communion with the Father in love, with the Son in grace, and with the Spirit in comfort. Union with Christ serves as the Christological center of this communion: the Spirit’s uniting act incorporates us into Christ’s mystical body, by virtue of Christ’s federal headship as Second Adam. For Owen, this union is real and spiritual, not imaginary or merely notional; it has an ontic dimension grounded in Christ’s assumed human nature and covenantal office.

Yet Owen is perhaps more explicit than Calvin in locating union within the architecture of the covenants. As federal head, Christ stands in the place of his people in the eternal covenant of redemption and in its historical outworking in the covenant of grace. Union with Christ therefore has a forensic dimension from the outset: to be in Christ is to be legally represented by him in his obedience and satisfaction. Owen insists that there is no communication of the divine essence to believers; the union is not essential but relational and covenantal, mediated by the Spirit. Here he sharply differentiates his view from any notion of deification in a strong ontological sense. When an examiner asks whether union with Christ entails theosis, an Owenic answer will be that believers are indeed made “partakers of the divine nature” in the sense of 2 Peter 1, but only morally and relationally, through participation in Christ’s human obedience and in the Holy Spirit’s indwelling, not by participation in the incommunicable divine essence. The ontological line between Creator and creature, demanded by **divine simplicity** and **immutability**, remains intact.

Francis Turretin, representing high Reformed scholasticism, contributes decisive conceptual clarity to the logical relations between union and the ordo salutis. Turretin is acutely aware that careless language about union could appear to threaten the Reformed doctrine of justification over against Rome. Thus he draws tight distinctions. There is, first, an eternal, decretal union of the elect with Christ in the decree of election. Second, there is a redemptive-historical union with Christ as he bears their sins on the cross as federal head. Third, there is an actual, existential union formed in time, by the Spirit through faith. It is this last that is the subject of ordo salutis reflection. Turretin insists that this existential union is not temporally prior to justification but logically prior: faith as the instrument of union is simultaneously the instrument by which we receive justification. Union in this sense is correlatively ordered with justification, adoption, and all benefits. Turretin’s central thesis is that union cannot be conceived as a prior ontological change that then becomes the ground of a later forensic verdict, for that would inferentially reintroduce an infused righteousness model. Instead, **union with Christ** and justification are distinct aspects of one complex of benefits received in the same moment by faith.

At this point internal Reformed debates emerge. Some later Reformed theologians, influenced by Turretin, tend to speak of union functionally as a “package term” for the complex of benefits in their ordered relation, while others, retrieving Calvin, speak of union as the overarching existential bond that provides a Christological framework for the ordo salutis. Herman Bavinck stands here as a critical mediator. Bavinck insists that the ordo salutis is subordinate to the historia salutis. The work of Christ in his incarnate obedience, death, resurrection, and exaltation is the objective accomplishment of salvation; union is the Spirit’s application of this work, incorporating us into Christ as the eschatological life-giving Second Adam. Yet Bavinck maintains the full forensic clarity of Reformed justification. Union with Christ is both judicial and renovative: judicial, in that Christ’s righteousness is imputed to those who are in him; renovative, in that his resurrection life is communicated to them by the Spirit, producing sanctification and perseverance. Bavinck is sensitive to the charge that robust union language could blur the distinction between justification and sanctification. His response is that while both flow from union, they are distinguishable not only notionally but also in their formal nature: justification is a once-for-all declarative act of God as judge, sanctification is a progressive work of God as life-giver. The fact that both are given in union ensures that neither is ever isolated from Christ himself.

Richard Gaffin, as a contemporary Reformed theologian, presses the historia salutis dimension even further, especially with reference to Paul. For Gaffin, union with Christ is fundamentally union with the whole of Christ’s once-for-all redemptive history: believers are united to him in his death, resurrection, and exaltation. Drawing on Romans 6 and Ephesians 2, Gaffin argues that the believer’s existential participation in Christ is eschatological: the age to come has broken into the present through this union. This leads him to reframe the ordo salutis not as a sequence of discrete steps, but as the differentiated outworking of a single, comprehensive union with the crucified and risen Lord. Gaffin thereby resists any attempt to subordinate union to a preexisting ordo in which justification or regeneration stand as independent starting points. Instead, regeneration, faith, justification, adoption, and sanctification are all aspects of being united to Christ in his death and resurrection, each aspect highlighting a distinct relation to the same Christ-event.

This Gaffinian reframing has triggered internal debate. Some worry that if union is made too comprehensive and temporally coextensive with all benefits, the precious forensic distinctness of justification is obscured. Examiners will press you: does Gaffin subordinate justification to union and thereby endanger sola fide? A defensible position is to affirm Gaffin’s historia salutis insight while refusing to blur the nature of justification. Union with Christ, in the existential ordo, is Spirit-wrought through faith; justification is one benefit, distinguished by its forensic character, communicated in that same moment by virtue of that same union. The logical, not temporal, priority of union lies in the fact that it is the relation to the person of Christ from which the different relations of forensic declaration, adoptive sonship, and moral renovation flow. To say that union is the “great central benefit” is not to demote justification but to insist that justification, like sanctification, is always in Christ and never apart from him.

Having traced these pivotal figures, we can now frame the internal, external, and contemporary debates more sharply. Internally, three issues recur. First, the nature of union: is it primarily mystical, federal, or transformative? The best Reformed answer integrates all three under the primacy of Christ’s federal headship. The union is mystical in that it is a spiritual, mysterious bond wrought by the Spirit, not reducible to social or institutional relations. It is federal in that it is structured by Christ’s covenantal representation of his people. It is transformative in its effects, but that transformation is the fruit, not the ground, of the union. Second, the relation to justification: is union logically prior, simultaneous, or subsequent to justification? You should maintain the Turretin–Bavinck–Gaffin line: union and justification are simultaneous in time; union is logically prior as relation to the person from whom the forensic benefit flows, yet justification is not inferred from an intrinsic moral change, but is a distinct forensic event grounded solely in Christ’s imputed righteousness. Third, the role of faith: is faith the instrument of union or of justification? The classical Reformed answer is both, in different respects. Faith is the Spirit’s created act by which we are united to Christ and by which we receive and rest upon him alone for salvation; by that same faith we are justified, since faith is the sole instrument that apprehends Christ’s righteousness. To divide these is to tear apart what Scripture joins; to confuse them is to forget that faith, as act, is not the ground of justification.

External debates sharpen these contours. Against Roman Catholic theology, union with Christ cannot be construed as the communication of an infused habitus of charity which then becomes the formal cause of justification. The Council of Trent’s view that God justifies by making the ungodly inherently just mistakes the relation between union, sanctification, and justification. Reformed theology insists that the sanctifying, habit-forming effects of union are real, but they are never the basis of the forensic verdict. The righteousness on which that verdict rests remains wholly Christ’s, extra nos, and is communicated only by **imputation**, not by infusion, even though that imputation occurs in the context of **union with Christ**. Against Lutheran theology, a contrast must be drawn between sacramental modes of union and the broader ordo salutis function. Lutherans often focus on Christ’s sacramental presence and on the believer’s assurance grounded in the external Word and sacrament. While Reformed theology affirms real, spiritual communion with Christ in the Supper, it does not collapse the doctrine of union into sacramental union. Nor does it share every aspect of the Lutheran simul iustus et peccator emphasis, though it broadly agrees on the simultaneity. The Reformed insist that the same union that grounds justification also effectuates definitive and progressive sanctification; the dominion of sin is broken even though its presence remains. This means that union must be articulated in a way that upholds both definitive forensic declaration and genuine moral transformation.

Modern existential and liberal Protestant accounts offer yet another external foil. For some, union with Christ becomes a symbol for the religious consciousness’ self-transcendence, a way of expressing the believer’s experience of divine nearness. Others reduce union to ethical participation in Jesus’ way of life, or to the community’s corporate identification with the “Christ-event.” These moves evacuate union of its ontological and federal depth. The Reformed doctrine insists that union is first an objective, Spirit-wrought reality grounded in the once-for-all accomplishment of the incarnate Son. Subjective experience and ethical participation are real but derivative expressions of a prior bond to the resurrected Lord who, by the Spirit, dwells in believers and incorporates them into his body. The ontological integrity of the incarnate Mediator, protected by classical **Christology** and **trinitarianism**, is nonnegotiable: we are united, by the Spirit, to Christ according to his glorified human nature and to the one divine person who subsists in that nature, but we never cross the Creator–creature distinction.

Contemporary debates often circle around whether a robust doctrine of union with Christ can coexist with Protestant commitments on justification. The new perspective on Paul and the Federal Vision movement, in different ways, have leveraged participation language to relativize classic imputation. By emphasizing participation in Christ’s covenantal community or participation in his resurrection life, some have suggested that justification is no longer a central or controlling category. A Reformed response must insist that Paul’s teaching on being “in Christ” does not displace but rather grounds his teaching on justification. Because God reckons us in union with his righteous Son, he reckons Christ’s righteousness to us. Federal Vision’s tendency to locate union primarily in visible church membership and sacramental participation confuses the external administration of the covenant with its internal substance. The Spirit’s uniting work is sovereign and effectual, producing faith and perseverance; sacramental union is a divinely appointed means, not an automatic mechanism.

Another contemporary fashion is to construe union with Christ in therapeutic or psychological terms, emphasizing belonging, identity formation, or emotional intimacy, sometimes detached from the hard edges of sin, wrath, and judgment. While pastoral application properly draws comfort from union, doctrine must not be collapsed into therapy. Union with Christ is first a judicial and eschatological reality: God transfers sinners from the old Adamic realm of sin and death into the new realm of righteousness and life in Christ. This transfer is forensic in its inauguration and transformative in its ongoing effects. Under examiner questioning, you must be able to show how union simultaneously answers to guilt, corruption, alienation, and mortality, without giving away the forensic core.

Union with Christ also intersects crucially with adjacent loci. In Christology, it presupposes the hypostatic union: only if the eternal Son has assumed a true human nature and lives as the eschatological Second Adam can believers be united to him and share in his benefits. There is no union with the Logos asarkos in a way that bypasses Christ’s incarnate, crucified, and risen humanity. In pneumatology, **union with Christ** is the chief work of the Spirit in the application of redemption: the same Spirit who anointed Christ in his earthly ministry is poured out to join believers to the exalted Christ. In ecclesiology, union is the ontological basis of the church as Christ’s body, the communion of saints. The sacraments, especially baptism and the Lord’s Supper, are signs and seals of this union: baptism visibly marks incorporation into Christ’s name, and the Supper nourishes believers’ ongoing communion with his body and blood, though the mode of presence is spiritual, not local or carnal. In eschatology, union with Christ guarantees glorification: as Christ the firstfruits has been raised, so those who are in him will be raised in due order.

One common examiner objection concerns the relation of union with Christ to **divine simplicity** and immutability. If believers are united to Christ, does God change, or does some new reality come to be in God? The classical answer is that union is a relation that is real in the creature and only logically in God. God does not undergo change or composition by entering into union; rather, by the Spirit he brings creatures into a new relation to the unchanging Christ. The ontological basis of union lies in the hypostatic union that the Son, immutable in his divinity, has freely willed from eternity and executed in time. The relation of union, considered from the side of the believer, is a created effect of grace, not an alteration in the divine essence.

Another objection asks whether union with Christ entails a species of theosis. Here nuance is important. Reformed theology can affirm a qualified participation in God: believers become partakers of the divine nature in the sense of sharing in God’s communicable attributes through conformity to Christ and indwelling of the Spirit. Yet this participation is always by **analogical knowledge** and grace, never by essence. There is no ontological elevation into the divine being. Any use of theosis language must be tightly controlled by classical theism and Christology.

The position to be defended, therefore, is as follows. **Union with Christ** is the comprehensive, Spirit-wrought, covenantal participation in the crucified and risen Second Adam, the incarnate Son, by virtue of which the elect are transferred from the old to the new humanity and receive every saving benefit. This union is mystical, federal, and transformative in its effects; it is grounded in Christ’s mediatorial work in the historia salutis and applied in the ordo salutis. It is logically prior to and the ground of justification, adoption, sanctification, and glorification, yet distinct from them and never collapsible into them. Justification remains a purely forensic benefit, consisting in the imputation of Christ’s righteousness to the believer, and is received by faith alone, which is itself the Spirit’s instrument of union. Union does not internalize justifying righteousness as a habitus but secures that this alien righteousness is always possessed in and with Christ himself. The Creator–creature distinction, safeguarded by **divine simplicity** and **immutability**, remains absolute: believers do not become divine by essence, but are graciously conformed to the image of Christ and brought into filial communion with the triune God.

A candidate who can articulate this position with Calvin’s insistence on Christ’s person, Owen’s trinitarian communion and federal clarity, Turretin’s scholastic distinctions, Bavinck’s integration of ordo and historia salutis, and Gaffin’s eschatological framing will be well equipped to withstand rigorous examination. The doctrine of union with Christ will then appear not as a pious metaphor or a marginal theme, but as the organizing center of Reformed soteriology, radiating into every locus while anchored in the unchanging glory of the triune God.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: **Union with Christ** is the Spirit-wrought, covenantal participation in the crucified and risen Second Adam, by which believers are joined to his person and so receive every saving benefit, without confusion of natures or erosion of the Creator–creature distinction.  
- Decisive figures:  
  - Calvin: union as duplex gratia framework, Christ’s benefits useless unless we are in him, union as context for forensic justification and sanctification.  
  - Owen: trinitarian communion, mystical yet federal union grounded in Christ’s headship, sharp rejection of ontological deification.  
  - Turretin: scholastic clarification of logical relations, multiple “unions,” union and justification simultaneous, union not a prior ontic ground for a later verdict.  
  - Bavinck: synthesis of ordo and historia salutis, judicial and renovative aspects from one union, clear distinction between justification and sanctification within union.  
  - Gaffin: eschatological and historical framing, union with Christ in death and resurrection as central, ordo as differentiated application of one union.  
- Debates:  
  - Internal: nature of union mystical or federal or transformative, logical relation to justification, role of faith as instrument, ordo salutis versus historia salutis.  
  - External: against Rome’s infused habitus as formal cause of justification, against Lutheran reduction of union to sacramental presence, against liberal and existential reductions of union to experience or ethics.  
  - Contemporary: union in justification debates, Federal Vision’s sacramentalizing of union, existential or therapeutic accounts that minimize guilt, wrath, and forensic categories.  
- Defended position: Union is the comprehensive, Spirit-wrought bond to the incarnate and exalted Christ, logically prior to all benefits and their common source, while justification remains a distinct, purely forensic benefit of that union, grounded in imputed righteousness and received by faith alone, all under the constraints of **classical theism**, **federal theology**, and **analogical knowledge**.  
- Likely examiner questions and one-line answers:  
  - Does union with Christ threaten forensic justification?  
    - No, union is the relational context within which an alien righteousness is imputed; it does not make inherent renewal the basis of the verdict.  
  - Is union with Christ the same as theosis?  
    - Only in a very qualified sense of participatory conformity and communion, never as participation in the divine essence.  
  - Is union temporally or logically prior to justification?  
    - Existentially simultaneous, but logically prior as relation to Christ’s person from which the forensic benefit of justification flows.  
  - How does union relate to faith?  
    - Faith, wrought by the Spirit, is the sole instrument by which we are united to Christ and by which we receive justification in him.  
  - How does union preserve the Creator–creature distinction?  
    - Union is a created, Spirit-given relation to the incarnate Son; God remains simple and immutable, and no creature ever shares in the divine essence.
