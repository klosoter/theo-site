# Westminster Standards (1646–1647)

The **Westminster Standards** provide a definitive statement of the **Reformed doctrine of justification by faith alone**, emphasizing that **faith** is the sole instrument of justification and that the **imputed righteousness of Christ** is the only ground of acceptance before God. The Standards draw a sharp distinction between **justification** and **sanctification**, rejecting any role for works in the act of justification.

- "Faith, thus receiving and resting on Christ and his righteousness, is the alone instrument of justification." (Westminster Confession of Faith 11.2)

- Westminster Larger Catechism
- Westminster Shorter Catechism
- The Sum of Saving Knowledge
- The Directory for Public Worship

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Justification by Faith Alone**
    - Justification is an act of God’s free grace, not based on any works or inherent righteousness.
    - Faith is the only instrument by which Christ’s righteousness is received.
    - Works have no role in the ground or instrumentality of justification.
    - Assurance of justification rests on Christ’s finished work, not covenantal faithfulness.
    "Faith, thus receiving and resting on Christ and his righteousness, is the alone instrument of justification." (WCF 11.2)

2. **Imputation of Christ’s Righteousness**
    - Christ’s obedience and satisfaction are imputed to believers.
    - The believer’s own works or faithfulness are not counted as righteousness.
    - Imputation is forensic, not transformative.
    - The ground of acceptance is entirely external to the believer.
    "He freely justifieth... by imputing the obedience and satisfaction of Christ unto them." (WCF 11.1)

3. **Distinction Between Justification and Sanctification**
    - Justification and sanctification are inseparably joined but distinct.
    - Justification is a legal declaration; sanctification is a work of God’s Spirit within.
    - Good works are the fruit and evidence of faith, not the basis of justification.
    - Confusing these undermines the gospel’s integrity.
    "Although sanctification is inseparably joined with justification, yet they differ..." (WCF 13.1)

4. **Covenant of Grace and the Role of Faith**
    - The **Covenant of Grace** offers salvation by faith in Christ alone.
    - Faith is required, but as an instrument, not a meritorious cause.
    - The promises of the covenant are received by faith, not by covenantal faithfulness or works.
    - The sacraments confirm faith but do not confer justification apart from it.
    "The grace of faith... is the work of the Spirit of Christ in their hearts." (WCF 14.1)

5. **Rejection of Works as Instrumental in Justification**
    - No works, even those done in faith, contribute to justification.
    - The believer’s obedience is a response to, not a condition of, justification.
    - Any teaching that makes works instrumental in justification is condemned.
    - Assurance is grounded in Christ’s work, not in the believer’s covenantal performance.
    "God doth continue to forgive the sins of those that are justified... yet they can never fall from the state of justification." (WCF 11.5)

## Legacy and Impact

The **Westminster Standards** have served as a cornerstone of **Reformed orthodoxy** on justification, profoundly shaping Presbyterian and broader Protestant theology. Their precise articulation of **justification by faith alone** has provided a lasting defense against movements that conflate faith and works in justification. The Standards remain a vital benchmark for confessional integrity and doctrinal clarity in ongoing theological debates.
