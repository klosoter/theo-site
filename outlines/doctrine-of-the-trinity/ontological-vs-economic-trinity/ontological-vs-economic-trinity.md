### TITLE
One God in Three, the one who is eternally triune and the one who acts toward us, without confusion and without separation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: fourth century conciliar settlement to twentieth century retrievals within **Theology Proper** and **Pneumatology**.  
- Core question: how to relate the **ontological Trinity** to the **economic Trinity** so that God’s eternal life and God’s saving acts are neither divided nor collapsed.  
- Principal figures or schools: **Athanasius** and the Cappadocians defend *homoousios* and eternal generation, **Augustine** and **Aquinas** articulate processions and missions, **Calvin** and Reformed scholastics clarify **Calvins autotheos** and **inseparable operations**, **Rahner** formulates **Rahners Rule**, **Barth**, **Moltmann**, **Zizioulas**, and analytic philosophers debate models.  
- Key concepts or loci connections: **divine simplicity**, **eternal generation and procession**, **perichoresis**, **inseparabile operum**, **taxis** of persons, and **pactum salutis** linking Trinity and **Soteriology**.  
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: Nicaea 325 and Constantinople 381, **Augustine De Trinitate** 400–420, **Aquinas Summa Theologiae I 27–43** 1265–1274, **Calvin Institutes** 1559, **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647, **Council of Florence** 1439 on the **filioque**, **Rahner The Trinity** 1967, **Moltmann The Trinity and the Kingdom** 1980.  
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: classic Reformed retrieval of **inseparable operations** and **eternal generation** versus **social trinitarianism** and **process theologies** adjusting **divine immutability**.  
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: enduring questions about **eternal functional subordination**, **divine simplicity** and **Rahners Rule**, and the coherence of **Latin** versus **social** models.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The distinction between the **ontological Trinity** and the **economic Trinity** marks two inseparable aspects of one doctrine, God as God is in the eternal divine life and God as God acts in creation and redemption. The classical rule insists that God’s **being and act** are coherent, yet we must not identify temporal missions with eternal processions in a way that erases **divine freedom**. The ontological Trinity concerns *theologia*, the eternal **processiones ad intra** of the Son by **eternal generation** and the Spirit by eternal **spiration**, as named by **Aquinas**. The economic Trinity concerns *oikonomia*, the **missiones ad extra** whereby the Father sends the Son and Spirit in history for our salvation, as witnessed in the gospel and apostolic writings.

**Rahners Rule** states that the economic Trinity is the ontological Trinity and vice versa, which guards against speculation that would detach God’s saving self-revelation from God’s reality. Yet, in classical **Theology Proper**, this identity is analogical rather than univocal, since **divine simplicity** and **aseity** mean God is not conditioned by temporal processes. The dogmatic location of this distinction sits at the heart of **Trinitarian doctrine**, with implications for **Christology** through the missions and for **Pneumatology** through the Spirit’s indwelling.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture discloses the **economic Trinity** in the missions of the Son and Spirit, yet intimates the **ontological Trinity** through names, relations, and divine acts. The Shema, Deuteronomy 6:4, affirms **monotheism**, while the Johannine prologue, John 1:1–18, reveals the Word as *pros ton Theon*, with the Word being God, grounding **homoousios**. Jesus speaks of the Paraclete whom the Father will send in his name, John 14:26, and of sending the Spirit from the Father, John 15:26, language that later bears on **filioque** and the Spirit’s procession.

Paul’s benediction, 2 Corinthians 13:14, and the baptismal formula, Matthew 28:19, give a triadic pattern for **baptism and doxology**, signifying shared divine name and work. Galatians 4:4–6 and Romans 8:9–11 present the **mission of the Son** and the **mission of the Spirit** as the Father’s saving action, which the scholastic tradition reads as **mission grounded in procession**. Philippians 2:6–11 confesses the Son’s equality with God and his assumed servanthood, preserving **impassible love** without denying the real history of the cross. Hebrews 1 portrays the Son as the radiance of God’s glory, a text central for **eternal generation** and **inseparable operations**, since the Son creates and sustains all things with the Father.

A canonical pattern emerges. The one God of Israel acts through the Word and Spirit, a unity of **essence and operation** with a diversity of personal relations. The missions unveil what is eternally true, yet they do so in a form adapted to history, so **Rahners Rule** must be read through the biblical depiction of **mission-procession coherence** without collapsing the *ad extra* into the *ad intra*.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
At Nicaea in 325 and Constantinople in 381, the church confessed the Son’s **consubstantiality** with the Father and the Spirit’s full deity. **Athanasius**, against Arius, argued that only one who is **homoousios** with the Father can deify us, linking **Soteriology** to **Theology Proper**. The Cappadocians, **Basil**, **Gregory of Nazianzus**, and **Gregory of Nyssa**, refined the distinction between *ousia* and *hypostases*, showing that one **divine essence** subsists in three persons, a framework that safeguards **inseparable operations** and the **taxis** within God.

**Augustine**, in De Trinitate 400–420, deepened the account of **processions and missions**, teaching that temporal missions reveal eternal processions and that operations *ad extra* are indivisible. Augustine’s psychological analogies are not the core but serve the grammar that one **simple God** acts undividedly, while personal relations are real. In the medieval period, **Aquinas**, Summa Theologiae I 27–43, articulated how **mission** presupposes **procession**, so that the Son is sent because he is eternally begotten, while the Spirit is sent because he eternally proceeds, thus protecting both **divine freedom** and real **historical revelation**.

The West’s confession of the **filioque** gained conciliar status at Florence in 1439, claiming the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son as from one principle. The East maintained the monarchy of the Father and rejected Latin formulations that seemed to compromise **personal taxis**. In the Reformation, **Calvin**, Institutes 1559, defended **Calvins autotheos** for the Son with respect to essence, while maintaining **eternal generation** with respect to personal mode, thereby supporting **inseparable operations** without subordinationism. Post-Reformation scholastics like **Turretin**, 1679–1685, stabilized the classic distinction, rooting missions in processions and stressing **appropriations** as pedagogical signs of the one undivided work.

Modernity raised new pressures. **Hegelian** and historicist currents tempted theologians to read the **ontological Trinity** as emerging through history. **Barth**, Church Dogmatics I–IV beginning 1932, affirmed **revelation and being** identity while upholding **freedom** and **simplicity**, though his language of *modes of being* prompted debate. **Rahner**, The Trinity 1967, framed **Rahners Rule** to preclude a gap between God and God-for-us, which later thinkers like **Moltmann**, 1980, **Zizioulas**, 1985, and **Jenson**, 1997, extended in more social or history-shaped directions, sometimes pressing hard on **impassibility** and **immutability**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
In the Reformed tradition, the ontological and economic distinction is held together by **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations**. **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647 confesses one God in three persons, the Son eternally begotten and the Holy Spirit eternally proceeding, with the divine decrees and the work of redemption proceeding from the Father through the Son by the Spirit. This grammar aligns the **pactum salutis** with the **taxis** of persons without positing three wills, since classic Reformed divinity affirms a single **divine will** in three hypostases.

**Old Princeton** exemplified this catholic Reformed stance. **Warfield**, The Biblical Doctrine of the Trinity 1915, argued that Scripture yields one **divine essence** and triune personal distinctions, and that the economy reliably reveals the **ontological Trinity** without implying change in God. **Hodge**, Systematic Theology 1871–1873, repeated the Augustinian rule that operations *ad extra* are indivisible and that personal actions are those of the one God according to **personal properties**. This school stood firmly against any speculative subordination, while maintaining **eternal generation** and the **filioque**.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** stream, especially **Bavinck**, Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901, showcased a richly synthetic account. Bavinck held **divine simplicity** and **perichoresis** together, insisting that God’s **immanent life** is the archetype of all communion yet is utterly self-sufficient. He linked **creation and covenant** to the **Trinity**, developing the **pactum salutis** as an economic expression of the **ontological taxis**, not as a contract among three centers of will. **Kuyper**, Lectures on Calvinism 1898, located culture and churchly life within the overflow of **triune grace**, without sliding into **social trinitarianism**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic ressourcement drew from **Augustine** and **Aquinas**, with **Rahners Rule** functioning as a hermeneutical safeguard that the God who reveals is the God who is. **De Lubac** and **Congar** advanced a participatory vision where **grace and deification** relate to the missions of the Son and Spirit. Twentieth-century Catholic theology often stressed **mission-procession identity** while retrieving **divine simplicity** and **impassibility**, though debates continue about how to speak of **divine suffering** without compromising aseity.

Lutheran scholastics like **Chemnitz** and **Gerhard** affirmed **consubstantiality** and **inseparable operations**, while their Christology pressed on the communion of attributes. The **genus majestaticum** risked being misread as a trinitarian redistribution of divine attributes, which classic Lutherans rejected, since the trinitarian **taxis** is not modulated by the **incarnation**. Lutheran liturgy and catechesis maintained the confession of **eternal generation** and **procession**, anchoring **baptismal practice** in Matthew 28:19.

Modern constructive proposals vary. **Barth** links revelation and **immanent Trinity** while rephrasing person as **mode of being**, anxious to avoid tritheism. **Moltmann** treats the cross as an event in the **trinitarian life**, emphasizing mutual indwelling and **history of God**, which risks conflating the **economic** with the **ontological**. **Zizioulas** stresses **personal ontology** and **communion**, giving prominence to the Father’s monarchy and **ecclesial being** as icon of the Trinity. In analytic philosophy, **Leftow** defends a Latin model under **divine simplicity**, **Swinburne** and **Hasker** sketch more **social trinitarian** frameworks, and **relative identity** accounts attempt to reconcile numerical oneness with threefoldness. These models debate whether **Rahners Rule** is semantic, metaphysical, or both, and how **inseparable operations** constrain divine action talk.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Three debates dominate recent discussion. First, the retrieval of **eternal generation** and **inseparable operations** after 2010 has been motivated by fears that **social trinitarianism** dissolves the one God into a committee of wills. Writers like **Gilles Emery**, **Matthew Levering**, and **Adonis Vidu**, The Same God Who Works All Things 2021, defend classical **simplicity** and undivided operations while honoring the missions as revelatory of **processions**.

Second, the controversy over **eternal functional subordination** erupted between 2014 and 2016, with **Ware** and **Grudem** proposing asymmetrical eternal relations of authority. Critics from Reformed and evangelical circles, including **Goligher**, **Trueman**, and **Barrett**, argued that such claims compromise **homoousios**, contradict **Calvins autotheos**, and undermine **inseparable operations**. The resolution among many confessional theologians has been to affirm the **taxis** grounded in eternal relations of origin, while rejecting novel hierarchical structures in the **ontological Trinity**.

Third, philosophers and theologians dispute **Rahners Rule**. Some fear it invites collapse of **immutability** by reading historical suffering back into God, while others warn that without it, **revelation** becomes opaque. A careful articulation keeps the rule as a soteriological and epistemic anchor while maintaining **analogical identity** between the economic missions and the eternal processions. Objections about biblical silence regarding **processions** are answered by showing Scripture’s **mission language**, when read with the whole canon, does disclose eternal relations, as in John 1 and 15 and Hebrews 1.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
In **Christology**, the missions ground the incarnation and atonement. The Son who is eternally begotten is the one who becomes man, so **communicatio idiomatum** rests on the unity of person, not a mingling of natures, preserving **impassible divinity** and passible humanity in the one Christ. The Spirit’s mission at Pentecost reveals the one who eternally proceeds, so **Pneumatology** receives its shape from the **ontological Trinity**, not merely from ecclesial experience.

In **Soteriology**, union with Christ and adoption are participations in the **economic Trinity**, which communicates the fruits of the **ontological Trinity**. The Father elects in the Son by the Spirit, and the **pactum salutis** echoes the **taxis** without multiplying wills. In **Ecclesiology**, the church is created by the **triune Word and Spirit**, a communion that images **perichoresis** analogically. Baptism into the triune name and the Eucharistic epiclesis pattern worship after **inseparable operations**.

In **Anthropology**, the image of God has relational and rational aspects that reflect, by analogy, **triune life**, though classical doctrine resists a one-to-one mapping that would imply **social trinitarianism**. In **Eschatology**, the beatific vision is communion with the **triune God**, seeing the Father through the Son in the Spirit, which confirms that the **economic path** consummates in the unveiled **ontological glory**. In **Bibliology**, Scripture as a spirited Word is the **mission of the Spirit** delivering the **mission of the Son**, a trinitarian economy rooted in the eternal **processions**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the distinction and unity of the **ontological** and **economic Trinity** secure assurance. God does not put on a mask in salvation, since the one who justifies us in Christ is the God who is eternally Father, Son, and Spirit. Preaching the gospel announces the **mission of the Son** and the **gift of the Spirit**, thereby inducting believers into the triune communion that proceeds from the **ontological life** of God. This grounds prayer to the Father, through the Son, in the Spirit, a doxological pattern that embodies **inseparable operations** and **taxis**.

Liturgically, historic worship centers on **baptism into the triune name** and the Eucharist prayed in a trinitarian pattern. Hymnody confesses **consubstantial glory**, creeds rehearse **eternal generation** and **procession**, and pastoral care rests in **divine immutability** rightly understood as the steadfastness of love. Mission is not a human program but participation in the **missions of the Son and Spirit**, which extend the Father’s purpose to bless the nations. The church’s unity and diversity reflect, by analogy and under creaturely limits, the **perichoretic peace** of the triune God.

Catechesis should form believers to distinguish without dividing. Children learn that the Son is eternally begotten, not made, that the Spirit eternally proceeds, and that the works of God toward us are one **undivided act** with personal manifestations. This catechesis guards against **modalism** and **tritheism**, and it stabilizes the soul in the confidence that the God who will glorify us is the God whose glory already is **triune and simple**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The one God who is eternally Father, Son, and Holy Spirit is the same God who saves us through the missions of the Son and Spirit, so the **ontological Trinity** and the **economic Trinity** must be confessed together without confusion and without separation.
