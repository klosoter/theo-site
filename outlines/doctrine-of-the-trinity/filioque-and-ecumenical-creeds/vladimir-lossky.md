# Vladimir Lossky (1903–1958)

Vladimir Lossky was a leading Orthodox theologian who vigorously opposed the **Filioque** addition to the **Nicene Creed**, arguing that it distorts the original **Trinitarian theology** of the **ecumenical councils**. He emphasized the necessity of preserving the **monarchy of the Father** and maintained that the **procession of the Holy Spirit** from the Father alone is essential to Orthodox doctrine and the unity of the Church.

- "The Filioque, by modifying the doctrine of the monarchy of the Father, introduces a latent modalism into the doctrine of the Trinity." (The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church)

## Key Works

- The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church
- In the Image and Likeness of God
- Orthodox Theology: An Introduction
- "The Procession of the Holy Spirit" (essay in In the Image and Likeness of God)
- "Theology and Mysticism in the Tradition of the Eastern Church"

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Rejection of the Filioque Clause**
    - Insists that the **Holy Spirit** proceeds from the **Father alone**, as stated in the original **Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed**.
    - Views the Filioque as a theological innovation that disrupts the balance of **Trinitarian relations**.
    - Argues that the Filioque undermines the unique role of the Father as the sole source (**arche**) within the Trinity.
    - Sees the addition as a cause of division between **Eastern and Western Christianity**.
    "The Filioque is not a mere addition to the Creed, but a radical alteration of the doctrine of the Trinity." (The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church)

2. **Primacy of the Ecumenical Creeds**
    - Upholds the authority of the **ecumenical councils** and their creeds as the definitive expression of Christian faith.
    - Emphasizes the unalterability of the **Nicene-Constantinopolitan Creed**.
    - Critiques unilateral changes to the creed as breaches of ecclesial unity.
    - Argues that fidelity to the original creeds is essential for true **catholicity**.
    "The Creed, as defined by the councils, is the common property of the undivided Church." (The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church)

3. **Monarchy of the Father**
    - Asserts that the Father is the sole **arche** (source) in the Trinity.
    - Maintains that the Spirit’s procession from the Father alone preserves the distinctiveness of each **hypostasis**.
    - Warns that the Filioque risks collapsing the personal distinctions within the Godhead.
    - Links the monarchy of the Father to the integrity of **Trinitarian doctrine**.
    "The Father is the sole principle, the unique cause of the Son and of the Spirit." (The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church)

4. **Theological Method: Apophaticism**
    - Advocates for an **apophatic** (negative) approach to theology, especially regarding the mystery of the Trinity.
    - Cautions against rationalizing or systematizing the inner life of God.
    - Sees the Filioque as an example of over-speculation about the divine processions.
    - Emphasizes the limits of human language in expressing **Trinitarian relations**.
    "The mystery of the Trinity cannot be reduced to human categories or logic." (The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church)

5. **Ecclesiological Implications**
    - Connects **Trinitarian doctrine** to the unity and catholicity of the Church.
    - Argues that doctrinal changes like the Filioque have ecclesiological consequences.
    - Sees the preservation of the original creed as vital for **ecumenical dialogue**.
    - Believes that true unity requires a return to the faith of the undivided Church.
    "The restoration of unity demands a return to the common faith of the first ecumenical councils." (In the Image and Likeness of God)

## Legacy and Impact

Lossky’s robust defense of the **monarchy of the Father** and critique of the Filioque have shaped modern Orthodox engagement with Western theology and ecumenical dialogue. His writings remain influential in discussions of **Trinitarian theology** and the authority of the **ecumenical creeds**. Lossky’s insistence on fidelity to the original formulations of the faith continues to inform Orthodox responses to doctrinal developments and calls for Christian unity.
