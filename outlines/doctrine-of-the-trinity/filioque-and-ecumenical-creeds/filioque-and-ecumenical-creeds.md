### TITLE
The Filioque, Procession, and the Creedal Boundaries of Trinitarian Monarchy

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Fourth to fifteenth centuries, locus in Trinity and Pneumatology with ecclesiological implications concerning creed authority.
- Core question: Does the Holy Spirit proceed eternally from the Father and the Son, and may that be confessed within an ecumenical creed.
- Principal figures: **Augustine** constructive Latin source, **Cappadocians** Eastern balance of hypostases, **Photius** critic of Filioque, **Aquinas** Latin synthesis, **Calvin** Reformed reception, **Gregory Palamas** Eastern developments, **Council of Florence** attempted reconciliation.
- Key concepts: **relations of origin**, **monarchy of the Father**, **procession and mission**, **one principle and one spiration**, **through the Son** as mediating formula, links to Christology and Ecclesiology.
- Major texts and events: **Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed 381**, **Third Council of Toledo 589**, **Aachen 809**, **Rome liturgical insertion 1014**, **Schism 1054**, **Lyons II 1274**, **Florence 1439**, modern clarifications 1995 and 2003.
- Reformed and alternatives: Reformed confessions adopt Filioque while many modern Reformed omit the clause liturgically for ecumenical use; Roman Catholic double procession as one principle; Orthodox through the Son.
- Legacy: Continuing debate over semantics, authority, and pneumatology with proposals for ecumenical convergence.

### ESSAY
The Filioque names the clause and the Son and identifies a contested claim about the Holy Spirit’s eternal procession. It belongs to the doctrine of the Trinity, specifically the ontology of hypostatic relations and their grammar within the ecumenical creeds. It also touches Pneumatology in the doctrine of the Spirit, Christology in the relation of Son and Spirit, and Ecclesiology in questions of creed formation and reception. The Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed of 381 confesses the Spirit who proceeds from the Father. The Western church later confessed and the Son. The theological task is to ask what procession signifies, whether the Spirit has an eternal relation to the Son that warrants confessional articulation, and whether the unilateral insertion belongs in liturgical recitation of an ecumenical symbol.

Biblically, the key text is John 15.26. Jesus says the Paraclete proceeds from the Father. The Greek verb ekporeuetai, from ekporeusis, in Johannine usage marks ultimate origin. This is the biblical basis for the Eastern insistence that the Father alone is the source or archē of the Spirit. Yet the same Johannine corpus binds the Spirit to the Son. In John 16.14 to 15 the Spirit will take what is mine and declare it to you, and everything that the Father has is mine. John 20.22 narrates the risen Christ breathing on the disciples and saying Receive the Holy Spirit. This sanctified exegesis connects to Genesis 2.7 and Psalm 33.6 By the word of the Lord the heavens were made and by the breath of his mouth all their host. Pauline texts speak of the Spirit of Christ in Romans 8.9 and the Spirit of his Son in Galatians 4.6, where God sends the Spirit into our hearts, crying Abba, Father. Revelation 22.1 images the river of the water of life proceeding from the throne of God and of the Lamb. The triadic missions are clear: the Father sends the Son and Spirit, and the Son sends the Spirit from the Father, as in John 15.26 and 16.7. The dogmatic question is how the economic missions reveal the immanent processions. Western theology reads the missions as faithful revelations of eternal relations, with the Latin procedere covering both ekporeusis of origin and proienai of going forth through the Son. Eastern theology distinguishes strictly, reserving ekporeusis for the Father alone and allowing a through the Son in the economy and perhaps in an eternal manifestation without implying a second principle of origin.

Historically, the biblical material was received through different linguistic lenses. The Cappadocians in the 370s to 390s defended the full divinity of the Spirit while guarding the monarchy of the Father. **Basil of Caesarea** in On the Holy Spirit defends the doxology Glory to the Father with the Son together with the Holy Spirit and points to the Spirit’s being from God without fixing a double origin. **Gregory of Nazianzus** speaks of the Spirit as proceeding from the Father and receiving from the Son, a phrase that later Westerners hear as consonant with procession through the Son. **Gregory of Nyssa** holds the Spirit’s origin from the Father and through the Son language, mapping an asymmetrical order of relations or taxis. **Augustine** in De Trinitate, composed roughly 399 to 419, develops the Latin synthesis. He calls the Spirit the Gift and the Love of the Father and the Son and argues that the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son as their mutual love and common gift. The Latin procedere does not force the Greek reserve of ekporeusis. Augustine’s influence spread in the West through the early medieval period.

The Filioque entered a creed text in the Iberian context. The Third Council of Toledo in 589 adopted the clause to combat Arianism among the Visigoths. Frankish usage in the Carolingian era pressed the issue into controversy, especially at Aachen in 809, where theologians defended Filioque against Greek objections. Rome resisted adding the clause in the creed recited at the altar for centuries, even while Latin theologians taught the doctrine. Under **Pope Benedict VIII**, the clause was introduced into the Roman liturgy in 1014 at the coronation of Henry II. Between 867 and 879, the **Photian** controversy sharpened the polemics. **Photius of Constantinople** in his Mystagogy of the Holy Spirit attacked the Filioque as introducing two principles and violating the creed. The formal rupture of 1054 did not occur only because of this doctrine, yet the clause is emblematic of the broader rift. Reconciliation attempts at Lyons II in 1274 and Florence in 1439 culminated in Laetentur Caeli, the Decree for the Greeks, which confesses that the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son as from one principle and by one spiration and adds that the phrase through the Son also expresses the same truth. The union was not received in the East beyond limited contexts. The Latin West continued to confess the Filioque, now with a carefully articulated single-principle formula designed to preserve the monarchy of the Father while affirming the Son’s eternal role.

The ecumenical creeds frame this debate. The Apostles Creed is silent on procession. The Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed of 381 contains from the Father only. The Athanasian Creed, a Latin symbol from the late fifth or early sixth century, explicitly affirms the Spirit is from the Father and the Son. The Council of Ephesus in 431 forbade composing a different faith than that of 381, which the East later invoked against Western insertion. The West replied that it confessed the same faith and that local creedal expansions in the Latin liturgy did not legislate for the whole church. The reception of the clause in Rome in 1014 made the divide more acute.

Reformed theology received the Latin tradition while refining its biblical grounding and ecumenical posture. **Calvin** in the Institutes Book I, chapter 13, affirms the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son and defends the Augustinian reading of the missions. He warns against speculative prying and insists on Scripture’s economy revealing the eternal truth without collapsing categories. The **Belgic Confession 1561**, Article 11, states that the Holy Spirit proceeds eternally from the Father and the Son, who is neither made, nor created, nor begotten, but only proceeds from them both. The **Second Helvetic Confession 1566** confesses the Spirit as the true eternal God, proceeding from the Father and the Son. The **Westminster Confession 1646**, II.3, declares that the Holy Ghost is eternally proceeding from the Father and the Son. Old Princeton theologians, such as **B. B. Warfield**, defended the exegetical case by focusing on the Spirit’s designation as Spirit of the Son and the mission from the Son in John 15 and 16. Dutch Neo-Calvinists such as **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck** cemented the Augustinian-Florentine line that the Father remains the sole fountain of Deity while the Son, eternally begotten, together with the Father is the one spirative principle of the Spirit. Many contemporary Reformed churches retain the doctrine, teach the Florence formula, and have adopted ecumenical practice of reciting the Nicene Creed without the Filioque in contexts of wider communion while catechetically maintaining the doctrine of double procession under the Augustinian semantics of procedere.

Alternative accounts frame the spectrum. Roman Catholic doctrine is settled by Florence and the magisterial teaching that the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son as from one principle and by one spiration. **Thomas Aquinas** argues that the personal property of the Spirit is passive spiration that has its source in the Father and the Son in virtue of their consubstantiality and the Son’s reception of all that the Father has. The Lutheran tradition, codified in the Book of Concord in 1580, uses the Western text of the Nicene Creed with the Filioque and confesses a classical Augustinian pneumatology. Eastern Orthodox theology rejects the Filioque as creed clause and resists the ontological claim that the Spirit has origin in the Son. **John of Damascus** speaks of the Spirit proceeding from the Father and resting in the Son and of an eternal shining forth through the Son. **Gregory Palamas** in the fourteenth century secures the monarchy and the freedom of the Spirit by distinguishing essence and energies and retains a through the Son mode without double origin. Modern Orthodox voices such as **Vladimir Lossky** and **John Zizioulas** stress the monarchy of the Father as personal cause and the primacy of hypostasis over nature, judging the Filioque as a doctrinal distortion that jeopardizes personal primacy and ecclesial unity. In the twentieth century, the Roman Catholic church issued clarifications in 1995 that distinguish Latin procedere from Greek ekporeusis and permit Eastern Catholics to recite the creed without the Filioque. The North American Orthodox-Catholic Consultation in 2003 urged that the Filioque no longer be a church-dividing issue and recommended the use of the 381 creed text in ecumenical worship.

Modern theology and analytic accounts revisit the logic of procession. **Karl Barth** describes the Spirit as the love and fellowship of Father and Son, echoing Augustine, yet resists any ontological derivation that would subordinate the Spirit, insisting instead on the triune perichoresis and coordinate deity. **Karl Rahner** argues that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity and the immanent Trinity is the economic Trinity, a rule that presses the Western intuition that the missions disclose real eternal relations. **Jürgen Moltmann** counsels against Filioque as metaphysically rigid and proposes a communion model that emphasizes the Spirit’s freedom and eschatological character. Analytic theologians debate whether double procession entails two causes. Latin theology answers with one principle and one spiration, with the Father and Son as a single joint spirative cause, a position defended by contemporary analytic Thomists. Some propose a minimal ecumenical settlement that confesses the Father as sole ekporeusis while admitting an eternal through the Son that avoids talk of a second principle.

Current debates and objections revolve around three nodes. First, the authority of creed alteration. The East claims that the West violated conciliar prohibition. The West responds that the Filioque is not a different faith but an explication necessitated by heresy and that its later universalization was a matter of reception. The wiser ecumenical practice has been to use the 381 text in common recitation while allowing the doctrine to be taught within Western churches. Second, the semantics of procession. Greek ekporeusis is stricter than Latin procedere. A promising convergence states that the Spirit ekporeuetai from the Father alone and eternally proienai through the Son, while in Latin terms the Spirit procedit a Patre Filioque ut ab uno principio. Third, pneumatological marginalization. Critics argue that the Filioque contributed to the eclipse of the Spirit in the West. Defenders reply that Augustine’s theology celebrates the Spirit’s personal distinctiveness as Gift and Love and that the doctrine binds soteriology and ecclesiology to the Spirit’s agency without rendering him derivative or subordinate.

Systematically, the Filioque touches the whole dogmatic field. Trinity and Christology are linked because the Son’s eternal relation to the Spirit grounds his messianic anointing and his role as sender in the economy. Pneumatology and Soteriology are tied because the Spirit who unites believers to Christ is the Spirit of the Son, conforming them to the image of the firstborn. Ecclesiology is implicated because unity in the Spirit is the ground of catholicity and because creed reception tests synodality and primacy. Sacramentology bears the mark in the epiclesis and in the Lord’s Supper, where communion is fellowship with the Father through the Son in the Holy Spirit. Eschatology is joined because the Spirit is the arrabōn, the pledge of the coming glory, who proceeds from the Father and rests upon the Son and is poured out on all flesh. Anthropology is not untouched, since the image of the triune God in rational, relational creatures is animated by the Spirit who draws them into filial cry, Abba, Father.

Pastorally and liturgically, clarity is crucial. Catechesis should teach that the Father remains the sole fountain of deity, that the Son is eternally begotten, and that the Spirit eternally proceeds from the Father and the Son as from one principle. This guards the monarchy of the Father and the consubstantial communion of the three. Preaching on John 14 to 16 should articulate how the Son who receives all from the Father sends the Spirit to glorify the Son, not as a subordinate messenger but as the personal Love who brings the triune life to the church. Doxologically, East and West can both honor biblical idiom: Glory to the Father through the Son in the Holy Spirit, and also Glory to the Father and the Son with the Holy Spirit. Liturgically, many Western churches now recite the Nicene Creed without the Filioque when worshiping with Eastern Christians, not as doctrinal surrender but as an act of reception of the 381 text for common prayer. In confessional and catechetical settings, they teach the doctrine in the Augustinian-Florentine form. Hymnody such as Veni Creator Spiritus and Come, Holy Ghost can be explicated to show how the Spirit’s mission reveals his eternal relation.

In closing, the Filioque dispute presses a precise claim about the eternal relation between the Son and the Spirit and a sobering question about the authority of ecumenical symbols, and the path forward lies in exegetical sobriety, historical humility, and dogmatic charity that honors the monarchy of the Father and the reciprocity of Son and Spirit for the life of the church.

### RECAP NOTES
- Setting: Fourth to fifteenth centuries, Trinitarian and Pneumatological doctrine framed by the ecumenical creeds and the authority of creed reception.
- Core focus: Whether the Spirit proceeds eternally from the Father and the Son and whether that belongs in the Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed.
- Figures: **Augustine** De Trinitate supports double procession, **Cappadocians** safeguard monarchy with through the Son motifs, **Photius** Mystagogy rejects Filioque, **Aquinas** argues one principle and one spiration, **Calvin** receives Augustinian line, **Palamas** sustains monarchy with essence-energies distinction.
- Concepts: **relations of origin**, **monarchy of the Father**, **procession versus mission**, **procedere versus ekporeusis**, **through the Son**, **one principle and one spiration**.
- Turning points: Toledo 589 insertion, Aachen 809 defense, Rome 1014 liturgical adoption, Schism 1054 emblematic divide, Lyons II 1274 and Florence 1439 union attempts with one-principle formula, modern clarifications 1995 and 2003.
- Reformed distinctives: Confessional affirmation of Filioque in Belgic 1561, Helvetic 1566, Westminster 1646, Augustinian-Florentine semantics, ecumenical practice of reciting the 381 creed without the clause.
- Legacy: Ongoing debate over semantics, creed authority, and pneumatology, with convergences around Father as sole ekporeusis and eternal through the Son, and liturgical strategies for common confession.
