# Abraham Kuyper (1837–1920)

Abraham Kuyper, while not directly engaging with **Van Til’s one person language**, laid foundational ideas in **Reformed epistemology** and **Trinitarian theology** that influenced later discussions. Kuyper emphasized the **antithesis** between belief and unbelief and the sovereignty of God in all spheres, shaping the context in which Van Til’s formulations arose. His approach to **theological method** and the relationship between **revelation** and human knowledge provided a backdrop for critiques and developments in **Reformed apologetics**.

- "There is not a square inch in the whole domain of our human existence over which Christ, who is Sovereign over all, does not cry: 'Mine!'" (Lectures on Calvinism)

## Key Works

- Lectures on Calvinism
- Principles of Sacred Theology
- Encyclopedia of Sacred Theology
- Common Grace (De Gemeene Gratie)
- Pro Rege

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Sovereignty of God over all life**
    - God’s rule extends to every aspect of creation, including theology and philosophy.
    - All human knowledge is subject to divine authority and revelation.
    - Theological language must reflect God’s comprehensive sovereignty.
    - This principle undergirds Kuyper’s critique of any anthropocentric theological method.
    - "There is not a single area of life in which the lordship of Christ does not assert itself." (Lectures on Calvinism)

2. **Antithesis between belief and unbelief**
    - Human thought is fundamentally divided by the presence or absence of regeneration.
    - Theological language and categories are shaped by one’s spiritual state.
    - Kuyper’s antithesis challenges attempts to use neutral philosophical categories for divine mysteries.
    - This commitment indirectly critiques any reductionist language about the Trinity or personhood.
    - "The regenerate and unregenerate move in two different spiritual worlds." (Principles of Sacred Theology)

3. **Organic inspiration and revelation**
    - Revelation is not mechanical but organically connected to the whole of reality.
    - Theological language must respect the organic unity of Scripture and doctrine.
    - Kuyper’s view resists overly simplistic or reductionist formulations of divine mysteries.
    - This perspective informs later critiques of Van Til’s “one person” language as potentially flattening Trinitarian distinctions.
    - "The inspiration of Scripture is organic, not mechanical." (Encyclopedia of Sacred Theology)

4. **Doctrine of the Trinity as mystery**
    - The Trinity is a revealed mystery that transcends human categories.
    - Kuyper warns against rationalizing or over-systematizing the doctrine of God.
    - Theological language must preserve the mystery and majesty of the triune God.
    - This commitment stands behind concerns about any language (such as “one person”) that might obscure the Trinity’s mystery.
    - "The Trinity is not a problem to be solved, but a mystery to be adored." (Lectures on Calvinism)

5. **Sphere sovereignty and theological method**
    - Each sphere of life, including theology, has its own God-given integrity.
    - Theological language must arise from within the sphere of faith, not imposed from philosophy.
    - Kuyper’s method resists importing alien concepts (like “person” from modern philosophy) into theology.
    - This provides a framework for critiquing Van Til’s adaptation of “person” language for the Godhead.
    - "Every science has its own principle, and theology is no exception." (Principles of Sacred Theology)

## Legacy and Impact

Kuyper’s **theological method** and emphasis on the **antithesis** profoundly shaped the context for 20th-century Reformed theology, including Van Til’s apologetics. His insistence on the sovereignty of God and the organic unity of revelation provided resources for both the development and critique of Van Til’s “one person” language. Kuyper’s legacy endures in ongoing debates about the proper use of philosophical categories in **Trinitarian theology** and the preservation of mystery in speaking about God. His influence remains significant in discussions of **Reformed epistemology** and the relationship between faith and reason.
