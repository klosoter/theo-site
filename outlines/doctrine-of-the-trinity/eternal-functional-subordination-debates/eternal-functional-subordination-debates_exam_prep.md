### TITLE
Authority, Will, and the Trinity: Why Eternal Functional Subordination Collides with Nicene Orthodoxy

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: Whether an eternal hierarchy of authority and submission among the divine persons can be affirmed without violating **divine simplicity**, **single will**, **relations of origin**, and **inseparable operations**.
- Decisive figures: Gregory of Nazianzus, Augustine, Herman Bavinck, Wayne Grudem, Bruce Ware, Kevin Giles.
- Traditions: Nicene patristic trinitarianism, medieval and Reformed classical theism, contemporary evangelical theology.
- Debates:
  - Internal: authority and taxis within the Trinity, retrieval of **eternal generation**, single vs multiple wills, use of economy to infer immanent relations.
  - External: social trinitarianism and tritheism, subordinationism old and new, Arian echoes.
  - Contemporary: complementarian ethics and trinitarian analogy, scriptural hermeneutics of obedience texts, confessional boundaries.
- Position defended: Eternal Functional Subordination is a category mistake that contradicts **divine simplicity**, **single will**, and **inseparable operations**; authority asymmetry belongs to the economy and to Christ’s human will, while ad intra distinctions arise solely from **relations of origin**.

### ESSAY
The phrase Eternal Functional Subordination names a proposal that within the eternal life of the triune God there exists an asymmetry of authority, such that the Father eternally has authority over the Son, and the Son eternally and willingly submits to the Father, all without implying any inferiority of essence. Its defenders insist that this is a functional rather than ontological distinction and that it preserves the equality of persons. Its critics counter that such an eternal relation, if placed ad intra, reshapes the doctrine of God at precisely those points the Nicene tradition guarded with maximal vigilance. The question before an examiner is not merely whether Scripture speaks of the Son’s obedience but whether the conceptual grammar of **divine simplicity**, **single will**, **relations of origin**, and **inseparable operations** can admit an eternal authority structure without reconfiguring the metaphysics of the Trinity. The claim here is decisive: it cannot, and for that reason EFS stands outside classical trinitarianism.

To see why, we must begin with the Nicene architecture. Gregory of Nazianzus, preaching in Constantinople amid the closing stages of the Arian and Eunomian conflicts, articulated a grammar of divine unity and triune distinction that still governs orthodoxy. For Gregory the monarchy of the Father is real, but it names the Father as unoriginate source with respect to **relations of origin**, not a dominion of will or authority over the other persons. The Son is from the Father by **eternal generation**, the Spirit from the Father through the Son, but all three are one God, possessing one nature, one power, one glory, and one operation. The orations insist that taxis, or order, does not imply inequality. The Son is second with respect to order, not nature. When Scripture speaks of sending, being sent, or learning obedience, Gregory reads such texts in the light of the missions and the incarnation. They refer to the economy, not to a change or hierarchy in the **immanent Trinity**. This hermeneutic, which distinguishes what is said of the Son according to the form of a servant from what is said according to the form of God, blocks any inference from economic obedience to eternal submission. Gregory’s goal was not to silence the missions but to secure the confession that the operations are undivided, the will is one, and the personal distinctions arise solely from origination, not from authority structures.

Augustine consolidates and systematizes this in De Trinitate. He builds from Scripture a conceptual account in which the persons are coequal and coeternal, distinguished only by **relations of origin**, and he presses two hinges that are fatal to EFS when misapplied. First, the doctrine of **inseparable operations** claims that all external works of God are indivisible because they are the works of the one undivided nature. The Father sends, the Son is sent, but the one God acts in every work. Augustine interprets sending as a relation of mission grounded in procession, not a relation of authority grounded in superior and inferior agency. Second, Augustine’s mature exegesis of texts like John 14 and First Corinthians 15 depends on Christ’s two states and two wills. When Jesus says the Father is greater than I, this refers to the incarnate economy, to the Son in the form of a servant. When he obeys, he obeys according to the human will, not the divine. In light of **dyothelitism** and the **hypostatic union**, the obedience of Christ remains fully real and salvific without supplying a template for an eternal submission in God. Augustine thus provides the conceptual path to honor every biblical assertion of the Son’s obedience while avoiding the collapse into a unilateral divine hierarchy.

Herman Bavinck carries this Nicene Augustinian framework into modernity with clarity and precision. He insists on **divine simplicity**, **single will**, and **inseparable operations** as the load-bearing beams of Christian theism. For Bavinck the only eternal differences among the persons are the personal properties of paternity, filiation, and spiration, which are modes of subsistence that do not introduce composition or hierarchy into the divine essence. He is explicit that any language of subordination must be confined to the economy of salvation and that it is dangerous to import social or political structures into the **immanent Trinity**. In Reformed Dogmatics he rebukes both tritheistic social models and unitarian reductions. On missions he affirms that being sent reveals order grounded in **relations of origin**. The Son’s being sent does not imply a superior and subordinate divine agency but a taxis that manifests eternal generation in the economy. Bavinck also shines light on the ontology of will in God. Because of **divine simplicity**, God’s will is identical with his essence. To posit distinct streams of authority among the persons would implicitly posit distinct wills or modes of willing in a way that threatens unity. That Rubicon cannot be crossed without revising classical theism.

Into this well-settled field stepped a contemporary evangelical proposal. Wayne Grudem advanced a sustained argument that Scripture itself teaches an eternal relation of authority and submission between Father and Son. In his Systematic Theology and in essays connected to debates about gender, Grudem contends that calling the Father Father and the Son Son already implies authority and submission. He catalogs passages that speak of the Son seeking the will of the Father, doing the Father’s will, and being sent, and he argues that these are not merely economic. Grudem maintains the ontological equality of the persons and denies that EFS implies Arianism or ontological subordination. He has at points wavered on the status of **eternal generation**, but his later work attempts to affirm it alongside EFS, reinterpreting generation as consistent with, even explanatory of, an eternal taxis of command and submission. The animating concern is exegetical fidelity and the desire to ground complementarian ethics in the very life of God without surrendering orthodoxy.

Bruce Ware gives the most sustained theological exposition of this model. In Father, Son, and Holy Spirit and other works, Ware argues that eternal authority and submission distinguish the persons and underwrite the missions. He emphasizes the Father’s primacy, the Son’s eternal filial obedience, and the Spirit’s role in exalting the Son. Ware insists that there is one divine will but that this one will is hypostatically expressed in ways that fit the personal properties. Authority and submission then name ordered expressions of the one will. He also ties the theology to pastoral and ethical application, suggesting that the harmony of order and equality in the Trinity provides an analogical basis for male headship and female submission in church and family. Ware rejects charges of Arianism and pleads that his proposal preserves the Athanasian pattern of equality in power and glory while clarifying the concrete personal relations.

Kevin Giles became the principal public critic of EFS inside evangelical theology. In works devoted to the Trinity and to the doctrine of the eternal generation of the Son, Giles argues that EFS is a novel addition to orthodoxy driven not by the Nicene heritage but by the desire to buttress complementarian ethics. Giles collects historical evidence that the fathers, medievals, and Reformers rejected any eternal subordination within the Godhead, even when they used economic language of subordination. He warns that the distinction between functional and ontological subordination collapses inside a **simple** God with one will, and he reads EFS as reintroducing the logic of subordinationism under a new label. Giles’s public interventions forced a broad retrieval of **eternal generation**, including by some who had earlier questioned it, and helped to turn the debate toward metaphysical first principles rather than isolated proof texts.

At stake are three layers of debate that examiners will press. Internally, within the evangelical and Reformed world, opinion divides on whether a taxis of authority is admissible ad intra. Some complementarians defend EFS, while others, including many confessional Reformed theologians, reject it as inconsistent with the Westminster Confession’s claim that the three are equal in power and glory. The internal debate also encompasses the retrieval of **eternal generation** and **procession** as the only legitimate eternal distinctions, and whether there is a **single will** in God or three personal wills. EFS has sometimes been aligned with a three-wills account, though its careful proponents deny this. That denial is crucial, for three wills would produce tritheism. The orthodox position maintains one will, personally subsisting in three hypostases, which rules out an eternal command relation.

Externally, the debate interfaces with social trinitarianism and with subordinationism. Social trinitarianism often depicts three centers of consciousness or agency in a community of love. EFS can be drawn into this orbit by its emphasis on interpersonal authority structures, risking an accidental tritheism where persons are agents alongside the essence. Classic subordinationism posited ontological inferiority. EFS disavows that, yet by positing a necessary, eternal pattern of command and obedience, it incarnates the old concern under a functional guise. The classical response points back to **inseparable operations**, where the one simple God is the single agent of every external work, and to **relations of origin** as the only adequate source of eternal taxis. Any other differentiation threatens to reify social roles within God.

Contemporary pressures complicate the picture. Some have used EFS to provide a direct analogy between the triune life and complementarian ethics. Others argue such analogies are theologically reckless, as if the ad intra taxis was a template for human roles. Many retrievalists urge a decoupling: whatever one concludes about gender roles, the Trinity should not be recruited as an ethical proof text. Confessional boundaries enter here. To confess equality in power and glory means more than denying ontological inequality. It means the refusal to place structures of command and submission into the eternal life of the one God, because such structures presuppose differentiated agency and will. In preaching and catechesis, the pastoral implications are weighty. The obedience of the Son is central to the gospel, but it is the obedience of the incarnate Son according to his human will. The doctrine of **dyothelitism** protects both atonement and orthodoxy.

The principal charge against EFS is a conceptual one. It misconstrues the relation of economy to immanent life by making a real eternal property out of what is a fitting order of mission grounded in **relations of origin**. The missions reveal the processions, but they do not unmask new immanent features. The Father sends the Son because the Son is from the Father, not because the Son is eternally subordinate to the Father’s will. This is how Augustine and the Cappadocians interpret Scripture’s language. In John the Son repeatedly speaks of doing the Father’s will. The classical interpreter says these are economic appropriations. The Son, having assumed our nature, lives and dies in perfect human obedience. The obedience is real and supreme, yet it does not instantiate a divine relation of submission running from eternity to eternity.

EFS also collides with **divine simplicity** and the **single will**. Authority and submission are relations irreducibly ordered to commands issued and obeyed. Commands presuppose a will that is superior and a will that is subordinated. In a simple God the will is numerically one and identical with the essence. There is no surplus room for an irreducible authority structure among hypostases that share one will. Attempts to rescue EFS by speaking of one will hypostatically expressed fall short, for they relocate authority into the personal manner of willing without any rational purchase on what would make one hypostasis necessarily command and another necessarily obey if the will is one. The classical account already assigns ordered manners of subsistence by **relations of origin**, which sufficient account explains missions without introducing a new relation. To add authority is to add.

A third collision is with **inseparable operations**. If the Father commands and the Son obeys eternally in their common act, then either the operation can be distributed into parts of commanding and parts of obeying, which is separable, or the distinction is verbal only. The Nicene fathers insisted that all God does ad extra is done by Father, Son, and Spirit as one. The missions, too, are undivided operations with distinct personal notes. The Father sends, the Son is sent, the Spirit prepares, but they effect one work with one power. That is an order of manifestation, not superior and inferior agency.

Proponents of EFS raise important exegetical objections. What of First Corinthians 15, where the Son hands over the kingdom to the Father and is subjected so that God may be all in all. Augustine and the catholic tradition answer that this is the mediatorial kingdom of the incarnate Son as last Adam. Subjection here names the eschatological telos of the economy in which the Son’s messianic office reaches its goal and is handed over to the Father, never implying that the Son as Son is subordinate to the Father ad intra. What of headship in First Corinthians 11. Head can mean source or authority depending on context. Even if authority is in view, Paul speaks of the economy and of Christ incarnate as head of the church, not of an eternal authority structure. What of the repeated Johannine sending and obedience. Again, the economy is the frame, and the Son’s human obedience is the mode.

Another frequent defense is that EFS is merely functional. The persons are equal in power and glory but relate in order. The classical reply is not that order as such is impossible but that the only eternal order that is not an ontological degradation is the order of **relations of origin**. Functional authority adds something more than origin. It establishes a relation in God that is neither essence nor relation of origin nor mission. That is why the fathers refused it. Gregory of Nazianzus judged that saying the Son is from the Father suffices to explain why the Son is sent. Adding submission adds an alien category. Augustine insisted that any subordination language must be placed in the form of a servant or else one risks denying equality. Bavinck observes that subordinating the Son in any sense ad intra almost inevitably leads toward social trinitarianism or kenoticism, both of which reject **divine simplicity**.

Here the role of Giles as critic and retrievalist is instructive. By forcing the question, he pushed evangelicals back to **eternal generation**. Grudem and Ware both moved to affirm generation more clearly, which is a welcome development, yet their project still redefines the personal properties by adding authority as a necessary personal note. The patristic and Reformed streams never identified paternity with authority over the Son. Paternity is begetting. Filiation is being begotten. A father can command a son in time, but the eternal Father does not command the eternal Son. To say otherwise is to mistake the economic analogy for an immanent truth.

An examiner will stress the unity of this topic with adjacent loci. If EFS were true, how would it square with Christology. The council of Chalcedon and the sixth council’s confession of two wills ground the Son’s obedience in his human will. If we posit an eternal divine submission, we either diminish the salvific weight of the human obedience or we double the obedience in two registers, inviting confusion. Soteriology also is at stake. The Son learned obedience and became the source of salvation as the new Adam. That obedience is meaningful because it is voluntary for the man Christ Jesus, not because his deity supplies an eternal template of submission. Ecclesiology is in view insofar as the rule of Christ is mediated through the Spirit to the church. Ethics are related but should not be built upon a speculative account of ad intra authority.

This does not leave us without an account of biblical order. The Father is monarch by **relations of origin**. The Son is eternally begotten and thus eternally from the Father, not after him. The Spirit proceeds and perfects, manifesting the fullness of triune life in creation and redemption. Missions express this order. The Father sends the Son and the Spirit, yet not as a superior using inferiors but as the one God revealing himself in ordered ways. All the works of God are undivided. This framework is strong enough to receive every obedience text and every sending text without collapsing into an eternal command structure. It also allows us to separate the Trinity from contemporary gender debates without silencing those debates on their own biblical terms.

One might still ask whether the language of authority could be carefully used ad intra as a mere semantic variation on taxis. The historical record is clear. The fathers and the schoolmen refrain. Order never means subordination within God. The West’s appropriation of missions as manifestations of processions aims precisely to prevent such category mistakes. Any talk of authority in the Trinity is at best a pedagogy of appropriation or, more safely, a description of the economy. Thus a confessional Protestant, committed to **divine simplicity**, **single will**, **eternal generation**, and **inseparable operations**, should reject EFS as an immanent structure.

To anticipate a final objection, does rejecting EFS imply denying that Scripture can analogically inform ethics. No. It insists that analogies must respect **analogical knowledge**. We reason from the Trinity to creation by analogy, not by univocity. The analogies that survive are those that follow relations of origin and missions, not those that import a hierarchy into God. The Son’s incarnate obedience is a proper basis for Christian discipleship, and Christ’s headship over the church is authoritative over both men and women. That is sufficient ethical freight without pinning gender roles on a speculative trinitarian authority model.

The history of the doctrine urges caution and courage. Gregory and Augustine forged a grammar that both honors Scripture and protects divine unity. Bavinck retrieved that grammar for modern theology. Grudem and Ware, seeking to uphold biblical authority and complementarian convictions, introduced a novel layer of immanent authority; Giles and many others compelled the field to return to the Nicene and Reformed sources. The result, at its best, is increased clarity. The doctrine of the Trinity requires us to locate authority and obedience within the economy and within the human will of Christ, to confess eternal distinctions only by **relations of origin**, and to guard **divine simplicity**, **single will**, and **inseparable operations** as nonnegotiables of Christian confession. By doing so, we avoid both Arian echoes and social trinitarian drift, and we honor the biblical presentation without confusion of persons or division of substance.

Thus the defended position is not a retreat from Scripture but fidelity to its catholic reading. Eternal Functional Subordination imposes an alien relation ad intra. The orthodox alternative confesses the Father, Son, and Spirit as one God, equal in power and glory, distinguished by **eternal generation** and **procession**, manifest in ordered missions, and united in one will and one operation. On that basis a candidate can answer objections concisely, refuse false analogies, and defend the Trinity with the conceptual precision Westminster examiners will expect.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: EFS claims an eternal authority-submission relation within the **immanent Trinity**, allegedly functional not ontological; classical orthodoxy denies any ad intra subordination beyond **relations of origin**.
- Decisive figures:
  - Gregory of Nazianzus: taxis grounded in **relations of origin**; missions economic; no eternal subordination.
  - Augustine: **inseparable operations**, **single will**, economic obedience tied to the incarnation and two wills.
  - Herman Bavinck: **divine simplicity**, **single will**, and **eternal generation** rule out ad intra authority structures.
  - Wayne Grudem: defends EFS from exegesis and applies it to complementarian ethics; later affirms eternal generation alongside EFS.
  - Bruce Ware: systematizes ERAS, emphasizing eternal filial obedience and ordered personal roles.
  - Kevin Giles: critic and retrievalist, argues EFS revives subordinationism and distorts Nicene grammar.
- Debates:
  - Internal: single vs multiple wills, retrieval of **eternal generation**, admissibility of ad intra authority, confessional boundaries.
  - External: social trinitarianism and tritheism pressures, subordinationism old and new, Arian echoes.
  - Contemporary: use of Trinity in complementarian ethics, exegesis of obedience and sending texts, pastoral catechesis.
- Defended position: EFS is incompatible with **divine simplicity**, **single will**, and **inseparable operations**; authority-submission belongs to the economy and to Christ’s human will; eternal distinctions are only by **relations of origin**.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - How do you read John 14 and First Corinthians 15? Economic obedience and mediatorial subjection, not ad intra hierarchy.
  - Does the Father’s monarchy entail authority over the Son? Monarchy names **relations of origin**, not a chain of command.
  - Can one will have hypostatic expressions of command and submission? No, that would add a new ad intra relation beyond origin and compromise **simplicity**.
  - Are missions evidence of eternal subordination? Missions manifest processions; they do not introduce authority structures.
  - Must complementarianism rely on EFS? No; ethics should not be grounded in speculative ad intra relations but in clear biblical mandates.
