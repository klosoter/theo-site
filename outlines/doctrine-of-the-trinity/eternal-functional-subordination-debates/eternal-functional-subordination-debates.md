### TITLE
Eternal Relations and One Will: Testing EFS against Nicene Orthodoxy

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **twenty-first century evangelical trinitarianism** under the locus of **Theology Proper** with implications for **Christology** and **Anthropology**.  
- Core biblical or theological question: whether the Son eternally relates to the Father by **functional subordination** without compromising **homoousios** and the **single divine will**.  
- Principal figures or schools: **Wayne Grudem** and **Bruce Ware** advocating EFS or ERAS; **Liam Goligher**, **Carl Trueman**, **Fred Sanders**, **Matthew Barrett**, and **Kevin Giles** critiquing it as non-Nicene; **CBMW complementarianism** as a contextual driver.  
- Key concepts or loci connections: **eternal generation of the Son**, **inseparable operations**, *ad intra* versus *ad extra*, **taxis without hierarchy**, and **Rahners Rule**.  
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **Nicene Creed 325** and **Constantinople 381**; **Calvins autotheos** in 1559; **Aquinas Summa Theologiae I**; **2016 evangelical Trinity debate**; **Grudem Systematic Theology 2nd ed. 2020**; **Ware Father, Son, and Holy Spirit 2005 revised**.  
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Old Princeton** and **Westminster orthodoxy** retrieve **eternal generation** and **one will**; **Dutch Neo-Calvinism** emphasizes **taxis** and **mission**; **Roman Catholic** and **Lutheran** accounts refuse eternal authority hierarchy, while **social trinitarian** models vary.  
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: ongoing assessment of **gender complementarian appeals to the Trinity** and the coherence of **ERAS** with **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The debate over **Eternal Functional Subordination** concerns whether the Son eternally and personally relates to the Father in an order of authority and submission that is functional, not ontological, within the one divine nature. Proponents claim the Son is equal in **essence** yet eternally under the Father's authority, often reframed as **Eternal Relations of Authority and Submission**. Critics argue that to posit a timeless hierarchy of authority collapses the distinction between *ad intra* processions and *ad extra* missions and risks denying **one divine will**.

Placed within **Theology Proper**, the issue touches **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations** and immediately affects **Christology** and **Anthropology**. If the Son's filial willing is eternally subordinate, the relation of **filiation** is recast as authority-relation rather than **eternal generation**, and the dynamics of obedience in the economy are projected into the **immanent Trinity**. The stakes are Nicene and catholic: the **homoousios** of 325 and the taxis confessed by 381 are the boundaries within which the debate must be conducted.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
At the exegetical level, EFS proffers texts like John 5.19, 14.28, and 1 Corinthians 15.28 to argue an eternal authority relation. John 5.19 speaks of the Son doing only what he sees the Father doing, which the fathers glossed under **inseparable operations** and **eternal generation**, not authority-subordination. John 14.28, the Father is greater than I, was read by **Athanasius** as a statement of mediatorial economy with respect to the incarnate state, not a denial of **homoousios**. In 1 Corinthians 15.28, when the Son hands over the kingdom to the Father so that God may be all in all, the tradition construes a terminal eschatological act within the **economic mission**, not a window into an eternal submission.

Contested lexemes include *kephale* in 1 Corinthians 11.3 and *hupotasso* in Christological passages like Luke 2.51. EFS often reads *kephale* as authority rather than source and extends that reading to the **immanent Trinity**, while critics employ **Rahners Rule** to insist the economic submission of the incarnate Son reflects but does not reproducibly mirror the eternal **relations of origin**. The Johannine *monogenes* is central: the Son is only-begotten, not inferior, and his reception from the Father concerns **eternal generation** and **procession**, not an eternal ranking of wills.

A classic biblical control is the doctrine of **one power and one will**. In Matthew 28.18 the risen Christ receives all authority as the second Adam, which the tradition assigns to the **mediatorial office**, not to a preincarnate subordination. Likewise the baptismal name of Matthew 28.19 and the high-priestly prayer of John 17 show **taxis without hierarchy**, with mission from the Father through the Son in the Spirit, grounding liturgical life while safeguarding **divine simplicity**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
From **Nicaea 325** through **Constantinople 381**, the church settled the Arian dispute by affirming the Son as **homoousios** with the Father and distinguishing the persons by **relations of origin**. **Athanasius** and the **Cappadocians** insisted that the taxis is Father-Son-Spirit, yet without subordinating divinity or will. They articulated **inseparable operations** and the shared divine energy, reading Scriptures of obedience as economic manifestations of the eternal **filiation**, not signals of an authority gradient.

In the West, **Augustine's De Trinitate** developed a robust doctrine of **one will** and **one operation**, locating distinction in the eternal begetting and spiration. Obedience belongs to the incarnate mission and is indexed to the mediatorial form. Medievals such as **Thomas Aquinas** codified this in Summa Theologiae I, treating **processions** as relations constitutive of persons, and rejecting any real priority of authority within the essence. For Thomas the Father is principle without principle, yet no person wields authority over another in the **immanent Trinity**, since authority presupposes command and compliance, which presuppose potency and act that are inapplicable to the simple God.

The **Reformed orthodox** in the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries reaffirmed this tradition. **Calvins autotheos** asserted that the Son as to his deity has divinity from himself, while still from the Father as to person, thereby clarifying **eternal generation** and resisting ontological subordination. Confessions such as the **Westminster Confession of Faith 1646–1648** confessed equal in power and glory while acknowledging order of persons. Debates about the **pactum salutis** - the covenant of redemption - in the late seventeenth century articulated an eternal counsel concerning the Mediator without positing multiple divine wills, typically speaking of **appropriation** rather than intratrinitarian command and obedience.

The modern period saw **social trinitarian** proposals that emphasized relationality and sometimes implied coordination rather than **inseparable operations**. Critics of EFS argue that such modern currents form the background that makes an eternal authority model thinkable. The **2016 evangelical Trinity debate** surfaced these tensions, as scholars retrieved **eternal generation** and **one will** against the proposal of **ERAS**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
At **Old Princeton**, **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield** defended **eternal generation** and the unity of divine will, arguing that all divine acts are the act of the undivided essence. Warfield warned against reading creaturely social analogies into God, thereby upholding **inseparable operations** and a robust **taxis without hierarchy**. For Old Princeton, the economy reveals the order of persons but never institutes an eternal authority-submission within the Godhead.

**Dutch Neo-Calvinism** through **Abraham Kuyper** and especially **Herman Bavinck** maintained the catholic consensus. Bavinck's Reformed Dogmatics, developed 1895–1901 and in English 2003–2008, teaches that the Father is the fountain of deity, the Son **eternally generated**, and the Spirit **eternally proceeding**, yet in the essence there is **one will** and **one power**. Bavinck concedes an economic subordination correlative to the mediatorial office but denies any eternal authority hierarchy, linking this to **divine simplicity** and the doctrine of **perichoresis**.

Within the **Westminster** tradition, catechetical and confessional materials confess equality in glory and an order of subsistence. Reformed scholastics like **Francis Turretin** explain the **pactum salutis** as a free decree of the one God, appropriated to Father and Son according to **taxis**, not a contract between discrete centers of will. **John Owen** can speak of the Son's consent to the covenant yet frames this within the **mediatorial economy**, preserving the **unity of will** in God and the voluntariness of the incarnate obedience of the God-man.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The **Roman Catholic** tradition, from **Augustine** through **Aquinas** to contemporary ressourcement voices like **Gilles Emery**, rejects any eternal authority-submission. It grounds personal distinction in **relations of origin**, insists on **one divine will**, and reads the economic obedience of the incarnate Son as proper to the assumed nature. **Rahners Rule** remains a guardrail: the economic reveals the immanent, yet not in a one-to-one mapping of economic roles to eternal properties.

**Lutheran orthodoxy** likewise affirms **homoousios**, **inseparable operations**, and the single will of God, while its Christology stresses the genus majestaticum and communication of idioms in the person of Christ. While some modern Lutheran kenotic strands have flirted with divine self-limitation, confessional Lutherans reject **kenoticism** that would locate submission in the divine nature. Obedience is mapped onto the **state of humiliation** and the life of the God-man, not onto eternal relations of authority.

In the **modern** period, **social trinitarianism** in **Jürgen Moltmann** and **John Zizioulas** accentuated interpersonal communion, sometimes suggesting quasi-coordination rather than the traditional Latin stress on **simplicity**. Though not endorsing EFS, such models can be read as congenial to eternal relational structuring. **Analytic theology** has debated triune consciousness and will, with **Richard Swinburne** and **William Hasker** entertaining models that approach three centers of consciousness, while **Brian Leftow** and **Jeffrey Brower-Michael Rea** defend monotheistic identity accounts. Across these debates, the question of **one will or three** remains decisive for EFS, since an eternal authority structure presupposes distinguishable volitional acts.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The **2016 Trinity debate** catalyzed by blogs and conferences brought **Wayne Grudem** and **Bruce Ware** into sustained dialogue with **Liam Goligher**, **Carl Trueman**, **Fred Sanders**, **Matthew Barrett**, and **Kevin Giles**. Critics charged that EFS or **ERAS** is incompatible with **divine simplicity** and **eternal generation**, edging toward **subordinationism**. In response, Ware and Grudem clarified that they affirm full equality of essence and, in later editions - Grudem 2020 and Ware's revisions after 2012 - affirmed **eternal generation**, but retained a claim of eternal authority relations.

A central objection is the doctrine of **one will**. The catholic tradition denies a real composition of will in God; wills are properties of natures, so the Trinity has one will. EFS seems to require either distinct volitional properties or some kind of personal ordering of the one will that amounts to a command-obedience form, which critics say introduces potency and imperfection into the **immanent Trinity**. If the Father commands and the Son complies eternally, the relation resembles lord-servant rather than **begetter-begotten**, thereby redefining **filiation**.

Exegetically, critics insist that 1 Corinthians 15.28 refers to the eschatological obedience of the God-man returning the kingdom to the Father, that John 5.19 exhibits **inseparable operations**, and that *kephale* in 1 Corinthians 11.3 can be read as source rather than **authority**. Theologically, **Rahners Rule** is invoked: the missions reveal the processions, but the economic obedience must be located in the incarnate economy. Moreover, the **pactum salutis** is not a treaty between parties with discrete wills; it is the eternal decree appropriated personally. Proponents reply with the category of **eternal taxis**, yet their language of authority and submission often appears to critics as a category mistake.

A further pressure point is the appeal to **gender complementarianism**. Some EFS arguments used the Trinity as an archetype for male-female relations, pressing 1 Corinthians 11.3 and Ephesians 5 into a trinitarian template. Many theologians across the spectrum warned against deriving **anthropological norms from intratrinitarian life**, because analogy cannot bear that load and risks idolatrous projection. The pastoral concern mingles with dogmatic caution, urging retrieval of **eternal generation** and **inseparable operations** as protective norms.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Within **Trinity**, EFS leans on **taxis** but threatens to redefine it as hierarchy, which collides with **divine simplicity** and the classic claim of **one will**. In **Christology**, the obedience of the Son is best grounded in the **hypostatic union** and **mediatorial office**, where the God-man obeys according to the human will and perfectly enacts the one divine will in the economy, not as a window into a hierarchical **immanent Trinity**.

In **Soteriology**, the **pactum salutis** and **inseparable operations** assure us that redemption is the work of the triune God, appropriated to the Son as Mediator without positing eternal submission. In **Anthropology**, appeals to **gender complementarianism** must proceed from creation and redemption texts, not from speculative intratrinitarian analogies. In **Ecclesiology**, liturgy addressed to the Father through the Son in the Spirit reflects **taxis without hierarchy**, forming prayer that is ad Patrem per Filium in Spiritu, echoing the church's doxological grammar. In **Eschatology**, 1 Corinthians 15.28 is read as the consummation of the **mediatorial kingdom**, after which God is all in all without implying an eternal subordination.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, catechesis should teach **eternal generation**, **inseparable operations**, and **one divine will**, so that believers worship one God in three persons without confusion or division. Preaching on John and 1 Corinthians should distinguish the **economic missions** from the **immanent processions**, showing that Christ's obedience is the obedience of the incarnate Son and the triumph of the **mediatorial office**. This guards against projecting human hierarchies into God and secures assurance that salvation is the indivisible work of the **triune God**.

Liturgically, a renewed sense of **taxis without hierarchy** shapes prayer to the Father, through the Son, in the Spirit. Hymns that accent **homoousios** and **perichoresis** can counter practical subordinationism. In discipleship, avoid making the Trinity a prop for **gender complementarianism** or egalitarianism, since such moves distort **Theology Proper**. Instead, anchor ethics in creation and redemption while letting the Trinity ground the church's doxology and mission.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The church best honors Scripture and the creeds by affirming **eternal generation**, **one will**, and **inseparable operations**, locating all submission in the incarnate economy and refusing an eternal hierarchy within the **immanent Trinity**.
