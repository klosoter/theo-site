### TITLE
God’s being in saving act: Rahner’s Rule and Barth’s actualistic reformulation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late modern dogmatics, 1930s–1970s; locus: **Theology Proper** within **Doctrine of the Trinity**, interfacing with **Revelation** and **Soteriology**.
- How do we relate God’s eternal triune life to the history of salvation, safeguarding both **immanent Trinity** and **economic Trinity** without collapse or separation.
- Principal figures: **Karl Rahner** (Roman Catholic articulator of the Rule) and **Karl Barth** (Reformed actualist reformulation); with interlocutors like **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, and **Bavinck**.
- Key concepts: **divine simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, **missions and processions**, **Calvins autotheos**, and **election as immanent act**.
- Major works and dates: Rahner, The Trinity (1967); Barth, Church Dogmatics I/1 (1932), I/2 (1938), II/2 (1942); councils: **Nicaea 325**, **Constantinople 381**, **Lateran IV 1215**.
- Reformed trajectories: **Old Princeton** retrieval, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** synthesis, **Westminster** confessional balance; alternatives: **Roman Catholic ressourcement**, **Lutheran narrative ontology**, **analytic trinitarianism**.
- Legacy: continuing debate over **vice versa symmetry**, **divine impassibility**, and **inseparable operations** in contemporary trinitarian and christological theology.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Rahner’s Rule states: the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity and the immanent Trinity is the economic Trinity. Formulated programmatically by **Karl Rahner** in 1967, the claim binds God’s saving history to God’s eternal life, aiming to overcome a gap between **immanent Trinity** and **economic Trinity** inherited from certain neo-scholastic patterns. At stake are the classical claims of **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations**, on the one hand, and a robust theology of revelation and salvation, on the other, so that what God does *ad extra* truly reveals who God is *ad intra*.

**Karl Barth’s reformulation** is not a mere repetition. In the Church Dogmatics from 1932 onward he contends that God’s being is in act, insisting that God is identical with his triune self-revelation in Jesus Christ and the Holy Spirit. For Barth, the identity of **revelation and God** underwrites the identity of immanent and economic Trinity; yet his actualism and doctrine of **election as immanent act** recalibrate the rule by grounding it in God’s eternal, free decision to be God for us. The dogmatic location is Theology Proper, correlating directly with **Christology** and **Pneumatology** and normed by conciliar trinitarian grammar.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture renders the identity between God and God’s saving action without collapsing Creator into creation. The baptismal command in Matthew 28:19 names Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, rooting worship in the **triune name** and the unity of **inseparable operations**. John 1:18 declares that the only-begotten God, who is at the Father’s side, has made him known; this Johannine logic binds the **immanent relation of generation** and the **economic mission** of the Son such that the Son reveals the Father as who the Father eternally is.

Paul’s letters supply further grammar for **missions and processions**. In Galatians 4:4–6 God sends the Son and then the Spirit of the Son into our hearts, echoing the patristic linkage between *processio* and *missio*. Romans 8:9–17 ties the Spirit’s indwelling to filial adoption, making our soteriology a participation in the **triune life**. Meanwhile Ephesians 1:3–14 pushes back into eternity with language of election “before the foundation of the world,” suggesting that the economy refracts an eternal counsel without turning that counsel into a temporal process. The burning bush of Exodus 3:14 and the divine name anchor **divine simplicity**, while John 17:5 differentiates the Son’s preexistent glory from his incarnate mission, preserving **immanent relations** within the **economic narrative**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The patristic achievement fixed the grammar within which Rahner and Barth work. **Nicaea 325** and **Constantinople 381** confessed the Son as *homoousios* with the Father and the Spirit as Lord and giver of life, insisting upon **inseparable operations** with a clear **taxis** of persons. Augustine’s De Trinitate developed psychological analogies, carefully distinguishing *ad intra* processions from *ad extra* missions, and Aquinas later codified the maxim that opera Trinitatis ad extra sunt indivisa, thereby protecting **divine simplicity** and the unity of the divine will.

By the late scholastic period and into modernity, a functional detachment often appeared between **immanent Trinity** and **economy**, as speculative trinitarianism receded from preaching and catechesis. In response, **Karl Rahner** proposed his Rule to recover the immediacy of God’s self-communication in grace, while maintaining classical **processions** and **inseparable operations**. Simultaneously, **Karl Barth** re-centered the doctrine on revelation: in CD I/1 and I/2 he presents the triune God as the Subject, Act, and Object of revelation, and in CD II/2 he deepens this with the claim that **election as immanent act** is constitutive of God’s eternal intention to be God for us in Jesus Christ. Across these developments, the classical antinomies are not discarded but reframed in service of a robustly christological **theology proper**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Reformed tradition receives the Rule through its own grammar of **divine simplicity** and **autotheos**. **Old Princeton** theologians like Charles Hodge (1871–1873) and B. B. Warfield (1915) stressed **inseparable operations** and the consubstantiality of the persons, while grounding all knowledge of God in revelation, a stance that resonates with a chastened form of **Rahners Rule**. They affirmed that the missions reveal the processions but denied that economy exhausts the **immanent Trinity**, maintaining analogical knowledge and the Creator–creature distinction.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinists**—especially **Herman Bavinck** in Reformed Dogmatics (1895–1901)—provide a capacious synthesis. Bavinck endorsed **Calvins autotheos** of the Son with respect to the divine essence while safeguarding **eternal generation**, thereby holding together the Son’s aseity and filiality. He treats the economy as the revelation of the **immanent life** without historicizing God, and he defends **inseparable operations** alongside a real personal *taxis*. The **Westminster** standards (1646) enshrine the same balance: one God in three persons, without division of substance or confounding of persons, and with worship directed to Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as one Lord. In the twentieth century, Reformed retrievals, including T. F. Torrance’s actualist approach, converge with Barth on identifying God with his saving acts while refusing any collapse of **divine simplicity**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Within Roman Catholic theology, **Rahner** aims to re-embed grace as God’s self-communication, making the economy the arena where the **immanent Trinity** is known. Subsequent figures diverge: **Catherine LaCugna** in 1991 pushes conservation of the economic focus, warning against speculative detours into an unknowable **immanent Trinity**, while Thomist ressourcement, from **Garrigou-Lagrange** to contemporary **Gilles Emery**, insists the Rule must be read through **divine simplicity** and **processions** so that the vice versa does not licitly import temporal suffering into God.

Lutheran trajectories include **Robert Jenson’s narrative ontology**, identifying God with the one who raised Jesus from the dead, thus radicalizing the link between **immanent identity** and **economic history**. **Wolfhart Pannenberg** famously argues that God’s deity is eschatologically manifested, risking a version of reciprocity in which history conditions divine identity. Modern social trinitarians such as **Jürgen Moltmann** emphasize relationality and **perichoresis**, sometimes weakening **inseparable operations**. In analytic theology, **Brian Leftow** defends a Latin monotheism that effectively qualifies **Rahners Rule** by prioritizing one simple God over three centers, while **Richard Swinburne** leans social, closer to a robust **economic–immanent** parallelism. Across these alternatives, debate centers on how strictly to apply the vice versa and how to maintain **divine simplicity** without eclipsing the economy.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Three clustered worries persist. First, the symmetry of the **vice versa** is contested: does “and vice versa” entail that the immanent life of God is fully exhausted by the history of Jesus and the Spirit, or does it simply deny any contradiction between **immanent Trinity** and **economy**. Barth’s readers divide, with Bruce McCormack pressing that **election as immanent act** determines God’s identity, while John Webster and others insist on the primacy of **divine simplicity** and aseity.

Second, critics fear historicizing God or compromising **impassibility**. If the cross reveals God, does God suffer *ad intra* eternally. Classical defenders retrieve **inseparable operations** and the theopaschite formula “one of the Trinity suffered according to the flesh,” maintaining that the economy truly reveals the **triune love** without positing change in the **divine essence**. Third, there is renewed scrutiny of **eternal generation** and **eternal procession** in relation to the missions, especially in the wake of debates over eternal functional subordination after 2016, where appeals to the **economic Trinity** were made to infer immanent hierarchies. A careful application of **Rahners Rule** blocks such inference by distinguishing taxis from subordination and tying economy to eternal relations without importing temporal roles into **immanent life**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The Rule governs **Christology** by insisting that Jesus Christ is the truth of God, so that the Chalcedonian one person in two natures is the locus for **immanent–economic** disclosure. It stabilizes **Pneumatology**: the Spirit who indwells as firstfruits is the Spirit who eternally proceeds, enabling participation without confusing **Creator and creature**. In **Soteriology**, union with Christ entails communion with the **triune God**, in whom adoption corresponds to the Son’s eternal filiation and the Spirit’s mission corresponds to eternal **procession**.

In **Ecclesiology**, baptism into the triune name presupposes **inseparable operations**, while liturgy adores the one God in three persons, whose **divine simplicity** is not abstraction but the undivided plenitude disclosed in the gospel. For **Anthropology**, the imago Dei is reframed not as a social template but as covenantal fellowship grounded in **perichoretic** love, with caution against projecting creaturely relations into **immanent Trinity**. Eschatologically, the God who will be all in all is eternally the God he is for us; thus the beatific vision is communion with the **immanent Trinity** unveiled by the **economic**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, **Rahners Rule** tells preachers that in narrating Jesus and the Spirit they speak truly of God, not merely of divine effects. The cross and resurrection are not external instruments but revelation of the eternal **triune love**, so proclamation can be confident that grace is God’s own self-giving. Liturgically, confession of **inseparable operations** guards hymnody and prayer from tritheism, while the missions shape doxology to the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit, corresponding to **processions** and the economy.

Barth’s reformulation emboldens assurance: if **election as immanent act** is God’s eternal decision to be for us in Christ, then the gospel does not rest on contingencies of our history but in the steadfast freedom of the **divine simplicity**. Catechesis should teach the distinction without separation between **immanent Trinity** and **economic Trinity**, using baptismal texts, John’s Gospel, and Romans 8 to show how missions unveil processions and how salvation ushers believers into the life of the **triune God**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Rightly received, **Rahners Rule** and **Barths reformulation** bind God’s eternal triune life to the gospel’s saving history in a way that honors **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations** while making revelation determinative for theology.
