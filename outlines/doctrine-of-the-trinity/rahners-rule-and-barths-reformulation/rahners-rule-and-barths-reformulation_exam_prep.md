### TITLE
Identity without collapse: Rahner’s Rule, Barth’s reformulation, and the immanent–economic Trinity under classical constraints

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How to affirm that God’s saving self-disclosure is the truth of who God eternally is without collapsing the Creator–creature distinction or compromising **divine simplicity**.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, Karl Barth, Karl Rahner, Herman Bavinck.
- Traditions: Latin patristic trinitarianism, Thomistic scholasticism, Reformed classical theism, twentieth-century Catholic and Barthian dogmatics.
- Debates:
  - Internal: relation of **opera ad extra indivisa** to personal missions; whether Rahner’s Rule threatens **impassibility** and **aseity**; Barth’s act-identity and election in Christ.
  - External: Hegelian historicization of God, social trinitarianism, process and open theism.
  - Contemporary: analytic models of Trinity, biblicist objections to the immanent Trinity, christological suffering and **divine simplicity**.
- Position: Affirm Rahner’s Rule as a hermeneutical identity governed by asymmetry and **analogical knowledge**, and appropriate Barth’s reformulation through Augustinian–Thomistic and Reformed constraints so that the economy truly reveals, without exhausting or altering, the immanent Trinity.

### ESSAY
Rahner’s Rule claims that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity and the immanent Trinity is the economic Trinity. The aim is to deny any wedge between who God eternally is and who God is toward us in revelation and redemption. Barth’s reformulation intensifies this identity from a different angle: God is who he is in his act, and the God who reveals himself is none other than he who he is. In both, the triune God’s ad extra works are said to be the truth of the triune life. Yet the rule has generated worries on all sides. Does it obscure **divine simplicity**, blur the Creator–creature distinction, or license social trinitarianism and historicized metaphysics of God. Conversely, if it is diluted to a mere heuristic, does it leave us with a hidden God behind the gospel. The task is to retrieve the rule within classical trinitarian boundaries while honoring its antimythical thrust. I will argue that the economy is identical in referent to the immanent Trinity but not univocal in mode, so that the identity functions under the discipline of **analogical knowledge** and **opera ad extra indivisa**, secured in the Augustinian–Thomistic relation of missions to processions and strengthened by Reformed dogmatics on divine freedom and election. Barth’s Christological concentration provides a salutary rejection of speculation but must be harnessed to classical constraints; Rahner’s metaphysical insistence presses the unity of God’s being and act but requires asymmetry to avoid collapse. Properly ordered, the rule prevents dualistic accounts of revelation while stabilizing doctrinal coherence across Trinity, Christology, and soteriology.

The classical grammar within which this debate must be judged is Augustinian. Augustine of Hippo forged the Latin account of the triune mystery by binding Scripture’s narrative of sending and giving to an ontology of divine life. Two axioms guide him. First, **opera ad extra indivisa**: the works of God outward are inseparable because they are the undivided agency of the one simple God. Second, missions are grounded in processions: the Son is from the Father and the Spirit proceeds from the Father and the Son, and therefore the temporal sendings manifest the eternal relations of origin. The corollary is that the Father sends the Son and Spirit; the Son is sent and sends the Spirit; and the Spirit is sent and is the gift who unites believers to the Son. This pattern ensures that the economy bears an intrinsic intelligibility as disclosure of the eternal life of God. Yet Augustine refuses any inversion whereby the economy constructs the immanent Trinity. The immanent relations are logically prior, and the economic self-disclosure is true manifestation. If examiners press whether Augustine would endorse Rahner’s Rule, the answer is that he would affirm the first clause, in the sense that what is revealed economically is none other than the eternal relations of Father, Son, and Spirit, but he would discipline the vice versa with the claim that the immanent Trinity exceeds the economy as its archetype. On this terrain, **divine simplicity** is not an obstacle to revelation but its guarantor: because God is not composed or self-alienated, the one who acts toward us is not other than the one he eternally is.

Thomas Aquinas consolidates and sharpens this Latin inheritance in a metaphysical key. He refuses to treat the Trinity as a detachable appendix to the doctrine of God. In Thomas, **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, and **impassibility** are not denials of divine life but the conditions under which real personal distinctions can be affirmed without composition or change. The crucial move is the analysis of missions as temporal effects of eternal processions. The invisible missions of Son and Spirit in grace and the visible missions in incarnation and Pentecost are grounded in the eternal generation of the Son and the spiration of the Spirit. By this grammar, what occurs ad extra presupposes the ad intra and so manifests it. The Father’s paternity, the Son’s filiation, and the Spirit’s procession are not mirrored by three parallel social centers in history; they are eternally subsistent relations subsisting in the one essence, disclosed in modes of presence with creatures. Aquinas insists on **analogical knowledge**: divine names and predicates signify from the side of God but according to the mode of the creature. So the identity between immanent and economic must be real in referent and analogical in signification. To an examiner who asks whether the Thomistic doctrine undercuts Rahner’s Rule, one replies that Thomas supplies the structural grounds for its positive force while forbidding any reversal whereby the economy constitutes the immanent life. Within this framework, the rule becomes a hermeneutic axiom: the God who saves is none other than the God who is, and the patterns of saving action correspond to eternal relations without partitioning the essence.

Twentieth-century Roman Catholic theology had often drifted toward a practical binitarianism in which the Trinity is remote from salvation and spirituality. Karl Rahner sought to break that abstraction. He adopts a transcendental method that attends to the a priori openness of the human subject to God as the horizon of his self-communication. The decisive burden of his rule is theological rather than merely epistemic: God’s self-communication in grace and in the incarnation is the triune God’s own presence, and there is not a second God lurking behind the economy. In works like The Trinity, Rahner argues that the missions of Son and Spirit are themselves the self-giving of the triune God. The scandalous clause is the vice versa, which he uses to expunge any hints of speculative hypostatization unanchored in saving history. The rule is meant to tighten the bond: no doctrine of the Trinity may be constructed independently of God’s economy, and no economy may be construed as instrumental to something other than the triune life. Yet Rahner is not a historicist if read carefully. He does not say that the economy produces the immanent Trinity, but that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity as self-communicated. Critics rightly worry that the vice versa might invite a reduction whereby God’s eternal being is reconstructed from salvation history. Rahner answers by insisting on the freedom of God’s self-communication and the transcendence of the divine essence. He does not abandon **divine simplicity** or **impassibility**, though he reinterprets the latter in terms of personal self-giving that does not compromise immutability. The advantage of Rahner’s Rule is its soteriological seriousness: grace is nothing less than God himself given, and the incarnation is the assumption of human nature by the eternal Son. The risk is methodological: unless one guards the analogical gap and the ontological priority of processions to missions, the rule can be weaponized to absolutize contingent historical patterns as if they are exhaustive of God.

Karl Barth’s contribution is differently sourced and yet convergent in aim. Barth’s Church Dogmatics begins with a severe critique of speculative metaphysics and insists that theology speak only on the basis of God’s self-revelation. In the locus on the Trinity, Barth famously claims that God reveals himself as Lord and that in revelation God is subject, object, and revealing act. The triune identity is inseparable from God’s self-revelation. This is often summarized in the slogan that God’s being is in act. The target is the possibility that theology might posit a divine essence behind revelation of which we can say nothing. Barth insists that there is no God behind the back of Jesus Christ. The doctrine of election radicalizes this. In his revision of the decree of election, Barth holds that Jesus Christ is the electing God and the elected man. God eternally determines himself to be God for us in Jesus Christ. The determination is free and proper to the triune God and thus not a reaction to the creature. By grounding election in God’s triune life, Barth argues for an intrinsic tie between the immanent and the economic: the covenant of grace has its root in an immanent divine decision. In this sense, Barth reforms Rahner’s intuition from a christological center: the immanent is the ground of the economic, and the economic is the ordained manifestation of the immanent. An examiner will press whether Barth thereby historicizes God. The answer is that Barth maintains divine freedom and aseity by treating the decree as the eternal life of God rather than as a temporal choice. Still, the rhetoric of being-in-act has tempted some of his heirs to read God’s identity as dependent on history. A chastened Barthianism must therefore insist on the distinction between necessary triune life and free determination to create and redeem, even as it confesses that God freely and eternally identifies himself as the God who will be with us in Christ.

A Reformed classical voice helps adjudicate these tensions. Herman Bavinck articulates the unity of the divine being and the fullness of triune life in a robustly classical idiom while refusing any separation of the Trinity from revelation and redemption. For Bavinck, Scripture’s pattern of sending the Son and Spirit is grounded in eternal generation and spiration. He defends **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** not as denials of plenitude but as perfections of God’s life. At the same time, he insists that God’s revelation is self-presentation. The knowledge of God is **analogical knowledge**: true but not comprehensive, accommodated but not deceptive. Bavinck’s handling of the pactum salutis and election helps here. Election is in Christ and thus has a Christological focus that links immanent counsel and economic execution without rupturing divine freedom. God’s decree is immanent and necessary to God as actus purus, yet its content is freely determined. So the economy declares who God is without constituting God. If examiners ask whether this is functionally the same as Rahner’s Rule, one can say that Bavinck supplies the doctrinal architecture within which the rule becomes a disciplined hermeneutic rather than a metaphysical equation. The economy reveals the immanent Trinity because the immanent Trinity grounds the economy; the two are identical in the sense that the one God is the subject of both, and they are asymmetrical in the sense that ad intra norms ad extra.

Internal debates arise at each point. Within Latin trinitarianism, how are **opera ad extra indivisa** squared with the manifestly personal pattern of missions. Augustine and Thomas answer by distinguishing inseparable operations from personal modes of subsistence and personal properties. The one simple act of God terminates in effects that correspond to the persons by appropriation grounded in real relations of origin. This is why the Father alone is not incarnate and why the Spirit alone is poured out at Pentecost, though the one undivided essence acts. Rahner’s Rule can be accused of obscuring this by flattening missions into a general divine self-communication. The reply is to reinsert the Augustinian ground: missions presuppose processions, and the economy displays personal distinctions. Within Barth’s school, disputes turn on whether his act-identity thesis jeopardizes aseity. Barth himself guards divine freedom in insisting that God’s act of revelation is the free act of the one who is Lord in himself. But some heirs extract from Barth a historicized ontology. The corrective is John Webster’s retrieval of classical theism within a Barthian frame, but the dogmatic point can be made without further figures: Barth can be read as preserving the ontological priority of the immanent even while denying that we know any God other than the one revealed.

External debates help sharpen the stakes. Hegelian systems dissolve Creator and creature into the process of Geist, for whom the economy is necessary for God to become God. Process and open theism similarly treat divine relationality as history-dependent. Social trinitarians often turn the persons into centers of consciousness whose interrelations become the archetype for human community, reading the economy as a window into eternal society. All of these stand or fall with the denial of **divine simplicity** and the redefinition of person and essence. Rahner’s Rule is sometimes enlisted to support these alternatives by claiming that what appears in history must be what God is, full stop. The classical reply is that identity of subject does not imply identity of mode. The same God who acts in history is eternally triune; the same Son who is sent is eternally from the Father. But this identity presupposes that the Lord who sends and is sent is the one simple essence subsisting in three relations. If asked to articulate the red line, one should say that any account which posits any perfection of God as perfected by history violates the rule’s classical sense. The economy is revelation, not constitution.

Contemporary debates further test the rule. Analytic models of the Trinity probe the metaphysics of three and one. Latin and social models contend over whether divine persons are individuated by relations alone or by centers of consciousness. The rule can seem to favor social models by elevating the interpersonal dynamics of Jesus’ life and the sending of the Spirit. But the Augustinian–Thomistic architecture frames those dynamics within **opera ad extra indivisa** and the missions-processions tether, which resists tripersonal substances. A different debate raises biblicist worries: does Scripture teach an immanent Trinity, or only the economy. The missionary language of John 1 and John 14 to 17, Galatians 4, and Romans 8 binds revelation to eternal relations. The Son is from the Father, the Spirit proceeds and is sent. Scripture discloses the immanent by means of the economic. A further modern pressure point is divine suffering. If the immanent and economic are identical, does the cross entail suffering in the divine nature. The classical response maintains **impassibility** while granting real suffering in the incarnate person. The Son truly suffers according to the human nature he assumed; in that personal union, God is the subject of the suffering without the divine nature undergoing change. The economy reveals the self-giving love that is eternally in God, not a new attribute produced by passion.

Having set the field, we can now state the positive proposal with precision. Rahner’s Rule should be affirmed as an identity of referent under the discipline of asymmetry and analogy. The identity denies a hidden God other than the one revealed in the gospel. The asymmetry preserves the ontological priority of the immanent Trinity and guards the freedom and fullness of God in himself. The analogy specifies that our language, even at its truest, is accommodated. This yields a principled reading of Barth and Rahner. Of Barth we receive the determination to let God’s self-witness norm our discourse, the Christological concentration of election, and the rejection of speculative surplus beyond revelation. Of Rahner we receive the refusal to treat the Trinity as abstracted from salvation, the insistence that grace is God himself, and the unity of being and act. But we insist with Augustine and Thomas that missions presuppose processions, with Bavinck that divine freedom and **aseity** govern all ad extra acts, and with all three that **divine simplicity** is the theological grammar preventing collapse or composition. This construal allows us to say, with Barth, that Jesus Christ is the electing God and elected man without making election a temporal event in God. It allows us to say, with Rahner, that the Spirit’s indwelling is the immanent Trinity’s self-gift without construing the immanent Trinity as constituted by grace.

Anticipating examiner challenges clarifies the argument. First, does the identity not erase the Creator–creature distinction. No, because identity here is of the acting subject, not of ontological mode. The God who acts toward us is the Lord who is in himself; his act is a free determination, and our knowledge is **analogical knowledge** of that free self-presentation. Second, does Barth’s doctrine of election make God dependent on the world. No, because election is an eternal act of the triune God, a free self-determination antecedent to and not conditioned by creation. The economy is the execution of that decree in time, not its cause. Third, does the cross imply divine passibility. No, because the subject of the passion is the person of the Son who assumed human nature. The suffering is real and communicable at the level of person, and impassibility remains a perfection of the divine nature that renders God’s love unchangeably faithful. Fourth, does Rahner’s Rule entail modalism. No, because the missions are the actions of distinct persons whose eternal relations ground their distinct missions. The rule denies a gap between immanent and economic, not the reality of personal distinctions. Fifth, does this approach endanger biblical clarity by importing metaphysics. No, because the metaphysical grammar is the church’s disciplined attempt to say only what Scripture compels while protecting its coherence. John’s Gospel demands that the Son be from the Father, that his sending is a manifestation of that eternal relation. Paul’s pneumatology demands that the Spirit’s indwelling be the Spirit of the Son from the Father. The metaphysics furnishes the only non-contradictory way to confess this.

This position also stabilizes adjacent loci. In revelation, it means that Scripture is the authoritative witness to God’s triune self-presentation, and that the canon’s economy bears the selfsame Lord who is its referent. In Christology, it means that the identity of Jesus as Son is not constructed by mission but revealed as mission grounded in generation. In soteriology, it means that adoption and sanctification are participations in the life of God by the Spirit who proceeds and is sent, binding us to the Son. In election, it means that God’s decree is triune and christological without making God history-bound. In ecclesiology, it means that the church’s sacramental and doxological life is a participation in the missions of Son and Spirit that disclose the eternal relations.

The rule, so constrained, also helps resist contemporary distortions. It prevents a unitarian soteriology in which Jesus and the Spirit are mere instruments by insisting that salvation is the work of the triune God as triune. It prevents social trinitarianism from projecting creaturely society into God by reasserting that the interpersonal dynamics revealed are those of subsistent relations in one simple essence. It prevents process theism from imagining a God who becomes by making clear that the economy is the manifestation of eternal plenitude. It guards against biblicism by showing why Scripture’s economy legitimately grants knowledge of immanent relations: because missions manifest processions. And it blocks speculative detachment by refusing any distinction between a supposed deus nudus absconditus and the Father who sends the Son and pours out the Spirit.

Finally, we return to Barth’s reformulation because it exerts continuing pressure on Reformed dogmatics. Barth’s being-in-act thesis, if read without classical guardrails, tempts a conflation of ontology and economy. But the better reading is to say that God’s act of revelation is a mode of the life of the triune God who, as **aseity** and **simplicity**, is actus purus. The act does not add to God; it is the free communication of what God is. Election in Christ names the eternal truth that God is not otherwise than the one who wills to be with us. Yet the will is free and grounded in the immanent relations of Father, Son, and Spirit. Rahner’s Rule, read thus, is not a rival to classical theism but a retrieval of its deepest intention: that the God of the gospel is the only God there is, and that we do not need to climb past the Son and Spirit to reach a hidden deity. The vice versa is then clarified. The immanent Trinity is the economic Trinity not because God is constituted by the economy but because the economy is the faithful self-exegesis of the triune God. The arrow of norming is from immanent to economic, and the arrow of knowing is from economic to immanent. In that mutuality lies the difference between identity and collapse.

The Westminster examiner’s vocation is to test whether the candidate can hold together classical theism, trinitarian confession, and the gospel’s economy. The safe and strong way to do so is to affirm the rule as identity of referent under asymmetry of mode. Augustine and Thomas supply the grammar of **opera ad extra indivisa**, missions grounded in processions, and **divine simplicity**. Rahner reminds us that the Trinity is the content of grace itself. Barth refuses any God behind Jesus Christ and grounds the economy in God’s own eternal determination. Bavinck secures the whole from the side of Reformed dogmatics in which **analogical knowledge**, divine freedom, and election in Christ bind the loci together. That composite position is both rigorous and pastoral. It tells us that in Jesus and by the Spirit we truly encounter the triune God, and it tells us that the God we meet is not fragile or time bound. Identity without collapse is the right reading of Rahner’s Rule and the right appropriation of Barth’s reformulation.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: Rahner’s Rule affirms an identity of referent between the immanent and economic Trinity, rightly constrained by asymmetry and **analogical knowledge** so that the economy truly reveals without constituting the immanent life; Barth’s reformulation grounds this identity in God’s self-revelation and election in Christ.
- Decisive figures:
  - Augustine: **opera ad extra indivisa**, missions grounded in processions; economy as manifestation of eternal relations.
  - Thomas Aquinas: **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **analogical knowledge**; missions as temporal effects of eternal processions.
  - Karl Rahner: identity of immanent and economic as self-communication of God; corrective to Trinity–soteriology separation, needing asymmetrical guardrails.
  - Karl Barth: being-in-act and election in Christ; no God behind Jesus Christ; must be read with classical constraints on **aseity** and divine freedom.
  - Herman Bavinck: Reformed synthesis; election in Christ, **covenantal condescension**, and classical perfections securing identity without collapse.
- Debates:
  - Internal: inseparable operations and personal missions; Rahner’s vice versa and risk of collapse; Barth’s act-identity and aseity.
  - External: Hegelian historicization, social trinitarianism, process and open theism versus **divine simplicity**.
  - Contemporary: analytic Trinity models, biblicist denial of immanent Trinity, divine suffering and **impassibility**.
- Defended position: Affirm Rahner’s Rule as a hermeneutical identity under Augustinian–Thomistic constraints, received through a chastened Barthian Christocentrism and Reformed commitments to **simplicity**, freedom, and missions-processions asymmetry.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - Does the rule collapse Creator and creature? Answer: No; identity of subject, asymmetry of mode, and **analogical knowledge** preserve the distinction.
  - Does Barth historicize God through election in Christ? Answer: No; election is an eternal, free triune act grounding the economy without dependence.
  - Is impassibility compatible with the cross? Answer: Yes; the Son truly suffers according to his assumed human nature, with God as the suffering subject without change in the divine nature.
  - Does Rahner’s Rule imply modalism? Answer: No; missions presuppose processions and thus distinct personal agents in the one essence.
  - Where is this in Scripture? Answer: The sending of Son and Spirit in John 1 and 14–17, Galatians 4, and Romans 8 manifests eternal generation and procession.
