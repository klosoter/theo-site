### TITLE
Before Grace, a Gifted Law: The Covenant of Works and Adam’s Federal Headship

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: Reformed federal theology, 1560s–1670s consolidation, lodged in Anthropology and Soteriology under **covenant theology**.
- Core question: Did God enter a prelapsarian covenant with Adam promising eschatological life upon perfect obedience, grounding **federal headship** and **imputation**?
- Principal figures: **Bullinger** (testament schema), **Calvin** (law/gospel unity), **Ursinus/Olevianus** (federal roots), **Turretin/Witsius** (systematization), **Westminster divines** (confessional form), **Vos/Bavinck/Kline** (modern retrievals), critics like **Barth** and **N. T. Wright**.
- Key concepts: **voluntary divine condescension**, **ex pacto merit**, **tree of life sacrament**, **eschatological Sabbath**, **active obedience of Christ**, and **pactum salutis**.
- Major texts/events: Gen 1–3; Hos 6:7; Lev 18:5; Rom 5:12–21; 1 Cor 15:45–49; **Westminster Confession 7.2 and Larger Catechism** 1646–1648; **Witsius, Economy of the Covenants** 1677; **Turretin, Institutes** 1679–1685.
- Reformed and alternatives: Classic Reformed affirm **covenant of works**; Lutherans stress **law–gospel** without covenantal merit; Roman Catholics speak of **donum superadditum**; Barthians collapse into one **covenant of grace**; **New Perspective** and **Federal Vision** reject merit schemas.
- Legacy cue: Ongoing debates over **republication at Sinai**, **Hosea 6:7**, and the ground of **Christ’s active obedience**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The Reformed doctrine of the **covenant of works** proposes that God, by **voluntary divine condescension**, entered into a covenant with Adam as public person for his posterity, promising eschatological life upon perfect and personal obedience, threatening death upon transgression. The Westminster Confession 7.2 (1646) summarizes: “The first covenant made with man was a covenant of works, wherein life was promised to Adam; and to his posterity in him, upon condition of perfect and personal obedience.” The **federal headship** of Adam grounds the subsequent **imputation** of guilt and corruption to humanity, while also preparing the typological field for Christ as the second Adam and mediator of the **covenant of grace**.

Dogmatically, the doctrine sits at the crossroads of **Anthropology**, **Soteriology**, and **Christology**, and is framed within classic **covenant theology** alongside the intratrinitarian **pactum salutis** and the historical **covenant of grace**. It safeguards that God’s demand of righteousness is not arbitrary but covenantal, and that human destiny was ordered to an eschatological Sabbath rest through **ex pacto merit** rather than strict condign merit. The end was not mere Edenic maintenance but a higher, confirmed life—what many summarize as **eschatological advancement** signified by the tree of life.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Genesis 2:16–17 sets the probation: “You may surely eat... but of the tree of the knowledge of good and evil you shall not eat, for in the day you eat of it you shall surely die.” The Hebrew idiom *mot tamut* intensifies certainty; the prohibition presupposes a rule structure, while the presence of the **tree of life** in Genesis 2:9 and 3:22 indicates a sacramental sign of promised, confirmed life. Though the noun *berit* does not appear in Genesis 2–3, Reformed exegetes argue for a covenantal form in the elements of parties, condition, promise, and sanction, culminating in Adam’s **federal headship** expressed in the spread of death in Romans 5:12–21. The structural echo in 1 Corinthians 15:45–49—Adam and the **last Adam**—locates the protology-eschatology link: Adam under a probation in the garden, Christ under a **works principle** to fulfill righteousness.

Hosea 6:7 remains crucial: “But like Adam they transgressed the covenant.” Read as “like Adam” rather than “at Adam,” this text provides explicit covenantal vocabulary, joining the prelapsarian arrangement to *berit*. Leviticus 18:5 and its Pauline reprises—Galatians 3:12 and Romans 10:5—state “the one who does them shall live by them,” which Reformed interpreters take as a positive statement of the **do-this-and-live** principle, not as a postlapsarian path to justification but as an echo of the earlier **covenant of works** pattern. Ezekiel 20:11 and Deuteronomy 27 reinforce the blessing-curse structuring of covenant obedience, setting the hermeneutical field for a republication debate. The Greek *diatheke* in the New Testament frames salvation history as covenantally ordered, while Romans 5:15–19 unfolds **imputation**: Adam’s one trespass bringing condemnation, Christ’s one act of righteousness securing justification and life, thereby presupposing the first covenant’s federal structure.

Finally, the Sabbath motif in Genesis 2:1–3 intimates **eschatological Sabbath** rest, which Hebrews 4 reads as a still-open promise. In Reformed reading, Adam’s obedient completion of his probation would have issued in that rest, so that Christ’s **active obedience** recapitulates and surpasses the broken covenant of works to gain the promised life for his people.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic writers lacked the full Reformed construction but supplied seeds. **Irenaeus’s recapitulation** doctrine positioned Adam and Christ as two heads of humanity, while **Augustine’s original sin** and **imputation** of guilt shaped the Western grammar. The medievals discussed **merit** and **pactum**: **Scotus** and **Ockham** stressed divine freedom to appoint reward to acts by covenant, and **Gabriel Biel** elaborated the *facientibus quod in se est Deus non denegat gratiam*. Though often criticized, these pactum notions prepared the Reformed for **ex pacto merit** language without adopting medieval soteriology.

Early Reformed figures such as **Bullinger** in De testamento (1534) and **Calvin** in the Institutes (1559) forged a law-gospel and testamentary schema. **Ursinus** and **Olevianus** at Heidelberg (1563) emphasized covenant to structure catechesis. By the late seventeenth century, **Turretin** (Institutes, 1679–1685) and **Witsius** (Economy of the Covenants, 1677) codified the **covenant of works** with clarity: Adam’s probation, the promise of life, the threat of death, and Adam’s public personhood. The **Westminster Standards** (1646–1648) gave the confessional definition, with the Shorter Catechism 12 speaking of a “covenant of life,” later called **covenant of works**, to accent the condition of perfect obedience.

Intra-Reformed debates sharpened detail. The **Cocceian-Voetian** tensions in the Dutch Republic wrestled with redemptive-historical rhythms and Sabbath scholarship while keeping the covenant substrate. The **Marrow Controversy** in Scotland (1717–1723) addressed the relation of the law and grace in assurance, with **Thomas Boston** and the Marrow brethren defending free grace while not denying the prelapsarian covenant. In the modern period, **Geerhardus Vos** (d. 1949) deepened biblical-theological typology, **Herman Bavinck** integrated the doctrine with **creation grace**, and **Meredith Kline** argued for treaty-form patterns and **Sinai republication** within the Mosaic economy.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster** articulation concentrates the essentials: WCF 7.2 asserts the first covenant as works; WCF 19 delineates the law’s moral content; and WSC 12 calls it a “covenant of life” upon perfect obedience. The logic secures both **divine condescension**—God was not obligated to enter covenant—and **federal headship**, grounding the need for Christ’s **active and passive obedience**.

**Old Princeton** defended the doctrine’s soteriological payoff. **Charles Hodge** argued that Adam’s representative status explains **imputation** in Romans 5 without reducing it to mere realism, and **B. B. Warfield** tied the **active obedience of Christ** to the forfeited obedience under the first covenant. They resisted any reduction of salvation to mere pardon by emphasizing a positive righteousness that satisfies the original **works principle**.

**Dutch Neo-Calvinists** nuanced the doctrine by embedding it within **creation grace** and cultural calling. **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck** distinguished nature and grace without separation, so the **covenant of works** did not oppose grace but presupposed God’s free benevolence. **Vos** emphasized the eschatological telos latent in Adam’s task, while **Meredith Kline** advanced the thesis of **Sinai republication**: the Mosaic covenant, in a typological and national layer, reasserted a law-based principle for Israel’s tenure in the land, thereby echoing the earlier **do-this-and-live** pattern without undermining the overarching **covenant of grace**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology does not employ the **covenant of works** idiom but speaks of Adam in a state of integrity endowed with **donum superadditum**. Classic scholastic distinctions between **condign and congruent merit** situate Adam’s obedience as grace-enabled yet not covenantal-legal in the Reformed sense. The Council of Trent (1545–1563) affirmed original sin and the need for grace but avoided a federal structure of **imputation** parallel to Romans 5 in Reformed readings.

**Lutheran** theology retains a strong **law–gospel** contrast and acknowledges Adam’s probation under law, but it resists speaking of **merit** or a covenant that grounds an eschatological reward by obedience. The Formula of Concord (1577) upholds original sin and Christ’s satisfaction but typically interprets Romans 5 in terms of solidarity rather than a formal **covenantal works** treaty.

In the modern period, **Karl Barth** rejected a dual-covenant schema, collapsing everything into the one **covenant of grace** in Christ (Church Dogmatics II/2, ca. 1942), thereby reframing election and covenant christologically. The **New Perspective on Paul** and allied readings of **covenantal nomism** reconsider “works of the law,” decentering a universal **works principle** and raising questions about **imputation** in Romans 5. The **Federal Vision** movement (2002 onward) criticizes the **covenant of works** and merit language, favoring a monocovenantal emphasis on faithfulness, while some **New Covenant Theology** proponents deny classical tripartite law and minimize the prelapsarian covenant. In analytic theology, figures like **Oliver Crisp** defend the coherence of **ex pacto merit** and the necessity of Christ’s **active obedience**, pressing metaphysical clarity on federal claims.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A first objection targets textual basis: the absence of *berit* in Genesis 2–3. Reformed responses appeal to covenantal form and to **Hosea 6:7**, arguing that the Bible can describe a covenant without naming it, and that Paul’s **imputation** logic presupposes Adam’s public office. A second objection disputes **merit**. The Reformed answer employs **ex pacto merit**: God, by **voluntary condescension**, promised life upon obedience, so the reward would be just by covenant rather than intrinsic condignity.

The **republication at Sinai** debate remains sharp. **Klineans** argue for a typological works principle for Israel’s tenure, keyed to **Leviticus 18:5** and Deuteronomy’s blessings and curses, while opponents fear legalism or dualism. Many reconcile this by distinguishing Israel’s typological, national layer from individual salvation under the **covenant of grace**. A further debate concerns the **active obedience of Christ**: detractors claim Scripture emphasizes the cross, but Reformed orthodoxy reads Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15 as requiring a positive fulfillment of the **original law** to secure righteousness, with Christ as **second Adam** keeping what Adam failed to keep.

Finally, ethical worries ask whether **federal headship** violates personal responsibility. The Reformed tradition replies that corporate personality pervades Scripture, that Adam’s headship parallels Christ’s, and that the symmetry of **two Adams** secures both the justice of condemnation and the gratuity of salvation, ensuring that justification is not by our works but by Christ’s obedience imputed.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
To **Theology Proper**, the doctrine exhibits **divine condescension** consistent with **divine simplicity**: God freely relates without compromising his aseity. The **pactum salutis** in the Trinity ad intra grounds the ad extra economy, so that the failed first covenant is answered by the Son’s covenantal undertaking as mediator.

To **Anthropology**, the covenant of works specifies **original righteousness**, the unity of the **moral law**, and human telos as **eschatological Sabbath** communion. Adam is priest-king, with the cultural mandate tethered to covenant faithfulness, suggesting that vocation is covenantally charged. To **Christology**, the doctrine sharpens the **two Adams** structure, the necessity of **active obedience**, and the role of Christ as federal head who achieves the promised life. To **Soteriology**, it underwrites **imputation**, **justification by faith alone**, and the distinction between the covenants while avoiding antinomianism. To **Ecclesiology**, it illumines sacraments as covenant signs, with baptism naming us into the **second Adam** and the Lord’s Supper sealing participation in the new covenant. To **Eschatology**, it frames history as movement from probation to consummation through Christ’s victory, fulfilling the promise implicit in Eden’s **tree of life**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the doctrine clarifies the **law–gospel** rhythm of preaching: the law’s demand is unflinching because it is covenantal, and the gospel’s comfort is deep because Christ has fulfilled the **works principle** on our behalf. Catechetically, **WSC 12** provides a crisp summary to teach children that humanity’s plight begins in a broken covenant and ends in a kept one. This promotes assurance, since our hope rests not in mixed moral performance but in the **active obedience of Christ** imputed to us.

Liturgically, confessing sins acknowledges our Adamic solidarity, while absolution declares the **new covenant** verdict secured by the **second Adam**. The **Sabbath** becomes a weekly sign of the coming **eschatological rest**, not an attempt to re-earn life. Vocation is dignified, not salvific: the cultural mandate belongs within **covenant theology**, but justification is anchored in Christ’s merit. The sacraments, especially the Lord’s Supper, rehearse the movement from failed Eden to the marriage supper of the Lamb, where the **tree of life** reappears in Revelation 22 as the sacrament of consummated communion.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The **covenant of works** secures the justice of God’s demand, the depth of Adam’s failure, and the glory of Christ’s **active obedience**, so that the gospel is seen as the triumphant keeping of the first covenant for us.
