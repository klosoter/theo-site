### TITLE
One Covenant of Grace, Many Administrations: Adamic Headship, Mosaic Republication, and the Unity of Scripture

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: how **federal theology** unifies Scripture through the **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, and **covenant of redemption**, while explaining the Mosaic economy and the continuity of the people and signs of God.
- Decisive figures: John Calvin, Johannes Cocceius, Herman Witsius, Geerhardus Vos, Meredith G. Kline.
- Relevant traditions: Reformed scholastics and federal theologians; post-Reformation covenant thought; biblical-theological trajectory from Vos; ancient Near Eastern treaty analysis.
- Debates:
  - Internal: reality and content of the **covenant of works**; the nature of the Mosaic covenant and **republication**; relation of covenant to election; Abrahamic continuity and baptism; the **pactum salutis**.
  - External: dispensationalism’s Israel–church dualism; progressive covenantalism’s reconfiguration of continuity and law; Lutheran anxieties about covenant schemas; New Perspective’s covenantal nomism.
  - Contemporary: Federal Vision on justification by covenantal faithfulness; the republication controversy; typology and land promise fulfillment.
- Position: I defend classic Westminster **federal theology** with a real Adamic **covenant of works**, a Trinitarian **covenant of redemption**, and a single **covenant of grace** unfolding through Abrahamic, Mosaic, and Davidic administrations, with a typological **republication** of the works principle under Moses, and sacramental continuity grounding infant baptism.

### ESSAY
Biblical covenants are the divinely instituted, oath-bound structures by which God administers his kingdom to humanity, defining the terms of communion and the sanctions of life and death. Scripture’s berith and diatheke language bears the weight of sovereign initiative, sworn promise, and mandated loyalty, with blessings and curses that disclose not only the shape of redemptive history but also the architecture of law and gospel. Defended at Westminster and tested by rival frameworks ancient and modern, **federal theology** claims that Scripture coheres through three architectonic covenants: a real **covenant of works** with Adam as **federal head**, a **covenant of redemption** among the persons of the Trinity as the eternal foundation of salvation, and a **covenant of grace** made with sinners in Christ and administered through the successive economies culminating in the new covenant. The conceptual center of the doctrine is unity with ordered diversity: there is one gracious promise unfolding in multiple administrations, and one righteous demand fulfilled in the second Adam. The Mosaic covenant stands at the crux of interpretation, because its compounded legal and typological features must be coordinated with Abrahamic promise and Christological fulfillment without collapsing grace into law or severing the testaments.

John Calvin (1509–1564) made the decisive early turn that set Reformed covenant thought on its trajectory by rooting the unity of Scripture in a single **covenant of grace** administered in diverse forms. In the Institutes and his commentaries, Calvin argues that the covenant with Abraham is the definitive public form of the gospel, received by faith, sealed by sacramental signs, and transmitted to the corporate people of God. He insists on continuity between circumcision and baptism as signs and seals of the same promise, thereby grounding the **visible church** across the testaments. Calvin does not articulate a formal **covenant of works**, though the substance is present in his Adam–Christ contrast and his account of the law as a demand for perfect righteousness coupled with the gospel as gratuitous promise. His aim is not speculative architecture but the pastoral unification of Scripture in Christ, such that the ceremonial structures serve as **typology** of the spiritual realities and the moral law abides as the rule of gratitude. Calvin’s distinction between the substance and the accidents of the covenant provides the structural principle later federal theology will develop: the one substance is the promissory communion with God through Christ, and the accidents are the administrative forms under different epochs. When examiners press whether Calvin’s accent on unity submerges Mosaic law, the reply is that his reading of Galatians 3 preserves promise priority while respecting the law’s role as pedagogue unto Christ. Calvin’s emphasis on sacramental continuity likewise foreshadows the Reformed case for household initiation and corporate holiness rooted in the Abrahamic promissory structure.

Johannes Cocceius (1603–1669) reframed the field by making the covenant the historiographical key that orders the whole Bible. In his Summa Doctrinae de Foedere, he explicated a successive series of covenants that structure redemptive history: Adamic, postlapsarian evangelical, Abrahamic, Mosaic, Davidic, and new covenant. Cocceius explicitly formulated the **covenant of works** with Adam and the **covenant of grace** with sinners, and he developed a law–gospel hermeneutic that treated the Mosaic covenant as bearing a renewed legal character in redemptive history. He accelerated the shift from purely dogmatic to biblical-theological method, arguing that the Sabbath, the sacrificial system, and Israel’s national polity function as covenantal signs dialectically related to the progress of redemption. His school provoked debates over Sabbatarianism, confessional subscription, and the status of the Mosaic economy. Because Cocceius stressed historical progress and covenantal discontinuity at certain points, critics feared that he diminished the abiding normativity of the decalogue and flattened ecclesial continuity. The best reading is that Cocceius did not deny the moral law’s permanence but insisted that the Mosaic order as treaty had a covenantal legal aspect tied to Israel’s national administration, anticipating later articulation of **republication**. Exam questions often press whether this makes Moses a covenant of works for Israel’s salvation. The correct cocceian answer, and the one I defend, is no: the legal form under Moses typologically re-enacts an Adamic probation at the national level for the sake of revelation, not justification, which remains by faith in the promised Messiah. Cocceius therefore reframed the conceptual center by showing how the same gracious promise can take on a legal form for pedagogical and typological ends.

Herman Witsius (1636–1708) provided the classic synthesis and stabilization of **federal theology** in The Economy of the Covenants. Writing after the Cocceian controversies, he mediated between progressive biblical-theological insights and scholastic precision, and his system secured confessional Protestantism’s covenantal grammar for centuries. Witsius argues for three foundational covenants: the **covenant of redemption** as the eternal counsel and oath between Father and Son in the Spirit, the **covenant of works** made with Adam with life promised on condition of perfect obedience, and the **covenant of grace** administered through successive economies, one substance under diverse administrations. He grounds the Adamic **federal headship** in Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15, defends the reality of probation in Genesis 2, and integrates the active and passive obedience of Christ by presenting the Son as fulfilling the broken covenant of works and securing the blessings of the covenant of grace. Witsius’ development of the **pactum salutis** is not a speculative multiplication of will in God but a way of speaking of the ordered roles of the persons in the one divine will as it concerns redemption. Confronted with the objection that an intratrinitarian covenant violates **divine simplicity**, Witsius’ line can be defended: it is an economic-terminological way of marking the eternal counsel whereby the Father gives a people to the Son and the Son undertakes their obedience, all within the undivided operations of the Trinity. Witsius also stabilizes the relation of law and gospel by distinguishing the moral law in itself from the Mosaic covenant as a whole. For him, Moses is an administration of the **covenant of grace**, yet with an added legal component oriented to typology and national life. Against the claim that this mixture collapses grace, he distinguishes the soteric substance, always Christ by faith, from the civil-ceremonial overlay in which Israel’s obedience is instrumentally related to temporal blessings and curses. Witsius thereby answers the examiners’ insistence on doctrinal coherence across loci: he grounds justification in Christ alone, maintains the third use of the law, secures sacramental continuity, and keeps the church visible and corporate according to the Abrahamic covenant.

Geerhardus Vos (1862–1949) recast covenant theology along a rigorous redemptive-historical axis without abandoning its dogmatic core. In his Biblical Theology and Pauline Eschatology, Vos shows that covenant is the form of kingdom and communion from creation onward. Pre-fall communion had an eschatological goal, and the sabbath stamped that telos onto the Adamic probation. Thus the **covenant of works** is not a Pelagian ladder but a divinely appointed pathway to higher, immutable life. Vos’s method is to let the epochs of revelation speak their own idiom, and he strengthens the tie between covenant and eschatology: the last Adam comes under the law to fulfill the probation and inaugurate the new creation. By distinguishing historia salutis and ordo salutis, he clarifies how Christ’s representative obedience is historically accomplished and then applied to believers. Vos’s biblical theology also sharpens the reading of the Mosaic economy as typological and prophetic. The national form of Israel’s life prosecutes an **eschatological intrusion** of judgment and blessing that anticipates final realities in proleptic form. This, in turn, demands a refined account of **typology** that both secures fulfillment in Christ and guards ecclesial continuity with Abraham, since the church inherits the promises not by natural descent but by union with Christ. Vos’s contribution matters because it gives exegetical ballast to the older federal structure and directly counters the charge that covenant architecture was a scholastic imposition upon the text. His work makes covenantal unity a function of Scripture’s own historical progression, not merely a systematizer’s grid.

Meredith G. Kline (1922–2007) advanced federal theology by bringing ancient Near Eastern treaty studies to bear on biblical covenants and by clarifying the Mosaic economy’s legal form in relation to grace. In Treaty of the Great King and Kingdom Prologue, he shows that the structure of Sinai mirrors **suzerain-vassal treaty** patterns, with preamble, historical prologue, stipulations, witnesses, blessings, and curses. This illuminates Mosaic law as the treaty constitution of a theocratic kingdom. In By Oath Consigned, he argues that circumcision and baptism are oath-signs that bind to covenant sanctions, and that the sacraments function as rites of commitment to the divine Lord who swears to save and to judge. Kline’s most controversial contribution is his articulation of **republication**: the thesis that the Sinai covenant, while an administration of the **covenant of grace** in terms of ultimate soteriology, republishes in typological form the works principle for Israel’s tenure in the land. He contends that Leviticus 26 and Deuteronomy 28 tie national blessing and curse to corporate obedience, that the land promise functions typologically of the new creation, and that exile’s curse images Adam’s expulsion. An examiner will ask whether this fractures justification by faith alone or makes Moses a legal covenant unto salvation. The Klinean and the position I defend answer no, because the same covenant administration bears multiple layers: in relation to eternal life, the Abrahamic promise remains in force and salvation is by faith in Christ; in relation to the typal kingdom, works function as theocratic conditions for the enjoyment or loss of temporal blessings. This dual-aspect reading explains Paul’s complex handling of law and promise in Galatians 3 and the tearing down of the dividing wall in Ephesians 2. It guards the unity of the **one covenant under diverse administrations** while honoring the legal cast of Sinai in the ANE treaty sense. Kline’s oath and curse analysis further secures the sacraments as covenantal seals of both promise and warning, answering the charge that Reformed sacramental theology licenses presumption: to receive the sign is to be bound to the Lord who sanctifies and judges.

These figures together chart the field and frame the three sets of debates examiners will press. Internally, the first question is the reality and content of the **covenant of works**. Is there a covenant in Genesis 2 without the term, and does life hinge on obedience? The best case is cumulative: the life sanction tied to the tree, the curse warning, Hosea 6.7’s probable allusion to Adam’s transgression of a covenant, Paul’s federal reading in Romans 5 and the parallel between the first and last Adam in 1 Corinthians 15 together establish a probationary economy with Adam as **federal head**, promising eschatological advancement on condition of obedience. The covenant of works is not a denial of grace in creation but a denial that saving grace is necessary to merit what is due by God’s original justice. It is the necessary backdrop for the Son’s obedience and thus for imputation. The second internal debate is the nature of the Mosaic covenant. Is it an administration of grace, a covenant of works, or a tertium quid. The position defended here, following Witsius, Vos, and Kline, is that Sinai, in its soteric substance, is an administration of the **covenant of grace**, yet as a theocratic treaty it contains a principled, typological **republication** of the works principle for Israel’s national life in the land. This resolves the law–gospel tension in Galatians 3 by locating the law’s curse-bearing economy typologically and by preserving Abrahamic promise as the higher, prior principle. Third, the relation of covenant to election must be clarified. Election is the decree of God’s gracious choice in Christ; covenant is the historical administration of communion. Not all covenant members are elect, which explains warning passages, apostasy from the visible church, and the reality of hypocrites. This distinction undergirds both assurance and ecclesial discipline. Fourth, the continuity of the Abrahamic covenant and the question of baptism. If the covenant with Abraham is the form of the **covenant of grace** under which the church lives until Christ, then the principle of household administration and the extension of the sign to believers and their children carries into the new covenant, though the ceremonial form of the sign changes. Paul’s identification of circumcision and baptism as signally related in Colossians 2, his call that the promise remains for you and your children in Acts 2, and his assertion that believers are the seed of Abraham in Galatians 3 together argue for sacramental continuity. To the examiner who objects that the new covenant is only with the regenerate and so excludes infants, the response is that Jeremiah’s newness is located in efficacy and extent, not in the abolition of a mixed **visible church**. The apostles’ handling of the household principle and the practice of including entire households accord with the Abrahamic administration of promise. Fifth, the **pactum salutis**. Critics argue that a covenant among the persons of the Trinity compromises **divine simplicity**. The Reformed answer is to construe the pactum as a way of indexing the trinitarian taxis in the one simple will, not a contract among three wills. The Father purposes, the Son undertakes, the Spirit applies, and this ordered unity grounds the certainty of the **covenant of grace** in history.

Externally, rival frameworks sharpen the contours of the doctrine. Dispensationalism insists on an Israel–church dualism and a hard discontinuity of law and gospel. It often resists the unitive force of the Abrahamic covenant and restricts the fulfillment of land promises to an ethnic nation. The Reformed answer is to argue from Paul’s own hermeneutic: the seed is Christ and those united to him by faith, the dividing wall has been removed, and the land functions as a type of the new creation, now inherited by all the saints. Hebrews reads the cultus as shadow fulfilled in Christ’s priesthood and sacrifice, uniting the people of God under a single priest-king. Progressive covenantalism reconfigures continuity by collapsing the old covenant into a non-salvific national charter and arguing for the new covenant as exclusively regenerate, thereby denying infant baptism while maintaining a canonical and Christocentric unity. The Reformed case presses the Abrahamic backbone and the abiding household structure of administration, and it queries whether their view undervalues the soteric nature of the old covenant’s promise albeit under type and shadow. Lutheran concerns about covenant schemas often focus on the law–gospel relation and a fear that covenant introduces a legalistic mode. The Reformed response is to anchor the law–gospel distinction in the two Adams and to insist that the **covenant of grace** is unconditional in its establishment yet conditional in its administration, where Spirit-wrought faith receives Christ and Spirit-wrought obedience evidences union. The New Perspective’s covenantal nomism claims that first-century Judaism was about covenant maintenance rather than works righteousness, and it reads Paul’s contrast as sociological boundary markers rather than soteriological antithesis. The Reformed position answers that Paul’s polemic encompasses both boundary markers and deeper legalism, because law, as works principle, cannot justify fallen sinners, and the grace of the new covenant fulfills the older promises.

Contemporary stress tests erupt at the nexus of justification and covenant membership. The Federal Vision movement tried to relocate justification within the matrix of covenantal faithfulness, thereby blurring the instrumentality of faith alone and suggesting a form of final justification by Spirit-wrought loyalty. A Westminster defense must be sharp: the covenant structures do not alter the material cause of justification, which is Christ’s righteousness imputed and received by faith alone. The sacraments, as covenantal oath-signs, bind to blessing and curse, but they do not create a second instrument beside faith. The republication controversy raises fears that affirming a works principle under Moses will make sanctification the ground of assurance. The reply is that typological works concerned national tenure and were never the path to eternal life; they functioned as prophecy in event. In personal soteriology, Christ’s fulfillment of the **covenant of works** as last Adam is the sole ground of life, and the Spirit applies that righteousness. Finally, modern hermeneutical challenges demand clarity on **typology**. Does typology dissolve historical reality into allegory. Vos and Kline secure the typological shape of the old covenant by rooting it in covenantal oaths, sanctions, and kingdom administration. The types are historical and covenantal, ordered by oath and curse, and fulfilled in Christ and his body, the church.

Having set the historical and polemical frame, the constructive defense proceeds by defining the essence and the order of the covenants. A biblical covenant is a divinely sanctioned relational arrangement, instituted by oath, governing communion and mission, and carrying **oath and curse** sanctions. From Eden, God structured human destiny by covenantal ordering. In Adam, the race stood under a probationary **covenant of works**, with a promissory telos beyond mere natural life. When Adam broke the covenant, the curse fell and death reigned. The gospel does not negates the demand for righteousness but provides it in the second Adam. Before time, the Father and the Son, in the Spirit, determined a **covenant of redemption** whereby the Son would assume human nature, fulfill the covenant of works, bear the curse, merit glory, and receive a people, and the Spirit would apply those benefits. In history, the **covenant of grace** is the administration of that plan to the elect and to the visible people that surround them. It begins in embryo with the protevangelium, assumes ecclesial form in Abraham, takes on law-treaty structure at Sinai to prosecute holiness and typology, focuses royal hope in David, and arrives at eschatological fullness in Christ’s new covenant, which is new in efficacy, scope, and clarity, not in substance of promise. Under this one covenant in its various administrations the people receive sacramental signs that seal the promise and summon to covenant loyalty. Because the church remains visibly mixed until judgment, the signs bind both wheat and tares to the Lord of the covenant.

This structural account coheres across loci. In Christology, the second Adam’s active obedience is located in the **covenant of works** without which his righteousness cannot be vicariously imputed. In soteriology, justification, adoption, and sanctification are distributed as benefits of the **covenant of grace**, not rewards for meeting conditions. In ecclesiology, the church is the household of God and the new covenant people, continuous with Abraham’s seed by faith, incorporating Jew and Gentile, and marked by a **sacramental union** of sign and thing signified that neither collapses sign into thing nor severs them. In ethics, the moral law abides as norm, now written on the heart in new covenant power. In eschatology, the typological land gives way to the renewed heavens and earth, the theocracy to the kingdom of Christ seated at God’s right hand. In Scripture, covenant is the architectonic principle that holds together narrative, law, wisdom, and prophecy under a unified plan.

Examiner objections concentrate at critical joints. Does the Adamic covenant impose merit foreign to grace. The answer is that prelapsarian obedience is not meritorious in the condign sense but covenantally rewarded according to divine ordination, and that the grace of creation does not exclude justice. Does **republication** contradict Paul’s emphasis on promise. It clarifies Paul by distinguishing the soteric core from the typal layer, ensuring that the promise remains prior and superior and that law’s pedagogical function is honored. Does the new covenant exclude infants. The prophetic promise of newness refers to internalization and forgiveness adequacy in Christ’s priesthood, not to a dismantling of the household principle; the apostolic practice of household baptism and the Abrahamic thread support the inclusion of children in the visible people. Does the **pactum salutis** divide the divine will. No, it articulates the personal ordering of one will in God, safeguarding the trinitarian ground of redemption and strengthening assurance. Does covenant theology flatten biblical diversity. Properly done, as Vos and Kline show, covenant is how diversity is ordered: each administration brings distinct features to the one promise and always drives forward to Christ.

Among the decisive figures we have seen how the doctrine acquired its present form and how it answers present challenges. Calvin establishes the unity of the **covenant of grace** and sacramental continuity. Cocceius accentuates historical unfolding and law–gospel differentiation within covenant, pushing toward a more robust reading of Mosaic law. Witsius stabilizes the system, securing the **covenant of works**, the **covenant of grace**, and the **covenant of redemption**, and fencing justification. Vos re-roots the architecture in the Bible’s own redemptive-historical progression, integrating eschatology and covenant. Kline sharpens treaty form, sacramental oath, and **republication**, explaining how the Mosaic legal layer can coexist with Abrahamic promise without compromising sola fide. These complementary achievements yield a defensible position: the triune God administers his kingdom through the covenants; Adam’s probation is real and foundational; Christ fulfills the broken covenant by his obedience; the Mosaic law–treaty republishes works in a typal national sphere; the Abrahamic promise structures the people of God and grounds infant initiation; and the new covenant is the eschatological arrival of the one gracious promise in Christ.

The result is conceptual mastery and pastoral coherence. Biblically, it explains why Paul argues as he does about promise and law, and why Hebrews can both honor and transcend the old covenant. Theologically, it integrates justification, sanctification, and ecclesiology under the same federal headship. Historically, it accounts for the crises of exile and restoration and for the emergence of a transnational church as the true Israel. Ethically, it secures the abiding moral law without subjecting believers to the civil-ceremonial yoke. Sacramentally, it explains both the gravity and the comfort of the signs as oath-seals. Philosophically, it preserves divine initiative, not contractarian parity. And apologetically, it counters external systems by offering a single, Christ-centered plotline that is both comprehensive and flexible enough to honor the textual details. This is not a speculative grid but the Bible’s own covenantal scaffolding. That scaffolding prepares a minister to stand under examination and to preach law and gospel with clarity, to administer the sacraments with sobriety, to guard justification with vigilance, and to guide the church as the Abrahamic household moving toward the city whose architect and builder is God.

- Core definition: A biblical covenant is a divinely instituted, oath-bound administration of God’s kingdom that establishes communion and obligations with sanctions of **oath and curse**, structuring history under the **covenant of works**, **covenant of redemption**, and **covenant of grace**.
- Decisive figures:
  - John Calvin: unity of the **covenant of grace**, Abrahamic continuity, sacramental signs and the **visible church**.
  - Johannes Cocceius: historical unfolding, explicit **covenant of works**, Mosaic law’s legal form, proto-**republication**.
  - Herman Witsius: classic synthesis; **pactum salutis**; stabilizes law–gospel, Mosaic as grace with legal overlay.
  - Geerhardus Vos: redemptive-historical method; eschatological telos of the **covenant of works**; typology anchored in history.
  - Meredith Kline: **suzerain-vassal treaty** form; sacraments as oath-signs; typological **republication** under Moses.
- Debates:
  - Internal: reality of Adamic **covenant of works**; Mosaic character and **republication**; covenant–election relation; Abrahamic continuity and baptism; coherence of the **pactum salutis** with **divine simplicity**.
  - External: dispensational Israel–church dualism; progressive covenantalism’s regenerate-only ecclesiology; Lutheran law–gospel worries; New Perspective’s covenantal nomism.
  - Contemporary: Federal Vision on justification; republication controversy; typology and land promise fulfillment.

- Likely examiner questions:
  - Is the covenant of works biblical without the term? Yes, by Genesis 2’s sanctions, Hosea 6.7, and Paul’s Adam–Christ **federal headship**.
  - Does republication compromise sola fide? No, it conditions typal land tenure, not eternal life, preserving promise priority.
  - Does the new covenant exclude infants? No, Jeremiah’s newness concerns efficacy; Abrahamic household continuity and apostolic practice support inclusion.
  - Does the pactum salutis violate divine simplicity? No, it names the ordered roles of one will in God, grounding redemption.
  - How does this bear on justification? Christ fulfills the **covenant of works**; his righteousness is imputed and received by faith alone.
