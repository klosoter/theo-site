# Friedrich Schleiermacher (1768–1834)

Friedrich Schleiermacher reinterpreted **covenant theology** through the lens of **religious consciousness** and the **feeling of absolute dependence**, rather than traditional confessional formulations. He critiqued the historic **Reformed loci** of the **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, emphasizing instead the communal and experiential dimensions of faith as expressed in the church. Schleiermacher’s approach marked a significant departure from classical **confessionalism** by prioritizing the inner life of the believer over external doctrinal systems.

- "Dogmatic formulas are only the outer expression of the inner life of the church." (The Christian Faith)

## Key Works

- The Christian Faith (Der christliche Glaube)
- Brief Outline of the Study of Theology
- On Religion: Speeches to Its Cultured Despisers
- Christian Ethics

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Primacy of Religious Experience**
    - The essence of religion is found in the **feeling of absolute dependence**, not in external covenants.
    - Confessional formulations are secondary to the lived experience of faith.
    - The **covenant** is understood as the communal consciousness of redemption within the church.
    - Doctrinal systems must serve the vitality of religious life, not constrain it.
    - "Religion’s essence is neither thinking nor acting, but intuition and feeling." (On Religion)

2. **Rejection of Traditional Covenant Schemes**
    - Schleiermacher does not affirm the classic **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** dichotomy.
    - He views such distinctions as speculative and lacking experiential grounding.
    - The unity of redemption is stressed over historical or legal covenants.
    - The focus shifts from legal arrangements to the transformative relationship with God.
    - "The distinction between a covenant of works and a covenant of grace is not essential to the Christian faith." (The Christian Faith)

3. **Church as Covenant Community**
    - The church is the living context where the **covenant** is realized existentially.
    - Confessional statements are expressions of the church’s evolving self-understanding.
    - The communal aspect of faith supersedes juridical covenant categories.
    - The church mediates the ongoing experience of redemption.
    - "The church is the fellowship of believers in whom the Spirit of Christ dwells." (The Christian Faith)

4. **Doctrine as Expression, Not Foundation**
    - Doctrines, including those of the **covenant**, are articulations of the church’s faith experience.
    - Confessional formulations are historically conditioned and open to revision.
    - Theological statements must be rooted in the actual piety of the community.
    - The authority of doctrine is derivative, not primary.
    - "Dogmatics is the science which presents the Christian faith as it exists at the present time in a given Christian community." (The Christian Faith)

5. **Christ as the Fulfillment of Covenant**
    - Christ embodies the unity between God and humanity, transcending legalistic covenant frameworks.
    - The person and work of Christ are central to Schleiermacher’s understanding of redemption.
    - The **new covenant** is realized in the transformative presence of Christ within the church.
    - The focus is on participation in Christ rather than adherence to confessional formulas.
    - "In Christ, the divine and the human are united in a way that constitutes the true covenant." (The Christian Faith)

## Legacy and Impact

Schleiermacher’s reconfiguration of **covenant theology** profoundly influenced modern Protestant thought, shifting the focus from external confessional formulations to the inner dynamics of faith and community. His emphasis on **religious experience** and the evolving nature of doctrine challenged traditional Reformed categories and paved the way for later liberal and existential theologies. While often critiqued by confessional theologians, his legacy endures in ongoing debates about the relationship between doctrine, experience, and the life of the church.
