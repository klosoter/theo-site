### TITLE
One Covenant or Two? Federal Vision, monocovenantalism, and the Reformed law-gospel architecture

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: late twentieth to early twenty-first century debates within Reformed **covenant theology** touching **justification** and **ecclesiology**.
- Core question: Is there one covenant of grace before and after the fall (**monocovenantalism**) or a real prelapsarian **covenant of works** distinct from the postlapsarian **covenant of grace**.
- Principal figures or schools: **Norman Shepherd** (obedient faith), **Douglas Wilson/Peter Leithart** and Auburn Avenue/CREC (Federal Vision), **Meredith Kline** (republication and works principle), **John Murray** (Adamic administration), **Old Princeton** and **Westminster divines** as confessional anchors.
- Key concepts/loci: **objectivity of the covenant**, **visible/invisible church**, **union with Christ**, **imputation of active obedience**, **law/gospel contrast**, **sacramental efficacy**, and **final judgment according to works**.
- Major texts/events/works: **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647; **Witsius, Economy of the Covenants** 1677; **Murray, Covenant of Grace** essays 1954; **Kline, By Oath Consigned** 1968 and Kingdom Prologue 2000; **Shepherd controversy** 1975–1981; **Auburn Avenue Conference** 2002; **Joint Federal Vision Statement** 2007; **PCA report** 2007; **OPC report** 2006; **URCNA Nine Points** 2007.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: Confessional **bi-covenantalism** with typological **republication** at Sinai versus **monocovenantalism** with covenant objectivity; parallel contrasts with **Lutheran law/gospel** and **Roman Catholic new law** accounts; modern influence from **New Perspective on Paul**.
- Legacy cue: Ongoing denominational discipline and scholarship over **justification sola fide**, **sacramental practice**, and the interpretive weight of **Romans 5** and **Galatians 3**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Federal Vision names a cluster of proposals within contemporary Reformed theology that emphasize the **objectivity of the covenant**, robust **sacramental efficacy**, and a covenantal account of **union with Christ** in which all the baptized are said to be truly, though not indefectibly, united to Christ. The label **monocovenantalism** designates the claim that from creation to consummation God relates to humanity by one gracious covenant, rejecting the classic Reformed distinction between a prelapsarian **covenant of works** and a postlapsarian **covenant of grace**. These proposals sit at the juncture of **soteriology** and **ecclesiology**, but their fulcrum is in **covenant theology**, where they press the relation of law and gospel, the nature of faith and faithfulness, and the status of sacramental signs.

Within the Reformed confessions, the **Westminster Standards** articulate a bicovenantal architecture: the first covenant with Adam promised life upon perfect and personal obedience, and after the fall God established a **covenant of grace** administered through promise, law, and sacrament until its new covenant realization in Christ. Federal Vision and monocovenantalist writers dispute either the confessional exegesis of the prelapsarian covenant or its descriptive labels, appealing instead to **covenant continuity** and to the unity of divine favor from Eden to Zion. The dogmatic location of the dispute therefore includes **Bibliology** in terms of hermeneutics, **Theology Proper** in terms of divine benevolence and justice, and **Anthropology** regarding Adam as federal head and the meaning of obedience.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The foundational locus is Genesis 1–3, with debate centering on whether an Edenic **works principle** is taught even without the explicit use of *berith*. Confessional defenders point to the probationary command and sanction in Genesis 2:16–17, the tree of life as sacramental pledge of eschatological advancement, and to Hosea 6:7, where Israel "like Adam" transgressed the **covenant**. They argue that this suggests an original **covenant of works** oriented to confirmed life upon obedience. Monocovenantalists reply that the narrative displays unmerited divine favor from the outset, that Hosea 6:7 is textually ambiguous, and that Eden already exhibits **grace and gift**, not merit in the strict sense, thus supporting **monocovenantal grace**.

Pauline texts loom large. Romans 5:12–21 frames Adam and Christ as two representatives such that condemnation and righteousness arrive through their respective **federal heads**. Confessional readers infer a law-based probation for Adam, given that Christ secures righteousness by his **obedience**, classically articulated as the **imputation of active obedience**. Federal Vision writers emphasize the communal and ecclesial dimensions of being in Adam or in Christ, arguing that the text foregrounds the **objective covenantal status** into which baptism places a person, even as perseverance is required. Galatians 3 and 4 treat Abrahamic promise vis-a-vis Mosaic law: confessional interpreters discern a typological **republication** of the works principle at Sinai, while Federal Vision and monocovenant proponents stress that the Mosaic administration is fundamentally one with the **covenant of grace**.

Texts on judgment and obedience are also pressed. Romans 2:6–13, James 2:14–26, and Matthew 7:21–23 declare that God will judge according to works, and that faith without works is dead. Federal Vision voices appeal to such passages to articulate a **final vindication according to works**, not as meritorious ground but as the Spirit-wrought evidential criterion for covenant members. Confessional exponents reply that this is standard **Reformed orthodoxy**, provided the exclusive instrumental role of faith is maintained in **justification**, where *pistis* receives Christ and his righteousness apart from *erga nomou*. The debate is not the existence of judgment according to works but its relation to **forensic justification**.

Ecclesial and sacramental texts animate the discussion of **objectivity of the covenant**. Acts 2:39, 1 Corinthians 10:1–5, and Hebrews 6:4–6 are marshaled to show that some truly partake of new covenant privileges and yet fall away. Federal Vision insists that baptism indeed unites to Christ covenantally, rendering apostasy a real falling from **covenantal union**. Confessional critics alternate between conceding a real but non-salvific participation and insisting on the **visible/invisible church** distinction, appealing to John 6:37–40 and Ephesians 1:3–14 to maintain that decretally elect are infallibly saved while hypocrites share only in external benefits.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Seventeenth-century Reformed scholasticism codified the bi-covenantal schema. **Ursinus and Olevianus**, catechists of the Palatinate, adumbrated the relation of law and gospel as distinct yet complementary. **Turretin** in his Institutes 1679–1685 and **Witsius** in the Economy of the Covenants 1677 articulated a robust **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, giving precise status to Adamic probation and to Christ as the second Adam. The **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647 canonized this, reading Eden as a covenant with a works principle and the fall as the hinge for the advent of the **covenant of grace**.

In the twentieth century, **John Murray** questioned terminology while retaining substance. In essays from 1954 he framed an **Adamic administration**, preferring not to speak of merit, yet still asserting an eschatological confirmation conditioned on obedience. **Meredith Kline**, by contrast, defended the full **works principle**, developing a typological **republication** at Sinai in By Oath Consigned 1968 and later in Kingdom Prologue 2000, arguing that the Mosaic economy recapitulated the covenant of works on a national, typological plane. This intramural divergence set the stage for later appeals to **monocovenantalism**.

The watershed for contemporary controversy was the **Norman Shepherd** affair at Westminster Theological Seminary 1975–1981. Shepherd stressed **obedient faith**, eschewed a sharp **law/gospel antithesis**, and adjusted classroom formulations of **justification** and **assurance**. Although he affirmed sola fide, his teaching on final judgment and covenant conditionality provoked institutional review and his dismissal in 1981. His later The Call of Grace 2000 gathered these themes, and several future Federal Vision voices trace lines to his stress on **covenant faithfulness**. In 2002 the Auburn Avenue Pastors Conference catalyzed a movement, with **Douglas Wilson**, **Peter Leithart**, **Steve Schlissel**, and **John Barach** advocating a recovery of the **objectivity of the covenant**. The ensuing literature, including The Federal Vision 2004 and Auburn Avenue Theology, Pros and Cons 2004, pressed monocovenantal notes and sacramental realism. Denominational responses followed: the **OPC** report on justification 2006, **PCA** Ad Interim Report 2007, and **URCNA Nine Points** 2007, each warning against doctrinal formulations deemed contrary to the **Reformed confessions**. In 2007 a Joint Federal Vision Statement articulated theses on covenant, election, and justification, defending **monocovenantal grace** and covenantal union.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton exemplifies the confessional paradigm. **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield** upheld a real **covenant of works**, Adamic federal headship, and the **imputation of active obedience**, with a firm **visible/invisible church** distinction that protected the perseverance of the saints alongside the mixed character of the church. Their exegesis of **Romans 5** and **Galatians 3** stressed the forensic texture of **justification** and a strong law-gospel coordination in the economy of salvation.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism is more variegated. **Abraham Kuyper** affirmed covenant theology yet introduced **presumptive regeneration** within a robust ecclesiology, while **Herman Bavinck** maintained the bi-covenantal scheme and underscored the unity of special revelation. **Klaas Schilder** critiqued presumptive regeneration and emphasized **covenant objectivity and conditionality**, themes that resonate in later Federal Vision rhetoric. Still, the mainstream Dutch tradition did not deny a **covenant of works** and retained clear confessional boundaries around **justification sola fide**.

The Westminster tradition, read through the Standards and catechisms, preserves the classic **bi-covenantalism** that structures the relationship of **law and gospel**. The divines speak of God as condescending to reward obedience in the first covenant and of the **covenant of grace** as the sole postlapsarian means of life. This architecture grounds Christology, since the Mediator as second Adam fulfills all righteousness, and anchors **sacramentology**, since baptism and the Supper signify and seal benefits to the elect by the Spirit, without conferring an automatic **covenantal union** upon all participants.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The Roman Catholic tradition after **Trent 1547** construes salvation under the **new law of grace**, where the Spirit enables infused charity and the believer cooperates toward final justification. From the Reformed vantage, this is a kind of **monocovenantalism** in which the law/gospel distinction is largely collapsed and the moral life is internal to the covenant of salvation. Federal Vision sometimes sounds similar when it speaks of **final vindication according to works**, though its proponents insist on a forensic core; critics warn that the proximity to a **new law** paradigm is perilous.

Lutheran theology is marked by a principled **law/gospel distinction** and does not organize dogmatics under covenantal heads. It strongly resists a prelapsarian **covenant of works** as an organizing category, yet it preserves the condemning function of law from Adam onward. Lutheran worries about **monocovenantalism** mirror Reformed ones about blurring the forensic and the renovative, and caution against locating **assurance** in covenantal status rather than in Christ's alien righteousness received by faith alone.

Modern proposals such as the **New Perspective on Paul** associate justification with **covenant membership** and stress **covenantal nomism**. While the Federal Vision is not identical with the New Perspective, some FV writers draw from **N. T. Wright** and **E. P. Sanders** to reframe **justification** ecclesiologically and eschatologically. Analytic theology has revisited the coherence of **imputation**, with figures like **Oliver Crisp** defending the logical tenability of both passive and **active obedience imputation**, thereby supporting the classic **bi-covenantal** structure that Federal Vision often questions.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
First, the status of the **covenant of works**. Confessional polemicists argue that denying a works principle in Eden undermines Christ as second Adam and erodes the **imputation of active obedience**. Federal Vision and monocovenantalists counter that insisting on prelapsarian merit impugns **divine generosity** and misconstrues filial obedience. The exegetical crux remains **Hosea 6:7**, the tree of life, and the Adam-Christ parallel in **Romans 5**.

Second, **justification sola fide**. The 2006 OPC and 2007 PCA reports concluded that certain FV formulations compromise the exclusive instrumentality of faith by construing **faithfulness** as co-instrumental or by importing covenantal obedience into the ground of justification. Federal Vision replies that it distinguishes the ground, which is Christ alone, from the instrument, which is living faith that is never alone, and that its language of **final vindication** tracks James and Paul. The fear of a tilt toward a **new law** remains a central objection.

Third, **objectivity of the covenant** and sacramental efficacy. Critics allege that FV baptismal theology approaches **baptismal regeneration**, confuses the **visible/invisible church**, and renders apostasy a fall from true salvific union. FV proponents insist on a real but covenantal union distinct from decretal election, appealing to **1 Corinthians 10** and **Hebrews 6** to assert that one may be truly sanctified in some sense yet perish. The ecclesial boundary between sign and thing signified is the flashpoint.

Fourth, practical matters: **paedocommunion** and church discipline. Some FV churches practice paedocommunion on the basis of covenant membership, a practice at odds with many Reformed churches that require **credible profession**. Denominational cases, including the **Leithart trial** 2011–2013 within the PCA, reveal ongoing friction about how far one may go in emphasizing **covenant objectivity** without contravening subscription to the **Westminster Standards**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The debate implicates **Theology Proper** because it concerns how divine justice and goodness are displayed in prelapsarian promises; a properly ordered **law/gospel** grammar reflects the simplicity and faithfulness of God whose single will is executed diversely in redemptive history. In **Christology**, the dignity of Christ's mediatorial work is tied to a real **covenant of works**, for only then does his active and passive **obedience** fulfill what Adam failed to render. In **Anthropology**, Adamic **federal headship** grounds the solidarity of the human race and sets the stage for a corporate soteriology that must nevertheless safeguard personal faith.

In **Soteriology**, the status of **imputation**, **union with Christ**, and the instrumentality of faith are central. If all the baptized are in some sense united to Christ, one must articulate how **perseverance of the saints** and **assurance** are maintained without collapsing union into mere externality. In **Ecclesiology**, the distinction between the **visible and invisible church** must be carefully phrased so that sacramental realism does not relativize decretal election while still honoring the public, covenantal means by which God ordinarily saves. In **Eschatology**, the theme of **final judgment according to works** must be integrated with present justification without threatening the once-for-all verdict rendered in Christ.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Properly framed, the controversy prompts pastors to proclaim both the **free grace of the covenant** and the necessity of **Spirit-wrought obedience**. Catechesis can teach believers that God has always dealt with humanity by covenant, that Adam's failure is remedied only by Christ's **obedience**, and that faith alone receives him even as love necessarily follows. This guards against antinomianism without sliding into a **new law** mentality that conditions God's favor upon our performance.

Sacramentally, the church is challenged to recover the weight of **baptismal naming** and the gravity of the Lord's Table while retaining the confessional caution that sacraments do not confer **grace ex opere operato**. The language of **covenant objectivity** can be used to press home the responsibilities of church membership and the reality of **church discipline**, without conflating sign and thing signified. Pastors can preach **assurance** from Christ's finished work, while exhorting to perseverance as the ordinary path of the saints.

Liturgically, emphasizing the covenant's public character can shape services around **covenant renewal** structures, such as call, confession, consecration, communion, and commissioning, provided that the order of **gospel then obedience** remains clear. The result is a community that knows it is addressed by God, bound to Christ, and sent into the world, rejoicing that the **covenant of grace** does not abolish the law but writes it on the heart.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
At stake in the Federal Vision and monocovenantalism debate is whether Reformed theology can retain the confessional **covenant of works/covenant of grace** architecture while honoring the **objectivity of the covenant** and thereby safeguard both **justification sola fide** and the church's sacramental integrity.

### RECAP NOTES
- Setting and context: Late twentieth and early twenty-first century Reformed dispute over **covenant theology**, catalyzed by the **Shepherd controversy** 1975–1981 and the **Auburn Avenue Conference** 2002.
- Core focus: Whether Scripture teaches a prelapsarian **covenant of works** distinct from a postlapsarian **covenant of grace**, or a single **monocovenantalism** emphasizing covenant continuity and objectivity.
- Key figures and stances: **Meredith Kline** for works principle and **republication**; **John Murray** for terminological revision of the Adamic arrangement; **Norman Shepherd** for obedient faith; **Douglas Wilson/Peter Leithart** for **Federal Vision** emphases on covenant objectivity; **Old Princeton** defending **imputation** and bi-covenantalism.
- Concepts and definitions: **objectivity of the covenant** asserts real covenantal union for the baptized; **imputation of active obedience** undergirds Christ as second Adam; **visible/invisible church** distinguishes covenant membership from decretal election; **final vindication according to works** as evidential judgment.
- Turning points and outcomes: **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647 established bi-covenantalism; **OPC report** 2006, **PCA report** 2007, and **URCNA Nine Points** 2007 rejected FV distinctives as out of accord with the **Standards**; **Joint Federal Vision Statement** 2007 defended FV theses.
- Distinctive Reformed contrasts: Maintain **law/gospel** coordination without collapse; affirm **forensic justification by faith alone**; uphold sacraments as means of grace without **ex opere operato**; distinguish **covenant administration** from eternal election.
- Legacy and relevance: Ongoing debates over **Romans 5** and **Galatians 3**, pastoral practice in **baptism and the Supper**, and safeguarding **assurance** while exhorting **perseverance** within a confessional **covenant framework**.
