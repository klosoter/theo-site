### TITLE
One saving covenant, many administrations: grace promised and fulfilled in Christ

### PREVIEW NOTES
- 16th–21st centuries; dogmatic location at the nexus of Theology Proper, Soteriology, and Ecclesiology within a Reformed federal framework.
- Core question: How does the one saving purpose of God relate to multiple covenants across Scripture, and what is the nature of the **covenant of grace** with respect to faith, Christ, and the church?
- Figures and schools: **Heinrich Bullinger** (covenant continuity), **John Calvin** (foedus as gospel form), **Johannes Cocceius** vs **Francis Turretin** (developmental vs scholastic federalism), **Westminster Divines** (1646–1648), **Old Princeton** (A. A. Hodge, B. B. Warfield), **Dutch Neo-Calvinists** (Kuyper, Bavinck), **Meredith Kline** (republication).
- Key concepts: **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, **pactum salutis**, **federal headship**, **substance and administration**, **covenant signs and seals**, **law-gospel distinction**, **union with Christ**.
- Major texts and events: Gen 3:15; Gen 12, 15, 17; Exod 19–24; 2 Sam 7; Jer 31; Heb 8–10; Luke 22:20; WCF ch. 7 (1646–1647); Witsius, Economy of the Covenants (1677); Kline, Treaty of the Great King (1963).
- Reformed and alternatives: Reformed federalism vs **Roman Catholic sacramental economy**, **Lutheran law-gospel without covenant of works**, **Dispensational economies**, **Barth’s election-as-covenant**.
- Legacy: Ongoing debates on **Mosaic republication**, **conditions in the covenant**, **paedobaptism**, and Israel-church continuity.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The **covenant of grace** is the postlapsarian divine arrangement by which God freely promises and secures salvation for sinners through faith in the Mediator, Jesus Christ, administered under multiple historical forms yet possessing one saving substance. In classic Reformed usage, it is distinguished from the **covenant of works** with Adam as federal head prior to the fall, and related to the **pactum salutis** or covenant of redemption as the intra-trinitarian ground of history. As stated in Westminster Confession of Faith 7.1–7 (1646–1647), God, by voluntary condescension, sovereignly enters into covenant, revealing the one way of life through Christ before and after his advent. This doctrine occupies a central dogmatic location because it coordinates **divine decree and economy**, grounds the unity of Scripture, and orders soteriology and ecclesiology around **federal headship** and sacramental practice.

The material content of the **covenant of grace** is the communication of Christ and his benefits to the elect by the Holy Spirit through the Word and sacraments, received by faith alone. Its formal principle is promissory and sworn, fitting covenantal idioms of oath, sign, and mediation. The doctrine also governs the relation of **law and gospel**, since the law as covenant of works condemns, while the gospel as covenant of grace justifies the ungodly in Christ. This organization of loci safeguards **divine immutability** and **mercy**: God saves in one way in many administrations, without change in his purpose, yet with redemptive-historical progress fitting his wisdom.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture’s covenant idiom employs *berith* in the Hebrew Bible and *diatheke* in the Greek New Testament to denote God’s binding, oath-structured dealings with his people. The protoevangelium of Genesis 3:15 is the first promissory shape of the **covenant of grace**, where the seed promise anticipates a victorious mediator. The Abrahamic iterations in Genesis 12, 15, and 17 deepen the promise by oath and sign: God alone passes between the pieces in Genesis 15, enacting a **unilateral, grace-grounded pledge**, while circumcision in Genesis 17 and Romans 4:11 functions as a **covenant sign and seal** of the righteousness of faith. Paul’s exegesis in Galatians 3:16 construes the *zera* as ultimately singular in Christ, thereby identifying the Abrahamic covenant’s substance with **Christological fulfillment**.

The Sinai covenant in Exodus 19–24 introduces conditional language and commands, yet it is nested within promissory deliverance, as indicated by the redemption preface in Exodus 20:2. The Reformed tradition commonly construes Sinai as an administration of the **covenant of grace**, either with a law covenant overlay to expose sin or with typological **republication of the covenant of works** to drive Israel to the promise. Deuteronomy’s oath-renewal and the blessings and curses underscore covenant sanctions while keeping a promissory line toward heart circumcision in Deuteronomy 30:6, which Paul reads as Spirit-wrought renewal. The Davidic covenant in 2 Samuel 7 and Psalm 89 locates royal mediation in a line culminating in the **Messianic king**, integrating political and priestly hopes into the single saving covenant.

Prophetic new covenant promises gather the threads of forgiveness and renewal. Jeremiah 31:31–34 and Ezekiel 36:25–27 anticipate **new covenant** realities of law internalization and Spirit empowerment that Hebrews 8–10 applies to Christ’s priestly work and once-for-all sacrifice. Jesus’ words at the Last Supper, Luke 22:20, ratify the new covenant in his blood, fulfilling the sacrificial oath structure in a **mediator’s death**. Romans 5:12–21 and 1 Corinthians 15:45–49 provide the federal frame: Adam and Christ as two heads whose acts are imputed to their people, revealing that the **covenant of grace** realizes the eschatological righteousness of the second Adam. Hebrews 11’s parade of faith confirms one people of God across time, sustained by one promise in diverse administrations, uniting **typology and fulfillment** under a single salvific covenant.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic voices lacked a formed federal schema but supplied crucial motifs. **Irenaeus’s recapitulation** gathered Adam and Christ into one narrative of undoing and restoration, anticipating **federal headship**. **Augustine** articulated the unity of the two Testaments and the priority of grace against Pelagius, prefiguring the **law-gospel** pattern embedded in covenant theology. Medieval thinkers used covenant sparingly, though **Peter Lombard** linked sacraments to promise, and **Aquinas** articulated one divine economy culminating in Christ, even if he did not formalize a **covenant of grace**. Late medieval **pactum theology** in nominalism, emphasizing God’s covenantal demands and acceptation, sometimes skewed toward merit, provoking Reformation corrections about **grace alone**.

The Reformation era matured covenant thought. **Heinrich Bullinger’s De Testamento (1534)** framed the unity of the one testament administered before and after Christ, and **Calvin’s foedus** theme coordinated election, Word, and sacrament without yet positing a two-covenant schema as later scholastics would. The **Heidelberg Catechism (1563)** and **Olevianus** treated the gospel as covenantal, centering on Christ the **mediator of the covenant**. Seventeenth-century federalism crystallized in **Cocceius** and **Voetius** debates: Cocceius stressed historical progression and Sabbath eschatology, while **Turretin** and later **Witsius, The Economy of the Covenants (1677)**, stabilized definitions, distinguishing **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, and **pactum salutis** in a scholastic yet pastoral register.

The **Westminster Standards (1646–1648)** canonized the doctrine in English-speaking Reformed churches: WCF 7 asserts the one **covenant of grace** variously administered under law and gospel, with Christ as substance and the sacraments as seals; Larger Catechism 20–36 coordinates **federal headship** and imputation. Post-Westminster, debates on the **Marrow Controversy (1717–1723)** in Scotland centered on free grace and conditions, with **Thomas Boston** and the Erskines defending the unconditional offer against neonomian tendencies. In the nineteenth century, **Old Princeton** preserved federal imputation and covenant unity, while the **Dutch Neo-Calvinists** like **Kuyper** and **Bavinck** broadened covenant’s scope to culture and common grace, retaining the **covenant of grace** as the church’s formative charter. In the twentieth century, **Vos** deepened biblical-theological grounding; **Meredith Kline** reread Sinai through Ancient Near Eastern treaty forms, renewing debate over **Mosaic republication** within the architecture of the covenant of grace.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster** account defines one **covenant of grace** administered under the law and the gospel, with the Old Testament administrations foreshadowing Christ and the New Testament exhibiting greater clarity and efficacy. The sacraments of each administration are **covenant signs and seals** of Christ and his benefits, with baptism and the Lord’s Supper succeeding circumcision and Passover. The law as a covenant of works condemns, while the moral law continues as rule of life within the covenant of grace, preserving the **law-gospel distinction** without antinomianism.

**Old Princeton** emphasized **federal headship and imputation** as covenantal structures anchoring justification. A. A. Hodge and B. B. Warfield saw Romans 5 as paradigmatic, defending Adam-Christ parallelism and covenant unity against both moral influence and evolutionary historicism. This federalism underwrites **forensic justification** by the imputed righteousness of Christ, with faith as the sole instrument in the **covenant of grace**. Their ecclesiology preserved visible and invisible church distinctions within the one covenantal people, protecting assurance and catholicity.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** stream treated covenant as the organizing principle of redemptive history and Christian life. **Abraham Kuyper** linked the **covenant of grace** to the doctrine of the church and the family, developing the practice of presuming covenant children as recipients of covenant nurture. **Herman Bavinck** integrated covenant with **union with Christ**, common grace, and cultural mandate, insisting that grace restores and perfects nature within the covenantal trajectory. Later, **Meredith Kline** employed suzerainty treaty patterns to argue that Sinai bears a **works principle** as a typological overlay within the administration of the **covenant of grace**, clarifying how law functions to drive to Christ while preserving the unity of the way of salvation.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The **Roman Catholic** tradition frames salvation as a sacramental economy rather than a discrete **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** contrast. The Council of Trent (1547) presented justifying grace as infused and cooperated with, interpreting the new covenant as **new law** written in the heart that enables obedience, with the sacraments as instrumental causes. Vatican II’s Lumen Gentium (1964) expands the covenantal people to include degrees of participation. While covenant language appears, the structural role assigned in Reformed federalism to **federal headship** and forensic imputation is reconfigured toward sacramental participation.

The **Lutheran** tradition accentuates the **law-gospel distinction** without positing a prelapsarian **covenant of works** or a unifying covenant of grace across testaments in the same sense. Lutheran theology stresses promise in Christ and treats Old Testament covenants as preparatory but is cautious about covenant as a doctrinal architectonic, fearing a slide into legal conditions. Justification by faith alone stands as the center, with sacramental union rather than **covenant signs and seals** as the dominant idiom.

Modern evangelical options include **Dispensationalism**, which maps multiple economies or dispensations with varying divine administrations, often distinguishing Israel and the church more sharply than Reformed federalism. Classic Scofieldian forms (1909) fragment the unity of the **covenant of grace**, while **progressive dispensationalism** (Blaising and Bock, 1993) moves toward continuity and inaugurated kingdom themes. **Karl Barth** proposed a Christocentric alternative: election is covenant and covenant is election, grounding all in **Christological election** rather than in a three-covenant schema; this stresses the universality of grace yet raises questions about federal imputation. In analytic theology, some have parsed covenants as **promissory and deontic structures**, probing conditions, counterfactuals, and assurance; debates consider whether divine promises underwrite **moral transformation** or stand as purely forensic pledges, refining the logic of the **covenant of grace**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A first debate concerns **Mosaic republication**: Did Sinai republish the **covenant of works** in some sense within the administration of the **covenant of grace**? Following **Meredith Kline** (1963; 1997), some argue for a typological works principle tied to the land and the theocratic order, securing pedagogical use and typology. Others, like **John Murray** (1953), resist republication language, emphasizing moral law continuity but not covenantal works. Denominational reports, such as PCA (2007) and OPC (2007), warn against collapsing justification into covenantal faithfulness while allowing intramural diversity on republication.

A second debate focuses on **conditions in the covenant**. Are repentance and obedience conditions or consequences within the **covenant of grace**? Against neonomian proposals, Reformed confessions maintain that faith alone is the instrument of justification, with obedience as necessary fruit, not co-instrumental condition. The **Marrow** legacy returns here: the free offer is unconditional in its offer and particular in its efficacy, preserving **assurance and perseverance** while upholding sanctification.

A third debate surrounds ecclesiology and sacramental practice, especially **paedobaptism**. The Reformed argue that the **covenant of grace** includes believers and their children, appealing to Genesis 17, Acts 2:39, and the household baptisms, with baptism replacing circumcision as **covenant sign and seal**. Baptistic interlocutors insist that new covenant membership is by regenerate faith alone and that the sign belongs to professing believers, highlighting Jeremiah 31’s internalization. This dispute tests how one relates **substance and administration** across covenants.

A fourth debate engages Israel–church continuity and charges of supersessionism. Reformed federalism affirms one **covenant of grace** and one olive tree, with grafting in Christ as the key, per Romans 11, while attending to the irrevocable gifts to Israel. Progressive covenantalists and progressive dispensationalists urge a more detailed account of national-Israel promises within the **new covenant**, pushing Reformed thinkers to refine typology, **eschatological fulfillment**, and the place of land.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
In Theology Proper, the **pactum salutis** connects the **economic covenant** to the **immanent Trinity** without implying subordination. The Father appoints, the Son consents, and the Spirit applies, revealing that the **covenant of grace** is grounded in triune love and wisdom. Christologically, Jesus is the **mediator of the covenant**, prophet, priest, and king, whose active and passive obedience secure the terms of the covenant for his people. Romans 5 and Hebrews 7–10 exhibit **federal headship** and priestly mediation as covenantal categories.

Anthropology is framed by **covenant of works** origins: Adam’s original righteousness, probation, and fall explain the need for a **second Adam** and the logic of imputation. Soteriology is governed by **union with Christ** within the **covenant of grace**, coordinating justification, adoption, and sanctification as benefits of the one Mediator. Pneumatology emphasizes the Spirit as the **earnest of the inheritance**, sealing believers within the covenant and empowering obedience promised in the **new covenant**. Ecclesiology flows from covenant identity: one people across administrations, visible and invisible church distinctions, and sacraments as **covenant signs and seals**. Bibliology is covenantal: Scripture functions as **covenant charter**, a canonical treaty corpus with oath and witness structure. Eschatology is the telos of the covenant: the **new creation** is the perfected communion promised to Abraham, realized in Christ, and sealed by the Spirit, integrating kingdom and covenant.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the **covenant of grace** underwrites assurance because it rests on God’s oath in Christ, not on human performance. Preaching can trace **redemptive-historical unity** from promise to fulfillment, avoiding moralism by locating imperatives within the **gospel covenant**. In catechesis, families are taught to live as **covenant households**, nurturing children in the faith with confidence in God’s promises. Liturgically, baptism and the Lord’s Supper are administered as **covenant signs and seals**, with language of remembrance and oath-renewal that highlights **grace and gratitude**.

In pastoral care, the **law-gospel distinction** is clarified: the law exposes sin and guides the redeemed; the gospel as **covenant of grace** absolves and empowers. Church discipline is framed as **covenant faithfulness**, aiming at restoration. Missionally, the church proclaims the **universal offer of the covenant** while trusting the Spirit to effectually call. The Psalms and prophets can be preached as covenant documents, strengthening the congregation’s grasp of **God’s steadfast love** and the reliability of his sworn promises.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The **covenant of grace** names God’s one sworn way of saving sinners in Christ, uniting Scripture’s many administrations into a single, triune promise that forms the church’s faith, worship, and hope.
