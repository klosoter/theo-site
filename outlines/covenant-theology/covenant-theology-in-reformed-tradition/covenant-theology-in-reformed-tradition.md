### TITLE
Promise and Obligation: Reformed Federalism from Adam to Christ

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Sixteenth to seventeenth century Reformed orthodoxy as dogmatic architecture of providence and redemption, centering the **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, and **pactum salutis** across Theology Proper and Soteriology.
- How God relates to humanity through sworn bonds with sanctions, uniting **law and gospel** while preserving **justification by faith** within a unified canon.
- Key figures include **Heinrich Bullinger** as early architect, **Johannes Cocceius** and **Herman Witsius** as systematizers, **Francis Turretin** as scholastic defender, **Westminster Divines** as confessional framers, and later **Vos, Murray, and Kline** as biblical-theological renovators.
- Concepts include **federal headship**, **suzerainty-treaty form**, **sacramental signs and seals**, **republication at Sinai**, and debates on **paedobaptism versus credobaptism**.
- Anchor texts and events: Genesis 15 and 17, Exodus 19–24, Jeremiah 31, Galatians 3, Romans 5, Hebrews 8–10; **Synod of Dort 1618–1619**, **Westminster Confession of Faith 1646–1647**, **Witsius 1677 Economy of the Covenants**, **Kline 1963 Treaty of the Great King**.
- Reformed trajectories contrast **covenantal continuity** with **dispensational discontinuity**, and engage **Barth’s election-as-covenant** and **Lutheran law-gospel without federalism**.
- Ongoing debates include **Federal Vision** and assurance, **republication and works principle**, as well as **progressive covenantalism** and analytic accounts of **federal imputation**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Reformed covenant theology defines a covenant as a divinely initiated, sworn bond with promises and sanctions that orders communion with God and creates a people under God. The classic triad names a prelapsarian **covenant of works** with Adam, a postlapsarian **covenant of grace** administered in diverse economies, and an eternal **pactum salutis** between the Father and the Son. These are not three gospels but one economy of mercy grounded in the triune God, where the works principle reveals the demands of divine justice and the grace principle reveals the freeness of **justification by faith**.

Dogmatically, covenant theology serves as an architectural principle that locates soteriology within providence and the church within salvation history. Because the **pactum salutis** is an eternal resolve within the Trinity, covenant theology touches Theology Proper and Christology; because the **covenant of works** concerns Adam as federal head, it touches Anthropology and Hamartiology; because the **covenant of grace** is administered in word and sacrament, it touches Ecclesiology and sacramental theology.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The Hebrew *berit* and the Greek *diatheke* carry the conceptual field of oath, bond, and inheritance arrangement. The verb to cut a covenant, *karat berit*, points to blood-sanctioned promises seen in Genesis 15 as God passes between the severed pieces, pledging himself by a self-maledictory oath. This solemn ritual identifies the **Abrahamic promises** as unilateral in foundation and bilateral in response, which later Pauline exegesis in Galatians 3 interprets as gospel according to promise.

Though the term covenant is not used in Genesis 2–3, the structure of command, sanction, and eschatological Sabbath promise supports a prelapsarian **covenant of works**. Hosea 6:7 speaks of Israel transgressing the covenant like Adam, which many read as an Adamic covenantal frame. Romans 5:12–21 interprets Adam and Christ in terms of **federal headship**, grounding imputation of guilt and righteousness in covenantal solidarity.

Sinai reveals a mixed typology. Exodus 19–24 forms Israel as a **suzerainty treaty** people with a works principle at the national, typological level, while Deuteronomy 28 threatens exile for corporate disobedience. Yet the Mosaic economy is an administration of the one **covenant of grace**, since sacrificial mediation and priestly intercession signify divine mercy pointing to Christ. Jeremiah 31 and Ezekiel 36–37 promise a new covenant with internalized law and the Spirit’s gift, which Hebrews 8–10 interprets as inaugurated in Christ’s priestly sacrifice and mediated presence. The Lord’s Supper words in Luke 22 identify Christ’s blood as the **new covenant Eucharist**, sealing forgiveness and forming a renewed people.

Royal theology completes the arc. Second Samuel 7 and Psalm 89 articulate a **Davidic covenant** that secures a son upon the throne, which the New Testament ties to Jesus as faithful Israel and obedient Son. Galatians 3–4 frames the Mosaic law as a guardian until Christ, protecting the Abrahamic promise that is ultimately Christological. Thus the Bible’s own grammar sustains a unity through diversity, culminating in the person and work of the mediator of the **covenant of grace**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Early fathers did not systematize federalism, though they valorized the twofold economy of law and gospel. Augustine’s account of two cities and sacrament as signum et res anticipates covenantal bonds, while patristic typology nurtured a canonical sense of **one economy under diverse administrations**. Medieval theologians spoke of *foedus* in sacramental and canon-law contexts, preparing categories for later **Reformed federalism**.

Reformed articulation blossoms in the sixteenth century. **Heinrich Bullinger’s De testamento 1534** grounds promise and law in a unitary divine testament, while **Olevianus 1567** and **Ursinus 1563** locate the gospel as covenant fellowship restored by Christ. The **Westminster Confession of Faith 1646–1647** codifies the **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, and implies the **pactum salutis** by its Christology of mediator and surety. In the seventeenth century, **Johannes Cocceius 1603–1669** advances a historical federalism that stresses progressive epochs, while **Francis Turretin 1679–1685** defends a more scholastic, timeless presentation of the covenants. **Herman Witsius 1677 Economy of the Covenants** harmonizes the schools by integrating exegesis, dogmatics, and piety.

The Anglo-American and Dutch traditions carry federalism into the modern period. Scottish and New England Puritans explore **federal church polity** and assurance, while Princeton divines in the nineteenth century fold federal headship into debates on **original sin and imputation**. Dutch Neo-Calvinism with **Kuyper 1898** and **Bavinck 1895–1901** renews the organism of revelation and the family character of the covenant, while twentieth-century biblical theology through **Geerhardus Vos 1948** and **Meredith Kline 1963** recovers treaty forms and eschatological structure within a Reformed frame.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton represents a classic federal orthodoxy. **Charles Hodge 1857–1873** presents **federal imputation** as the juridical backbone of Adam-Christ parallelism, reading Romans 5 and 1 Corinthians 15 in a representative key. **B. B. Warfield** defends the forensic center of **justification by faith** while recognizing the organic unity of Scripture, locating the **covenant of grace** as the public form of salvation through the ages.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism nuances the picture. **Abraham Kuyper** emphasizes covenant as family and nation-making, connecting **sphere sovereignty** with churchly nurture and paedobaptism. **Herman Bavinck** harmonizes **covenant communion** and forensic grace, grounding the **pactum salutis** in the Trinity while insisting that covenant is the form of all of God’s relation to the world. Later, **Klaas Schilder** stresses covenant obligation and conditionality, prompting debates on assurance and the nature of **covenant membership**.

The Westminster tradition spans confessional clarity and biblical-theological development. The **Westminster Standards 1646–1647** set the template with a **works covenant** in Adam and a single covenant of grace under various administrations. In the twentieth century **John Murray** questioned the nomenclature of a covenant of works while retaining Adamic probation, and **Meredith Kline** argued for **republication of the works principle** at Sinai in a typological, national register, reinforcing the eschatological movement of the **covenant of grace** to fulfillment in Christ.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology frames covenant within sacramental economy and ecclesial mediation. Vatican II 1962–1965 speaks of salvation history as a **covenantal sacramentum**, emphasizing the church as the people of God and the Eucharist as the **new covenant** sacrifice. The focus is less on **federal imputation** and more on infused charity and ecclesial participation.

Lutheran theology champions the **law-gospel distinction** but generally resists a prelapsarian **covenant of works**. Classic Lutheran confessions do not adopt tripartite federalism, preferring to stress promise and faith over covenantal structure. This generates convergences with Reformed soteriology on **justification by faith**, yet maintains distance from **federal headship** as a structuring principle.

Modern biblical theology often makes covenant the center without Reformed federal triads. **Walther Eichrodt 1930s** saw covenant as the Old Testament’s organizing idea, while **E. P. Sanders 1977** proposed **covenantal nomism**, arguing that Second Temple Judaism grounded obedience in grace. **N. T. Wright** interprets Paul’s justification within **covenant membership**, challenging Reformed accounts of imputation. **Karl Barth** reconceives election as **covenant** in Church Dogmatics II.2, rejects a **covenant of works**, and identifies **Jesus Christ as both electing God and elected man**, thereby relocating federalism within Christological concentration.

Analytic theology revisits representation and imputation with philosophical tools. **Oliver Crisp** explores **federal union and original sin**, assessing whether imputation requires realism or can be grounded in divine promise and corporate identity. Others debate the metaphysics of **federal headship**, the grounding of covenantal obligations, and the nature of sacramental **signs and seals** as performative divine speech acts.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
One objection charges the **covenant of works** with being unbiblical since the term does not appear in Genesis 2–3. Reformed replies appeal to the textual structure of command and sanction, to Hosea 6:7, and to the necessity of a works principle for the logic of **federal imputation** in Romans 5. The related **republication** debate asks whether Sinai reissues a works principle. Klineans argue for a typological works principle for the national theocracy, while others fear it imperils **grace alone**, and still others like Murray prefer to subsume Mosaic law wholly under the **covenant of grace**.

The **Federal Vision controversy** raised questions on faith, works, and assurance in the early 2000s, with the Auburn Avenue events in 2002 and denominational reports in the PCA 2007 and OPC 2009. Reformed churches reaffirmed **justification by faith alone** and cautioned against collapsing **covenant membership** into election. Baptismal debates continue between Westminster paedobaptism grounded in **covenant continuity** and Baptist critiques that privilege **new covenant regenerate membership**. New Covenant Theology and **progressive covenantalism** seek mediating models that affirm continuity in promise while restricting old covenant signs.

Wider Pauline debates press the doctrine. Proponents of **covenantal nomism** minimize legalism as Paul’s target, pushing Reformed theologians to clarify how **law and gospel** relate within the one **covenant of grace**. Analytic concerns about imputation’s justice challenge defenders to refine the moral accounting of **federal headship**. These debates have sharpened exegesis and dogmatics without overturning confessional federalism.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The **pactum salutis** expresses the eternal counsel of the triune God, an *ad intra* ordering of missions to redemption that safeguards both **divine simplicity** and the free resolve of grace. Christology stands at the heart, for Jesus is mediator and surety of the **new covenant**, fulfilling Abrahamic and Davidic promises and obeying where Adam and Israel failed under the **works principle**.

Anthropology and Hamartiology pivot on **federal headship**. Adam’s disobedience constitutes many sinners and Christ’s obedience constitutes many righteous, grounding **imputation** and union with Christ. Soteriology unfolds as covenantal application, where effectual calling, faith, and adoption induct believers into **covenant communion**. Ecclesiology is covenantal peoplehood, defining the church as the visible assembly under **word and sacrament** with **baptism and the Lord’s Supper** as signs and seals. Eschatology presents the new creation as the consummated **covenant of grace**, when the dwelling of God is with humanity in unveiled glory.

Bibliology and ethics are reframed by covenant. Scripture is a canon of **covenant documents**, with treaty preambles and stipulations shaping genre and canon formation. Morality is participation in the **new covenant law of Christ**, which is the Spirit-inscribed telos of the Decalogue. Public theology draws on **covenant and common grace** to parse civil order, while the church remains a pilgrim people whose primary polity is **covenant worship**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Covenant theology gives pastors a map of redemption that coherently preaches Christ from all Scriptures. The **Abrahamic promises** ground assurance, the **Sinai covenant** surfaces the gravity of sin, and the **new covenant Eucharist** offers the pledged presence of Christ. Believers are catechized to see baptism and communion as **signs and seals** of a real bond, not bare symbols.

In parish life, the **covenant of grace** shapes vows, discipline, and mutual care as a family under oath. Parents present children for baptism under **covenant continuity**, while Baptist congregations commend a regenerate **new covenant membership**, and both are urged to nourish faith within a story that moves from promise to fulfillment in Christ. Liturgically, Lord’s Day worship reenacts **covenant renewal** through call, confession, consecration, communion, and commission, placing **law and gospel** in doxological rhythm.

Mission and ethics also gain clarity. The church announces the **new covenant** to the nations, forms disciples under the **law of Christ**, and resists antinomianism and legalism alike. Work, family, and public service are caught up into **covenant vocation**, animated by the Spirit who writes the law on the heart and binds the body to the head.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Reformed covenant theology is the grammar of God’s sworn mercy in Christ that orders Scripture’s story, the church’s life, and the believer’s assurance under the one covenant of grace.

### RECAP NOTES
- Sixteenth to seventeenth century Reformed orthodoxy made **covenant theology** an architectonic for dogmatics, binding providence, redemption, and church under divine oath.
- Core question: how **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, and **pactum salutis** cohere to unite law and gospel without compromising grace alone.
- Figures and stances: **Bullinger** unitary testament, **Cocceius** historical federalism, **Turretin** scholastic defense, **Witsius 1677** harmonization, **Hodge and Warfield** forensic federalism, **Murray** cautious of works nomenclature, **Kline** republication and treaty form.
- Key concepts: **federal headship**, **suzerainty-treaty pattern**, **signs and seals** in sacraments, **republication at Sinai**, **paedobaptism versus credobaptism**.
- Turning points: **Dort 1618–1619** confirms grace against Arminianism, **Westminster 1646–1647** codifies federal schema, **Federal Vision 2002–2009** prompts reaffirmation of **justification by faith alone**.
- Distinctive Reformed contrasts: affirmation of a prelapsarian **works covenant**, one **covenant of grace** in multiple administrations, and an eternal **pactum salutis**, over against **Lutheran law-gospel without federalism** and **dispensational discontinuity**.
- Legacy: ongoing debates on **republication**, **assurance and covenant membership**, **progressive covenantalism**, and analytic accounts of **imputation** keep the tradition exegetically and dogmatically generative.
