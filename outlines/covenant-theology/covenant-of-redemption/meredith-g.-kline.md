# Meredith G. Kline (1922–2007)

Meredith G. Kline advanced a robust doctrine of the **Covenant of Redemption**, emphasizing its role as an intra-Trinitarian pact that grounds the history of redemption. He argued that this **eternal covenant** between the Father and the Son provides the legal and theological foundation for the **Covenant of Grace** and the accomplishment of **redemption**. Kline’s approach is marked by his distinctive use of **ancient Near Eastern treaty forms** to elucidate biblical covenants.

- "The pretemporal covenant between the Father and the Son is the archetype of all redemptive covenants." ("Kingdom Prologue")

## Key Works

- Kingdom Prologue: Genesis Foundations for a Covenantal Worldview
- By Oath Consigned: A Reinterpretation of the Covenant Signs of Circumcision and Baptism
- God, Heaven and Har Magedon: A Covenantal Tale of Cosmos and Telos
- The Structure of Biblical Authority
- Treaty of the Great King

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Covenant of Redemption as Intra-Trinitarian Pact**
    - The **Covenant of Redemption** is an eternal agreement within the Godhead, particularly between the Father and the Son.
    - It establishes the Son’s mediatorial role and the conditions for the accomplishment of redemption.
    - This covenant is the archetype for all subsequent redemptive covenants in Scripture.
    - The Son voluntarily undertakes the work of redemption, receiving promises from the Father.
    - "The Father and the Son entered into a pretemporal covenantal arrangement, the foundation for all redemptive history." (Kingdom Prologue)

2. **Legal-Forensic Structure of the Covenant**
    - Kline interprets the **Covenant of Redemption** using the legal forms of ancient Near Eastern treaties.
    - The covenant includes stipulations, sanctions, and promises, mirroring biblical and extra-biblical suzerainty treaties.
    - The Son fulfills the covenant’s requirements as the representative of the elect.
    - The legal structure underscores the objective, binding nature of redemption.
    - "The covenantal structure is not merely relational but judicial, with binding stipulations and sanctions." (By Oath Consigned)

3. **Typological Relationship to Historical Covenants**
    - The **Covenant of Redemption** serves as the pattern for the **Covenant of Works** and the **Covenant of Grace**.
    - Historical covenants in Scripture reflect the eternal covenant’s structure and purpose.
    - The typology underscores the unity and coherence of God’s redemptive plan.
    - The Son’s obedience in the eternal covenant is mirrored in his earthly work.
    - "The historical covenants are temporal replicas of the eternal pact within the Trinity." (Kingdom Prologue)

4. **Christ’s Active and Passive Obedience**
    - The Son’s fulfillment of the **Covenant of Redemption** entails both active obedience (fulfilling the law) and passive obedience (bearing the curse).
    - This obedience is the meritorious ground for the salvation of the elect.
    - The covenant requires the Son to secure all the blessings promised to his people.
    - The Father rewards the Son with the exaltation and inheritance of the redeemed.
    - "The Son’s obedience under the covenant is the legal basis for the justification of the elect." (God, Heaven and Har Magedon)

5. **Covenant and Eschatology**
    - The **Covenant of Redemption** is oriented toward the consummation of God’s kingdom.
    - It guarantees the ultimate triumph of the Son and the realization of the new creation.
    - The eschatological promises to the Son shape the trajectory of redemptive history.
    - The covenant’s fulfillment is both inaugurated and consummated in Christ.
    - "The eschatological goal of the covenant is the glorification of the Son and his people." (Kingdom Prologue)

## Legacy and Impact

Kline’s articulation of the **Covenant of Redemption** has significantly influenced Reformed covenant theology, especially in its emphasis on the legal and forensic dimensions of redemption. His use of **ancient Near Eastern treaty forms** provided a fresh framework for understanding biblical covenants and their theological implications. Kline’s work continues to shape discussions on the unity of Scripture, the structure of redemptive history, and the centrality of Christ’s mediatorial work. His covenantal insights remain foundational for contemporary Reformed theology.
