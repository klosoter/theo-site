# Johannes Cocceius (1603–1669)

Johannes Cocceius was a leading figure in the development of **federal theology**, articulating a robust doctrine of the **Covenant of Redemption** (pactum salutis) as an eternal agreement within the **Trinity** for the salvation of the elect. He viewed this covenant as the foundation for all subsequent covenants in redemptive history, emphasizing the unity and progression of **God’s covenantal dealings** with humanity. Cocceius’s approach highlighted the centrality of Christ’s mediatorial work as established in this eternal pact.

- "The covenant of redemption is the foundation of all the covenants that God made with man." (Summa Doctrinae de Foedere et Testamento Dei)

## Key Works

- Summa Doctrinae de Foedere et Testamento Dei (1648)
- Commentarius in Epistolam ad Hebraeos
- The Doctrine of the Covenant and Testament of God (English translation selections)
- Scholarly analysis: Willem J. van Asselt, The Federal Theology of Johannes Cocceius (1603–1669)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Covenant of Redemption as Eternal Intra-Trinitarian Pact**
    - The **Covenant of Redemption** is an eternal agreement between the Father and the Son, with the Spirit’s involvement.
    - It precedes and grounds the **Covenant of Grace** made with humanity.
    - The Son voluntarily undertakes the role of **Mediator** and Surety for the elect.
    - The Father promises the Son a people and the Spirit’s enabling for his redemptive work.
    - "This covenant was made before the foundation of the world between the Father and the Son for the salvation of the elect." (Summa Doctrinae)

2. **Christ as Mediator Appointed in the Covenant**
    - Christ’s mediatorial office is established by the eternal pact.
    - His obedience and suffering are stipulated as covenantal conditions.
    - The rewards for Christ’s obedience are the salvation of the elect and his exaltation.
    - The Spirit applies the benefits of Christ’s work to the elect as part of the covenant’s fulfillment.
    - "The Son undertook to fulfill all righteousness for those given to him by the Father." (Summa Doctrinae)

3. **Unity and Progression of the Divine Covenants**
    - All historical covenants are rooted in the eternal **Covenant of Redemption**.
    - The **Covenant of Works** and **Covenant of Grace** flow from this foundational pact.
    - Redemptive history is a progressive unfolding of God’s covenantal plan.
    - The unity of the covenants underscores the consistency of God’s purpose in Christ.
    - "The whole of Scripture is the history of the covenant and its fulfillment." (Summa Doctrinae)

4. **Emphasis on Divine Initiative and Sovereignty**
    - The covenant is initiated solely by God’s sovereign will.
    - Election and redemption are grounded in God’s eternal decree.
    - Human response is enabled by grace, not by merit or foreseen faith.
    - The security of the elect is guaranteed by the faithfulness of the Triune God.
    - "All things are from God, through God, and to God, even the covenant itself." (Summa Doctrinae)

5. **Scriptural Foundation for the Covenant of Redemption**
    - Cocceius appeals to passages such as Psalm 2, Psalm 110, and John 17 to support the doctrine.
    - He interprets these texts as revealing intra-Trinitarian dialogue and agreement.
    - The biblical covenants are seen as historical manifestations of the eternal pact.
    - Exegesis is central to his theological method, grounding doctrine in Scripture.
    - "The Scriptures themselves testify to the eternal counsel and agreement within the Godhead." (Commentarius in Epistolam ad Hebraeos)

## Legacy and Impact

Cocceius’s articulation of the **Covenant of Redemption** profoundly shaped Reformed federal theology, providing a systematic framework for understanding the unity of God’s redemptive plan. His influence extended to later theologians such as Herman Witsius and shaped the confessional standards of the Reformed tradition. The Cocceian emphasis on the progressive unfolding of the covenant continues to inform contemporary discussions of biblical theology and the doctrine of Christ’s mediatorial work.
