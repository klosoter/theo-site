# Charles Hodge (1797–1878)

Charles Hodge approached **theodicy** and **divine justice** from a classic **Reformed** perspective, emphasizing the harmony of God's **sovereignty**, **holiness**, and **goodness** with the reality of evil. He argued that God's justice is not arbitrary but grounded in His immutable nature, and that all divine actions, including the permission of evil, ultimately serve the manifestation of God's **glory** and the fulfillment of His **redemptive purposes**.

- "God does not will sin for its own sake, but as an incident in the accomplishment of wise and benevolent ends." ("Systematic Theology," Vol. 1)

## Key Works

- Systematic Theology (3 vols.)
- Commentary on the Epistle to the Romans
- The Way of Life
- Princeton Sermons
- "The Plan of Salvation"

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **God’s Justice is Essential and Immutable**
    - Divine justice is an intrinsic attribute, not a voluntary or arbitrary quality.
    - Justice requires that God always acts in accordance with His own holy nature.
    - God’s justice is the foundation for the moral order of the universe.
    - The punishment of sin is a necessary expression of divine justice.
    - "Justice is not merely a determination to do right, but a necessity of the divine nature." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 1)

2. **Theodicy Upholds Divine Sovereignty**
    - God ordains all that comes to pass, including the existence of evil, without being the author of sin.
    - Evil is permitted for wise, holy, and ultimately benevolent reasons.
    - Human responsibility is preserved within God’s sovereign decree.
    - The ultimate purpose of permitting evil is the greater manifestation of God’s glory.
    - "God is not the author of sin, but He does permit it for wise and holy ends." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 2)

3. **Sin and Evil as Means to a Greater Good**
    - The existence of evil is not an end in itself but serves God’s redemptive plan.
    - God overrules evil for the good of His people and the display of His attributes.
    - The cross of Christ is the supreme demonstration of God bringing good out of evil.
    - The final defeat of evil will vindicate God’s justice and goodness.
    - "The permission of sin is overruled for the highest good of the universe." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 2)

4. **Retributive and Remedial Justice**
    - Divine justice is both retributive (punishing sin) and remedial (restoring order).
    - The atonement satisfies the demands of retributive justice.
    - God’s justice ensures that no sin goes unpunished, either in the sinner or in Christ as substitute.
    - Remedial justice is seen in God’s work of redemption and restoration.
    - "The satisfaction rendered by Christ was necessary to render the exercise of mercy consistent with justice." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 2)

5. **Human Accountability and Divine Justice**
    - Humans are morally accountable because they act freely within God’s providence.
    - Divine justice is not compromised by God’s foreordination of all things.
    - The distinction between God’s decretive and preceptive will preserves human responsibility.
    - Final judgment will reveal the perfect justice of God in all His dealings with humanity.
    - "The Scriptures everywhere assume that men are free, and therefore accountable." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 2)

## Legacy and Impact

Hodge’s articulation of **divine justice** and theodicy shaped generations of Reformed theology, especially through his influence at Princeton Seminary. His insistence on the compatibility of **sovereignty** and human responsibility provided a framework for later discussions on the problem of evil. Hodge’s careful distinctions and biblical grounding continue to inform debates on the nature of God’s justice and the rationality of Christian theodicy. His legacy endures in both academic and pastoral contexts, particularly within confessional Reformed traditions.
