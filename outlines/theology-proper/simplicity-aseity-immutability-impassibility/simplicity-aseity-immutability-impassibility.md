### TITLE
One God without parts or passions: the constancy of the divine life amid the economy of salvation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Dogmatic location: **Theology Proper** in dialogue with **Trinity** and **Christology**, framed by classical metaphysics across Patristic, Medieval, Reformation, and modern eras.
- Core question: How do **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** secure God’s perfection while allowing real relation to a changing world.
- Principal figures: **Athanasius** defender of the unchangeable *homoousios*; **Aquinas** developer of **pure act** and **divine simplicity**; **Calvin** on **autotheos**; **Turretin** on **immutability**; **Barth** on **constancy-in-act**; **Moltmann** on suffering; **Dolezal** on classical retrieval; **Mullins** and **Craig** as critics.
- Key concepts: **act and potency**, **analogical predication**, **pure actuality**, **impassible passion**, **eternity and time**, **extra Calvinisticum**.
- Major texts and events: Nicaea 325 and **homoousios**; Council of Constantinople II 553 theopaschite formula; **Summa Theologiae** 1265–1274; **Westminster Confession** 1646–1648; modern debates 1960s–present.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Reformed scholasticism** and **Old Princeton** maintain classical attributes; **Lutheran** emphasis on communicatio idiomatum; **Roman Catholic** Thomist continuity; **modern relational theisms** revise impassibility and simplicity.
- Legacy and debate: Tensions over **modal collapse**, **divine temporality**, and the **suffering of God** continue to shape contemporary dogmatics and analytic philosophy.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The attributes of **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** articulate what it means for God to be the one Lord who is utterly perfect and self-sufficient. **Divine simplicity** states that God is not composed of parts, whether physical, metaphysical, or logical, and that in God essence and existence are identical. **Aseity** confesses that God is from himself, having the ground of his being and beatitude in himself, often called **Calvins autotheos**. **Immutability** affirms that God does not change in being, character, or decree. **Impassibility** asserts that God is not passively affected or overcome by created causes, so that his love and joy are uncaused, steadfast, and free.

These four attributes belong to **Theology Proper** and orient the whole system, since **Trinity**, **creation**, and **Christology** presuppose the living God who is simple, a se, immutable, and impassible. Classical accounts explain these with the metaphysic of **pure act** and **analogical predication**. The divine perfections underwrite providence and grace, so **Soteriology** and **Eschatology** rely on them for the security of God’s promises.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture’s metaphysical density is implicit yet real, as names, doxologies, and contrasts shape the doctrine of **divine simplicity** and **aseity**. Exodus 3:14 gives the divine name as *ehyeh asher ehyeh*, I am who I am, which early and medieval interpreters took to express **aseity** and **pure actuality**. Acts 17:25 insists that God is not served by human hands as though he needed anything, since he gives life and breath and all things, a direct witness to **aseity** and to the gratuity of creation. John 5:26 declares that the Father has life in himself and has granted the Son to have life in himself, integrating **aseity** with **Trinitarian** relations.

The biblical witness to **immutability** is concentrated yet pervasive. Malachi 3:6, I the Lord do not change, grounds covenant fidelity. James 1:17 speaks of the Father with whom there is no variation or shadow due to change. Psalm 102:26–27 sets the Creator’s permanence over against the mutability of creation, a passage cited in Hebrews 1 to magnify the **homoousios** Son. Romans 11:29 marks the irrevocability of gifts and calling, while Ephesians 1 presents an eternal counsel realized in time, stabilizing **providence** in the eternal will of God.

The apparent biblical tension around **impassibility** concerns divine compassion and wrath on the one hand and statements of God’s not repenting or being moved like a creature on the other. Passages like 1 Samuel 15 deny that God is a man that he should change his mind, even as the narrative can ascribe regret to the Lord to register real historical judgments. The cross invites special focus. Acts 20:28 speaks of the church purchased with the blood of God, and the theopaschite formula of 553 summarises the New Testament claim that one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh, preserving **impassibility** while confessing the costly love revealed in Christ. This supports the classical distinction between God in himself and God toward us, the **ad intra** and **ad extra**, without denying that the eternal will embraces temporal enactment.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
The Fathers forged these attributes to safeguard the Creator against mythic and philosophical rivals. **Athanasius** argued in the 320s–350s that the Son’s **homoousios** with the Father secures the divine **immutability** and **impassibility** of salvation, since only the unchangeable God can deify. **Cappadocian** theology, especially in Gregory of Nyssa, held that God is without composition and beyond passion, using **analogical predication** to speak truly. The Council of Chalcedon in 451 and the later theopaschite formula in 553 balanced **impassibility** with the confession that the Word truly suffered, a decisive christological calibration.

The medieval synthesis elevated precision. **Aquinas** in the Summa Theologiae 1265–1274 articulated **divine simplicity** as the font of all attributes, arguing from **pure act** and the identity of essence and existence in God. He held **aseity** as *ipsum esse subsistens*, **immutability** as absence of passive potency, and **impassibility** as freedom from passion, while affirming the most perfect love and joy. The medievals used **analogy** to avoid collapsing divine perfections into creaturely modes and to rule out univocal predication that would threaten **divine simplicity**.

Reformers and Post-Reformation scholastics received and refined the tradition. **Calvin** in 1559 confessed **autotheos**, insisting the Son’s deity is from himself while eternally from the Father with respect to person, a careful distinction that guards **divine simplicity** and **Trinitarian** relations. **Turretin** 1679–1685 codified **immutability** and **impassibility** as essential to God’s perfection, distinguishing the will’s unchangeable decree from mutable effects in history. Confessions like the **Westminster Confession** 1646–1648 describe God as without body, parts, or passions, summing up **simplicity** and **impassibility** as doxological axioms.

Modernity introduced pressure. **Barth** in the Church Dogmatics 1932–1967 recast **immutability** as the constancy of God’s loving freedom, a **Barths actualism** that tries to avoid static readings while keeping divine perfection intact. **Moltmann** in The Crucified God 1972 argued for the suffering of God as the heart of love. Process and open theism advanced a mutable and passible deity for relational reasons. The late twentieth century saw retrievals by **classical theists** who defended **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** as the grammar of the gospel.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The Reformed tradition defended the classical package with confessional clarity. The **Westminster Confession** calls God a most pure spirit, without body, parts, or passions, infinite and immutable, establishing **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** in catechesis and worship. Reformed scholastics such as **Polanus** and **Voetius** unfolded **act and potency** distinctions to explain how an immutable God produces new effects without change in himself, a cornerstone for **providence** and prayer.

**Old Princeton** represented by **Charles Hodge** 1871–1873 and **B B Warfield** maintained **immutability** and **impassibility** as the presupposition of revelation and atonement. Warfield defended the unchangeable divine love that freely responds without being conditioned, an expression of **aseity** and sovereign grace. They avoided speculative extremes by anchoring **divine simplicity** in biblical exegesis and catholic consensus, while warning that denial of these attributes tends toward the erosion of inspiration and the cross.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** stream combined retrieval with cultural engagement. **Herman Bavinck** 1895–1901 called **divine simplicity** the beam of white light that refracts into attributes only in our conception, not in God himself. He argued for **impassible passion**, that the immutable God is infinitely moved by his own love, not by exterior compulsion, a way to combine **impassibility** with covenant pathos. **Kuyper** affirmed **aseity** as the point of departure for worldview, grounding the antithesis of Creator and creature.

Contemporary Reformed retrievals by **James Dolezal**, **Steven Duby**, and **Scott Swain** recover **simplicity** and **impassibility** within **Trinitarian** and christological frameworks, often appealing to the **Westminster** standards and the catholic tradition. They argue that only **divine simplicity** protects the unity of God and avoids tritheism, and that only **impassibility** safeguards the liberty of divine love.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology preserves the Thomistic core. **Thomas Aquinas** remains normative on **divine simplicity** and **pure act**, with modern defenders such as **Garrigou-Lagrange** and analytic Thomists like **Eleonore Stump** who integrates **impassibility** with robust accounts of love. **Brian Leftow** and **Jeffrey Brower** propose models that align **simplicity** with **Trinity**, while **Edward Feser** articulates the metaphysical underpinnings for **aseity** and **immutability**.

Lutheran trajectories vary. The confessional Lutheran consensus affirms **impassibility** and **immutability**, while the **communicatio idiomatum** gives strong language for the suffering of God in Christ. **Chemnitz** stressed that the divine nature does not suffer, yet the person of the Son truly suffers in the assumed nature, which aligns with the theopaschite confession. Modern Lutheran voices such as **Robert Jenson** revise **divine simplicity** and **immutability** toward a temporally extended divine life, a departure from the older **impassibility**.

Modern Protestant and ecumenical theology pressed for a suffering and relational deity. **Moltmann** interpreted the cross as intra-trinitarian suffering love, questioning classical **impassibility**. **Pannenberg** sketched a historized deity whose identity unfolds eschatologically. **Barth** recalibrated **immutability** as constancy in free love, holding more closely to **aseity** than to the denial of change in the modern sense. Process and open theism by **Hartshorne**, **Pinnock**, and **Sanders** asserted real change and passibility in God for the sake of responsive love.

Analytic philosophy of religion generated technical critiques and defenses. **Plantinga** questioned **divine simplicity** in 1980 on the grounds of property identity and necessary existence. **Craig** and **Mullins** reject classical **timelessness** and often **simplicity**, proposing a temporal, relational God. In response, **Dolezal** and **Duby** defend **simplicity** and **impassibility** using **act and potency** and **analogical predication**, while others like **Pruss** and **Brower** craft models to avoid **modal collapse** and preserve contingency.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The charge of **modal collapse** claims that **divine simplicity** makes all of God’s acts necessary and erases contingency. Classical replies appeal to **pure act** and the distinction between necessary willing of himself and free willing of creatures, the latter being contingent in the order of effects. The will is identical to the essence, yet the relation to a contingent effect is not identical to the effect itself, which secures real contingency while maintaining **simplicity**.

Critics of **impassibility** argue that a God without passions cannot love. Classical defenders answer that **impassibility** denies passion as passivity, not affection as perfection. The immutable God loves with maximal intensity, grounded in **aseity**, and cannot be manipulated or diminished. The Christological witness that the Son suffers in the assumed nature confirms divine empathy without surrendering **impassibility**, a point shared by the conciliar theopaschite formula and the **extra Calvinisticum**.

Questions about **immutability** and prayer arise. If God does not change, why pray. The classical answer is that prayer is an ordained means by which the immutable decree is executed in time, so that the unchangeable God brings about new effects through secondary causes. Biblical idioms of repentance or relenting are accommodated as anthropomorphic, yet they communicate real historical change in creatures as they are turned by the living God, which protects both **immutability** and **providence**.

Divine temporality remains contested. Some argue for a timeless God who is beyond before and after, using Boethius and Aquinas to anchor **immutability** in **eternity**. Others prefer an everlasting temporal deity to preserve interaction. Analytic debates weigh whether **simplicity** requires timelessness or whether a temporal but immutable God is coherent. The classical package maintains **eternity** as the mode befitting **pure act**, while admitting the difficulty of expressing this mystery with univocal terms.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
In **Trinity**, **divine simplicity** anchors unity of essence across three hypostases, avoiding tritheism and safeguarding consubstantiality. The persons are not parts of God, and the relations of origin give personal distinction without composition. **Aseity** is common to the essence, while **paternity** and **filiation** express personal properties, a balance already sensed in **Calvins autotheos** and refined by catholic tradition.

In **Christology**, **immutability** and **impassibility** condition a coherent incarnation. The Word assumed a passible human nature without change in the divine nature, which guards both the reality of the cross and the perfection of deity. The **extra Calvinisticum** states that the Word is never circumscribed by the flesh, preserving **simplicity** and **aseity**, while the **communicatio idiomatum** assigns predicates of either nature to the one person, allowing the theopaschite confession that one of the Trinity suffered in the flesh.

In **Anthropology** and **Soteriology**, **aseity** and **immutability** ground grace as wholly gratuitous. God’s will to save is not elicited by human merit, and the immutability of promise assures perseverance. **Impassibility** protects divine justice and mercy from sentimentality or coercion, while **divine simplicity** prevents the fragmentation of God into competing attributes, integrating holiness and love. In **Eschatology**, the beatific vision culminates in participation in the immutable good, and in **Ecclesiology**, worship names God as without parts or passions, forming the church in stable hope.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastors should teach **impassibility** as the victory of steadfast love over caprice. A God who cannot be overwhelmed cannot abandon his people, which makes **immutability** the comfort of the afflicted. The proclamation of **aseity** humbles pride, since God needs nothing, and dignifies prayer, since the self-sufficient God freely wills to hear. **Divine simplicity** helps believers read Scripture without setting holiness against love or justice against mercy.

Liturgy should echo the **Westminster** and catholic doxologies that praise God as without body, parts, or passions, cultivating reverence before mystery. Hymns that confess **immutability** and **eternity** tutor the affections to trust in trials. In catechesis, the language of **impassible passion** can help explain how God is unchangeably loving and yet deeply involved in our sufferings, especially at the Lord’s Table where the crucified and risen Lord communicates his immortality.

Pastoral care for sufferers benefits from the union of **impassibility** and compassion. The cross shows that God’s love does not require inner turmoil to be authentic, and that the Son truly suffered in our nature. This secures hope that God’s purposes do not fail, because **immutability** guarantees the unbreakable covenant, and **aseity** ensures that nothing external can compromise his saving will. Preaching and prayer should invite believers into the stability of **divine simplicity**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The classical confession that God is simple, a se, immutable, and impassible secures the freedom and fidelity of divine love for the life of the world.
