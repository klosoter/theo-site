# Karl Rahner (1904–1984)

Karl Rahner approached **simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** through the lens of his transcendental theology, seeking to reconcile classical doctrines of God with the existential realities of human experience. He affirmed the traditional attributes of God but reinterpreted them in light of **God’s self-communication** and the dynamic relationship between **God’s transcendence and immanence**. Rahner emphasized that these attributes must be understood analogically, safeguarding both the mystery of God and the possibility of genuine encounter with the divine.

- "God is incomprehensible, and yet he communicates himself to us in a real and radical way." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

## Key Works

- Foundations of Christian Faith (Grundkurs des Glaubens)
- Theological Investigations (multiple volumes, especially vols. 4, 9, 18)
- Hearer of the Word
- Spirit in the World
- Encyclopedia of Theology: A Concise Sacramentum Mundi (editor)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Simplicity as Mystery, Not Mere Negation**
    - God’s **simplicity** is affirmed but understood as the fullness of being, not mere absence of composition.
    - Rahner insists that simplicity must be approached through **analogy**, avoiding univocal or equivocal predication.
    - The doctrine protects the mystery of God’s inner life while allowing for real self-communication.
    - Simplicity is compatible with God’s dynamic relationality to creation.
    - "God’s simplicity is not the simplicity of a mathematical point, but the fullness of absolute being." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

2. **Aseity as Self-Communicating Freedom**
    - God’s **aseity** is the foundation of divine freedom and self-sufficiency.
    - Rahner links aseity to God’s capacity for **self-gift** in grace and revelation.
    - Divine aseity does not preclude God’s free decision to enter into relationship with creation.
    - God’s being-from-himself grounds the possibility of genuine encounter with humanity.
    - "God is the one who is absolutely from himself, and yet he is the one who gives himself." (Theological Investigations IV)

3. **Immutability Reinterpreted Through Self-Giving**
    - Rahner affirms **immutability** but redefines it as the constancy of God’s self-giving love.
    - Immutability does not mean static inactivity but the unwavering faithfulness of God’s will.
    - God’s immutability is compatible with the temporal unfolding of salvation history.
    - The doctrine must be balanced with the reality of God’s engagement in the world.
    - "God’s immutability is the immutability of love, not of lifelessness." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

4. **Impassibility and the Mystery of Divine Suffering**
    - Rahner maintains **impassibility** in the sense that God is not subject to involuntary change or suffering.
    - However, he allows for a real sense in which God is affected by the world through **self-communication**.
    - The Cross reveals a paradox: God’s impassibility and the reality of divine compassion meet.
    - Impassibility must not be understood as apathy but as the perfection of divine love.
    - "God’s impassibility is not indifference, but the absolute freedom of love." (Theological Investigations IX)

5. **Analogy and the Limits of Human Language**
    - All divine attributes, including simplicity, aseity, immutability, and impassibility, are known analogically.
    - Human concepts cannot exhaust the mystery of God; theological language is always provisional.
    - Rahner warns against both anthropomorphism and empty abstraction in speaking of God.
    - The analogical approach preserves both divine transcendence and the possibility of revelation.
    - "We must always remember that our language about God is analogical and points beyond itself." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

## Legacy and Impact

Rahner’s reinterpretation of **divine attributes** has profoundly influenced contemporary Catholic and ecumenical theology, especially in discussions of God’s transcendence and immanence. His analogical approach to simplicity, aseity, immutability, and impassibility has encouraged theologians to hold together classical doctrine and the reality of God’s self-giving in history. Rahner’s work remains a touchstone for debates on the compatibility of traditional theism with the dynamic, relational God revealed in Christ. His legacy endures in ongoing efforts to articulate the mystery of God in a way that is both faithful to tradition and responsive to modern existential questions.
