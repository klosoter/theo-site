# Meredith G. Kline (1922–2007)

Meredith G. Kline approached the question of Adam’s hypothetical obedience through the lens of **covenant theology**, emphasizing the **Covenant of Works** as a real probation with eschatological implications. Kline argued that had Adam not sinned, he would have attained **consummate life**—a glorified, unlosable state—by fulfilling the probationary command, thus moving humanity beyond mere innocence to **eschatological confirmation**. This view highlights the forward-looking, typological structure of **redemptive history** and the significance of **meritorious obedience**.

- "Had Adam kept the covenant, he would have attained for himself and his posterity the blessing of glorified life beyond probation." (Kingdom Prologue)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Covenant of Works as Eschatological Probation**
    - Adam was placed under a real, historical **Covenant of Works** with a probationary command.
    - The covenant offered more than continued life; it promised advancement to a higher, glorified state.
    - The probation was not arbitrary but oriented toward an eschatological goal.
    - Adam’s obedience would have resulted in irreversible blessing for himself and his descendants.
    - "The probation was a test with the prospect of confirmed, consummate life as the reward." (Kingdom Prologue)

2. **Meritorious Obedience and Eschatological Reward**
    - Adam’s obedience would have been the ground for attaining the promised eschatological blessing.
    - Kline distinguished between **works** (Adam) and **grace** (Christ), emphasizing the merit principle in the prelapsarian covenant.
    - The reward was not simply restoration but advancement to a new, unlosable state.
    - This meritorious principle undergirds the typology of Christ as the second Adam.
    - "The principle of works, not grace, governed the original probation." (Kingdom Prologue)

3. **Typology and Redemptive-Historical Structure**
    - Adam’s probation prefigured Christ’s redemptive work as the last Adam.
    - The structure of promise, probation, and reward is foundational for understanding biblical covenants.
    - Kline saw the original creation as inherently typological, pointing to ultimate consummation.
    - The history of redemption recapitulates and fulfills the pattern set in Eden.
    - "The original covenant arrangement was a typological anticipation of the eschatological kingdom." (Kingdom Prologue)

4. **Heavenly Consummation as the Goal**
    - The goal of Adam’s obedience was not mere earthly continuation but heavenly, glorified life.
    - Kline rejected the idea that Eden was the final state; it was a stage toward a greater consummation.
    - The **Sabbath** pointed to this eschatological rest and glory.
    - The movement from protology to eschatology is central in Kline’s biblical theology.
    - "The tree of life symbolized the higher, heavenly life to be attained by obedience." (Kingdom Prologue)

5. **Corporate Solidarity and Federal Representation**
    - Adam acted as the federal head for all humanity in the covenant arrangement.
    - His obedience or disobedience would have affected all his descendants.
    - The principle of **corporate solidarity** is essential for understanding both Adam and Christ.
    - The outcome of Adam’s probation would have set the destiny for the entire human race.
    - "Adam’s probation was representative, determining the destiny of his posterity." (Kingdom Prologue)

## Legacy and Impact

Kline’s articulation of the **Covenant of Works** and its eschatological orientation has profoundly shaped contemporary Reformed theology, especially in discussions of **federal headship** and the structure of biblical covenants. His insistence on the typological and redemptive-historical framework has influenced generations of biblical theologians. By emphasizing the forward-looking nature of Adam’s probation, Kline clarified the ultimate goal of creation and redemption as consummate, glorified life, not mere restoration. His work remains a touchstone for debates on covenant theology and the relationship between law, grace, and eschatology.
