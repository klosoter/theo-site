### TITLE
Sovereignty without Complicity: Divine Foreknowledge, Providence, and Evil under Classical Theism

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Core issue: How to affirm exhaustive **foreknowledge** and meticulous **providence** under **divine simplicity** and **eternity** while denying that God is the author of sin and offering a credible response to evil.
- Decisive figures: Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, John Calvin, Luis de Molina, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, Alvin Plantinga.
- Relevant traditions: Augustinian patristics, medieval scholasticism, Reformed orthodoxy, contemporary analytic philosophy of religion.
- Debates:
  - Internal: permission and ordination within **providence**, primary and **secondary causality**, compatibilist freedom, supralapsarian versus infralapsarian order of the **decree**, divine timelessness versus temporality.
  - External: Molinism and **middle knowledge**, libertarian free will, Open Theism and Process theism, atheistic logical and evidential problems of evil.
  - Contemporary: modal collapse and **divine simplicity**, the grounding objection to Molinism, foreknowledge fatalism, Frankfurt-style cases and responsibility, horrendous evils and skeptical theism.
- Position: An Augustinian–Reformed, classical theist account in which God’s eternal **decree** grounds exhaustive **foreknowledge** and all-encompassing **providence** through **primary** and **secondary causality**, evil is a **privation** permitted and governed for greater goods centered in Christ, and human responsibility is compatible with meticulous sovereignty.

### ESSAY
Divine foreknowledge and providence name God’s comprehensive cognitive grasp and governance of the world in a manner fitting his **simplicity**, **immutability**, and **eternity**. **Foreknowledge** is God’s infallible knowledge of all truths, including future contingents. **Providence** is the ratio of God’s wise orderings of all things to their ends, encompassing preservation, **concurrence**, and government. The problem of evil presses whether such a God can be just and good given the realities of moral wickedness and natural suffering. The thesis defended here is that an Augustinian–Reformed, classical account yields the most coherent resolution: God timelessly knows and freely decrees all things, ordaining and permitting through **primary** and **secondary causality** in such a way that evil is neither authored by God nor outside his governance but is a **privation** subordinated to greater goods, principally the display of divine justice and mercy in Christ. This position underwrites robust human responsibility by a compatibilist account of freedom as reason-responsive agency within God’s exhaustive plan.

Augustine is the decisive architect of this grammar. Against the Manichaeans, he rejects any dualist metaphysics that would locate evil in a rival substance. In Confessions and the Enchiridion, evil is a **privation** of good, a defective mode of a good nature’s action, not a positive thing requiring a positive first cause. Against the Pelagians, Augustine insists that fallen will is bound and that grace is efficacious, locating salvation in God’s predestining mercy rather than foreseen merits. Augustine’s God is not surprised by human choices; divine **foreknowledge** does not produce necessity of the consequent but secures necessity of the consequence in light of the eternal **decree**. He insists that God’s willing of good uses even evil wills for good ends, as the crucifixion showcases: the gravest moral evil is the instrument of the greatest good. These themes yield a template that retains divine sovereignty without complicity in sin. The metaphysical work is done by a tight asymmetry. God as **primary cause** gives existence and order to all acts; the defect that constitutes evil rides on a finite will’s disorder and so is not positively caused by God. The ethical work is done by teleology: God’s providential ordering encompasses evil as permitted and governed for superior goods that could not otherwise be attained, the felix culpa motif in City of God. When examiners press whether permission is merely a euphemism for causation, the Augustinian answer is that permission names a mode of providential willing distinct from efficient causing of the defect. God wills to allow, sets the conditions in which the fallen will defects, and orders the resulting privations to good ends, while not being the efficient cause of that privation. This distinction is neither verbal nor evasive but follows from the metaphysics of **privation** and the layered structure of causes.

Thomas Aquinas receives and systematizes Augustine’s insights under the strong constraints of **divine simplicity** and **eternity**. God is pure act in whom essence and existence are identical, so his knowledge is identical with his being and his will. On this basis Aquinas argues that God knows future contingents not by looking ahead but by knowing himself as the cause of all being. The Boethian account of **eternity** places God outside temporal succession; all times are present to him in one simple nunc. The worry that timeless **foreknowledge** imposes fatalism is answered by Aquinas’s famed distinction: God’s knowledge is the cause of things, and he knows contingent things contingently. There is necessity of the consequence, given the eternal decree, but not necessity of the consequent relative to proximate causes. Here Aquinas’s doctrine of **primary and secondary causality** is crucial. God, as **primary cause**, gives act and order to every creaturely event; creatures, as **secondary causes**, act according to their natures and powers. **Concurrence** is the joint causality in which God’s universal causation and creaturely particular causation are not competitive but asymmetrically ordered. When examiners push on whether this makes God the author of sin, Aquinas’s reply follows Augustine’s metaphysics: God causes the act insofar as it is being, while the moral defect inheres in the creaturely mode of acting falling short of right order. God is thus implicated in the positive being of the act but not in its privation. When pressed on modal collapse, Aquinas’s insistence that the simple God’s will is free with respect to all things ad extra is central. The distinction between God’s necessary willing of himself and his free willing of creatures preserves true contingency at the level of effects, secured by the non-identity of the divine willing of himself and the content of the decree. The necessity lies in God’s act, not in the creaturely modes of causation that he freely wills to obtain. Aquinas does not, therefore, reduce contingency to appearance; he places it where it belongs, in secondary causes whose effects are contingent relative to their proximate principles. The theodicy strategy here is not a detailed mapping of all goods but a metaphysical assurance that permission is wise and that evil is ordered to the good by the highest Good.

John Calvin reframes providence pastorally and polemically in the Institutes and sermons, emphasizing the exhaustive scope of God’s **decree** and hence the comprehensiveness of **foreknowledge**. Nothing falls outside God’s providence, not even the seemingly random fall of a sparrow. Calvin’s signature contribution is to deny all autonomy to chance and to assert that even sinful acts fall within God’s plan without making him the author of sin. His method is exegetical and doxological, leaning on narratives such as Joseph’s brothers and the Assyrian rod. Calvin underscores the two-wills doctrine, distinguishing God’s preceptive will from his secret will, and insists that humans act voluntarily and are accountable, even when their actions are foreordained. The Reformed tradition develops this into **compatibilism**, in which freedom is acting according to one’s strongest reasons or desires without external coercion, secured by God’s decrees that include the means. Examiner concern about moral responsibility under compatibilism is answered by appeal to sourcehood and guidance control: agents are the appropriate sources of their actions and respond to reasons in ways that track the content of moral norms, even when their psychology is itself within God’s plan. The infra versus supralapsarian debate shows an internal Reformed effort to articulate how God’s decree relates to the fall. Both deny that God is the efficient cause of sin; both affirm that the decree antecedently includes the fall. The difference is logical order, not temporality. The pastoral force of Calvin’s doctrine appears in suffering: nothing is meaningless, and the cross is the interpretive center of **providence**.

Luis de Molina offers a structurally different path with **middle knowledge**. Seeking to harmonize libertarian freedom and exhaustive providence, Molina posits that between God’s natural knowledge of necessary truths and free knowledge of the contingent decree, God has **middle knowledge** of counterfactuals of creaturely freedom. On this view, God knows what any possible libertarian creature would freely do in any possible circumstance. By selecting a world in which free choices line up with his providential ends, God coordinates without causally determining creaturely acts. The attractive feature is a maximal chessmaster providence that preserves libertarian freedom and avoids divine complicity. Yet the grounding objection presses: what grounds the truth of counterfactuals of libertarian choices prior to God’s decree and apart from creaturely essences or God’s will. If they are brute truths, God’s knowledge of them does not arise from his **simplicity** or his role as cause. If they are grounded in God, then they collapse into decree and undercut libertarianism. If they are grounded in creaturely essences, then essences independent of God limit his sovereignty. Further, under classical **divine simplicity**, all God’s knowledge is identical with his being; discriminating modes of knowledge by logical moments risks multiplying real distinctions in God. A defender can reply that logical priority does not impose real composition, but the aggregation of commitments strains the unity of God’s simple act. Moreover, the problem of evil remains: God freely actualizes a world foreseen to contain these horrors when other feasible worlds without them might have existed. Molina responds that feasible worlds without those evils may not exist, given the pattern of libertarian choices, but the result is a reducible providence that can be thwarted by creaturely counterfactuals, and the theodical appeal to feasibility collapses into speculative epistemology.

Leibniz codifies theodicy in modern form with the claim that God chooses to create the best of all possible worlds, consistent with the principle of sufficient reason. Evil is permitted as the necessary cost of a world whose total balancing of goods, including the goods of freedom and redemption, is maximal. The metaphysical optimism provoked a familiar caricature, but the core is not trivial. Under **divine simplicity** and maximal rationality, God never acts without sufficient reason, and the permission of evil will have such reason even if unknown to us. Critics object that the world appears far from optimal and that the calculus instrumentalizes persons. The stronger objection is epistemic: we are ill placed to assess the global balance of values in complex histories. Here the Augustinian and Thomist strands offer a better posture: rather than calculate, they anchor theodicy in metaphysics and Christology, insisting that evil as **privation** cannot defeat God’s purposes and that the cruciform shape of providence reveals how God brings the highest good from the deepest evil. Leibniz’s contribution is the insistence that the permission of evil is not arbitrary; the caution is to avoid hubris in specifying God’s reasons.

Alvin Plantinga’s Free Will Defense targets the logical problem of evil. If it is even possible that creatures have significant freedom and that God cannot strongly actualize a world in which they always freely do right because of transworld depravity, then the existence of God and evil is consistent. Plantinga does not provide a theodicy but blocks the inference from evil to contradiction. This has been widely regarded as successful. The lingering question is whether the move depends on libertarianism. A compatibilist can adapt the insight by noting that if God ordains that agents act freely in a sense defined by reasons-responsive agency, it may still be impossible to create a world with the same structure of goods but no evil, given the goods that God aims to secure, such as the display of mercy and justice. Plantinga’s modal rigor and his articulation of possible free essences also provoke the later grounding objection to Molinism. Contemporary debates about horrendous evils push beyond the logical problem to evidential weight. Here a modest skeptical theism emerges: given the cognitive distance between divine wisdom and creaturely assessment, the absence of known justifying reasons is weak evidence of their absence. The classical theist can accept this modest skepticism while insisting that God’s character is revealed in Christ and that the cross functions as the hermeneutical key for trust amid opacity.

Internal debates within the classical stream turn on the exact architecture of **providence** and creaturely freedom. Thomists and Reformed scholastics parsed **concurrence** differently, with debate over physical premotion. Whatever the mechanics, the structure is fixed: God’s universal causality establishes the being of acts while leaving the defect to the finite agent, thereby denying that God is the author of sin. The Reformed compatibilist tradition, developed by Jonathan Edwards, understands freedom as doing what one most wants in light of one’s motives and reasons, which themselves are included within **providence**. Examiners often ask whether this reduces responsibility by eliminating alternate possibilities. Frankfurt-style examples challenge the principle of alternate possibilities, showing that robust responsibility does not require access to doing otherwise if the agent acts from his own will. The conditions for praise and blame are thus source-oriented and reasons-responsive. A further internal debate concerns divine temporality. Some contemporary evangelicals argue for God’s temporal eternity and sempiternity to preserve dynamic relations. The Boethian–Thomistic account, however, better preserves **immutability** and the independence of God’s act from the vicissitudes of time, while allowing that temporal effects are truly responsive to prayers and actions that God has eternally decreed as means. Petitionary prayer functions as a real secondary cause ordered by the decree. When challenged that timeless **foreknowledge** and fixed decree make prayer useless, the answer is that God ordains not only ends but the means, and prayer is one such means. To ask whether prayer changes God is to mistake categories; prayer changes the world in a way God has freely willed.

The external debates are sharper. Open Theism and Process theism deny exhaustive **foreknowledge** or divine power sufficient to determine all events. The price tags are heavy: loss of the biblical pattern of prophecy, erosion of confidence that God can finally overcome evil, and revision of **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility**. The classical position maintains that God knows and wills all things without being morally stained by evil, because evil lacks positive ontological status. Molinism attempts to preserve libertarian freedom while retaining providential control; we have already registered the metaphysical and theological tensions regarding **simplicity** and grounding. Libertarian theodicies often appeal to soul-making, following Irenaean themes, but the teleology can be secured without libertarianism by grounding virtue development in God’s wise ordering of trials through **secondary causes**. Atheistic evidential arguments claim that gratuitous evils exist, such as horrors apparently unredeemed by any benefit. The classical reply integrates three moves. First, the metaphysical anchor: evil is a **privation**, so God’s causal involvement is asymmetrically related to the goodness of being, not the moral defect. Second, the epistemic caution: we lack the standpoint from which to judge gratitiousness in a global, diachronic order whose goods include intimate union with God. Third, the Christological center: the cross and resurrection exhibit God’s practice of bringing unsurpassable goods through the permission of unsurpassable evils, which underwrites rational trust.

Contemporary challenges press on **divine simplicity** and modal collapse. If God is simple, then his will and knowledge are identical; does this not entail that all that is, is necessary? The classical response recovers the necessary versus free willing distinction with respect to objects. God necessarily wills himself and freely wills all else. The necessity of God’s act is not transferred to the effects, because the relation between simple act and diversified effects is not a relation of one univocal modality. Effects are contingent relative to their proximate causes because God freely wills that they be such. The principle of proportionate causality applies analogically. Fears that simplicity flattens distinctions in God’s knowledge of logical moments should be reframed: logical priority does not entail real composition in God. To the worry that such appeals are ad hoc, one must recall that **analogical knowledge** is a central axiom of classical theism; univocal transfer of creaturely modal categories to God is disallowed by the doctrine.

Another contemporary challenge concerns foreknowledge fatalism. If God infallibly knows today that I will perform some act tomorrow, is it now impossible for me to refrain. The answer turns on the difference between hard and soft facts and on the modal fallacy that infers necessity from certainty. God’s timeless knowledge is not a past hard fact in the way the fatalist requires. Moreover, the necessity involved is conditional necessity: given the decree, the act will occur, but not of absolute necessity. This is compatible with the claim that the act proceeds from the agent’s reasons-responsive capacities as a **secondary cause**, which is all that moral responsibility requires. When examiners insist that this sounds like compatibilist determinism, the candid answer is yes, it is a compatibilist and meticulously determined order, but one in which determination does not equal coercion, and responsibility does not require self-determination in the libertarian sense. Scripture itself affirms both truths explicitly in the crucifixion: the deeds were done by wicked hands and yet by God’s definite plan.

We can now assemble the defended position. God, under **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **eternity**, knows all things by knowing himself as their first cause. **Foreknowledge** follows the **decree**, not vice versa. **Providence** is the wise ordination of all events, with creatures as true **secondary causes**, such that God’s **concurrence** underwrites the being of their acts without causing the moral defect that makes an act evil. Evil, as **privation**, is permitted for greater goods, whose apex is the glory of God in the redemption accomplished by Christ. Human freedom is **compatibilism**: agents act voluntarily from their own reasons and desires, which are themselves part of God’s providential order. Moral responsibility tracks sourcehood and guidance control, not the availability of alternate possibilities. Against Molinism, we reject **middle knowledge** as metaphysically ungrounded and theologically dissonant with **simplicity**. Against Open Theism and Process theism, we maintain divine omniscience and omnipotence, grounding hope and worship. With Augustine, Aquinas, and Calvin, we hold that God’s governance of evil neither stains holiness nor undermines accountability. With Plantinga, we deny that evil entails contradiction, while avoiding the libertarian framework that spawns the grounding problem and fragile providence. With Leibniz, we affirm that God has good reasons for permission, while refusing speculative global optimizations and preferring a Christocentric teleology. Likely examiner objections can be squarely faced. Does this make God the author of sin. No, because authorship requires efficient causation of the moral defect, which God does not provide, while he does efficiently cause the being of the act and wisely order its consequences. Is permission simply soft causation. No, permission is a mode of willing determined by God’s choice to allow, which is distinct from inducing the defect. Does **simplicity** cause modal collapse. No, because the necessity of God’s act is not univocally predicated of effects, and God freely wills the order of contingent secondary causes. Does compatibilism erase responsibility. No, because responsibility does not require leeway freedom; it requires ownership and rational control, which are fully present. Is skeptical theism a retreat. No, it is a chastened epistemology that refuses overreach while refusing to suspend judgment where God has spoken, especially in the revelation of providence at the cross.

Two adjacent loci should be noted. First, the doctrine of Scripture: prophecy presupposes exhaustive **foreknowledge** and the capacity to bring about predicted ends through ordained means, binding together exegesis and metaphysics. Second, Christology: the hypostatic union and the mediatorial work set the content of the higher goods that calibrate our theodicy. Divine permission of evil serves the Son’s kingdom and the communion of saints in glory. That Christ’s human will acts under **providence** without sin yields the paradigmatic case of compatibilist agency ordered to the highest good. 

In sum, the classical theist, Augustinian–Reformed account supplies a theologically integrated and philosophically defensible framework. It secures God’s exhaustive **foreknowledge** under his **eternity**, it articulates **providence** through **primary and secondary causality**, it construes evil as **privation** permitted for greater goods, and it safeguards human responsibility through **compatibilism**. The result is not an evasion of mystery but the right placement of mystery: in God’s wise reasons for permission, not in the coherence of the conceptual scheme. The cross stands as both the hermeneutical key and the practical ballast. If the most heinous evil has been the instrument of the greatest good by God’s orchestration without complicity, then the paradigm case is established, and the defense of providence under evil is anchored where Scripture anchors it.

### RECAP NOTES
- Core definition: **Foreknowledge** is God’s infallible knowledge of all truths grounded in the eternal **decree**; **providence** is God’s wise preservation, **concurrence**, and government of all things through **primary** and **secondary causality**; evil is a **privation** permitted and ordered to greater goods.
- Decisive figures:
  - Augustine: evil as **privation**, grace and predestination, permission ordered to higher goods.
  - Aquinas: **simplicity**, **eternity**, God knows by causing, contingent effects via **secondary causality**, non-competitive **concurrence**.
  - Calvin: meticulous **providence**, two-wills doctrine, pastoral compatibilism, denial that God is author of sin.
  - Molina: **middle knowledge** to reconcile libertarian freedom and providence; faces grounding and simplicity tensions.
  - Leibniz: theodicy and sufficient reason, best-world selection tempered by epistemic caution.
  - Plantinga: Free Will Defense, logical consistency of God and evil, modal tools influencing contemporary debates.
- Debates:
  - Internal: permission versus ordination language, physical premotion and **concurrence**, compatibilism and responsibility, supra versus infra in the **decree**, divine timelessness.
  - External: Molinism and libertarian freedom, Open and Process theism, atheistic logical and evidential problems of evil.
  - Contemporary: modal collapse under **divine simplicity**, grounding objection to **middle knowledge**, foreknowledge fatalism, Frankfurt cases, horrendous evil and skeptical theism.
- Defended position: Augustinian–Reformed classical theism affirming exhaustive **foreknowledge** grounded in the **decree**, meticulous **providence** through **secondary causes**, evil as **privation** permitted for Christocentric greater goods, and **compatibilist** responsibility; rejecting Molinism and Open Theism.
- Likely examiner questions:
  - Does this make God author of sin? No; God causes the act’s being, not its privative defect.
  - Does simplicity cause modal collapse? No; God necessarily wills himself but freely wills contingent effects.
  - Is permission just causation in disguise? No; permission is a distinct mode of willing, not efficient causation of defect.
  - Can responsibility survive determinism? Yes; sourcehood and guidance control suffice without alternate possibilities.
  - Why trust God amid horrendous evils? The cross exhibits God’s pattern of bringing supreme goods from supreme evils.
