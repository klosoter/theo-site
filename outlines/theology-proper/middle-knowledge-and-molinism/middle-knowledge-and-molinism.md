### TITLE
Choosing a world by knowing counterfactuals — Molinism’s bid to reconcile meticulous providence and libertarian freedom

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late sixteenth century to early seventeenth century debate in **Theology Proper** on **divine knowledge and providence**, with ripple effects in **Soteriology** and **Anthropology**.  
- Can God sovereignly decree all things while preserving **libertarian freedom** by means of knowing **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**.  
- **Luis de Molina** as architect, **Domingo Banez** as Dominican critic, **Suarez** as Jesuit developer, **Turretin** and **Bavinck** as Reformed critics, **William Lane Craig** and **Thomas Flint** as analytic defenders.  
- Key notions include **natural knowledge, middle knowledge, free knowledge**, **divine decree**, **compatibilism vs libertarianism**, **concursus**, and **Congruism**.  
- Landmark texts and events include **Molina’s Concordia, 1588**, the **Congregatio de Auxiliis, 1597–1607**, **Westminster Confession, 1646–1647**, **Turretin’s Institutes, 1679–1685**, and **Flint, Divine Providence, 1998**.  
- Reformed trajectory affirms **meticulous foreordination and compatibilist freedom**, while alternatives include **Molinism**, **Banezian premotion**, **Lutheran monergism**, and modern **Open Theism**.  
- Ongoing debate centers on the **grounding objection**, **divine aseity and simplicity**, soteriological **Congruism**, and the feasibility of worlds under **middle knowledge**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Molinism proposes that God possesses, in addition to his natural and free knowledge, a third logical moment called **middle knowledge**. God knows, prior to his decree, true **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom** regarding what any possible creature would freely do in any complete set of circumstances. By deploying this **scientia media**, God decrees to actualize a world whose free histories accomplish his purposes without violating **libertarian freedom**.

The dogmatic location is **Theology Proper**, specifically within doctrines of **divine omniscience** and **providence**, yet it presses into **Anthropology** through the account of human freedom and into **Soteriology** through grace and election. Classical distinctions are at play: God’s *scientia naturalis* is knowledge of necessary truths and possibilities, God’s *scientia media* is knowledge of true conditionals of creaturely freedom, and God’s *scientia libera* is knowledge of the actual world consequent to the **divine decree**. The claim is that the logical order, not a temporal sequence, allows God to select a feasible world by consulting **counterfactuals of freedom**.

Molinists argue that this tri-fold ordering secures robust **providence** while retaining undetermined creaturely choices. Critics contend that this imposes on God a realm of truth not determined by the **divine will**, challenging **divine aseity** and even **divine simplicity**. The debate is thus a test case for how one articulates the relation between **divine sovereignty** and **creaturely contingency** in a classical theistic framework.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture features counterfactual conditionals that Molinists cite as data for **middle knowledge**. In 1 Samuel 23.9–13, David asks God whether Saul will come down to Keilah, and whether the men of Keilah will surrender David. God answers that they will come and they will surrender him, yet David departs and the predicted event does not occur. Molinists see in this a divine knowledge of **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**, where what would have happened under a different set of choices is distinctly known by God. This sits within a wider biblical portrait of **meticulous providence** that remains compatible with real contingencies.

Jesus’ woes in Matthew 11.21–23 provide another locus. He declares that if the mighty works done in Chorazin had been done in Tyre and Sidon, they would have repented long ago. The Greek is a robust second-class conditional with *ei* plus aorist and *an*, conventionally used for contrary-to-fact conditions, which Molinists read as an assertion about what free agents would have done under different historical circumstances. This text underwrites the claim that God can assert truths of **libertarianly free** hypothetical choices without coercion.

Paul’s claim in 1 Corinthians 2.8 that if the rulers had known, they would not have crucified the Lord of glory, and the conditional warning in Acts 27.31 that unless the sailors remained on board, you cannot be saved, also display divine knowledge of conditionals. These sit alongside texts that underscore **foreordination**, such as Acts 4.27–28 and Ephesians 1.11, as well as providential concurrence in Genesis 50.20. Molinists take the ensemble to show that God truly knows conditionals about human actions and that such knowledge may play into his governance, while Reformed critics insist these texts can be handled by **decretal concursus** without positing **middle knowledge** as logically prior to decree.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Hints of logical order in divine knowledge appear in **Boethius** and **Aquinas**, though **Thomas** did not articulate **middle knowledge**. For Thomas, God’s knowledge of future contingents is founded in the **divine decree** and **simplicity**, not in truths independent of God’s will. The sharp Molinist triad emerges with **Luis de Molina**, who in 1588 published his Concordia on grace and free will, arguing for **scientia media** as essential to reconcile providence and freedom. He framed God’s knowledge of counterfactuals of creaturely freedom as true prior to the decree, supplying God with a library of feasible histories.

The immediate opposition came from **Domingo Banez** and the Dominican school. They advanced **physical premotion** and strong **divine concurrence**, claiming that God premoves the will infallibly yet in a way consistent with contingency. The Roman Church convened the **Congregatio de Auxiliis** between 1597 and 1607 to adjudicate. The proceedings concluded without a formal condemnation. Jesuits like **Francisco Suarez** developed Molinist themes into **Congruism**, contending that God gives grace congruent with the disposition of the will as known by **middle knowledge**. The upshot within Roman Catholicism was a détente between **Banezianism** and **Molinism**, both remaining within the bounds of **classical theism**.

In the Protestant world, Reformed scholastics like **Francis Turretin** rejected **middle knowledge** as compromising the **simplicity** and **aseity** of God by grounding truths outside the divine will. The **Westminster Confession, 1646–1647**, affirms that God freely and unchangeably ordains whatsoever comes to pass, yet does so by establishing second causes that act contingently, which embeds **compatibilist freedom**. Nineteenth-century figures like **Charles Hodge** and **Herman Bavinck** maintained that God’s knowledge of counterfactuals is either included in **natural knowledge** as possibilities or in **free knowledge** as decreed actualities, leaving no place for a third logical moment independent of decree. In the twentieth century, analytic philosophers like **Alvin Plantinga** rehabilitated the utility of counterfactuals of freedom, and **William Lane Craig** and **Thomas Flint** explicitly defended **Molinism** as a coherent model of **providence**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster Standards** articulate comprehensive **foreordination** with real secondary causes. God ordains freely, yet establishes a world where events occur through necessary, free, or contingent causes. This encodes **compatibilism** rather than **libertarian freedom**. In this framework, God’s knowledge of what would happen under different conditions is covered by **decretal concursus** and divine wisdom, not by truths that float free of the decree. The Reformed insist that divine freedom grounds all possibilities and actualities within the horizon of **divine simplicity**.

**Old Princeton** theology, represented by **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield**, emphasized the unity of the divine plan and the certainty of prophecy and redemption under meticulous **providence**. Hodge, writing in the 1870s, argues that middle knowledge is either redundant or corrosive of **aseity**, since it posits a landscape of counterfactual truth not willed by God. Warfield’s attention to biblical providence in the crucifixion narratives underscores Acts 2.23 and Acts 4.27–28 to show that the most wicked free act is subsumed under the foreordained plan, compatible with **responsible agency**.

In the **Dutch Neo-Calvinist** stream, **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck** advance a rich doctrine of **concursus** and **sphere sovereignty**, affirming creaturely distinctness while preserving **divine sovereignty**. Bavinck argues that God knows the possible and the actual in one simple act, free of composition, which challenges the Molinist ordering of logical moments. The Reformed thus favor a twofold knowledge in God, valorize Scripture’s conditional prophecies, and maintain that all true counterfactuals are anchored either in the divine nature or decree, not in a prevolitional stratum akin to **middle knowledge**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
In Roman Catholic scholasticism, two rival models explain grace and freedom. **Banezianism** teaches **physical premotion**, an infallible influx by which God premoves the will to good acts, securing efficacy without violating contingency. **Molinism** asserts **middle knowledge** and contends that God ensures efficacy by selecting circumstances under which a free agent would freely consent. **Congruism** refines Molinism by positing graces congruent with the person’s dispositions as known through **scientia media**, thus uniting efficacy with freedom within a Jesuit soteriological grammar.

Lutheran theology, shaped by **Luther’s Bondage of the Will, 1525**, and the **Formula of Concord, 1577**, resists synergism and stresses monergistic grace. While affirming **divine foreknowledge**, Lutherans typically reject a need for **middle knowledge**, speaking instead of the **hidden will of God** and pastoral warnings based on revealed conditions. The accent falls on God’s saving initiative, the bondage of the will under sin, and the sufficiency of **law and gospel**, rather than on intricate taxonomies of divine knowledge.

Modern analytic philosophy of religion has served as a proving ground for **Molinism**. **Alvin Plantinga** harnessed counterfactuals in 1974 to mount the **Free Will Defense** against the logical problem of evil. **Thomas Flint** in 1998 provided a systematic Molinist account of **divine providence**, and **William Lane Craig** popularized **middle knowledge** in works from 1987 onward. Critics like **William Hasker**, representing **Open Theism**, deny that there are true future-tensed propositions about undetermined free actions, thus rejecting both **simple foreknowledge** and **middle knowledge**. Other critics grant truth values but press the **grounding objection**, contending that libertarian counterfactuals lack truthmakers accessible to God prior to decree.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The most discussed challenge is the **grounding objection**. If a counterfactual like Judas would freely betray Christ under circumstance C is true prior to God’s decree, what grounds its truth. If not grounded in the agent or in the decree, then the truth seems brute and external to **divine will**, threatening **aseity**. Molinists reply that truths of **counterfactuals of freedom** are grounded in the natures of creatures as they would be, or in God’s intuitive knowledge of possibilities, or that they need no further truthmakers. Reformed and Thomist critics argue that such replies either collapse into **natural knowledge** or smuggle in decree through the back door.

A second debate concerns **divine simplicity** and the logical order of knowledge. If God is simple and timeless, many Reformed and Thomists prefer to speak of a single act of **self-knowledge** by which God knows all possibilities and actualities. The Molinist ordering of logical moments can seem to introduce a compositional structure into the divine intellect. Molinists counter that logical order does not entail real composition, and that classical theology already distinguishes **ad intra** processions from **ad extra** decrees without compromising simplicity. The dispute hinges on whether logical dependence counts as a threat to divine **immutability** and **aseity**.

A third debate is practical and soteriological. Molinist **Congruism** says God selects circumstances where he knows a sinner would freely respond to grace, thereby securing salvation without irresistible grace. Reformed theologians worry that this renders grace reactive to creaturely conditionals and risks **soteriological luck**. Molinists reply that God’s choice of which world to actualize remains fully sovereign, hence grace remains prior though not necessitating. The stalemate reveals deeper divides over **libertarian vs compatibilist freedom** and over the nature of **effectual calling** and **irresistible grace**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
With respect to the **Trinity**, the question is whether the ordering of divine knowledge is consistent with **divine simplicity** and the one act of **self-knowledge** in God. A Reformed and Thomist reading harnesses **Rahners Rule** cautiously to say that God’s **ad extra** acts reveal the simple plenitude of the **ad intra** life without suggesting layered cognitive stages. Molinism must therefore show that its logical moments are conceptual distinctions within one simple act rather than real differences that compromise **aseity**.

In **Christology**, the decree and counterfactuals appear in the passion narratives. Acts 2.23 and 4.27–28 display a divinely foreordained crucifixion carried out by responsible agents. Molinists may say that God actualized a world in which authorities would freely crucify Christ under the right circumstances. Reformed theologians account for the same under **compatibilist freedom** and **concursus**. The doctrine of **dyothelitism**, the two wills of Christ, does not require **libertarian freedom**, yet the Molinist emphasis on undetermined choice can influence how one narrates Christ’s obedience as genuine and yet certain.

In **Anthropology and Soteriology**, the hinge is freedom and grace. Molinism affirms **libertarian freedom** and often **prevenient grace**, then explains divine selection of circumstances through **middle knowledge** to secure redemptive outcomes. Reformed theology affirms **compatibilist freedom**, **effectual calling**, and **irresistible grace**, denying that God’s salvific success depends on foreknown creaturely woulds. In **Bibliology**, prophecy and conditional warnings can be parsed by either view, though Reformed appeal to **conditional prophecy** and **decretal concursus**, while Molinists invoke knowledge of **counterfactuals**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
The pastoral energy of these doctrines is confidence in **providence** matched with earnest exhortation. On a Molinist frame, prayer matters because God has woven into the world those circumstances under which free agents would pray and God would respond, which honors both petition and sovereignty through **middle knowledge**. On a Reformed frame, prayer is ordained as a real secondary cause within the **divine decree**, so petitions are effectual through **concursus**.

For assurance and mission, Molinists can say God knows how to place the gospel where people would freely respond, which motivates **evangelism** under a wise God who does not coerce. Reformed preaching urges universal offer and confident proclamation, trusting **effectual calling** to overcome resistance. In both accounts, believers can worship a God of **wisdom and sovereignty**, give thanks for mercies that arrive through creaturely means, and lament evil while confessing God’s overruling rule.

Pastorally, debates over **middle knowledge** should be handled with humility and patience. The church can affirm together that God knows the end from the beginning, that human choices are real and significant, and that the cross stands as the supreme instance of planned redemption through responsible actions. Liturgically, this yields doxology for the God whose knowledge is unsearchable and whose judgments are past finding out, whether one locates counterfactuals within **middle knowledge** or within **simple omniscience**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Molinism’s **middle knowledge** is a daring attempt to state how God wisely chooses a world of real choices, and its enduring value is to force theology to sharpen how **providence** and **freedom** coinhere under **divine simplicity**.
