### TITLE
Knowing the Unknowable: Incomprehensibility and Revelation in the Knowledge and Existence of God

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: Theology Proper, prolegomena and epistemology spanning patristic to analytic, with decisive moments in 325, 1517, 1646–1648, 1870, 1934, and 1981.
- Core question: How can the God who is infinite and incomprehensible be truly known, and on what grounds do we affirm the existence of God.
- Principal figures: Augustine and Aquinas as classical anchors; Luther and Calvin shaping Reformation trajectories; Turretin consolidating Reformed scholastic method; Hodge and Warfield exemplifying Old Princeton; Kuyper and Bavinck integrating modernity; Barth challenging natural theology; Plantinga renewing epistemology.
- Key concepts: **general and special revelation**, **divine incomprehensibility and analogical knowledge**, **sensus divinitatis**, **theistic proofs**, **testimonium Spiritus Sancti**, **archetypal and ectypal theology**.
- Major texts and dates: WCF 1646–1648, Belgic Confession 1561, Vatican I Dei Filius 1870, Barth Brunner 1934, Plantinga 1981, Alston 1991; biblical anchors in Psalm 19, Romans 1, Acts 17, John 1.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Old Princeton evidentialism**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist antithesis and organic revelation**, **Westminster confessional synthesis**; Roman Catholic **analogia entis**; Lutheran **theologia crucis**; modern critique via **Kantian limits**.
- Legacy cue: Ongoing debates on **divine hiddenness**, **religious diversity**, and **classical theism versus theistic personalism**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The existence and knowability of God name a twofold claim in **Theology Proper**. First, that God exists as the necessary and ultimate reality. Second, that humans truly but not exhaustively know this God through **revelation**, by **general revelation** in creation and conscience and by **special revelation** in Scripture and Christ. The church binds these claims with **divine incomprehensibility**, insisting that God is knowable yet never circumscribed, and with **analogical predication**, confessing that our God talk is true though creaturely and ectypal rather than archetypal.

Dogmatically, the topic sits at prolegomena and grounds every locus by clarifying the **principia of theology**. God as principium essendi is the foundation of truth, Scripture as principium cognoscendi externum gives the canon, and the **Spirit's testimonium** as principium cognoscendi internum grants saving recognition. The **Westminster Confession of Faith** in 1.1 and 1.5 establishes these lines, while 2.1 affirms that God is infinite in being and perfection and therefore incomprehensible yet communicatively present, a hallmark of **classical theism**.

Two guardrails shape the discussion. Against rationalism, the tradition asserts **incomprehensibility** and the Creator creature distinction, so that all knowledge is accommodated and analogical. Against skepticism, it asserts **revelation's clarity and sufficiency**, including the **sensus divinitatis** as a natural awareness of God and the **claritas Scripturae** as the fitting medium of saving knowledge. The principle of **Calvin's accommodation** keeps revelation personal and covenantal rather than abstractly informative.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture testifies to God known and unknown, joining **incomprehensibility** with **illumination**. Psalm 19 announces that the heavens are telling the glory of God, an unvoiced yet ubiquitous proclamation of **general revelation**. Paul writes in Romans 1.19 to 21 that what can be known of God is manifest among humans because God has shown it, using phaneron and ephanerosen to stress divine initiative, and calls attributes like eternal power and deity noeta, perceivable to the mind, thus grounding **natural knowledge** even as he indicts idolatry through the **noetic effects of sin**.

Acts 17 portrays Paul on the Areopagus acknowledging human groping after God while proclaiming the unknown known in **resurrected Lordship**. John 1.18 confesses that no one has seen God, yet the only begotten Son has exegesato, has led God out into view, making **Christ's revelation** the key of knowledge. Exodus 3.14 gives the divine Name Ehyeh asher ehyeh, the I am who I am that underwrites **aseity** and free presence. First Timothy 6.16 declares God dwells in unapproachable light, while Ephesians 1.17 prays for the Spirit of wisdom and revelation in the knowledge of him, joining **apophatic reserve** to **pneumatological insight**.

Deuteronomy 29.29 distinguishes the secret things that belong to God from the revealed things, setting the economy of **mystery and disclosure**. First Corinthians 2.10 to 16 elaborates that the Spirit searches even the depths of God and grants the mind of Christ, so that **saving knowledge** is both cognitive and participatory. First Corinthians 13.12 adds an eschatological horizon, now seeing in a mirror dimly but then face to face, anticipating the **beatific vision** where knowledge is perfected by love. These texts together ground the Reformed pairing of **general revelation** and **special revelation** under the sovereignty of the triune God.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Early apologists like Justin Martyr and Athenagoras argued from **cosmic order** to the one God, while Irenaeus tethered knowledge to the **Rule of Faith** as the apostolic lens of Scripture. Origen emphasized **spiritual illumination**, and Augustine framed the knowing of God in terms of divine light and **illumined reason**, notably in Confessions and De Trinitate. Pseudo Dionysius supplied an influential **apophatic theology**, arguing that names are true yet always exceeded by the superessential God.

Medieval synthesis culminated in **Thomas Aquinas**. In Summa Theologiae Ia q2 he offered the Five Ways as demonstrations propter quid or quia, not from definitions but from effects to **first cause**. Aquinas defended **analogical predication** over univocity and equivocity, securing genuine yet proportional knowledge. John Duns Scotus proposed **univocity of being** to protect the intelligibility of metaphysics, sharpening the later debate on how creaturely concepts reach God. The East preserved a different note as Gregory Palamas in the fourteenth century distinguished **essence and energies**, maintaining divine transcendence while affirming real participation.

The Reformers reoriented method by revelation as covenantal gift. **Luther's theologia crucis** warns against a theology of glory that seeks God in speculative ascent rather than the cross, thereby chastening confidence in natural theology. **Calvin's accommodation** and **sensus divinitatis** in Institutes 1559 underscore innate awareness and the clarity of creation while stressing Scripture as spectacles by which we see God the Creator as Redeemer, a hallmark of **Reformed epistemology before modernity**. Post Reformation scholastics such as Junius, Polanus, and **Francis Turretin** refined these themes with the **archetypal and ectypal theology** distinction, the **analogy of being as analogy of names**, and measured use of proofs in the Institutes of Elenctic Theology 1679 to 1685.

Modernity pressed the question anew. **Kant's critical philosophy** in 1781 restricted theoretical reason from knowing noumenal realities like God, while proposing moral postulates. Schleiermacher in 1799 grounded religion in the feeling of absolute dependence, shifting from ontology to **religious consciousness**. Nineteenth century evidentialism sought harmony with science, and twentieth century dialectical theology, especially **Karl Barth**, rejected natural theology in 1934 with the famous Nein to Brunner, insisting on **revelation's self sufficiency**. The later analytic turn with **Alvin Plantinga** recast the debate in terms of warranted belief and **proper basicality**, while **William Alston** argued for the perceptual model of **mystical perception**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton exemplifies a calibrated **evidentialist realism**. **Charles Hodge** in Systematic Theology 1872 to 1873 affirms general revelation and theistic proofs as arguments that produce high probability rather than absolute demonstration. **B. B. Warfield** maintains that Scripture contains and invites historical and inductive argument, aligning with **common sense realism** while affirming the **Spirit's testimony** as decisive for saving certainty. The existence of God is rationally credible and cumulatively supported, and the knowability of God is authentic through revelation, yet finally grounded in **divine self disclosure**.

Dutch Neo Calvinism corrects the Enlightenment confidence with **antithesis** and **grace restoring nature**. **Abraham Kuyper** argues that there is no neutral ground, that principium of theology is faith, and that **regeneration** reorients cognition. **Herman Bavinck** gives a deeper synthesis in Reformed Dogmatics 1895 to 1901. He affirms **general revelation** as a rich, organic disclosure and sees the **sensus divinitatis** as ineradicable, while insisting that **special revelation** culminates in Christ and Scripture. Bavinck embraces the **archetypal and ectypal distinction**, underscores **divine incomprehensibility**, and treats proofs as ambiguous without the confessional frame of **classical theism**.

The Westminster stream codifies a confessional grammar. **WCF 1.1 and 1.5** affirm that while the light of nature and the works of creation manifest the goodness and wisdom of God, Scripture is necessary for saving knowledge because of **sin's noetic effects** and the need for the knowledge of Christ. **WCF 2.1** confesses God as infinite, eternal, and unchangeable, implying **incomprehensibility** alongside true knowledge by revealed names. Later Westminster heirs like **Cornelius Van Til** sharpened epistemic posture with **analogical reasoning** and presuppositional apologetics, arguing that the very possibility of knowledge presupposes the triune God, thereby reframing the existence question as a **transcendental argument**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic dogma at **Vatican I in 1870** taught that God can be known with certainty by the natural light of human reason from created things, an affirmation of **natural theology** rooted in **analogia entis**. Thomist revivals in the twentieth century through **Garrigou Lagrange** defended real knowledge of God by analogy of being, while **Henri de Lubac** retrieved nature and grace as intrinsically ordered, complicating pure nature accounts and shaping a Christocentric **participatory knowledge** without denying **general revelation**.

Lutheran voices vary. The early **Lutheran theologia crucis** is suspicious of ascent by theistic proofs, emphasizing that God is known under the contrary of the cross and that reason must be captive to Scripture. Later Lutheran scholastics sometimes used **natural theology** in a subordinate role, but the dominant note from Luther and Chemnitz stresses **hiddenness and revelation in the Word**. The existence of God is confessed and worshiped rather than syllogistically secured, a stance that sharpens the **apophatic and kerygmatic** dimensions.

Modern Protestantism underwent a threefold shift. Kant denied speculative access to God and displaced metaphysics with **practical reason**. Schleiermacher grounded the knowability of God in **Gefuhl**, reshaping dogmatics around experience. **Karl Barth** repudiated both by centering **revelation in Christ**, denying an independent **point of contact** in nature apart from grace. Brunner argued for a modified **eristics** in 1934 and was opposed by Barth's Nein, a defining moment in the rejection of classical natural theology within dialectical theology.

Analytic philosophy of religion has diversified strategies. **Alvin Plantinga** recovers **Reformed Epistemology**, arguing in 1981 and 2000 that belief in God can be **properly basic** if formed by the **sensus divinitatis** in a cognitive environment designed by God, and that Christian belief can have **warrant** through the **internal instigation of the Holy Spirit**. **William Alston** in 1991 proposes that doxastic practices of religious experience are epistemically permissible, integrating **perceptual models** of knowing God. **Richard Swinburne** employs Bayesian **cumulative case arguments**, while **William Lane Craig** rehabilitates the Kalam cosmological argument, thus offering a refurbished **natural theology** alongside epistemic externalism.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
The problem of **divine hiddenness** challenges the knowability of God by appealing to nonresistant nonbelief. J. L. Schellenberg argues that a perfectly loving God would ensure evident relational availability; many remain unconvinced, therefore God likely does not exist. Reformed responses invoke **noetic effects of sin**, **resistance of the will**, and the **fittingness of veiled glory** that preserves freedom and moral transformation. Others deploy **skeptical theism**, cautioning against confident inferences from our limited grasp of divine reasons, while maintaining **general revelation** remains clear yet suppressible.

Religious diversity raises a parity problem: if **general revelation** is real, why do divergent theisms persist. Reformed accounts answer by distinguishing **seed forms of truth** from saving doctrine, stressing **covenantal particularity** and the centrality of **Christic revelation**. Cognitive science of religion correlates belief with evolved hyperactive agency detection, suggesting a natural origin of **theistic cognition**. The Reformed tradition replies that design in human cognition is consistent with the **sensus divinitatis** and that natural mechanisms do not discredit **teleological intentionality** within providence.

On the concept of God, debates between **classical theism** and **theistic personalism** affect knowability. Critics allege that **divine simplicity** and impassibility make God unknowable in personal terms. Defenders argue that **analogical naming** protects meaningful predication and that biblical anthropomorphism is **Calvinian accommodation**, not literal composition. The upshot is renewed attention to **names of God**, **Scripture's metaphors**, and metaphysical grammar that makes worshipful knowledge coherent.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The Trinity frames knowability because **names of God** are revealed as Father, Son, and Spirit, and **Rahner's Rule** insists that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity, which directs us to **Christ as exegete of the Father**. Yet classical caution reminds that economic revelation is faithful without exhausting the **ad intra** life, so that **incomprehensibility** remains even as knowledge is secured through **perichoretic missions**.

Christology centers knowledge in the incarnate Word. John 1.18 presents the Son who has exegesato God, and Colossians 1.15 names him image of the invisible God, making **Christ the locus of knowability**. Soteriologically, **illumination and regeneration** by the Spirit heal the intellect and affections, and **union with Christ** grants participatory knowledge ordered to the **beatific vision**. Anthropology supplies the **imago Dei** as capacity and vocation for knowledge, while sin distorts by **noetic corruption** that only grace remedies.

Ecclesiology and Bibliology specify means. The church confesses and catechizes through **Word and sacrament**, and **Scripture's sufficiency and clarity** provide the external norm. The Spirit's **testimonium** persuades and assures, not by adding private revelations, but by enlightening to receive **divine speech**. Eschatology crowns knowledge with vision, not replacing faith but consummating it, so that **faith seeks understanding** here and grasp becomes **sight in glory** there.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally the doctrine inculcates **humble confidence**. Because God is incomprehensible we teach reverent limits, resisting speculative curiosities. Because God is revealed we encourage real assurance that one can truly know God by **hearing the Word** and by **prayerful illumination**. This balance equips saints to face **hiddenness and doubt** without despair and to pursue **wisdom and holiness**.

Catechetically we train believers to read **general revelation** with gratitude and **Scripture** with expectation. We teach the **sensus divinitatis** as a creational gift, the **noetic effects of sin** as a sober warning, and the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti** as the ground of assurance. Liturgically we practice knowledge by praise, singing the **names of God** and rehearsing mighty acts that interpret the world. In apologetics we commend **cumulative case arguments** while resting our confidence in **divine self disclosure** and the **transcendental necessity** of God for reason, morality, and meaning.

For ministers, this doctrine also guards speech. We preach with **Calvin's accommodation**, neither domesticating mystery nor wrapping gospel in obscurity. We lead the church into the **beatific hope**, where every Lord's Supper tutors desire for the face of God. We model intellectual charity toward interlocutors, convinced that **truth is God's** and that all knowing is finally **doxological participation**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The incomprehensible God makes himself truly known by general and special revelation, so that faith seeks understanding through analogical speech, Spirit wrought illumination, and a pilgrim hope aimed at the beatific vision.
