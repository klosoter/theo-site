# Wolfhart Pannenberg (1928–2014)

Wolfhart Pannenberg approached the **means of grace**—especially **preaching**, **sacraments**, and **prayer**—from the standpoint of God's **self-revelation in history** and the eschatological nature of the church. He emphasized that these means are not merely human activities but are grounded in the **objective reality of God's action** in Christ and the Spirit, serving as anticipatory signs of the coming **kingdom of God**. For Pannenberg, the means of grace are integrally related to the church's participation in God's ongoing revelation and the formation of the Christian community.

- "The sacraments are signs of the eschatological consummation of salvation, which is already anticipated in the life of the church." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Key Works

- Systematic Theology, Vol. 3
- Jesus—God and Man
- Theology and the Kingdom of God
- Revelation as History (editor and contributor)
- The Church (in various essays and collected works)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Means of Grace as Eschatological Signs**
    - The means of grace point to the **future consummation** of salvation in the kingdom of God.
    - They are not ends in themselves but serve as **anticipatory signs** of God's final revelation.
    - The church's use of these means is a participation in the unfolding of **God's eschatological plan**.
    - The sacraments, especially, embody the tension between the "already" and "not yet" of salvation.
    - "The sacraments are signs of the eschatological consummation of salvation, which is already anticipated in the life of the church." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

2. **Objective Grounding in Divine Action**
    - The efficacy of the means of grace is rooted in **God's objective action** in history, not in subjective experience.
    - Preaching, sacraments, and prayer mediate the reality of Christ's work and the Spirit's presence.
    - The church is the locus where God's self-revelation is made present through these means.
    - Human participation is necessary, but the initiative and power remain with God.
    - "The means of grace are effective because they are grounded in the reality of God's action in Christ." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

3. **Preaching as Proclamation of Revelation**
    - Preaching is the authoritative proclamation of **God's revelation** in Christ.
    - It is not merely instruction but a means by which the Spirit actualizes faith and community.
    - The Word preached is a vehicle for the self-disclosure of God in the present.
    - Preaching is central to the church's identity as the community of those who hear and respond to God's Word.
    - "The proclamation of the Word is the primary means by which the church participates in God's self-revelation." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

4. **Sacraments as Community-Forming Events**
    - Sacraments are not private acts but **public, communal events** that shape the identity of the church.
    - Baptism and the Lord's Supper signify participation in the death and resurrection of Christ.
    - They mediate the presence of Christ and the Spirit to the gathered community.
    - The sacraments are constitutive for the church's existence as the people of God.
    - "The sacraments are constitutive events for the community, making present the reality of salvation." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

5. **Prayer as Participation in Divine Life**
    - Prayer is a means by which believers participate in the **life of God** and the coming kingdom.
    - It is both personal and communal, expressing dependence on God's ongoing revelation.
    - Prayer aligns the church with God's will and mission in the world.
    - It is an act of hope, anticipating the fulfillment of God's promises.
    - "Prayer is the expression of the church's participation in the eschatological movement of God's kingdom." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Legacy and Impact

Pannenberg’s theology of the **means of grace** has significantly shaped contemporary ecumenical discussions, especially regarding the relationship between **Word and sacrament**. His emphasis on the eschatological and communal dimensions of these means has influenced both Protestant and Catholic thought. By grounding the efficacy of preaching, sacraments, and prayer in the objective reality of God's action, Pannenberg provided a robust alternative to both subjectivist and purely symbolic interpretations. His work continues to inform systematic theology, particularly in discussions of ecclesiology and the church's participation in God's revelation.
