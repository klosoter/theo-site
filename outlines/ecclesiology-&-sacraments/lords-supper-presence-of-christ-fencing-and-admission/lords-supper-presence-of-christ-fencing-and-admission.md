### TITLE
At Table with the Risen Lord: Real Presence and a Fenced Welcome

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Early church to modernity**, situated within **ecclesiology and sacramental theology** as a means of grace.
- Core question: How is **Christ present in the Supper**, and on what basis should the church **fence and admit communicants**?
- Principal figures or schools: **Augustine** on sign and thing; **Luther** on sacramental union; **Zwingli** on memorial; **Calvin** on spiritual presence; **Trent (1551)** on transubstantiation; **Westminster (1646–1648)** on admission; **Old Princeton** and **Dutch Neo-Calvinism** on means of grace and discipline.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **sacrament as sign and seal**, **union with Christ**, **manducatio impiorum vs spiritual feeding**, **extra Calvinisticum**, **church discipline and fencing of the table**.
- Major texts, events, works, dates: **1 Cor 10–11**, **Synoptic institution narratives**, **John 6**; **Lateran IV (1215)**, **Marburg (1529)**, **Trent (1551)**, **Directory for Public Worship (1645)**, **WCF 29 (1646–1648)**, **BEM (1982)**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Reformed spiritual real presence with guarded admission**; **Roman Catholic transubstantiation**; **Lutheran sacramental union with manducatio impiorum**; **Zwinglian memorialism**; **modern ecumenical open table**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Ongoing disputes over **paedocommunion, closed/open communion, virtual communion**, and the ethics of **discipline and hospitality**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The Lord's Supper is a **sacrament of the covenant of grace**, instituted by Christ for the church's nourishment, unity, and proclamation of his death until he comes. In classical terms, the Supper is both **sign and seal**: the visible elements of bread and wine signify Christ's body and blood and, by the Holy Spirit, seal to believers the benefits of his atoning work. This locus belongs to **ecclesiology and the means of grace**, but it touches **Christology** and **pneumatology** in how Christ is present and benefits are communicated. The central doctrinal axes are the mode of **Christ's presence**, the relation of sign to the res, and pastoral norms of **fencing and admission**.

Within confessional Protestantism, the Supper is neither a bare symbol nor a repeated sacrifice, but an ordained means of **communion with the risen Christ**. Reformed theology insists that reception is by faith, through **the Holy Spirit's agency**, anchored in believers' **union with Christ**. The questions of fencing and admission follow: who should eat and drink, under what conditions, and through what oversight of the **visible church's discipline**. The grammar is covenantal: the Supper presupposes **baptism** and a credible profession, with elders stewarding holiness and **table fellowship**.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The institution narratives in **Luke 22:14–20; Matt 26:26–29; Mark 14:22–25** provide the dominical warrant. Jesus' words, "This is my body... This is my blood," frame the sign-referent relation and the sacrificial horizon, while "Do this in remembrance of me" establishes **anamnesis** as covenantal re-presentation before God. **1 Corinthians 10–11** is programmatic: "The cup of blessing... is it not a **koinonia in the blood of Christ**?" and "The bread... is it not a **koinonia in the body of Christ**?" The term koinonia bears the weight of **real participation**, not mere recollection. In **1 Cor 11:27–34**, unworthy eating incurs judgment; the Greek diakrinein in "discerning the body" plausibly refers to recognizing both the **ecclesial body** and the **crucified Lord's body**, grounding the moral and ecclesial logic of **fencing the table**.

Admission and discipline flow from apostolic practice. In **Acts 2:42**, the baptized community "devoted themselves to the apostles' teaching and fellowship, to the breaking of bread and prayers," implying **baptismal priority** and catechetical formation. **1 Cor 5** enjoins excommunication for scandalous sin, which implicitly bars participation in the Supper's **covenant meal**. The eschatological cadence "you proclaim the Lord's death until he comes" weds the Supper to **the messianic banquet** of **Isaiah 25** and **Rev 19**, where table fellowship anticipates consummation and thus requires **holy expectancy** and reconciled relationships.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Patristic witnesses affirm a robust realism of presence without uniform metaphysics. **Ignatius of Antioch (c. 107)** speaks of the Eucharist as "the flesh of our Savior Jesus Christ," and **Justin Martyr (c. 150)** describes it as no common bread. **Irenaeus (c. 180)** balances creaturely elements and **eschatological transformation**, while **Augustine** articulates **sign and thing** with a moral and ecclesial realism: "Be what you see; receive what you are" draws the church into **the totus Christus** as the locus of sacramental participation. The patristic focus on **mystery and unity** grounds early eucharistic discipline, with the disciplina arcani and dismissal of catechumens embodying an **admission practice** ordered to holiness.

Medieval theology tightened metaphysical accounts. Controversy with **Berengar of Tours (1050s)** set the stage for **Lateran IV (1215)** to define **transubstantiation**, invoking **Aristotelian substance and accidents** to secure the reality of Christ's body and blood under the species of bread and wine. The laity's cup was often withheld after **Council of Constance (1415)**, reflecting a clericalized Eucharist; Hussite Utraquists protested. Scholastic theology refined **ex opere operato** efficacy and the role of **adoration**, while pastoral practice sometimes eclipsed communicant frequency in favor of viewing the host, straining the nexus of **presence and participation**.

The Reformation replanted the Supper in preaching, faith, and congregational communion. **Luther** insisted on **sacramental union** and the **manducatio impiorum**, defending Christ's corporeal presence against both Rome and **Zwingli's memorialism**, with the **Marburg Colloquy (1529)** dramatizing the "Hoc est corpus meum" standoff. **Calvin** charted a via media: **spiritual real presence** by the **Holy Spirit's ascent**, grounded in **union with Christ** and the **extra Calvinisticum** to avoid a ubiquitous body. Confessional codifications followed: **Augsburg Confession (1530)**, **Thirty-Nine Articles (1563)** with the **Black Rubric (1552, revised 1662)** clarifying no "corporal presence," the **Belgic Confession (1561)** and **Heidelberg Catechism (1563)** affirming spiritual feeding by faith.

Post-Reformation Reformed practice emphasized **fencing and admission** as pastoral applications of holiness and love. The **Directory for Public Worship (1645)** instructed ministers to warn the ignorant and scandalous, and the **Westminster Confession (1646–1648)** and **Larger Catechism** (esp. Q. 168–177) integrated **discipline and catechesis**: **WCF 29.7–8** denies the wicked's reception of Christ and forbids admitting the ignorant or scandalous; **WLC 173** charges church officers to keep back the unworthy. **Scottish communion tokens (17th–19th centuries)** embodied supervised admission, pairing **elder oversight** with a robust preaching-centered sacramental piety.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster Standards** consolidate classic Reformed convictions. **WCF 27–29** defines sacraments as **signs and seals of the covenant of grace**, denies any **re-sacrifice**, and asserts **spiritual real presence**: believers "really and indeed, yet not carnally and corporally, but spiritually, receive and feed upon Christ." **WCF 29.8** and **WLC 173** on **fencing the table** locate admission within **elderly discipline** and catechesis, bind the Supper to prior **baptism**, and require self-examination with an evangelical accent of assurance rather than scruple. Westminster denies the **manducatio impiorum** and invokes the **extra Calvinisticum** to resist any deification or ubiquity of Christ's human nature.

**Old Princeton** received Westminster faithfully while clarifying its metaphysics and pastoral accents. **Charles Hodge (Systematic Theology, 1871–1873)** argues for a robust **spiritual presence** and union with Christ, critiquing **transubstantiation** as violating **common sense realism** and **Lutheran ubiquity** as confusing **the natures in Christ**. **B. B. Warfield** underscores the Supper as a **means of grace** in union with Word and prayer, cautioning against both **sacramentalism** and **bare memorialism**. Admission is tethered to the **visible church's discipline** and a credible profession; Hodge defends **close communion** between churches sharing the gospel, while rejecting sectarian **closed communion** that makes denominational distinctives the terms of fellowship.

**Dutch Neo-Calvinism** deepens the covenantal and eschatological frame. **Abraham Kuyper** situates the Supper within church as **organism and institute**, treating admission as an act of the institute's keys while orienting the organism toward cultural witness nourished by **sacramental grace**. His peculiar **presumptive regeneration** proposal influenced debates on child communion but did not overturn the need for **credible confession**. **Herman Bavinck (Reformed Dogmatics, 1895–1901)** integrates **union with Christ**, **pneumatological agency**, and **eschatological communion**, affirming spiritual feeding and rejecting **manducatio impiorum**. Both stress **catechesis**, weekly or frequent celebration, and pastoral **fencing** ordered to doxological joy.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology, articulated at **Trent Session XIII (1551)**, teaches **transubstantiation**, the conversion of substance while accidents remain, guaranteeing a **real, substantial presence** suitable for adoration. The Eucharist is a **propitiatory sacrifice** only in an unbloody mode as the sacrament makes present the once-for-all sacrifice. Admission norms require **baptism**, state of **grace**, and often **confession**, with recent pastoral discourse under **Pope Francis** exploring complexities of the divorced and remarried while maintaining the **disciplinary canons**. The metaphysics of **substance and accidents** secures identity across appearances, distinguishing Roman presence from both **memorialism** and **Reformed spiritualism**.

Lutheran theology confesses **sacramental union**: Christ's body and blood are present "in, with, and under" the bread and wine, explicated by the **Formula of Concord (1577)** and anchored in the **genus maiestaticum** of **communicatio idiomatum**. Lutherans affirm **manducatio impiorum**, that the wicked truly eat Christ's body to judgment, and often practice **closed communion** to confess unity of doctrine. They reject both **transubstantiation** and **extra Calvinisticum**, preferring a communicatio that allows a certain **ubiquity** of Christ's human nature by virtue of the personal union.

Modern alternatives range from **Zwinglian memorialism** to liberal **symbolic meal** accounts that foreground communal identity. Many free churches practice **open communion**, inviting all who love Jesus, prioritizing **hospitality** over **discipline**. Ecumenical documents like **BEM (1982)** seek convergence on **real presence** without rigid metaphysics; **Orthodox** traditions affirm **mystery** and sometimes use **metousiosis** while highlighting the **epiclesis**. Recent **analytic theology** explores models of **sacramental presence** using **hylomorphism**, **relational tropes**, or **multi-location**, while Reformed analytic voices argue for **pneumatological presence** as a robust real relation without localizing Christ's body apart from **ascension logic**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Debates over **paedocommunion** weigh patristic infant communion in the East and covenantal inclusion against **1 Cor 11**'s demands for self-examination and **Reformed confessional norms**. Some argue that baptized children, like Passover infants, belong at the table; classic Reformed responses appeal to **catechesis, discernment, and profession** as prerequisites for participation. Questions of **intinction**, the use of **grape juice vs wine** since **temperance movements (19th century)**, and **gluten-free bread** raise issues of sign integrity and pastoral accommodation, with Reformed churches generally insisting on **wine** while accommodating **weaker consciences** for the sake of charity and health.

Admission practices face pressures from **ecumenism** and **pastoral trauma care**. Should the table be open to any baptized Christian, or limited to those under the **oversight of a gospel church**? Reformed churches typically practice **close communion**, requiring baptism and credible confession, and excluding those under **discipline**. The **COVID-19 pandemic (2020)** provoked disputes over **virtual communion**, with many Reformed bodies denying that livestreamed observance satisfied the **gathered assembly** requirement, thereby highlighting ecclesiological commitments to **embodied presence**. Objections to fencing as inhospitable are answered by framing discipline as **pastoral protection** that secures the Supper as a **means of grace** rather than an **identity marker**.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The Supper is a site of **Trinitarian action**: the Father gives the Son, the Son gives himself in the elements, and the **Holy Spirit** unites believers to the ascended Christ. The Reformed emphasis on the Spirit's **instrumentality** honors **divine simplicity** and the single opera ad extra while preserving **Christology**: by the **extra Calvinisticum**, Christ remains bodily in heaven according to his humanity, present to us by the Spirit without collapsing natures or compromising the **ascension**. This protects the Chalcedonian integrity of **two natures** and grounds a participation that is real yet non-local.

Anthropologically and soteriologically, the Supper strengthens **union with Christ** and fosters **sanctification** through enacted, embodied faith. Human beings as psychosomatic unities meet Christ by eating and drinking, befitting **sacramental realism** against disembodied spiritualism. Ecclesiologically, fencing and admission enact **the keys of the kingdom**, rendering church membership and **discipline** visible. Eschatologically, the Supper is a **pledge of glory**, sealing believers for the **marriage supper of the Lamb**. Bibliologically, sacramental practice is ruled by **Scripture's sufficiency**, interpreted in the communion of the **church's tradition**, with confessions as subordinate standards directing **pastoral prudence**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, **fencing the table** should be evangelical, not morose. A wise invitation names Christ's **real presence** for nourishment, calls for **self-examination** in faith, and extends assurance to the weak: "If you are baptized, trusting in Christ, at peace with your neighbor, and not under church censure, come and welcome to Jesus Christ." The minister may warn that those persisting in **open scandal** should refrain, not as punishment but as **pastoral care** to avert harm. Elders steward admission through **membership vows**, catechesis, and, if needed, **admonition and suspension**. Historic practices such as **tokens** teach that admission is ecclesial, while contemporary sessions may use pastoral interviews to preserve **holiness and hospitality**.

Liturgically, the Supper benefits from frequency, clear words of institution, thanksgiving, and **epiclesis-like prayer** invoking the **Holy Spirit's** work. Signs should be **ordinary bread and wine**, distributed in a manner that embodies the **one body** and **shared cup**; accommodations for health should preserve the sign's integrity and the **congregation's unity**. After communion, pastoral prayer should seal its benefits in **sanctification** and **mission**. Catechesis of children moves toward first communion upon credible profession, avoiding both **perennial exclusion** and **unreflective participation**. In all, the Supper as **means of grace** is guarded by discipline and opened by the gospel, forming a community marked by **holiness and joy**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The church meets the living Christ at his table in **spiritual real presence**, and in love it **fences and admits** so that this covenant meal remains both holy and hospitable.
