### TITLE
Word, Sacrament, and Discipline: Reformation Ecclesiology between Visibility and Confession

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Sixteenth-century upheaval to seventeenth-century confessionalization; dogmatic locus of **ecclesiology** within the economy of **Word and sacrament**.
- Core question: What constitutes the church of Jesus Christ and how is it discerned, governed, and nourished by **Word, sacraments, and discipline**.
- Figures: **Martin Luther** on the two marks; **Huldrych Zwingli** and **Martin Bucer** on discipline; **John Calvin** on **visible and invisible church**; **Cranmer** and **Knox** on liturgy and polity; **Council of Trent** codifies **Roman Catholic** response; later **Kuyper**, **Bavinck**, **Hodge**, **Warfield** consolidate Reformed lines.
- Key concepts: **marks of the church**, **priesthood of all believers**, **apostolic ministry**, **two kingdoms** and **sphere sovereignty**, **catholicity and unity**, **covenant theology** and **infant baptism**.
- Texts and events: 1517 Luther; 1520 reforming treatises; 1530 **Augsburg Confession** VII; 1541 Geneva **Ecclesiastical Ordinances**; 1545–1563 **Council of Trent**; 1559 **Calvin Institutes IV**; 1561 Belgic Confession; 1563 Heidelberg Catechism; 1566 Second Helvetic; 1646–1648 **Westminster Standards**.
- Reformed trajectory: **three marks**, **presbyterian polity**, **regulative principle of worship**; alternative trajectories in **Roman Catholic hierarchy**, **Lutheran two marks**, **Anabaptist believers church**, and **Anglican via media**.
- Legacy: continuing debates on **unity and diversity**, **discipline and abuse**, **online and multisite church**, **two kingdoms vs neo-Calvinism**, and **ecumenical recognition**.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Reformation ecclesiology centers on the claim that the church is the congregation of the faithful where the **gospel is purely preached and the sacraments rightly administered**, with many Reformed adding church **discipline** as a third mark. The church is both **visible and invisible**, visible as gathered communities under the means of grace and invisible as the assembly of the elect known to God. This location is irreducibly christological and pneumatological, since the **ascended Christ** gathers and feeds his body by the **Holy Spirit** through ordained ministries of **Word and sacrament**.

Within the loci of **Theology Proper** and **Christology**, the church bears the missions of the Son and Spirit into history as a communicative fellowship that is created and sustained by the **Word of God**. Ecclesiology interfaces with **Soteriology** through **union with Christ** and the **means of grace**, and with **Eschatology** through the church as **pilgrim people** anticipating the consummation. The Reformation reframed authority by locating it in **Scripture alone** and in ministerial rather than magisterial teaching, clarifying the derivative nature of ecclesial power as the **keys of the kingdom** exercised through preaching, sacraments, and censures.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The biblical term *ekklesia* names the gathered people of God in continuity with the *qahal* of Israel, as seen in **Acts 2:42–47**, where believers devote themselves to the apostles teaching, fellowship, the breaking of bread, and prayers. The **marks of the church** are adumbrated here and in **1 Corinthians 10:16–17** and **1 Corinthians 11**, where the Supper binds one body through one bread, and in **Ephesians 4:1–16**, where the ascended Christ gives pastors and teachers for the unity and maturity of the body. The **priesthood of all believers** is grounded in **1 Peter 2:9**, while ministerial offices of **presbyteroi** and **episkopoi** are delineated in **1 Timothy 3** and **Titus 1**.

Authority and discipline derive from the **keys of the kingdom** given in **Matthew 16:18–19** and enacted corporately in **Matthew 18:15–20**. The Reformed read the binding and loosing as ministerial stewardship of Christ’s authority, exercised through **church discipline** for restoration. The church’s holiness and mission are evident in **John 17**, where unity is a sign to the world, and in **Ephesians 5:25–27**, where Christ sanctifies the church by the washing of water with the word, linking **baptism**, **sanctification**, and **Word**.

A reformational sacramental realism is rooted in Paul’s language of koinonia in **1 Corinthians 10:16** and the warnings of **1 Corinthians 11:27–32**, supporting the Reformed **real spiritual presence** without localizing Christ’s body on earth. The risen Lord walks among the lampstands in **Revelation 1–3**, commending and correcting real assemblies, which grounds the **visible church** and its accountability under **Christ’s headship**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Late medieval **conciliarism** at Constance 1414–1418 and Basel 1431–1449 critiqued papal absolutism and prepared the ground for reform, while **Wyclif** and **Hus** advanced Scripture’s authority over errant hierarchies. In 1517, **Luther** challenged indulgences, and by 1520, in The Babylonian Captivity of the Church and the Address to the Christian Nobility, he tied the essence of the church to the **Word and sacrament** rather than to a sacrificial priesthood. Luther’s 1539 On the Councils and the Church articulated **two marks** alongside seven external signs, privileging **pure preaching** and **right sacramental administration**.

In Zurich, **Zwingli** emphasized **Word primacy** and a memorialist account of the Supper, while **Bucer** in Strasbourg pressed for **discipline** as constitutive of church life. **Calvin** systematized these insights in the **Institutes IV** 1559, distinguishing **visible and invisible church**, arguing for **three marks**, and articulating **presbyterial governance** anchored in Christ’s gifts. Geneva’s **Ecclesiastical Ordinances** 1541 established pastors, teachers, elders, and deacons, and a **consistory** for moral oversight, embodying the **keys of the kingdom** in communal form.

Confessionalization followed. The **Augsburg Confession** VII 1530 defined the church by **gospel and sacraments**, while the **Belgic Confession** 1561, article 29, lists **three marks** and distinguishes the true church from sects. The **Heidelberg Catechism** 1563 treats church and sacraments as **means of grace** and penitential discipline as medicinal. **Second Helvetic** 1566 famously declares the **preaching of the Word of God is the Word of God**. In England, **Cranmer**’s 1549 and 1552 Prayer Books and the **Thirty-Nine Articles** 1571 craft an **Anglican via media**, Reformed in soteriology yet episcopal in polity. **Trent** 1545–1563 reasserted **apostolic succession**, **seven sacraments**, and **Eucharistic sacrifice**, sharpening lines. The **Westminster Assembly** 1643–1653 codified **presbyterian** doctrine and discipline during the high tide of **Reformed confessionalism**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The **Westminster Standards** 1646–1648 articulate a mature Reformed ecclesiology. Westminster Confession 25 defines the **catholic or universal church**, distinguishes **visible and invisible** aspects, and insists that outside the visible church there is no ordinary possibility of salvation, binding soteriology to **ecclesial means**. Chapters 27–29 treat **baptism and the Lord’s Supper** as **means of grace**, neither empty signs nor ex opere operato, but seals annexed to the promise, effectual to the elect by the **Holy Spirit**. Chapters 30–31 expound **church censures** and **synods**, embedding connectional **presbyterian polity** and the **keys of the kingdom** in graded courts.

Under **Old Princeton**, **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield** defended the **spirituality of the church** and a robust **visible church** concept wedded to confessional standards. Hodge’s Church Polity 1878 upholds **presbyterian connectionalism** as a biblical median between **episcopacy** and **independency**, while refusing sacerdotalism by insisting on **ministerial not magisterial authority**. Warfield tied the **means of grace** to a theology of **inspiration and inerrancy**, guarding the pulpit’s primacy as Christ’s voice to his flock in a modern age.

In **Dutch neo-Calvinism**, **Abraham Kuyper** expounded **sphere sovereignty** and **pluriformity of the church** in 1898–1901, arguing that Christ’s lordship disperses authority through distinct creational spheres, including church and state, while **Herman Bavinck** integrated **catholicity** and **organic life** of the church in Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901. Kuyper’s **institute and organism** distinction preserved the assemblies and offices while honoring the **priesthood of all believers** in the diffusion of Christian life through culture. Both reaffirmed **covenant theology** as the ground for **infant baptism** and catechesis, and both retained a Calvinian **real spiritual presence** at the Supper as communion with the ascended Lord by the Spirit.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The **Roman Catholic** account, sharpened at **Trent**, understands the church as a visible **societas perfecta** marked by **apostolic succession**, **papal primacy**, and the **sacramental economy**. The Eucharist is a **propitiatory sacrifice** by **transubstantiation**, and ordination confers an indelible character upon priests. **Vatican I** 1870 defined papal infallibility, while **Lumen Gentium** 1964 nuanced visibility by teaching that the church of Christ subsists in the Catholic Church while acknowledging **elements of sanctification** outside, reframing catholicity for ecumenism without abandoning **apostolic hierarchy**.

The **Lutheran** tradition, starting with **Augsburg Confession** VII 1530, defines the church by **gospel and sacraments** as sufficient marks, often rejecting **discipline** as constitutive though not irrelevant. Luther’s two-kingdoms motif stresses the church’s proclamation and the Spirit’s hidden work, locating the **invisible church** in the assembly of true believers under the cross. Lutheran sacramental theology affirms **sacramental union** in the Supper, shaping liturgy and polity in ways that diverge from **Reformed spiritual presence** and presbyterian connectionalism.

The **Anabaptist** and Free Church streams, crystallized in **Schleitheim** 1527, construe the church as a voluntary **believers church** defined by **baptism upon confession**, separation from the world, and **the ban**. **Anglican** ecclesiology, codified in the **Thirty-Nine Articles** 1571 and the **Book of Common Prayer**, seeks a **via media** between **Reformed doctrine** and **catholic order**, retaining **episcopacy** while affirming the primacy of **Word and sacrament** and the sufficiency of two sacraments for salvation.

Modern and analytic developments have probed the **social ontology of the church**, authority as **speech-acts**, and the liturgy as a form of practical reason. **Kevin Vanhoozer** frames the church as a **dramatic company** rehearsing the script of Scripture under the direction of Christ, **Nicholas Wolterstorff** and **Terence Cuneo** treat worship as normative action, and analytic theologians such as **Oliver Crisp** have revisited **sacramental efficacy** using contemporary metaphysics. Ecumenically, the **BEM text** 1982 on Baptism, Eucharist, and Ministry and the **Lund Principle** 1952 seek visible unity where possible, prompting Reformed reflection on **catholicity** without sacrificing **confessional integrity**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
In a digital and mobile age, debates rage over **online church**, **multisite models**, and virtual communion, raising questions about the **visibility** of the church, **embodiment**, and the **regulative principle of worship**. Reformed responses typically tether assembly to word read and preached, water applied, bread and wine received, and elders overseeing, insisting that the **means of grace** require bodily gathering under **ordained ministry**. Others appeal to missional adaptability, testing the boundaries of **catholicity and locality**.

The crises of abuse and scandal highlight the fragility of **church discipline**. Critics allege that Reformed churches have sometimes shielded leaders through **connectional polity**, while defenders argue that graded **courts of the church** provide due process and protection for the vulnerable when properly used. Debates on **ordination of women**, **race and reconciliation**, and **LGBTQ+ inclusion** test appeals to **Scripture alone**, **catholic consensus**, and **natural law**, with intra-Reformed variance between **two-kingdoms** proposals and **Kuyperian cultural engagement** on public witness.

Sacramental practice remains contested. Questions about **frequency of communion**, **fencing the table**, **paedocommunion**, and **intinction** press for coherent application of **covenant theology** and **pastoral care**. Baptismal practice divides Reformed and Baptist heirs of the Reformation over **infant baptism** and church membership, with some pursuing renewed **Reformed-Baptist dialogues** that retrieve the **visible church** as a soteriological ordinary rather than a mere voluntary association.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Reformation ecclesiology is anchored in **Trinity** and **Christology**. The church is the **totus Christus** by union with Christ, yet Christ remains the head who alone saves, rules, and indwells by the **Holy Spirit**. The **missions of the Son and Spirit** ground the church’s being and act, while **Christ’s threefold office** of prophet, priest, and king structures ministry in **Word, sacrament, and discipline**. The Reformed **real spiritual presence** depends on Christ’s ascension and the Spirit’s descent, guarding the integrity of both natures in **Chalcedonian** terms.

Anthropologically, humans are social and liturgical creatures, thus **priesthood of all believers** shapes participation without collapsing **ministerial office**. Bibliologically, **Scripture alone** regulates worship and doctrine through the **regulative principle**, while tradition is received ministerially in **confessions** and **synods**. Eschatologically, the church is **already and not yet**, holy by divine election yet mixed in its **visible form**, which grounds patience in sanctification, hope in promises, and sobriety in governance.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Reformation ecclesiology yields a concrete pastoral program of **ordinary means of grace**. Weekly **Lord’s Day** assemblies center on **expository preaching**, **baptism**, and the **Lord’s Supper**, with **public prayer** and **psalmody** ordered by the **regulative principle of worship**. Catechesis through the **Heidelberg Catechism** and **Westminster Catechisms** forms minds and habits, while the **eldership** shepherds souls through **discipline** aimed at restoration. The **diaconate** embodies mercy as the church’s social witness to the kingdom.

Polity serves mission. **Presbyterian connectionalism** enables mutual accountability, church planting, and relief, while **sphere sovereignty** or **two-kingdoms** frameworks guide public discipleship without confusing church and state. Reformed sacramental piety sustains assurance and repentance, integrating **covenant promises** with embodied signs, and fosters **catholicity** by guarding a recognizable pattern of **Word, sacrament, and discipline** that is humble about boundaries yet serious about communion.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Reformation ecclesiology holds together **Word, sacrament, and discipline** under **Christ’s headship** to gather a holy yet pilgrim **visible church** that serves the salvation of the world.
