# John Frame (1939–)

John Frame’s understanding of **Reformation ecclesiology** is shaped by his distinctive **triperspectivalism**, emphasizing the church as the people of God, the centrality of the **Word and sacraments**, and the supreme authority of **Scripture** in all matters of church life. He upholds the **priesthood of all believers** and the **regulative principle**, while advocating for flexibility in church forms and a focus on the church’s mission and unity.

- "The church is the people of God, called together by his Word, indwelt by his Spirit, and sent into the world to bear witness to Christ." ("Systematic Theology")

## Key Works

- "Systematic Theology: An Introduction to Christian Belief"
- "Evangelical Reunion: Denominations and the One Body of Christ"
- "Worship in Spirit and Truth"
- "The Doctrine of the Christian Life"
- "The Doctrine of the Word of God"

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Scripture as the Supreme Authority for the Church**
    - The church’s doctrine, government, and worship must be normed by **Scripture alone**.
    - Rejects both ecclesiastical tradition and subjective experience as final authorities.
    - Upholds the **regulative principle of worship** as a Reformation legacy.
    - Sees **sola Scriptura** as foundational for all ecclesiological reflection.
    "The church must always be reformed according to the Word of God." ("Systematic Theology")

2. **Triperspectival Approach to Ecclesiology**
    - Applies **normative**, **situational**, and **existential** perspectives to church life.
    - Emphasizes the interplay between biblical commands, historical context, and the church’s spiritual experience.
    - Argues that healthy ecclesiology requires attention to all three perspectives.
    - Uses this model to critique both rigid formalism and unprincipled pragmatism.
    "We must look at the church from the perspectives of God’s norms, the facts of the situation, and the hearts of God’s people." ("Systematic Theology")

3. **Priesthood of All Believers and Church Unity**
    - Affirms the Reformation doctrine that all believers share in Christ’s priesthood.
    - Critiques denominationalism that undermines visible unity.
    - Stresses the importance of mutual ministry and spiritual gifts within the body.
    - Calls for visible expressions of unity without compromising truth.
    "The priesthood of believers means that all Christians have direct access to God and ministry to one another." ("Evangelical Reunion")

4. **Flexibility in Church Polity and Forms**
    - Argues that **Scripture prescribes the essentials** of church government but allows flexibility in non-essentials.
    - Critiques rigid adherence to one polity (e.g., Presbyterian, Episcopal, Congregational) as unbiblical.
    - Emphasizes the need for contextual adaptation while maintaining biblical principles.
    - Sees the diversity of Reformation polities as legitimate within the bounds of Scripture.
    "The New Testament gives us principles, not a detailed blueprint, for church government." ("Systematic Theology")

5. **Centrality of Word, Sacraments, and Discipline**
    - Identifies the **marks of the church** as the faithful preaching of the Word, right administration of the sacraments, and church discipline.
    - Insists these marks are essential for the church’s identity and health.
    - Warns against neglecting any of the marks as a departure from Reformation ecclesiology.
    - Connects these marks to the church’s mission and witness in the world.
    "The true church is where the Word is preached, the sacraments are administered, and discipline is practiced." ("Systematic Theology")

## Legacy and Impact

John Frame’s approach to **Reformation ecclesiology** has significantly influenced contemporary Reformed thought by integrating classic Reformation principles with his **triperspectival** method. His emphasis on the sufficiency of Scripture, the priesthood of all believers, and the need for unity has shaped ongoing discussions on church government, worship, and ecumenism. Frame’s flexible yet principled ecclesiology continues to challenge both traditionalist and pragmatic tendencies within evangelical and Reformed churches.
