# Paul Tillich (1886–1965)

Paul Tillich approached **Reformation ecclesiology** through the lens of his broader **systematic theology**, emphasizing the dynamic relationship between the **church as a spiritual community** and its historical, institutional expressions. He critically engaged with **Protestant principles** such as the **priesthood of all believers** and the **Word of God**, while also highlighting the dialectic between the **visible and invisible church**.

- "The Protestant principle is the constant judge over the church, calling it back to its essential nature." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Key Works

- Systematic Theology, Vol. 3
- The Protestant Era
- Dynamics of Faith
- Theology of Culture

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **The Protestant Principle as Critical Norm**
    - The **Protestant principle** critiques all ecclesiastical claims to absolute authority.
    - It insists that the church remains subject to the **Word of God** and never becomes identical with it.
    - This principle guards against idolatry of the institution.
    - It fosters ongoing **reformation** and self-critique within the church.
    - "The Protestant principle is the judge of every concrete church situation." (The Protestant Era)

2. **Dialectic of Visible and Invisible Church**
    - The **visible church** is the historical, organized community.
    - The **invisible church** is the spiritual reality of those united by faith.
    - Tillich emphasizes the tension and interplay between these two aspects.
    - The church must never equate its visible form with the fullness of the **Kingdom of God**.
    - "The church is always both a spiritual community and a sociological reality." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

3. **Priesthood of All Believers**
    - Every believer participates in the spiritual life and mission of the church.
    - This principle undermines hierarchical or clerical monopolies on grace.
    - It affirms the direct relationship between the individual and God.
    - Tillich sees this as a hallmark of **Reformation ecclesiology**.
    - "The Protestant principle means that no human mediation can claim final authority." (The Protestant Era)

4. **Church as Bearer of the New Being**
    - The church mediates the **New Being** revealed in Christ.
    - It is a community where the **Spiritual Presence** is manifest.
    - The church’s mission is to witness to the **reconciliation** accomplished in Christ.
    - Its structures are provisional, serving the ongoing work of the Spirit.
    - "The church is the place where the New Being is actualized in history." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

5. **Ecclesial Structures as Ambiguous**
    - All church forms are historically conditioned and subject to ambiguity.
    - Institutionalization is necessary but always in danger of distortion.
    - The church must continually reform its structures in light of the **Gospel**.
    - Tillich warns against identifying any form with the **absolute**.
    - "Every church is subject to the ambiguities of existence and must be reformed." (The Protestant Era)

## Legacy and Impact

Tillich’s interpretation of **Reformation ecclesiology** has influenced both Protestant and ecumenical discussions, especially through his articulation of the **Protestant principle** and the dynamic between the **visible and invisible church**. His thought challenges churches to maintain a posture of self-critique and openness to reform, resisting institutional absolutism. Tillich’s legacy endures in contemporary theology’s engagement with the church’s identity, mission, and relationship to culture.
