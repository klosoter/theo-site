### TITLE
Between Sign and Reality: The Church as Instrument, Witness, and Foretaste of the Kingdom

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **ecclesiology within eschatology**, the relation of **church and kingdom** across patristic to contemporary debates, 251 to 1965 to present.
- Core question: Is the **church identical with the kingdom**, a subset, or its **sign and instrument** within the **already-not-yet eschatology** of Scripture.
- Principal figures or schools: **Augustine's City of God** as paradigm, **Calvin's two kingdoms**, **Old Princeton and Vos on kingdom and church**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist sphere sovereignty**, **Vatican II Lumen Gentium** on subsistence, **Ladd on inaugurated kingdom**.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **Christ's threefold office**, **missio Dei**, **keys of the kingdom and church discipline**, **sacramental eschatology**, **marks of the church and catholicity**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **Cyprian On the Unity of the Church, c 251**, **Augustine City of God, 413 to 426**, **Calvin Institutes IV, 1559**, **Westminster Confession 1646 to 1648**, **Vos 1903**, **Kuyper 1898 lectures and Pro Rege 1911 to 1912**, **Vatican II Lumen Gentium 1964**, **Ladd 1959 and 1964**.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Westminster spirituality of the church**, **Kuyperian cultural engagement**, **Roman Catholic subsistit in**, **Lutheran two kingdoms**, **Anabaptist radical discipleship**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: **post-Christendom public theology**, **mission as kingdom proclamation**, **political theology** and the limits of **church power and civic power**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
The relation of **church and kingdom** locates ecclesiology within the horizon of **eschatology**, under the lordship of the risen Christ who reigns until all enemies are placed beneath his feet. The kingdom, announced by Jesus as the **reign of God**, is God’s royal saving action that has dawned in Christ and the Spirit, and will be consummated at his return. The church, gathered by the Word and Spirit, is the community that confesses Jesus as Lord, receives the **keys of the kingdom**, and lives as a **sign, instrument, and foretaste** of the kingdom’s arrival. This mapping guards two errors, on one side an identification of the kingdom with ecclesiastical institutions, on the other a privatized church that evacuates the **missio Dei** from history. Dogmatically, the topic connects to **Christ’s mediatorial kingship**, the economy of the **Holy Spirit**, and the **sacramental economy** through which the kingdom’s life is signified and sealed among the baptized. The thesis to defend is this, the church is not the kingdom in extension yet the church is where the kingdom is normatively known and enacted through Word, sacrament, and discipline, because the **already-not-yet eschatology** that structures the New Testament orders the church to the kingdom as its living witness.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
In the Old Testament, the **malkuth Yahweh**, the kingship of the Lord, structures Israel’s covenantal life, Psalm 93, Psalm 97 to 99, with eschatological horizons in Daniel 7 where the Son of Man receives an everlasting dominion. Prophetic hope links kingship and new covenant renewal, Isaiah 52 verse 7, Isaiah 61 verses 1 to 3, joined to the **Davidic promise**, 2 Samuel 7. Jesus proclaims, The time is fulfilled, the **basileia tou theou** is at hand, repent and believe the gospel, Mark 1 verse 15, and his parables describe the kingdom’s present hiddenness and future unveiling, Matthew 13, **already-not-yet eschatology**. He entrusts to Peter the keys of the kingdom, promising to build his **ekklesia** upon a rock confession and to bind and loose on earth as in heaven, Matthew 16 verses 18 to 19, with corporate adjudication in Matthew 18. John 3 speaks of seeing and entering the kingdom by new birth, linking **regeneration and kingdom entrance**, while Luke 17 underscores the kingdom in the midst through Jesus himself. Acts frames apostolic mission as kingdom witness, Acts 1 verse 3, Acts 28 verse 31, and Paul describes the kingdom as righteousness, peace, and joy in the **Holy Spirit**, Romans 14 verse 17, locating it in the ethical and pneumatic life of the body. Colossians 1 verse 13 speaks of transfer into the kingdom of the Son, while Ephesians 1 verses 22 to 23 identifies the church as Christ’s body, the **fullness of him** who fills all in all, a royal-cultic identity that manifests the manifold wisdom of God to the powers, Ephesians 3 verse 10. Hebrews 12 verse 28 calls the church to gratitude for receiving a **kingdom that cannot be shaken**, and Revelation climaxes with the kingdoms of this world becoming the kingdom of our Lord and of his Christ, Revelation 11 verse 15, and the descent of the new Jerusalem, Revelation 21 to 22. The biblical upshot is this, the church is gathered by the **kingdom gospel**, endowed with **kingdom authority** through the keys, and constituted to live out **kingdom holiness** in the Spirit as anticipatory polity.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Early writers oscillated between **pilgrim conviction and imperial hope**. **Cyprian’s On the Unity of the Church**, circa 251, equated communion with the bishop with communion with Christ, thereby strengthening the institutional link between **church and salvation**. **Eusebius of Caesarea** construed Constantine’s reign, after 312, as providential extension of the divine rule, risking a **Constantinian fusion** of church and empire. **Augustine’s City of God**, 413 to 426, corrected this by opposing the saeculum of the earthly city to the **City of God**, insisting that the two cities are intermixed in history, thus the church is the pilgrim society of love, not a realized political kingdom. Medieval developments like the **papal plenitudo potestatis**, eleventh to thirteenth centuries, accentuated hierarchical jurisdiction as vicar of Christ’s kingship, while monastic and mendicant movements lived the **eschatological counsels** as signs of the kingdom. The Reformers reconfigured the relation, **Luther’s two kingdoms** distinguished God’s rule through the gospel from his rule through law and civil sword, while **Calvin’s twofold governance** located Christ’s spiritual kingdom in the church’s ministerial authority and affirmed civil order as God’s gift under providence. Post Reformation confessions, **Westminster 1646 to 1648**, delineated the powers of **Word, sacrament, and discipline** as the church’s distinct jurisdiction. In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, **Kuyper’s sphere sovereignty** and **Bavinck’s organic motif** widened the scope of Christ’s kingship beyond the church, while **Vos, Ridderbos, and Ladd** articulated the kingdom’s inaugurated character and clarified that the church is its **historical embodiment as sign and instrument**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Within classic Reformed theology, **Calvin’s Institutes IV, 1559** locates the kingdom’s spiritual government in the church where Christ reigns through the preaching of the Word and the right administration of the sacraments, the **regulative presence of the keys**. **Westminster Confession**, chapters 23, 25, and 31, differentiates civil and ecclesial powers, civil magistrates are not to assume the minister’s office, while the church wields **ministerial not magisterial power**, that is declarative and spiritual. **Old Princeton**, with **Charles Hodge** on church polity and **B. B. Warfield** on the **kingdom as God’s redemptive reign**, defended a spirituality of the church that resists ecclesial capture by political programs while commending ethical witness consonant with **natural law under providence**. **Geerhardus Vos**, 1903, taught that Jesus’ kingdom teaching centers on the presence of the age to come in his person and mission, hence the church is the **community of the eschaton** summoned by that message. **Dutch Neo-Calvinism**, with **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck**, argued that Christ is **sovereign over every sphere**, education, arts, politics, and that the church exists as institute and as organism, the latter being the people of God scattered in vocations who manifest the **kingdom’s leavening power**. The Reformed synthesis holds three axioms, Christ’s kingship is universal, the church’s power is spiritual, and the church is the normative locus where the **kingdom’s Word and sacrament** are enacted as a foretaste of the new creation.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology after **Vatican II** affirmed that the kingdom is broader than the church, yet **subsists in the Catholic Church**, Lumen Gentium 1964, because the fullness of the means of grace are found there, while elements of sanctification and truth are present beyond its visible boundaries. **Henri de Lubac** argued that the church is born from the **Eucharist as eschatological body**, and **Joseph Ratzinger** emphasized that Jesus gathered Israel eschatologically, so **church and kingdom** are inseparable though not identical. Lutheran theology holds that the church bears the **marks of Word and Sacrament**, and that the **two kingdoms doctrine** preserves the freedom of the gospel from coercion, with the kingdom of the right hand hidden under the cross, while civic righteousness in the left hand is ordered by law for temporal peace. Modern proposals include **liberation theology**, **Moltmann’s Theology of Hope** and The Church in the Power of the Spirit 1975, and **political theology**, which often accentuate the **kingdom as social transformation** and call the church to embody anticipatory justice, while **Anabaptist traditions** like **Yoder’s Politics of Jesus**, 1972, argue that the church’s visible discipleship is itself the **politics of the kingdom**. In analytic and philosophical theology, work on **social ontology of the church**, such as discussion of the church as an enduring corporate person, and analysis of the **identity conditions for the kingdom** as a divine governance rather than a human institution, clarifies that the kingdom is primarily a **divine action concept**, while the church is a creaturely community that participates in that action.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
Contemporary debates revolve around **mission and public theology**, with some advocating a **kingdom without church** approach that prioritizes justice initiatives, and others defending a **church without kingdom** approach that narrows mission to revivalist expansion. Critics warn that equating the kingdom with the church feeds **triumphalism**, sanctifying ecclesial power, while separating them too sharply breeds **quietism** or activism detached from Christological and sacramental sources. Nationalist cooptation of the church raises alarms about **civil religion masquerading as kingdom**, while technocratic approaches to church growth risk reducing **kingdom proclamation** to market strategies. There is dispute over **two kingdoms and one kingdom** models within Reformed circles, with some retrieving a law and gospel distinction to protect conscience in plural societies, while others, often **Kuyperian**, insist on **public discipleship in every sphere**. The most generative proposals retrieve **already-not-yet eschatology** and the pattern of **sign, instrument, and foretaste**, coupled with the **keys of the kingdom and church discipline** as safeguards against drift, and a thick account of **sacramental eschatology** that funds justice and holiness without collapsing the church into the secular city.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Trinitarianly, the kingdom is the Father’s rule mediated through the **incarnate Son** and effected by the **Holy Spirit**, so the church stands under the **missio Dei** as the people gathered into the Son and sent in the Spirit to the world. Christologically, the **threefold office** governs the relation, as Prophet he proclaims the kingdom, as Priest he opens access into the kingdom by atonement, as King he rules the church by his Word and Spirit, so the church is the **royal priesthood** that lives from his victory. Pneumatologically, the Spirit is the **arrabon**, the down payment of the inheritance, Ephesians 1 verse 14, empowering **kingdom ethics** such as righteousness, peace, and joy. Anthropologically, the **imago Dei** is renewed in Christ and ordered to communion and dominion, thus the church becomes a **new humanity** that anticipates the restored creation. Soteriologically, **union with Christ** incorporates believers into the community where the **keys of the kingdom** open and close by the gospel, and **baptism and the Lord’s Supper** signify participation in the death and reign of Christ. Eschatologically, the church awaits the **parousia**, prays Thy kingdom come, and lives as an embassy of the world to come, while ecclesiologically the **marks of the church** define the place where the kingdom’s saving reign is concentrated and displayed.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, the church that knows it is a **sign and instrument of the kingdom** resists despair because Christ reigns, and resists triumphalism because the reign is **hidden and cruciform** until the last day. Preaching centers on the **gospel of the kingdom**, teaching the congregation to seek first the kingdom and his righteousness, and catechesis trains disciples in **kingdom practices** that mirror the Sermon on the Mount. Liturgy becomes **kingdom rehearsal**, the Lord’s Prayer forms the heart, Thy kingdom come, and the Lord’s Supper is tasted as **foretaste of the banquet**, Luke 22 verses 16 to 18, shaping a people of joy and generosity. Polity and discipline enact the **keys of the kingdom** for the sake of holiness and reconciliation, refusing both laxity and legalism. Diaconal ministry becomes the church’s **works of mercy** as public witness to the King’s justice, without confusing the church’s spiritual mission with the coercive powers of the state. Vocational discipleship forms the church as organism to permeate culture according to **sphere sovereignty**, while the institute remains faithful to **Word and sacrament**. The result is a people who engage public life with **humble courage**, repent readily, refuse idolatrous loyalties, and embody the **already-not-yet eschatology** with patience and hope.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
The church lives as the community gathered by the gospel to wield the **keys of the kingdom** and to become a **sign, instrument, and foretaste** of the Father’s reign in Christ through the **Holy Spirit** until the glory appears.
