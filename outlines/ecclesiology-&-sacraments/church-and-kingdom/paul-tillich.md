# Paul Tillich (1886–1965)

Paul Tillich understood the **Church** as the concrete community in which the **Kingdom of God** becomes manifest within history, yet always remains distinct from it. He emphasized the **dialectical relationship** between the Church as an institution and the **eschatological reality** of the Kingdom, arguing that the Church is both a sign and a bearer of the Kingdom but never its full realization. For Tillich, the **Spiritual Community** transcends denominational boundaries and points toward the ultimate unity of all being in God.

- "The Church is the community in which the Kingdom of God is anticipated, but it is never identical with the Kingdom itself." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

## Key Works

- Systematic Theology, Vol. 3: Life and the Spirit, History and the Kingdom of God
- The Protestant Era
- Theology of Culture
- The Interpretation of History
- Love, Power, and Justice

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Church as the Anticipatory Community**
    - The Church is the historical embodiment where the **Kingdom of God** is anticipated but not fulfilled.
    - It serves as a visible sign of the **Spiritual Community** that transcends history.
    - The Church is always subject to ambiguity and sin, reflecting the tension between the **already** and the **not yet**.
    - Its structures are provisional, pointing beyond themselves to the eschatological Kingdom.
    - "The Church is the anticipation of the Kingdom of God in history, but it is not the Kingdom itself." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

2. **Kingdom of God as Eschatological Reality**
    - The **Kingdom** is not a political or institutional reality but the ultimate fulfillment of divine meaning and justice.
    - It represents the **New Being** inaugurated in Christ and consummated at the end of history.
    - The Kingdom is both present in the transformative power of the Spirit and future in its final realization.
    - All historical expressions of the Kingdom are fragmentary and provisional.
    - "The Kingdom of God is the ultimate aim of history, transcending every historical realization." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

3. **Dialectic of Church and Kingdom**
    - The Church and Kingdom are in a dialectical relationship: the Church points to the Kingdom but is never identical with it.
    - The Church must always be self-critical, aware of its own limitations and failures.
    - Institutionalization can obscure the Church’s calling as a sign of the Kingdom.
    - The tension between the Church’s historical reality and the Kingdom’s transcendence is essential to Tillich’s ecclesiology.
    - "The Church is always under the judgment of the Kingdom of God." (The Protestant Era)

4. **Spiritual Community Beyond Institutional Boundaries**
    - The **Spiritual Community** includes all who are grasped by the **New Being** in Christ, regardless of denominational lines.
    - The unity of the Church is grounded in the work of the Spirit, not in organizational structures.
    - The Spiritual Community is the true bearer of the Kingdom’s presence in history.
    - This community transcends the visible Church and anticipates the ultimate unity of all in God.
    - "The Spiritual Community is the real presence of the Kingdom of God in history." (Systematic Theology, Vol. 3)

5. **Eschatological Critique of the Church**
    - The Church is always provisional and must be open to reform in light of the coming Kingdom.
    - Eschatology provides a critical standard by which the Church judges itself and its mission.
    - The Church’s mission is to witness to the Kingdom, not to establish it by its own power.
    - The ultimate fulfillment of the Kingdom relativizes all ecclesiastical claims and achievements.
    - "The Church must always be reformed according to the criterion of the Kingdom of God." (The Protestant Era)

## Legacy and Impact

Tillich’s theology of **Church and Kingdom** has profoundly shaped modern Protestant thought, especially in its insistence on the provisional and self-critical nature of the Church. His dialectical approach influenced later discussions on the relationship between the visible Church and the eschatological hope of the Kingdom. Tillich’s emphasis on the **Spiritual Community** and the transcendent character of the Kingdom continues to inform ecumenical dialogue and critiques of ecclesiastical triumphalism. His work remains a touchstone for those seeking to articulate the Church’s mission in light of the ultimate purposes of God.
