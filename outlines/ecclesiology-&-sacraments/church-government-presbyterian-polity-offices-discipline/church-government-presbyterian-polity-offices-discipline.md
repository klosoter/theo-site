### TITLE
Elders in Connection: Presbyterian Polity, Ordered Offices, and Restorative Discipline under Christ the Head

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Dogmatic location: **ecclesiology and the marks of the church** within Reformed theology, focused on polity, office, and discipline, ca. 1541–present.
- Core question: What form of visible order best expresses **Christ's headship and the keys of the kingdom** for teaching, sacraments, and discipline?
- Figures and schools: **John Calvin** (Geneva Ordinances, 1541), **John Knox** and Scottish **Books of Discipline** (1560, 1578), **Westminster Assembly** (1643–1653), **Old Princeton** (C. Hodge), **Dutch Neo-Calvinism** (Kuyper, Bavinck).
- Key concepts: **presbyterian connectionalism**, **parity of elders**, **graded courts** (session, presbytery, synod, general assembly), **ruling and teaching elders**, **restorative discipline**.
- Major texts/events: Geneva Consistory (1542), Second Book of Discipline (1578), Synod of Dort and **Church Order** (1618–1619), **Form of Presbyterial Church Government** (1645), **Westminster Confession** chs. 30–31 (1646–1648), PCUSA **Form of Government** (1789).
- Reformed vs alternatives: **Presbyterian graded eldership** over against **episcopal hierarchy** and **congregational independency**; Lutheran and Anglican mixed patterns; modern **nondenominational pragmatism**.
- Legacy/debate: Ongoing questions about **women in office**, **discipline and abuse prevention**, **multisite and digital church**, and **ecumenical recognition** of orders.

### ESSAY 1500-1800 words.
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Presbyterian polity is the claim that Christ governs his church through **plurality of elders** gathered in **graded courts**, exercising **ministerial, not magisterial authority** for Word, sacrament, and discipline. The visible church possesses the **keys of the kingdom** to bind and loose, not by coercive power but by declarative judgments that obligate conscience insofar as they agree with Scripture. The offices ordinarily are **teaching elder (minister or pastor)**, **ruling elder**, and **deacon**, with some streams recognizing a distinct **doctor/teacher**. This locus stands within **ecclesiology** and touches the classic marks of the church: the pure preaching of the Word, right administration of the sacraments, and faithful exercise of **church discipline**.

Presbyterian claims are often advanced on a **jure divino presbyterianism** reading, namely that Scripture prescribes an eldership with parity and connectional courts, rather than merely permitting it. Yet even when held **jure humano** as prudent rather than divinely mandated, Reformed theology insists that polity must reflect **Christ's headship** and the church's **spirituality and ministerial power**. The conceptual core is that authority in the church is derivative, covenantal, and ordered to edification, so that **office and oversight** serve the gospel's public administration.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The New Testament presents a pattern of **plural elders** overseeing local congregations. In Acts 14:23 Paul and Barnabas appoint elders in every church, while Titus 1:5 assigns Titus to appoint elders in every town. The lexical overlap of *presbyteros* and *episkopos* indicates identity of office rather than a tiered hierarchy: Acts 20:17, 28 addresses the same men as **elders** and **overseers** who must **shepherd the flock**, and 1 Peter 5:1–4 exhorts **fellow elders** to exercise oversight. The parity of elders is nuanced by 1 Timothy 5:17, where some elders labor in preaching and teaching and receive double honor, grounding the distinction between **ruling and teaching elders** within **one presbyterial order**.

Connectionalism is biblically adduced from **Acts 15**, where a dispute about circumcision is resolved through a **Jerusalem council** of apostles and elders, whose letter is received as binding by the churches. The council's deliberative character, use of Scripture, and consensual judgment exemplify **graded courts** that render ministerial decisions. The church's power of the keys is instituted by Jesus in Matthew 16:19 and Matthew 18:15–20, where binding and loosing are connected to **discipline and reconciliation**. Paul exercises discipline in 1 Corinthians 5 against scandalous sin and commends restoration in 2 Corinthians 2, portraying discipline as both **corrective and restorative**.

Ephesians 4:11–16 lists **pastors and teachers** among Christ's gifts to equip the saints, showing that office is a means of **edification and unity**. Hebrews 13:7, 17 calls believers to obey leaders who keep watch over their souls, grounding **accountable oversight** in pastoral care, not domination. The New Testament, so read, commends an eldership with parity and distinction, connectional counsel for disputes, and **discipline as a mark of the church** that preserves holiness and communion.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
In the subapostolic era, **Ignatius of Antioch** (ca. 110) witnesses to emerging **monoepiscopacy**, yet also assumes presbyters and deacons. The consolidation of a single bishop per city intensified through **Cyprian's** episcopacy in the third century, eventually flowering into medieval hierarchical **canon law**. While elements of synodality persisted, the **Roman episcopal** model largely eclipsed presbyterial parity. The Reformation reopened the question by grounding church order anew in Scripture and the **priesthood of all believers**.

**John Calvin's Ecclesiastical Ordinances (1541)** in Geneva articulate four offices: pastors, teachers, elders, and deacons. The **Geneva Consistory (1542)**, a mixed body of pastors and lay elders, exercised **discipline** as moral and ecclesial censure, leaving civil penalties to the magistrate. In Scotland, **John Knox** and colleagues produced the **First Book of Discipline (1560)** and the **Second Book of Discipline (1578)**, which embedded **presbyterian connectionalism** and recognized the **parity of elders** across **sessions, presbyteries, synods, and General Assembly**. In the Netherlands, the **Synod of Dort (1618–1619)** adopted a **Church Order** governing consistories, classes, and synods, showcasing a continental presbyterian pattern.

The **Westminster Assembly (1643–1653)** codified presbyterian polity in English-speaking lands. The **Form of Presbyterial Church Government (1645)**, the **Westminster Confession of Faith (1646–1648)** chapters 30–31 on censures and synods, and the **Directory for Public Worship (1645)** together frame **office, worship, and discipline**. Scottish presbyterianism was settled by the **1690 Revolution Settlement**, while in America the **Presbyterian Church in the USA (1789)** adopted a **Form of Government** and Book of Discipline, reflecting both **connectional polity** and **voluntary church** realities. Subsequent controversies, such as the **Old Side–New Side** division (1741) and **Old School–New School** split (1837), contested the scope of confessional subscription and revivalism, but retained **graded courts** and **elder oversight**.

The twentieth century saw institutional fragmentation and renewal: the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church (1936)** and the **Presbyterian Church in America (1973)** reclaimed classic **Westminster ecclesiology**, while global presbyterianism grew in Korea and Africa. Throughout, the Reformed diaspora preserved the logic that **Christ rules his church by elders**, who gather in councils to guard doctrine, administer sacraments, and practice **restorative discipline**.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton supplies analytic clarity for **ministerial and declarative authority**. **Charles Hodge, Discussions in Church Polity (1878)**, argues that church power is spiritual, exercised by **courts of elders** whose decisions bind only insofar as they express Scripture. Hodge defends **parity of elders** with a functional distinction between teaching and ruling elders rooted in 1 Timothy 5:17, and affirms **graded courts** as a biblical expedient exemplified by **Acts 15**. For Hodge, discipline is an act of love aimed at **edification**, not domination, and excommunication is the church's gravest censure for persistent unrepentance.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism integrates **sphere sovereignty** and **office of believer**. **Abraham Kuyper** argues that Christ distributes authority across distinct creational spheres, so that **church courts** neither usurp civil jurisdiction nor relinquish their own. **Herman Bavinck** underscores that office is charismatically grounded by the Spirit, while the **consistory and classis** express the church's catholicity in **connectionalism**. In this frame, presbyterian polity guards liberty of the church under **Christ's kingship** while respecting differentiated societal authority.

The Westminster tradition remains the confessional backbone. **WCF 30–31** teaches that censures are necessary for reclaiming offenders, deterring others, vindicating Christ's honor, and preventing leaven in the church, and that **synods and councils** are necessary for better government and edification. The **Form of Presbyterial Church Government (1645)** delineates **ruling and teaching elders**, **deacons**, and **graded courts**, defining ordination as ecclesial recognition with prayer and laying on of hands. This doctrinal matrix ties polity to the **means of grace**, locating **discipline** alongside Word and sacrament as constitutive of the church's visible faithfulness.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic ecclesiology centers on **episcopal hierarchy** culminating in **papal primacy**, with sacramental ontologies of **Holy Orders** that distinguish deacon, priest, and bishop as distinct grades with ontological character. Synodality exists, but final jurisdiction is hierarchical and universal. By contrast, presbyterianism denies any necessary **monoepiscopacy**, holding that **presbyters are bishops** in the New Testament sense and that jurisdiction is ordinarily conciliar and local-to-regional.

Lutheran and Anglican patterns vary. Scandinavian Lutherans retained **bishops** but often stress **evangelical oversight**, while classic Anglicanism asserts **historic episcopate** as *bene esse* or even *esse* of the church, supplemented by **synods** and **convocations**. Congregationalists and Baptists defend **local autonomy**: the keys reside in the gathered congregation, which calls elders and deacons but remains the highest court. Reformed presbyterians respond that **connectionalism** is biblically prudent and historically catholic, providing mutual correction and **shared discipline**.

Modern ecclesial forms include **nondenominational networks**, **multisite churches**, and corporate governance boards, often prioritizing efficiency and brand unity over formal **courts of elders**. Presbyterian critique warns of **charismatic centralization** without accountability. In analytic theology and social ontology, some advance models of **group agency and authority** to clarify how corporate bodies act and bind members. Presbyterianism maps well onto such analyses, positing **deliberative councils** as agents that speak ministerially for the church under **Scripture's authority**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
First, the **exegetical case** for presbyterianism is contested. Episcopalians argue for **apostolic succession** and early monoepiscopacy, while Congregationalists deny that **Acts 15** forms a normative court pattern. Presbyterians reply that **presbyteral parity** with functional specialization, and **connectional adjudication**, best integrate the biblical data. The burden is to show that **parity, plurality, and graded counsel** are not mere pragmatics but **normed patterns**.

Second, questions regarding **women in office** divide presbyterians. Some communions ordain women as **deacons only** or to all offices, others restrict the eldership to qualified men per **1 Timothy 2** and **1 Timothy 3**. The debate turns on exegesis, the nature of **representative headship**, and the church's catholicity. A third cluster concerns **discipline and abuse prevention**: critics note past failures to protect the vulnerable. Presbyterian resources include **transparent process**, **appellate courts**, and **mandatory reporting** to civil authorities under **sphere differentiation**; reforms aim to ensure that **restorative discipline** never shields perpetrators.

Fourth, organizational questions proliferate: **multisite polity** challenges the identification of a local church and **sessional oversight**; **digital communion** raises whether discipline and sacrament can be administered online; **global presbyterianism** seeks unity amid diverse cultures. Responses appeal to **embodied assemblies**, clear jurisdictional lines, and renewed **catechesis** on membership vows, aiming to keep the church's **spiritual mission** clear and its structures humane.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
Christology grounds polity: the church is the body under **Christ the head**, who governs by Word and Spirit through **ordered offices**. The threefold office of Christ as **prophet, priest, and king** refracts into **teaching, diaconal, and ruling** ministries that serve his one mediatorial reign. Theology proper safeguards that church power is **ministerial and declarative**, not **lordly or coercive**, aligning with the **two kingdoms** or **sphere sovereignty** doctrine that differentiates ecclesial and civil authority.

Pneumatology supplies the gifts that constitute **office and discipline**, with ordination as ecclesial recognition of **Spirit-given charisms** for public service. Anthropology frames authority as **ordered freedom**, where submission to elders is a means of growth in holiness, not servility. Soteriology connects polity to the **means of grace**: the preached Word, sacraments, and **discipline** as remedial medicine for the soul. Eschatology warns that church courts are provisional anticipations of the **final judgment**, requiring humility and **repentant practice**. Thus, presbyterian polity is not an adiaphoron but an integrated expression of **catholic truth**.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Practically, **parity of elders** prevents clerical isolation and distributes pastoral care. Sessions shepherd through **oversight, visitation, and catechesis**, while presbyteries examine candidates, plant churches, and guard doctrine through **peer accountability**. The **diaconate** leads the church in mercy and stewardship, embodying the gospel's social care. This ordered life makes discipline intelligible: admonition, suspension from the Lord's Table, and **excommunication** are graded remedies oriented to **restoration and reconciliation**.

Liturgically, presbyterian worship remains under **sessional oversight** guided by the **regulative principle of worship**, protecting the centrality of Word and sacrament. Fencing the Table, receiving members by **credible profession**, and public restoration after repentance embed **discipline** within doxology. Pastors, as **teaching elders**, labor in preaching and sacraments; **ruling elders** guard doctrine, pray, and govern; **deacons** lead mercy ministries. Graded courts mean that when conflicts arise, the church has a path of **appeal and correction** without resort to schism, manifesting the unity of the body under **Christ's kingship**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Presbyterian polity orders the church's common life under **Christ the head** by **elders in connection** who administer Word, sacrament, and **restorative discipline** for the church's edification and the Lord's honor.
