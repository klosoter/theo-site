# Richard B. Gaffin Jr. (1936–)

Richard B. Gaffin Jr. emphasizes the **organic unity** and **eschatological nature** of the **one, holy, catholic, and apostolic Church**. He grounds the Church’s identity in its union with the resurrected Christ, highlighting the centrality of the **Spirit’s work** in constituting the Church as the new humanity. Gaffin’s ecclesiology is deeply shaped by **Pauline theology**, especially the Church’s participation in Christ’s death and resurrection.

- "The church is the eschatological people of God, constituted by the Spirit as the body of the resurrected Christ." (Perspectives on Pentecost)

## Key Works

- Perspectives on Pentecost: New Testament Teaching on the Gifts of the Holy Spirit
- By Faith, Not by Sight: Paul and the Order of Salvation
- "The Church as the New Humanity" (in The Glory of the Atonement, eds. Hill & Letham)
- "The Holy Spirit and Eschatology" (Westminster Theological Journal)
- Resurrection and Redemption: A Study in Paul’s Soteriology

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Eschatological Nature of the Church**
    - The Church is fundamentally an **eschatological community**, already participating in the age to come.
    - Its identity is shaped by the resurrection of Christ and the outpouring of the Spirit.
    - The Church’s holiness and unity are grounded in its inaugurated eschatological reality.
    - The Church is both a present reality and an anticipation of its future consummation.
    - "The church is the community of the resurrection, already sharing in the powers of the age to come." (Perspectives on Pentecost)

2. **Union with Christ as the Basis of Unity**
    - The Church’s **oneness** is rooted in the believer’s union with the risen Christ.
    - All ecclesial distinctions are secondary to the fundamental unity established by Christ’s work.
    - The Spirit applies the benefits of Christ’s resurrection to the whole body.
    - This union is both individual and corporate, forming the Church as Christ’s body.
    - "The unity of the church is nothing less than the unity of the body of Christ himself." (Resurrection and Redemption)

3. **The Holy Spirit as Constitutive Agent**
    - The **Holy Spirit** is the primary agent in constituting and sustaining the Church.
    - Spiritual gifts are given for the edification and mission of the Church.
    - The Spirit’s indwelling marks the Church as holy and set apart.
    - The Spirit’s work is inseparable from the Church’s apostolic foundation.
    - "The Spirit is the bond of union between Christ and his people, and so the bond of the church’s unity and holiness." (Perspectives on Pentecost)

4. **Apostolicity and the Normativity of Scripture**
    - The Church is **apostolic** in its foundation and ongoing life.
    - Apostolic teaching, preserved in Scripture, is the norm for faith and practice.
    - The Church’s mission is to bear witness to the apostolic gospel.
    - The Spirit ensures the continuity of apostolic doctrine in the Church.
    - "Apostolicity is not merely historical succession but fidelity to the apostolic word." (Perspectives on Pentecost)

5. **Catholicity as the Global Scope of the Church**
    - The Church is **catholic** in that it transcends all ethnic, social, and cultural boundaries.
    - The gospel creates a new humanity united in Christ from every nation.
    - Catholicity is expressed in the Church’s worldwide mission and fellowship.
    - The unity of the Church is visible in its confession of the one faith.
    - "The church’s catholicity is the manifestation of the new humanity in Christ, gathered from all peoples." ("The Church as the New Humanity")

## Legacy and Impact

Gaffin’s ecclesiology has significantly shaped contemporary **Reformed theology**, especially in its emphasis on the **eschatological and pneumatic dimensions** of the Church. His work has influenced generations of pastors and theologians at Westminster Theological Seminary and beyond, particularly in understanding the Church’s identity as grounded in union with Christ. Gaffin’s integration of Pauline theology with classic Reformed categories has enriched discussions on the nature and mission of the Church. His legacy endures in ongoing debates about the Spirit’s role and the Church’s apostolic foundation.
