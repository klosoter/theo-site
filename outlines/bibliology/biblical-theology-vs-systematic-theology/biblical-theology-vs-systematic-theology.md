### TITLE
One Bible, Two Sciences: Distinguishing and Integrating Biblical Theology and Systematic Theology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Prolegomena and Bibliology**, 1787 Gabler to the present, located at the interface of **canon, revelation, and dogmatic method**.
- Core question: How do **biblical theology as diachronic description** and **systematic theology as dogmatic synthesis** relate in norming doctrine for the church.
- Principal figures or schools: **J. P. Gabler** separates disciplines as 1787 programmatic, **Geerhardus Vos** pioneers Reformed biblical theology, **Old Princeton** defends **inerrancy and doctrine**, **Bultmann and Barth** contest method, **Childs and Vanhoozer** propose **canonical and theodramatic integration**.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **progressive revelation**, **canon and rule of faith**, **inspiration and inerrancy**, **typology and sensus plenior**, **analogia fidei and ecclesial confession**.
- Major texts or works: Luke 24 and Hebrews 1 on **Christological fulfillment**, 2 Timothy 3 on **theopneustos**, Gabler 1787 address, **Wellhausen documentary hypothesis** 1878, **Dei Verbum** 1965, **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** 1978.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Old Princeton realism**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist organic inspiration**, **Westminster redemptive-historical preaching**, **Roman Catholic ressourcement**, **Lutheran law-gospel hermeneutic**, **modern historical-critical** and **analytic theology**.
- Legacy cue or continuing debate: Ongoing tension between **theological interpretation of Scripture** and **historical criticism**, disputes over **Christotelic readings**, **inerrancy**, and **canonical synthesis**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Biblical Theology and Systematic Theology are not rivals but distinct sciences ordered to the one Word of God, tethered to **revelation and canon** and located within **prolegomena and bibliology**. By Biblical Theology we mean the descriptive and historical study of the Bible’s own theological discourse as it unfolds through the covenants, epochs, and literary corpora, that is, **biblical theology as diachronic** attention to progressive revelation. By Systematic Theology we mean the constructive and normative exposition of the whole counsel of God, ordered by topics and the **rule of faith**, to confess the truth for the church’s belief, life, and worship, that is, **systematic theology as dogmatic synthesis**.

These two sciences differ by formal object rather than by ultimate norm. The formal object of Biblical Theology is the canonical text in its own horizons of author, audience, genre, and redemptive stage, emphasizing **progressive revelation** and intra-canonical intertextuality. The formal object of Systematic Theology is the same canon read as a unified whole under **canonical unity** and ecclesial confession, aiming at truthful doctrinal judgments about God and all things in relation to God. The material norm for both remains Holy Scripture as **theopneustos Scripture**, received in the church, under the Spirit’s guidance. The question is how description yields norm and how norm respects description, a tension that must be disciplined by **analogia fidei** and by a chastened historical awareness.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The Scriptures commend both enterprises. Jesus in Luke 24 speaks of Moses, Prophets, and Psalms that testify to him, and he opens the disciples’ minds to understand, which underwrites **Christological fulfillment** and the coherence of **canonical unity**. Hebrews 1 affirms that God spoke of old in many portions and ways, then in these last days in his Son, which justifies **progressive revelation** and a redemptive-historical horizon. Matthew’s fulfillment formulae and Paul’s Adam–Christ typology in Romans 5 model **typology and figural reading** that Biblical Theology traces across covenants.

At the same time, the New Testament exhibits early dogmatic condensation. First Corinthians 15 preserves a received **creedal summary** of gospel events and meanings. Second Timothy 1 speaks of a pattern of sound words, and Acts 15 narrates a conciliar judgment that integrates text, tradition, and mission into **dogmatic synthesis**. Second Timothy 3 declares all Scripture God-breathed and profitable for teaching, reproof, correction, and training in righteousness, indicating that **theopneustos Scripture** is given not only for historical description but for doctrinal formation. The apostolic habit of reading Scripture with Scripture models **analogia fidei**, while the practice of catechesis and confession displays **rule of faith** dynamics already in the New Testament churches.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
In the patristic period, Irenaeus around 180 articulates the **economy of salvation** and the recapitulation of Adam in Christ as a single narrative guided by the **rule of faith**, displaying early forms of canonical and redemptive reading. Origen develops spiritual senses within a disciplined **Christological hermeneutic**, while Augustine in 397 to 430 unites **divine illumination** with a robust account of literal and figural senses, stabilizing the unity of Scripture through **Trinitarian doctrine**. The medievals, especially Thomas Aquinas around 1265 to 1274, place Scripture within **sacra doctrina**, where theological science proceeds from revelation as its principle and orders truths according to **divine simplicity** and ends. These moves converge toward Systematic Theology’s architectonic calling while preserving **canonical coherence**.

The Reformation re-centers exegesis. Luther in 1517 to 1546 posits a **law-gospel hermeneutic** and insists on the clarity and sufficiency of Scripture. Calvin in 1536 to 1564 integrates careful exegesis with the **Institutes’ dogmatic synthesis**, arguing for **Scripture’s self-authentication** and the Spirit’s internal testimony. The era of Reformed orthodoxy in 1560 to 1700 codifies doctrine in confessions such as the **Westminster Standards** in 1646 to 1648 while producing massive commentaries, signaling an integrated model where **exegesis and dogma** mutually inform.

The modern turning point arrives with J. P. Gabler’s 1787 address distinguishing **biblical theology as historical description** from **dogmatic theology as normative system**. Nineteenth-century historicism and the **Wellhausen documentary hypothesis** in 1878 press descriptive autonomy, while liberal theology from Schleiermacher in 1821 to 1822 to Ritschl in 1874 aligns theology with modern consciousness and value judgments. The twentieth century witnesses a **biblical theology movement**, with Eichrodt in 1933 and von Rad in 1957 centering Old Testament themes and tradition history, and Cullmann in 1946 emphasizing *heilsgeschichte* and the **Christ event as eschatological center**. James Barr in 1961 criticizes semantic fallacies and naive unity claims, sharpening the need for **methodological rigor**.

Evangelicals respond with **Old Princeton realism** and later with canonical and redemptive-historical strategies. Warfield in the 1880s to 1915 articulates **verbal-plenary inspiration** and inerrancy as corollaries of **divine truthfulness**. Geerhardus Vos’s Biblical Theology in 1948 sets Reformed biblical theology on a covenantal axis, marrying **progressive revelation** to **covenant theology**. Brevard Childs in 1970 to 1992 advances a **canonical approach**, and postliberal theologians such as Hans Frei in 1974 and George Lindbeck in 1984 reinstate **the primacy of scriptural narrative** in shaping doctrine. Dei Verbum in 1965 integrates **Scripture and Tradition** within Roman Catholic teaching, and the Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy in 1978 articulates an evangelical stance on **inerrancy and hermeneutics**. The field since 2000 sees renewed calls for **theological interpretation of Scripture**, represented by Kevin Vanhoozer’s The Drama of Doctrine in 2005, which frames doctrine as **theodramatic direction** for canonical performance.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Old Princeton from 1812 to 1929 presents a disciplined integration of **exegesis and system**. Charles Hodge’s Systematic Theology in 1872 to 1873 and B. B. Warfield’s essays on inspiration argue that **inerrancy as corollary of inspiration** follows from **divine veracity** and the nature of Scripture as product of the **Spirit’s concursive agency**. Exegesis is philological and historical, yet ordered toward **dogmatic synthesis** under the **analogia fidei**. The aim is truth to reality, not merely coherence, hence **Old Princeton realism** frames method and doctrine.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism injects a robust doctrine of **organic inspiration** and a cultural horizon. Abraham Kuyper’s Stone Lectures in 1898 and Herman Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics in 1895 to 1901 contend that revelation is historical, personal, and covenantal, that **Scripture’s unity is organic**, and that theology must honor **creation-redemption consummation**. Bavinck insists that biblical theology elucidates the progress of revelation, while systematic theology constructs the organism of truth, both ordered by **Trinitarian economy and immanent life**.

The Westminster tradition advances **redemptive-historical preaching** and covenantal structure. Geerhardus Vos, though at Princeton, seeds this trajectory with **progressive revelation** and the epochs of **covenant of grace**. John Murray emphasizes union with Christ as a **soteriological center**, Van Til presses **presuppositional critique** of autonomous method, and Meredith Kline develops **covenant and typology** with ancient Near Eastern treaty insights in 1968. Edmund Clowney in 1961 and O. Palmer Robertson in 1980 equip preaching and theology to read the canon as **Christ-centered and covenantal**, exemplifying Reformed integration of **biblical theology and systematics**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
Roman Catholic theology orders Scripture within **Scripture, Tradition, and Magisterium** as one sacred deposit. Dei Verbum in 1965 affirms **inspiration and inerrancy** in matters of salvation, commends **historical-critical method with theological controls**, and underscores the unity of both Testaments in **Christological fulfillment**. Ressourcement retrieves patristic figural reading in Henri de Lubac’s work, while the Pontifical Biblical Commission in 1993 defends **plural methods under ecclesial oversight**, yielding a model where Biblical Theology is descriptive yet normed by the **sensus fidelium and dogmatic development**.

Lutheran theology centers the **law-gospel hermeneutic** and the distinction between **theologia crucis and theologia gloriae**. From Luther to Gerhard Ebeling in 1963, the stress falls on the Word as event that delivers Christ’s promise, so biblical theology traces the promise’s history while systematic theology guards **justification by faith** as the hermeneutical criterion. This scheme prioritizes **Christocentric proclamation** and resists any synthesis that dilutes the **article by which the church stands**.

Modern Protestant critical approaches display a spectrum. Schleiermacher recasts doctrine as accounts of pious experience, which downplays **canonical authority** in favor of religious consciousness. Ritschl shifts to **value judgments** and the moral kingdom. Bultmann in 1941 proposes **demythologizing**, reading kerygma through existential categories that relativize **salvation history**. Pannenberg in 1968 contends for **revelation as history**, inviting a renewed **heilsgeschichte** that is publicly verifiable, a move that energizes some Biblical Theology but risks subsuming **dogma under historiography**.

Postliberal and canonical voices reopen the canonical form. Hans Frei in 1974 argues that scriptural narratives render the world, thus doctrine is **intratextual realism** rather than external correlation. Brevard Childs governs exegesis by **canonical shape and ecclesial use**, and Kevin Vanhoozer’s **theodrama and canonical-linguistic** proposal in 2005 frames doctrine as authoritative direction for performing Scripture, a constructive integration of **biblical theology and dogmatics**.

Analytic theology approaches unify **conceptual analysis** with **exegetical constraints**. Figures such as Oliver Crisp and Eleonore Stump work with metaphysical clarity under biblical control, seeking **doctrinal precision** that honors **canonical teaching**. This trajectory resists the false choice between descriptive history and constructive truth by submitting arguments to **Scripture’s final authority** while deploying contemporary tools for coherence and clarity.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
One debate asks whether Biblical Theology must remain purely descriptive. Some argue with Gabler that normativity belongs only to **systematic theology as dogmatic synthesis**, while others insist that the canon’s shape and Christ’s authority require **theological interpretation of Scripture** to be unapologetically normative. The Childs and Vanhoozer line contends that the final form functions as **canonical rule**, so description without confession becomes **methodological atheism**.

Another dispute concerns **inerrancy and hermeneutics**. Critics like Peter Enns in 2005 claim ancient context requires a revision of **evangelical inerrancy**, while the Chicago Statement in 1978 maintains **truthfulness without error** rightly defined. Related are clashes over **Christotelic readings**, New Perspective on Paul from 1977 to 1997, and the role of **Second Temple intertextuality**. Dispensational, progressive covenantal, and classic covenantal schemes offer rival **canonical frameworks** for biblical theology, each with dogmatic entailments. The best path disciplines both sciences with **canon, creed, and Christ**, allowing textual description to challenge system and dogmatic synthesis to gather the whole without erasing the parts.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The relation of the two sciences rests in the doctrine of God and revelation. **Rahners Rule** that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity and vice versa alerts us that **economy and theology proper** are inseparable yet distinct, so **progressive revelation** is ordered to the eternal truth of **divine simplicity and triune life**. The Son reveals the Father in the Spirit, and this economy structures both **biblical theology’s historical sequence** and **systematic theology’s dogmatic order**. The doctrine of **divine accommodation** guards against historicism, while **illumination of the Spirit** guards against rationalism.

Anthropology and hamartiology remind us of the **noetic effects of sin**, which require ecclesial checks such as **rule of faith and confessionalism**. Ecclesiology and sacramentology locate reading within the body that hears Scripture in worship, where **creeds and catechisms** summarize system while lectionary and lectio divina trace **canonical patterns**. Eschatology’s already and not yet frames **biblical theology’s epochal tensions** and **systematics’ teleology**, while Christology anchors **typology and fulfillment**, and soteriology orders **union with Christ** as doctrinal center gathered from many texts.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
For preaching, **redemptive-historical exposition** rescues sermons from atomistic proof texts and tells the one story that culminates in Christ, while **dogmatic catechesis** supplies the grammar by which believers confess that story. The pastor-teacher alternates between series that trace **covenant and kingdom** and doctrinal instruction that clarifies **Trinity, Christology, and justification**, trusting the Spirit to unite **exegesis and confession**.

For worship and formation, the church orders the year and its liturgy to **creation, cross, and new creation**, trains members in **creed and canon**, and disciplines ministries to read the Bible as **one coherent witness**. The result is a people who resist **biblicism without dogma** and **dogma without Bible**, embodying the gospel through **theodramatic performance** in word and sacrament.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Biblical Theology follows the path of God’s speech through history and Systematic Theology sets that speech into the church’s confession, and only together under **canon, Christ, and creed** do they yield wisdom.
