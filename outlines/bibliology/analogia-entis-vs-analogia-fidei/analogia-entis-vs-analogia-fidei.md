### TITLE
Between Being and Revelation: Negotiating the Analogia entis and the Analogia fidei

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late medieval to twentieth century, at the seam of prolegomena and Bibliology, touching Theology Proper and theological epistemology.
- How do creatures speak truly of God without collapsing transcendence or silencing revelation.
- Principal figures: **Thomas Aquinas** as architect of analogical predication; **John Duns Scotus** as univocity foil; **Erich Przywara** as twentieth-century retrieval; **Karl Barth** as **analogia fidei** reformulator; **Old Princeton and Van Til** as Reformed trajectories.
- Key concepts: **Creator-creature distinction**, **natural theology and general revelation**, **rule of faith and Scripture interprets Scripture**, **noetic effects of sin**, **grace and nature**.
- Major texts and events: Fourth Lateran Council 1215; **Summa theologiae** 1265–1274; **De nominum analogia** 1498; **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647; **Vatican I Dei Filius** 1870; Przywara **Analogia Entis** 1932; Barth **Church Dogmatics I.1** 1932 and Brunner exchange 1934.
- Reformed and alternatives: **Old Princeton** affirms bounded **natural theology**; **Dutch Neo-Calvinism** stresses **antithesis and common grace**; Roman Catholic retrieval by **Przywara and Rahner**; Barthian **Christological concentration**.
- Legacy: Ongoing dispute over **theological language**, **classical theism** retrieval, and whether **analogia entis** aids or undermines **Christocentric revelation**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Section Heading**
At stake in the contrast between *analogia entis* and *analogia fidei* is the grammar of God-talk under the **Creator-creature distinction**. The *analogia entis* claims a proportioned likeness between creaturely perfections and the divine perfections that warrants true though non-univocal naming of God, safeguarding **divine transcendence** through proportioned similarity. The *analogia fidei* claims that the creaturely likeness and the capacity to name God truly arise from the self-attesting act of **special revelation in Christ** and in Scripture, so that analogy is keyed to faith, not to metaphysics. Both seek to avoid univocity and equivocity, but they differ on whether nature already bears an ontological analogy to God or whether any analogy is generated by **revelatory grace**.

Dogmatically, this question belongs to **Bibliology and prolegomena** as it treats the principium cognoscendi, yet it reaches into **Theology Proper** because predication about God implicates **divine simplicity** and the naming of attributes. It shapes **Anthropology** in views of the **imago Dei** and the **noetic effects of sin**, and it informs **Apologetics and natural theology** by delimiting the reach of reason. The contrast is not a simple binary. The Reformed have long used an exegetical **analogy of faith** as a hermeneutical rule, while Roman Catholic thinkers insist that the *analogia entis* is chastened by the Fourth Lateran axiom of greater dissimilarity. The present essay tracks these lines without collapsing **grace and nature**.

#### 2.**Section Heading**
Scripture gives both promise and warning regarding creaturely knowledge of God. Romans 1.19–20 says that what is knowable of God is manifest in creation, using the phrase *to gnōston tou theou* to describe a general revelation that renders humans without excuse. Psalm 19 and Wisdom 13 amplify that the heavens declare glory and that from the goodness of created things one may infer the Maker. This is the exegetical cradle for **natural theology** and for a restrained **analogia entis** that discerns divine power and divinity from effects. Yet Isaiah 40 asks To whom will you liken me, intensifying **divine incomparability** and warning against idolatrous projection. Both disclosure and dissimilarity are woven together in the canonical text.

The New Testament sharpens the revelatory center. John 1.18 teaches that no one has seen God, but the only-begotten, who is at the Father’s bosom, has made him known. Matthew 11.27 declares that no one knows the Father except the Son and those to whom the Son wills to reveal him. First Corinthians 2 insists that the things of God are spiritually discerned, the *pneumatikos* judges all things. These texts energize **analogia fidei** as dependence on the **incarnate Word** and the **illumination of the Spirit**, challenging any confidence that unaided reason can render more than a dim apprehension. The Cross as **theologia crucis** overturns the wisdom of the world, orienting knowledge to the event of self-giving love.

Romans 12.6 contains the phrase *kata analogian tes pisteos*. Historically in the Reformation this generated the hermeneutical **analogy of faith** which means Scripture interprets Scripture under the **rule of faith**. While exegetically the phrase likely means in proportion to the faith allotted, dogmatic use made it a principle that the clearer passages govern the obscure. Barth repurposed this into **analogia fidei as epistemic dependence**, insisting that our only analogy arises as God makes us participants in the knowledge of the Son by the Spirit. The biblical witness thus both permits a qualified **general revelation** and binds all saving knowledge to **Christological revelation**.

#### 3.**Section Heading**
Patristic and medieval trajectories framed the options. **Augustine** taught that God is more interior than our inmost being, yet utterly beyond, developing **illumination** and the ascent of mind in De Trinitate around 400–420, combining **apophatic reserve** with cataphatic speech. **Pseudo-Dionysius** in the late fifth to early sixth century elaborated **negative theology**, insisting every name of God is exceeded by God. The Fourth Lateran Council in 1215 capped this with the axiom that between Creator and creature no similitude can be expressed without implying a greater dissimilitude, the fountainhead of **apophatic humility** within a robust metaphysics of participation.

**Thomas Aquinas** in the 1260s articulated analogical predication precisely. In Summa theologiae I q.13 he rejected both univocity and pure equivocity, positing **analogy of attribution and proportionality** so that perfections like goodness are said of God foremost and of creatures by participation. Being is neither univocal nor equivocal but analogical, preserving **divine aseity** and **divine simplicity** while enabling true knowledge. **Cajetan** in 1498 systematized these levels in De nominum analogia. Against this, **John Duns Scotus** in the early 1300s argued for a **univocity of being** as a minimal concept to allow metaphysical demonstration, fearing that pure analogy collapses into equivocity. The line between analogy and univocity became decisive for later **natural theology** and for the intelligibility of **theology as science**.

Reformational developments reframed the debate. **Martin Luther** attacked the ladder of ascent as a theology of glory, insisting in 1518 that God is known under the opposite in the Cross as **theologia crucis**. **John Calvin** in 1559 taught that God accommodated himself through **Scripture and the Spirit**, that nature declares God but that sin distorts our sight. The **Junius archetypal-ectypal distinction** in 1594 affirmed that God alone has archetypal theology and we have ectypal, creaturely knowledge by revelation. Seventeenth-century Reformed scholastics like **Turretin** allowed a bounded **natural theology** resting on **general revelation**, while the **Westminster Confession of Faith** in 1646–1647 affirmed the light of nature and the clearer requirement of Scripture for knowledge of salvation. This is neither a Thomist metaphysics nor a Barthian rejection, but a careful **Creator-creature distinction**.

Modernity split the options. **Kant** in 1781 restricted knowledge to phenomena, constraining **natural theology** and pushing theologians to moral or experiential bases. **Vatican I** in 1870 reaffirmed that God can be known with certainty from created things. In 1932 **Erich Przywara** published Analogia Entis, repristinating a dynamic tension of essence and existence in creatures as a rhythm of being-in-and-beyond, grounding **analogia entis** in a metaphysics of participation and in the Lateran greater dissimilarity. **Karl Barth** in 1932 and 1940 rejected this as the invention of the antichrist, contending in **Church Dogmatics I.1** that revelation alone establishes a **correspondence by faith**, and in II.1 he opposed **natural theology** as compromising **Christological concentration**. The Brunner exchange in 1934, with Barth’s Nein, cemented the twentieth-century polarity.

#### 4.**Section Heading**
Within the Reformed family, three strands can be distinguished. **Old Princeton** in the nineteenth century, with **Hodge and Warfield**, affirmed a modest **natural theology** as a preamble to faith and employed **analogy of faith** as hermeneutical rule. Warfield viewed **inspiration** and **inerrancy** as the authoritative principium, but he did not deny that Romans 1 warrants inferential knowledge of God’s power and divinity. This trajectory preserved **classical theism** while allowing evidences without surrendering to rationalism.

The **Dutch Neo-Calvinists** like **Abraham Kuyper** and **Herman Bavinck** stressed the **antithesis** as the ethical and cognitive rupture of sin, while also articulating **common grace** and the organic character of revelation. Bavinck in Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901 insisted that all our knowledge of God is analogical, because God is qualitatively other, yet he refused a metaphysic that would render grace a mere crown on nature. He kept the **Creator-creature distinction** rigorous and folded **natural knowledge** into the economy of **revelation and regeneration**.

The **Westminster and Van Til** stream intensified this. The Westminster Standards affirmed the light of nature and Scripture’s necessity for salvation. **Cornelius Van Til** recast analogy as the only mode of human knowledge of God. By **analogical reasoning** he meant that our thoughts must be reinterpretations of God’s self-knowledge under covenantal authority, rejecting **univocity** while also rejecting a Thomist **analogia entis** that grants autonomous metaphysics. He underscored **the noetic effects of sin** and the need for **the Spirit’s illumination**, claiming that apologetics is transcendental and revelational rather than evidentialist. This is a Reformed **analogia fidei** as principled dependence on revelation.

#### 5.**Section Heading**
Roman Catholic accounts center on **Aquinas, Cajetan, and Przywara**. Aquinas grounds **analogical predication** in participation and **divine simplicity**, claiming that names apply by priority to God as source. Vatican I reaffirms the knowability of God from effects. **Przywara** aligns the **analogia entis** with an ever greater dissimilarity that forbids domestication. Later Catholic thinkers like **Rahner** adapted this through the **supernatural existential**, suggesting that grace always already orients human existence to God, which reframes the relation of **nature and grace** without erasing transcendence. **Balthasar** defended the analogy through **Christological form**, placing beauty and glory within a participatory metaphysics.

Lutheran accounts emphasize **theologia crucis** and the primacy of **the Word and Sacrament**. While the orthodox Lutheran scholastics permitted a natural knowledge of God’s existence, they cautioned that saving knowledge is purely **Gospel revelation**. The **Barmen Declaration** in 1934, influenced by Barth, denied any other sources of revelation alongside Christ. Modern Protestant theology after **Schleiermacher** shifted toward **religious experience**, while **Barth and Torrance** insisted on **Christological and Triune revelation**. Analytic theology has revisited analogy using tools from philosophy of language. **Eleonore Stump** and **David Burrell** defend a Thomist **analogy of names**, while **Plantinga** and **Swinburne** reopen **natural theology** and **reformed epistemology**, without necessarily endorsing a metaphysical **analogia entis**.

#### 6.**Section Heading**
Objections cut both ways. Critics of the **analogia entis** worry that any pre-revelatory similarity compromises **sola gratia** and **Christological concentration**, opening the door to autonomous reason and to **ontotheology** as Heidegger charged. They fear conceptual idolatry that projects creaturely perfections upward. Defenders reply that the **Fourth Lateran greater dissimilarity** and **divine simplicity** prevent domestication, and that **Romans 1** commits the church to a real, if limited, **general revelation**. They add that participatory metaphysics itself witnesses to **God’s transcendence**.

Critics of **analogia fidei** in the Barthian register worry about **fideism** and an inability to engage **apologetics** or interreligious discourse. They point to biblical acknowledgments of general revelation and to the catholicity of the church’s speech about God outside explicit salvific contexts. Barthians respond that **revelation’s primacy** does not deny general revelation but judges it in Christ, and that the deepest analogy is **union with Christ** in the Spirit, not an ontological ladder. The debate continues over whether Barth can sustain any **natural theology** and whether Thomists can fully honor **the Cross’s scandal**.

#### 7.**Section Heading**
The analogies radiate into all loci. In **Trinity**, **Rahners Rule** that the economic Trinity is the immanent Trinity presses us to ask how analogical naming tracks the **ad extra** missions and the **ad intra** processions. **Divine simplicity** magnifies the need for analogical predication, since God is not composed of perfections that we grasp univocally. In **Christology**, the hypostatic union secures the concrete **image of the invisible God**, furnishing the most determinate **analogia fidei** as we read God’s face in Jesus.

In **Anthropology**, the **imago Dei** grounds a creaturely capacity for God, even if wounded, serving as the interior site for **general revelation** and for **illumination**. In **Soteriology**, the ordering of **grace and nature** makes the difference between elevating nature by participation or recreating nature ex nihilo by grace. **Ecclesiology and Bibliology** join in the **analogy of faith** as a hermeneutical habit: Scripture interprets Scripture, the **rule of faith** governs exegesis, and the Spirit binds readers to Christ. **Eschatology** reminds us that beatific vision consummates all analogy, transfiguring faith into sight while forever preserving **Creator-creature distinction**.

#### 8.**Section Heading**
Pastorally, this debate teaches humility and courage. The **analogia entis** inculcates reverence before **divine incomprehensibility** and encourages gratitude for creation’s sacramental hints, aiding **natural law** reasoning in public life. The **analogia fidei** trains congregations to read Scripture with **Christological focus**, to depend on **the Spirit’s illumination**, and to confess Christ as Lord of all knowing. Together they form a wise posture of confession, in which preaching avoids both rationalist overreach and anti-intellectual retreat, and worship orders creaturely language to the praise of **the triune God**.

Practically, catechesis can teach analogical language for attributes such as **divine goodness and wisdom**, showing both likeness and greater unlikeness. Apologetics can employ **general revelation** as contact point while keeping the Cross central. Liturgy can voice the **greater dissimilarity** in confession and the **nearness of revelation** in proclamation and sacrament, so that the church learns to speak truly without presumption.

#### 9.**Section Heading**
Rightly ordered, the church may receive from the *analogia entis* the grammar of creaturely likeness and from the *analogia fidei* the rule that only God’s self-revelation authorizes and heals our speech about him.
