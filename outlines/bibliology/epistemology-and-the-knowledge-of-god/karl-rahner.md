# Karl Rahner (1904–1984)

Karl Rahner’s approach to **epistemology and the knowledge of God** centers on the transcendental structure of human subjectivity, arguing that all human knowing is implicitly oriented toward the divine. He maintains that **God’s self-communication** is mediated through human experience, and that **revelation** is both historical and existential, grounded in the openness of the human spirit to the **supernatural existential**. Rahner’s thought bridges **Scholasticism** and **modern philosophy**, emphasizing the dynamic relationship between finite knowledge and the mystery of God.

- "Man is the being who is absolutely oriented toward God." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

## Key Works

- Foundations of Christian Faith (Grundkurs des Glaubens)
- Hearer of the Word
- Spirit in the World
- Theological Investigations (multiple volumes)
- Revelation and Tradition

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Transcendental Thomism**
   - Human knowledge is structured by a transcendental openness to **Being**.
   - Adapts **Aquinas’s epistemology** through a Kantian lens, focusing on the conditions for the possibility of knowing God.
   - The subject is always already oriented toward the infinite, making knowledge of God possible but never exhaustive.
   - The act of knowing is a dynamic event, not a static possession.
   - "The question about God is present in every question about reality." (Hearer of the Word)

2. **Supernatural Existential**
   - Every human is endowed with a **supernatural existential**, a permanent orientation toward God’s self-communication.
   - This existential is not a separate grace but the condition for the possibility of receiving revelation.
   - It grounds the universality of the offer of salvation and the possibility of implicit faith.
   - Human openness to God is both natural and graced, blurring strict nature/grace distinctions.
   - "Grace is everywhere, but it is not everywhere recognized." (Theological Investigations, vol. 1)

3. **God as Mystery**
   - God is the **incomprehensible mystery** at the horizon of all human knowing.
   - Knowledge of God is always analogical, never univocal or exhaustive.
   - The more one knows God, the more one realizes God’s radical otherness.
   - Theology must preserve the apophatic dimension of faith.
   - "God is not what is most luminous, but what is most mysterious." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

4. **Revelation as Self-Communication**
   - **Revelation** is God’s self-communication in history, culminating in Jesus Christ.
   - Human beings are the “hearers of the word,” capable of receiving and responding to revelation.
   - Revelation is both event and encounter, not merely propositional truth.
   - The Church mediates revelation, but God’s self-communication is not limited to explicit Christian proclamation.
   - "Revelation is the self-communication of God to man in history." (Foundations of Christian Faith)

5. **Faith and Knowledge**
   - **Faith** is a free, personal response to God’s self-disclosure, rooted in the structure of human knowing.
   - Faith and reason are not opposed but are mutually implicative in the search for truth.
   - The act of faith is always mediated by experience, tradition, and community.
   - Doubt and questioning are integral to authentic faith, given the mystery of God.
   - "Faith is the acceptance of the incomprehensible mystery of God’s self-communication." (Theological Investigations, vol. 5)

## Legacy and Impact

Rahner’s transcendental approach to **the knowledge of God** profoundly shaped post-Vatican II Catholic theology, influencing discussions on **revelation**, **grace**, and religious pluralism. His emphasis on the **supernatural existential** and the universality of grace opened new avenues for dialogue with modern philosophy and non-Christian religions. Rahner’s work remains foundational for contemporary debates on the relationship between faith, reason, and experience, ensuring his continued relevance in both academic and ecclesial contexts.
