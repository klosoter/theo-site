# Michael Horton (1964– )

Michael Horton emphasizes the **Holy Spirit’s** essential role in both the **objective revelation** of God in Scripture and the **subjective illumination** of believers’ minds and hearts. He maintains that while **revelation** is completed in Christ and inscripturated in the Bible, the **Spirit** continues to work by making this revelation effective, enabling true **knowledge of God** and fostering faith within the covenant community.

- "The Spirit is not the content of revelation but the agent who brings us into living contact with the Word." (The Christian Faith, p. 144)

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **Revelation is Christocentric and Completed**
   - God’s definitive self-disclosure is in **Jesus Christ**, witnessed by the **Scriptures**.
   - The **Holy Spirit** does not add new revelation but points to and applies what is revealed in Christ.
   - The canon is closed; the Spirit’s work is not to supplement but to illuminate.
   - The church’s authority is ministerial, not magisterial, under the Spirit’s guidance through the Word.
   - "The Spirit does not reveal new doctrines but leads us deeper into the truth once for all delivered to the saints." (The Christian Faith, p. 144)

2. **Illumination is Distinct from Inspiration**
   - **Inspiration** refers to the Spirit’s work in producing Scripture; **illumination** is the Spirit’s ongoing work in believers.
   - Illumination enables understanding, conviction, and acceptance of divine truth.
   - The Spirit’s illumination is necessary due to human sin and spiritual blindness.
   - Illumination does not grant new revelation but opens hearts to the meaning of the text.
   - "Illumination is the Spirit’s work of overcoming our resistance to the Word, not adding to it." (The Christian Faith, p. 146)

3. **The Spirit and the Means of Grace**
   - The Spirit works through **ordinary means**: Word, sacraments, and prayer.
   - The Spirit’s presence is covenantal, not mystical or independent of the means.
   - The Spirit unites believers to Christ and applies the benefits of redemption.
   - The church is the context in which the Spirit’s illuminating work is ordinarily experienced.
   - "The Spirit’s ministry is inseparable from the ministry of the Word and sacraments." (Rediscovering the Holy Spirit, p. 184)

4. **Objective and Subjective Dimensions of Revelation**
   - Revelation is objectively given in Scripture; subjectively received by the Spirit’s work.
   - The Spirit bridges the gap between the text and the believer’s heart.
   - Assurance and faith arise from the Spirit’s testimony, not from subjective experience alone.
   - The Spirit’s witness is always tethered to the Word, not private revelation.
   - "The Spirit’s inward testimony is not a new revelation but the confirmation of the Word’s truth." (The Christian Faith, p. 147)

5. **Trinitarian Economy of Revelation**
   - The Father reveals, the Son is the content, and the Spirit is the agent of revelation.
   - The Spirit’s role is to glorify Christ and bring believers into communion with God.
   - The unity of the **Trinity** ensures that the Spirit’s work never contradicts the Word.
   - The Spirit’s illumination is part of the broader economy of redemption and sanctification.
   - "The Spirit’s work is always to bring us to Christ, not to himself." (Rediscovering the Holy Spirit, p. 78)

## Legacy and Impact

Michael Horton has significantly shaped contemporary **Reformed theology** by clarifying the distinct yet inseparable roles of the **Holy Spirit** in revelation and illumination. His emphasis on the Spirit’s work through the **means of grace** and within the covenant community has reinforced a robust ecclesiology and a high view of Scripture. Horton’s writings continue to influence pastors, theologians, and laypeople in understanding the Spirit’s ministry as both objective and experiential, always centered on Christ and the gospel.
