# Michael S. Horton (1964– )

Michael S. Horton distinguishes **general revelation** as God's self-disclosure in creation and providence, accessible to all, and **special revelation** as God's redemptive communication through Scripture and Christ. He emphasizes the insufficiency of general revelation for salvation, highlighting the necessity of **Scripture** as the authoritative and clear means by which God reveals the gospel. Horton's approach is deeply rooted in **Reformed confessional theology**, stressing the unity and distinction between these two modes of revelation.

- "General revelation is sufficient to render all people without excuse, but only special revelation brings the knowledge of God as Redeemer." ("The Christian Faith")

## Key Theological Commitments

1. **General Revelation is Universal but Insufficient**
    - General revelation is available to all through creation and conscience.
    - It reveals God's existence, power, and divine attributes.
    - It leaves humanity without excuse for unbelief.
    - It cannot communicate the knowledge necessary for salvation.
    - "General revelation is not salvific; it is sufficient only to condemn, not to redeem." (The Christian Faith)

2. **Special Revelation is Redemptive and Christocentric**
    - Special revelation centers on God's redemptive acts and words.
    - It culminates in the person and work of Jesus Christ.
    - Scripture is the normative form of special revelation today.
    - Special revelation is necessary for a saving knowledge of God.
    - "Special revelation is the unfolding drama of redemption, climaxing in Christ and inscripturated in the Bible." (Pilgrim Theology)

3. **Scripture as the Final Authority**
    - Scripture is the inspired, inerrant, and sufficient Word of God.
    - It is the only infallible rule for faith and practice.
    - The clarity and authority of Scripture are foundational for theology.
    - All claims of revelation must be tested by Scripture.
    - "The canon of Scripture is the norming norm, the final court of appeal in all matters of faith and life." (The Christian Faith)

4. **Covenantal Structure of Revelation**
    - God's revelation unfolds through covenants in redemptive history.
    - Both general and special revelation are situated within God's covenantal dealings.
    - The covenant of grace is revealed progressively through special revelation.
    - The unity of Scripture is grounded in its covenantal framework.
    - "The drama of redemption is covenantal from beginning to end, shaping the form and content of revelation." (Covenant and Eschatology)

5. **Rejection of Natural Theology as a Path to Salvation**
    - Horton affirms natural theology's limited role in affirming God's existence.
    - He rejects the idea that natural theology can lead to salvific knowledge.
    - The effects of sin distort human perception of general revelation.
    - True knowledge of God as Redeemer comes only through special revelation.
    - "Natural theology cannot bridge the gap between the knowledge of God as Creator and the knowledge of God as Redeemer." (The Christian Faith)

## Legacy and Impact

Horton’s articulation of **general and special revelation** has reinforced classic Reformed distinctions while engaging contemporary challenges to revelation and authority. His work has shaped evangelical and Reformed understandings of **Scripture’s sufficiency** and the limits of natural theology. By emphasizing the covenantal and Christ-centered nature of revelation, Horton has contributed to renewed interest in confessional theology and the redemptive-historical reading of the Bible. His influence is evident in both academic and church contexts, especially among those seeking to uphold the authority of Scripture in a pluralistic age.
