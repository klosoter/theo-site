### TITLE
Principia and the Scientific Status of Theology: From Self-Attesting Scripture to Spirit-Given Understanding

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and dogmatic location: **Reformed prolegomena and Bibliology** from 1550 to the present, with medieval roots in **sacred doctrine as science**.
- Core question: What are the **principia of theology** and in what sense is **theology a science** ordered to God and revelation.
- Principal figures or schools: **Aquinas on sacred doctrine**, **Junius and Turretin on principia**, **Old Princeton induction**, **Kuyper and Bavinck on encyclopedia**, **Barth on actualistic revelation**, **Plantinga on Reformed epistemology**.
- Key concepts or loci connections: **principium essendi**, **Scripture as principium cognoscendi**, **Spirit as internal principle**, links to **Trinity, Christology, Anthropology, Ecclesiology**.
- Major texts, events, or works with dates: **Summa Theologiae I q.1** circa 1265–1274, **Westminster Confession** 1646–1647, **Kuyper Encyclopedia of Sacred Theology** 1894, **Warfield Revelation and Inspiration** 1881, **Barth Church Dogmatics I–II** 1932–1942, **Vatican I Dei Filius** 1870, **Plantinga Warrant** 1993–2000.
- Reformed and alternative trajectories: **Old Princeton evidentialism**, **Dutch Neo-Calvinist antithesis**, with contrasts to **Roman Catholic analogia entis**, **Lutheran Scripture principle**, and **Modern experiential or dialectical methods**.
- Legacy cue: Enduring debate over **theology's scientific status**, the **normativity of Scripture**, and **Spirit-wrought knowledge** in a pluralist academy.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Theological method asks what grounds and norms the Christian science of God, and what counts as genuine theological knowledge. In classic Reformed prolegomena, three **principia of theology** are distinguished. God himself is the **principium essendi** of theology, the one whose being and truth make theology possible. Holy Scripture is the **principium cognoscendi externum**, the external cognitive principle that bears divine authority as the canonical form of special revelation. The Holy Spirit is the **principium cognoscendi internum**, the internal cognitive principle who illumines the mind to receive the Word with faith. The question of **theology as science** follows, asking whether theology is a habitus ordered by true first principles, and thus a disciplined way of knowing whose methods are fitting to its object.

Although lodged within **Bibliology and prolegomena**, the method touches every locus, since the form of knowing tracks the form of being. If God is triune and reveals himself in Christ and in Scripture, then the mode of theology is covenantal listening under **divine lordship**. If human reason is fallen, then the intellect is dependent upon **Spirit-given illumination**. The Reformed scholastics framed this in a grammar of *principia*, while retaining a thick account of **theology as scientia** that is subordinate to Gods own knowledge and communicatively ordered to the church.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
Scripture names its own origin and function in ways that furnish the **principles of theology**. All Scripture is God breathed, *theopneustos*, in 2 Timothy 3:16, so **Scripture as principium cognoscendi** carries the authority of the God who speaks. Second Peter 1:21 says men spoke from God as they were carried along by the Holy Spirit, which grounds the nexus of **Word and Spirit** for revelation. Psalm 19 and Romans 1:19–21 affirm general revelation, yet Romans 1 declares the problem of **noetic effects of sin**, so the **Spirit as internal principle** is requisite if the mind is to know God aright in Christ.

Jesus promises in John 16:13 that the Spirit of truth will guide the apostles into all the truth, which funds an apostolic norm for the **regula fidei** and canon. Luke 1:1–4 offers an orderly account, a biblical witness to **theology as ordered inquiry**. Proverbs 1:7 calls the fear of the Lord the beginning of knowledge, so the first axiom of this science is **the fear of the Lord** rather than methodological neutrality. Deuteronomy 29:29 divides secret things belonging to God from revealed things for us, stabilizing the domain and limits of **sacred doctrine**. Isaiah 8:20 directs us to the law and testimony, and Jude 3 speaks of the faith once delivered to the saints, together undergirding **Scripture’s sufficiency** as the public norm for the church.

Paul in 1 Corinthians 2:14 says the natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, which clarifies why theology is not merely an empirical collation of data but a grace-enabled participation in **the mind of Christ**. The ecclesial in Ephesians 4, unity in the faith, situates the method within the communion of saints, thus giving theology a public and communal character under **apostolic teaching**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
In the medieval synthesis, **Aquinas on sacred doctrine** frames Summa Theologiae I q.1 as a prolegomenon to theology as *scientia*, subalternated to the knowledge of God and the blessed. Sacred doctrine proceeds from principles known by the light of higher knowledge, that is, by **divine revelation**, and is taught by God to us through the prophets and apostles. The formal object of this science is God and all things as they relate to God, so **theology as science** is disciplined by God’s self disclosure rather than by autonomous reason.

The Reformation retains the scientific aspiration while reforming its principia. **Luthers Scripture principle** insists on the clarity and sufficiency of the Word, though Lutheran dogmatics later distinguished between the material and formal principles of theology. In the Reformed line, **Junius on true theology** in 1594 and **Polanus and Turretin on principia** elaborated the triad of **principium essendi**, **Scripture as principium cognoscendi**, and **Spirit as internal principle**, while affirming that the church’s theology is an ectypal participation in God’s archetypal knowledge. The **Westminster Confession 1646–1647** codifies Scripture’s necessity, sufficiency, and clarity, while giving the Spirit’s testimony preeminence as the ground of persuasion.

Early modernity pressed a scientific turn. **Old Princeton theology** in the nineteenth century framed theology as the inductive science of the facts of Scripture, with **Hodge on method** in 1872 and **Warfield on revelation and inspiration** in 1881. This emphasized objectivity and coherence, often trading on common sense realism. **Dutch Neo-Calvinism**, in contrast, with **Kuyper’s Encyclopedia of Sacred Theology 1894** and **Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics 1895–1901**, argued that all science stands on faith-commitments, so theology as science lives from its own **principium** in God’s self-revelation and the regenerating work of the Spirit. In the twentieth century, **Barth’s actualistic revelation** in Church Dogmatics I–II rewrote the map by centering revelation as God’s free act in Jesus Christ, denying any natural access to God and reframing **theology as hearing** rather than a system built from static principles.

Roman Catholic developments, especially **Vatican I Dei Filius 1870** and later **Fides et Ratio 1998**, held together **analogia entis** and the gratuity of revelation, maintaining that theology is a science founded upon the authority of God revealing and clarified by reason. Modern Protestant liberalism, shaped by **Schleiermacher’s 1830 dogmatics**, located theology in religious consciousness, shifting from **principia in revelation** to principles in experience. Late twentieth-century **analytic theology** returned to clarity and argument, exploring epistemic principles with **Plantinga’s Reformed epistemology** and **Alston on perception of God**, posing new questions for the **scientific status of theology** in the academy.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
Classic Reformed teaching confesses that God is the **principium essendi** of sacred doctrine. He is not an inferred hypothesis but the triune Lord who gives himself to be known through **covenant revelation**. Scripture is the **principium cognoscendi externum**, the publicly given canon that bears divine authority through inspiration and providential preservation. The Spirit is the **principium cognoscendi internum**, the one who regenerates, illumines, and seals, creating a cognitive correspondence between the believer and the Word. Together these **principia of theology** secure objectivity, normativity, and receptivity.

The **Westminster Confession** gathers these insights. Scripture is necessary and sufficient, the rule of faith and life, with its authority grounded wholly in God who is truth itself, not in the testimony of any man or church. Assurance of its infallible truth and divine authority comes from the inward work of the **Holy Spirit bearing witness** by and with the Word in our hearts. This is the doctrinal ground of **Spirit illumination** as a principle of knowing.

**Old Princeton** prized the publicity of faith’s reasons with **theology as inductive science**. Hodge argued that theology arranges the facts of Scripture to form a connected system. Warfield defended **verbal plenary inspiration**, insisting that Christian theology is grounded in an objectively given body of truth. This yielded **apologetic engagement** through evidences and historical arguments, while asserting that faith finally depends on the Spirit’s work.

**Dutch Neo-Calvinism** pressed the antithesis between belief and unbelief, challenging the ideal of neutral science. **Kuyper’s principium of palingenesis** and **Bavinck’s organic worldview** articulated that all knowing is from, through, and to God, and that the **self-authenticating Word** stands as the sole norm for theology. This tradition developed a rich **encyclopedia of theology**, acknowledging the unity and diversity of disciplines under the one **principium of revelation**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The **Roman Catholic** tradition draws a twofold source grammar in Scripture and tradition, both issuing from one divine wellspring, articulated at **Trent 1545–1563** and **Vatican I 1870**, with magisterial authority as the agent of authentic interpretation. The **analogia entis** provides metaphysical footing for predication about God, while **Aquinas on subalternation** frames theology as a science under the light of God’s knowledge. Contemporary Catholic theology integrates **historical-critical exegesis** with a thick account of ecclesial authority and sacramentality.

The **Lutheran** stream centers the **Scripture principle** and the clarity of the gospel, with **Chemnitz and Quenstedt** supplying an orthodox method that privileges the distinction between law and gospel. While affirming **theology as sacred doctrine**, the Lutheran habit tends to guard the primacy of proclamation and the christological center as the lodestar for method.

In **modern theology**, **Schleiermacher** recasts theology as the science that orders the church’s self-consciousness, making the essence of religion a feeling of absolute dependence. **Ritschl** moves toward moral value judgments. **Barth’s actualistic revelation** rejects both natural theology and the objectification of the Word into a static deposit, describing **revelation as event** in which the Word of God is identical with Jesus Christ. This subordinates **theology as science** to an obedient listening, and construes **analogia fidei** as the only path to knowledge of God.

**Analytic theology** revisits method via contemporary philosophy. **Plantinga’s Reformed epistemology** argues that belief in God can be properly basic due to the operation of the **sensus divinitatis** and the internal instigation of the Holy Spirit. **Alston on mystical perception** and **Swinburne on explanation** offer models for rationality and confirmation. These proposals challenge the older demand for evidence and reconfigure **the principles of justification** for theological belief, often aligning with **Reformed insights** about the internal testimony of the Spirit.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
A first debate concerns **foundationalism and method**. Critics argue that talk of **principia of theology** embeds a problematic foundationalism that cannot survive the dynamics of textual interpretation and communal practice. Defenders respond that the **self-attesting Word and Spirit** do not mirror secular foundationalism but rather articulate a covenantal epistemology rooted in **divine authority** and filial trust.

A second dispute involves **natural theology and analogical predication**. Reformed voices influenced by **Kuyper’s antithesis** question the use of **analogia entis**, while others retrieve a chastened natural theology grounded in **Romans 1** and **Acts 14 and 17** and regulated by **Scripture’s sufficiency**. The **Barthian critique** insists that fallen reason cannot generate true knowledge of God apart from revelation, prompting ongoing discussion of **general revelation** and the role of **creation’s testimony**.

A third area concerns **science and theology**. Is theology a science in the modern academic sense, often marked by empiricism and falsifiability, or in the classical sense of **scientia** ordered to first principles and final ends. Many argue for a retrieval of **Aquinas on sacred doctrine**, recast through **Bavinck’s organic motif**, as a way to honor theology’s integrity while engaging the broader university. Others press **interdisciplinary methods**, seeking **public reasons** without surrendering **Scripture’s normativity**.

Finally, the field confronts **global and postcolonial critiques**. The insistence on a Euro-Atlantic encyclopedia is challenged by churches in the Global South who foreground **Scripture’s missionary authority**, the **Spirit’s immediacy**, and **communal discernment**. Here the Reformed grammar of principia can be heard afresh as a liberating centering of **God’s Word and Spirit** against oppressive traditions, while remaining open to correction in practice and pedagogy.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
The **Trinity** furnishes the ontological pattern for method. The Father is the **principium essendi** without superiority of nature, the Son is the **incarnate revelation**, and the Spirit is the **illumining bond of knowledge**, so theology’s form is from the Father, through the Son, in the Spirit. **Christology** sets the concrete medium of revelation, for the climactic Word is **the hypostatic union**, and Scripture bears apostolic witness to him. **Pneumatology** specifies that saving knowledge is **Spirit wrought**, aligning epistemology with sanctification.

**Anthropology** contributes the doctrine of **noetic effects of sin**, requiring regeneration and repentance for right knowing, and commending **intellectual virtues** such as humility and patience. **Soteriology** connects justification by faith with **assurance through the Word and Spirit**, shaping the confidence of theological claims. **Ecclesiology** locates theology within the **communion of saints**, under ordained ministry, creeds and confessions, and sacraments, with **Westminster’s confessionalism** exemplifying ordered submission to Scripture. **Eschatology** reminds that we know in part until the **beatific vision**, so theology is a pilgrim science advancing toward fuller sight in glory.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
The grammar of **principia of theology** grounds preaching and catechesis. The pulpit rests not on rhetorical force but on **Scripture as principium cognoscendi**, proclaimed in the confidence that the **Spirit as internal principle** opens hearts. Congregations can be taught to distinguish **God as the source of truth**, **the Word as the norm**, and **the Spirit as the efficient cause** of understanding, thereby fostering resilient faith in seasons of doubt.

Liturgically, a retrieval of **theology as science** orders worship around the **means of grace**. Prayers for illumination confess dependence on **Spirit-given light**. Lectionary or lectio continua practices embody confidence in **Scripture’s sufficiency**. Catechesis frames discipleship as growing in a habitus formed by **divine teaching**, not as mere information transfer. Pastoral counseling can appeal to **general revelation** while subordinating all counsel to **Christ’s lordship** as revealed in the gospel.

In mission and apologetics, the **Old Princeton confidence in public reason** and the **Neo-Calvinist insistence on antithesis** together encourage a double strategy. We commend the **self-authenticating Word** and invite neighbors into the plausibility structures of the church, while also offering **public arguments** that display the coherence, explanatory power, and moral beauty of the Christian vision. The heart of the method remains **witness to Christ**, attended by the Spirit, under the authority of Scripture.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Theological method is faith seeking understanding under the **principium of the triune God**, through **Scripture as the external norm**, by the **Spirit as the internal light**, thereby constituting a genuinely Christian **science of God**.
