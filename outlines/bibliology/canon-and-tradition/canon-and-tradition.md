### TITLE
One Canon, Two Hands: Scripture and Tradition under the Rule of Faith

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late apostolic through modern era; dogmatic location in **Bibliology and Prolegomena** with implications for **Ecclesiology** and **Theology Proper**.
- Core question: How do **canon and tradition** together norm Christian belief, and what is their principled relation under **divine revelation**.
- Principal figures: **Irenaeus** (regula fidei), **Athanasius** (Festal Letter 39), **Augustine** (De Doctrina Christiana), **Trent 1546** and **Dei Verbum 1965** (Roman magisterial account), **Luther** and **Melanchthon** (Reformation sola Scriptura), **Warfield and Hodge** (Old Princeton), **Kuyper and Bavinck** (Dutch Neo-Calvinism), **Barth** (Church Dogmatics), **Michael Kruger** (self-authenticating canon).
- Key concepts: **regula fidei and paradosis**, **sola Scriptura versus nuda Scriptura**, **material sufficiency versus two-source theory**, **self-authentication and internal testimony of the Spirit**, **apostolicity, catholicity, orthodoxy, antiquity**, **magisterium and reception**.
- Major texts and events: **Muratorian Fragment c. 180**, **Athanasius 367**, **Hippo 393 and Carthage 397**, **Trent 1546 Deuterocanon**, **Westminster Confession 1646–1647**, **Dei Verbum 1965**, **Childs 1979 canonical approach**.
- Reformed trajectories: **Old Princeton inerrantist canon recognition**, **Westminster self-attestation**, **Neo-Calvinist principium cognoscendi**; alternatives include **Roman Catholic one deposit/two modes**, **Lutheran confessional clarity**, **Orthodox Holy Tradition**, **Barth’s actualism**.
- Ongoing debate: Status of **deuterocanon**, **criteria versus reception models**, **historical criticism and canonical unity**, **analytic epistemology of authority and testimony**.

### ESSAY
#### 1.**Definition & Dogmatic Location**
Canon names the church’s normative corpus of writings as a **rule of faith**; tradition names the church’s living transmission of the **apostolic paradosis** by which the canon is confessed, read, and taught. The dogmatic location is **Bibliology** within the broader prolegomenon of revelation and authority, with immediate implications for **Ecclesiology** because the church receives and transmits the canon, and for **Theology Proper** because Scripture is a divinely authored instrument of the triune God. The question is not whether Scripture or tradition, but how **canon and tradition** coinhere as modes of the one divine economy of revelation under the governance of the **triune Lord**.

A principled definition sets three claims in tension. First, Scripture is uniquely normative as **prophetic-apostolic** witness to Christ and thus materially sufficient for saving knowledge. Second, tradition is necessary as the **regula fidei** in liturgy, catechesis, and hermeneutics that normatively guides reading under the Spirit. Third, the church is ministerial, not magisterial, relative to Scripture’s authority, even while it is instrumental for **canon reception** through history. How these claims are ordered divides **Reformed, Roman Catholic, Lutheran, and Orthodox** trajectories.

#### 2.**Biblical Foundations**
The New Testament itself distinguishes inspired writing from merely human tradition while affirming a binding **apostolic paradosis**. Paul urges believers to hold fast to the traditions he delivered, whether by word or letter, in 2 Thessalonians 2:15, linking oral and written **apostolic testimony** under one gospel. In 1 Corinthians 15:3–5 he speaks of what he received and delivered, an early creedal **kerygma** centered on Christ’s death and resurrection, showing that tradition is doctrinally contentful and public.

Scripture witnesses to its own divine provenance and sufficiency. Second Timothy 3:16–17 affirms the **theopneustos** character of Scripture for teaching and equipping, while 2 Peter 3:16 recognizes Paul’s letters among the **graphe**, expanding the canon horizon within the apostolic age. Deuteronomy 4:2, Proverbs 30:6, and Revelation 22:18–19 mark the covenantal logic of a closed, inviolable **canonical corpus**, even as Luke 1:1–4 shows diligent historical composition under the Spirit’s providence. Christ’s summary in Luke 24:44 of Law, Prophets, and Psalms presumes a **tripartite Tanakh**, while his rebuke of human traditions that nullify God’s word in Mark 7:13 guards against **traditionalism** that displaces divine command.

The biblical pattern is thus a dialectic: the gospel is transmitted as authoritative **paradosis**, immediately giving rise to inspired writings that norm and purify ecclesial tradition. Tradition bears Scripture to the church; Scripture judges and reformulates tradition by the **apostolic rule**.

#### 3.**Historical Development**
Second-century theologians forged the pattern of canon and tradition amid **Gnostic** challenges. **Irenaeus** circa 180 invoked the **regula fidei** as a summary of Scripture, arguing that the same Spirit who inspired the apostles secures the church’s **public apostolic succession** and reading of the fourfold Gospel and Pauline corpus. The **Muratorian Fragment** around 180 lists most New Testament books, appealing to **apostolicity and catholicity**. **Tertullian** deployed the rule of faith to exclude speculative novelty, insisting on a canon-shaped **ecclesial memory**.

By the fourth century, **Athanasius’s Festal Letter 39 in 367** lists the twenty-seven New Testament books, excluding the **deuterocanonical** writings from the canon while allowing them for edification. The African councils of **Hippo 393 and Carthage 397/419** echoed this list, integrating usage among the churches into **canonical recognition**. **Jerome** (c. 405) translated the Vulgate and distinguished the Hebrew canon from the **Apocrypha**, while **Augustine** defended the larger Old Testament on **ecclesial usage** grounds, a divergence that later shaped Western debates.

Through the medieval period, canon functioned within a broader **sacramental tradition**, with the liturgy and creeds serving as interpretive rule. The Fourth Lateran Council in **1215** presupposed this structure without a formal canon decree, while scholastic theology organized doctrine by **Scripture and patristic authorities**. The Reformation of **1517 onward** reasserted **sola Scriptura**, not as nuda Scriptura, but as Scripture as the only infallible norm within a ministerial, reformable **tradition**. **Luther** in 1522 ordered the New Testament with an awareness of **homologoumena and antilegomena**, though he retained the full 27 books; the **Book of Concord 1580** stabilized Lutheran confessional tradition. The **Council of Trent 1546** affirmed the deuterocanonical books and declared Scripture and unwritten traditions to be received with equal piety, entrenching a **two-mode account** of the one deposit.

Modernity complicated reception. **Old Princeton** in the nineteenth century emphasized **inspiration and inerrancy** alongside historical arguments for **canon recognition**. The **Westminster Confession 1646–1647** codified the Protestant list and the **self-attestation** of Scripture. In the twentieth century, **Barth** located Scripture’s authority in God’s free self-speaking, stressing that Scripture becomes the **Word of God** by the Spirit’s act. Roman Catholic ressourcement culminated in **Dei Verbum 1965**, which spoke of one deposit transmitted in **Scripture and Tradition** authentically interpreted by the **magisterium**, tempering earlier **partim-partim** readings. Eastern Orthodoxy continued to speak of **Holy Tradition** as the church’s life in the Spirit, with Scripture as the heart of that living tradition and the **Septuagint** as liturgical norm.

#### 4.**Reformed Doctrine (Old Princeton / Dutch Neo-Calvinist / Westminster)**
The confessional Reformed consensus centers on **sola Scriptura**, with strong commitments to **perspicuity, sufficiency, and self-attestation**. The **Westminster Confession 1.2** lists the canonical books and excludes the **Apocrypha**, while **WCF 1.4–1.5** grounds Scripture’s authority in God and the **internal testimony of the Holy Spirit**. Scripture is the supreme judge in all controversies, yet **tradition** is honored as subordinate help in **WCF 1.8**, which authorizes translations and public reading and assumes creedal catechesis.

**Old Princeton** articulated a robust doctrine of **inspiration and inerrancy** as properties of the autographa. **B. B. Warfield** argued the church recognizes rather than constitutes the canon and defended **apostolicity and corporate reception** as historical markers of recognition. **Charles Hodge** integrated the **regula fidei** within a Protestant system, insisting that confessions are binding only insofar as they faithfully summarize **biblical teaching**. This posture permits rigorous historical inquiry without ceding the normative primacy of the **prophetic-apostolic script**.

Dutch Neo-Calvinism deepened the foundational account by retrieving the **principium cognoscendi**. **Abraham Kuyper** spoke of Scripture as an organic whole given by **God’s sovereign initiative**, with the Spirit granting the faithful **habitus** for recognition. **Herman Bavinck** developed an organic theory of **inspiration and canon**, where form and content grow within the covenant community but derive their authority from the **triune author**. Tradition becomes ministerial and necessary as ecclesial **confession and liturgy**, but Scripture remains the principium normans. Together these strands yield a Reformed pattern: Scripture alone is infallible; tradition is normed norm; the church is recipient and witness under the **Spirit’s testimony**.

#### 5.**Alternative Accounts (Roman Catholic / Lutheran / Modern / Analytic)**
The Roman Catholic account after **Trent 1546** affirmed the deuterocanon and posited that Scripture and unwritten traditions are to be received with equal piety as transmission of the one **deposit of faith**. **Dei Verbum 1965** reframed this as one deposit with two modes of transmission, Scripture and Tradition, under the **magisterium’s** authentic interpretation. Material sufficiency is often affirmed, yet the necessity of **Tradition and teaching office** is insisted upon for definitive interpretation, with **apostolic succession** and conciliar judgments as the juridical organs of reception.

The Lutheran tradition shares **sola Scriptura**, distinguishing it from **nuda Scriptura** by the constitutive role of creeds and the **Book of Concord 1580** as normed norm. Werner **Elert** and later **Robert Preus** emphasized the authority of the ecumenical creeds and confessions as faithful witnesses to the **Scriptures’ doctrinal content**, while maintaining the primacy of the prophetic-apostolic canon. Luther’s frank appraisal of **antilegomena** never displaced the 27-book consensus, exemplifying a pastoral, Christological **canon-within-the-canon** sensitivity without an official reduction.

Modern and postmodern accounts vary. **Karl Barth’s Church Dogmatics I/1** (1932) treats Scripture as a fallible human witness elected by God to be the **Word of God**, stressing divine freedom and the **actualistic** event of revelation. **Brevard Childs’s canonical approach 1979** foregrounds the final canonical form as the theological **locus** for exegesis, aligning canon and theology against atomizing criticism. In analytic theology, **Alvin Plantinga’s** warrant and the **internal instigation of the Holy Spirit** provides an epistemic model for justified belief in Scripture’s authority; **William Abraham’s Canon and Criterion** distinguishes divine canons of authority from later criteria, proposing a **canonical theism** in which Scripture functions among other Spirit-given canons like creed and sacrament.

Eastern Orthodoxy, while not a separate heading here, offers a living paradigm of **Holy Tradition** where Scripture is the heart and criterion, received and confessed in the **phronema** of the church. The **Synod of Jerusalem 1672** affirmed an expanded Old Testament in continuity with the **Septuagint**, articulating a receptionist, liturgical logic of canon grounded in **conciliar discernment**.

#### 6.**Current Debates and Objections**
First, the status of the **deuterocanon** remains contested. Reformed and Lutheran churches continue to read these writings for edification but deny **canonical authority**, appealing to Jewish canon boundaries and **apostolic usage**, while Roman and Orthodox communions cite long-standing **ecclesial reception** and liturgical embeddedness. The debate turns on whether criteria like **apostolicity** are exclusive and on the weight of **canonical reception** across communions.

Second, criteria models versus reception models contest how canon is known. Some propose **apostolicity, catholicity, orthodoxy, and antiquity** as necessary and jointly sufficient conditions. Others, following **Michael Kruger’s self-authenticating model 2012**, argue these criteria describe rather than constitute recognition under the **Spirit’s testimony** to Scripture’s divine qualities. Critics press worries about circularity and sociological contingency, to which defenders reply that a **theological epistemology of testimony** is rightly circular at the level of **principium**.

Third, historical criticism raises fragmentation concerns. Does redactional layering undermine **canonical unity**. Canonical and theological interpretation respond that the final form is the **Spirit-governed** rule for ecclesial reading, integrating historical insight under the **rule of faith**. Postliberal and retrieval movements seek to re-anchor interpretation in **creed, liturgy, and catechesis**, resisting both naive **biblicism** and skeptical historicism.

#### 7.**System Links (Trinity / Christology / Anthropology etc.)**
With respect to **Theology Proper**, canon and tradition are instruments of the **triune self-communication**. The Spirit who breathes out Scripture indwells the church to hand on the **regula fidei**, securing both normative text and normative reading. In **Christology**, the canon centers on the identity of Jesus as **kyrios**; the ecumenical creeds are crystallized tradition guarding **homoousios** and the two natures, functioning as canonical hermeneutic.

In **Anthropology**, fallen reason requires the **illumination of the Spirit** and the guidance of tradition to read aright, while Scripture confronts and renews our interpretive habits. In **Soteriology**, Scripture is a means of grace, its preaching and sacramental proclamation in the church’s tradition mediating the **gospel’s power**. In **Ecclesiology**, the church is the canon’s servant, its councils and confessions ministerial to the **Word’s supremacy**; ministries of oversight are ordered to the preservation of the **apostolic deposit**. In **Eschatology**, the closure of canon witnesses to the sufficiency of Christ’s once-for-all work while the church lives in hopeful **traditioned obedience** awaiting the beatific vision.

#### 8.**Pastoral and Liturgical Payoff**
Pastorally, a right relation of **canon and tradition** guards congregations from two precipices: the drift of **traditionalism** that can obscure Scripture, and the volatility of **biblicism** that disregards the church’s hard-won rule of faith. Catechesis should embed believers in the **Apostles and Nicene Creeds**, the Lord’s Prayer, and the Decalogue as a practical **regula fidei**, while expository preaching honors Scripture’s **perspicuity and sufficiency**.

Liturgically, the lectionary, creeds, and sacraments enact the unity of **Word and tradition**. The public reading of Scripture, confession of the **creed**, and celebration of Eucharist discipline communities into a canonical imagination governed by the **triune God**. Ministerial use of confessions like the **Westminster Standards** or **Book of Concord** can stabilize doctrinal teaching without eclipsing the final authority of the **prophetic-apostolic canon**.

#### 9.**One concise closing sentence.**
Canon and tradition, under the **Spirit’s governance**, together serve the church such that Scripture alone norms absolutely while the church’s regula faithfully hands on, hears, and obeys the one **apostolic gospel**.
