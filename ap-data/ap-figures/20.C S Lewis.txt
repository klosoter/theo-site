### TITLE
C. S. Lewis, Rational Imagination and the Case for Mere Christianity

### PREVIEW NOTES
- 1918 to 1963, Belfast to Oxford and Cambridge, interwar skepticism, wartime crisis, and postwar analytic philosophy shaped a public that distrusted metaphysics yet craved moral clarity.
- Anglican lay thinker formed by classical theism, tutored by Kirkpatrick in logic, mentored in friendship by J. R. R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson, drawn to George MacDonald and Augustine.
- Aim to make historical Christianity appear both intellectually credible and existentially satisfying against scientism, emotivism, and biblical demythologizing.
- Principal works include The Problem of Pain 1940 for a suffering nation, The Screwtape Letters 1942 to 1943 for mass readers, Mere Christianity 1952 from BBC talks for listeners and seekers, Miracles 1947 revised 1960 for philosophers, The Abolition of Man 1943 for educators, Surprised by Joy 1955 as spiritual autobiography.
- Method described as cumulative case through imaginative reason and inference to the best explanation grounded in common experience.
- Distinctive arguments include Moral Law, a universal standard that implies a Lawgiver, Argument from Reason, naturalism undermines rational inference, Argument from Desire, deep longing points beyond nature, Myth become fact, the gospel as a true myth, Trilemma, Jesus as Lord or liar or lunatic.
- Interlocutors included A. J. Ayer, logical positivist denying metaphysical meaning, Elizabeth Anscombe, analytic critic of his argument in Miracles, Rudolf Bultmann, advocate of demythologizing, and Sigmund Freud, rival psychologist of religion.
- Legacy cue, a bridge between Chesterton style cultural apologetics and late twentieth century philosophical work by Plantinga, while shaping narrative evangelism in churches and universities.

### ESSAY
PERSONAL AND CULTURAL CONTEXT
Clive Staples Lewis was born in Belfast in 1898, wounded in France in 1918, and buried in Oxford in 1963. That life arc placed him at the junction of shattered European confidence and an English literary renaissance. After study at University College, Oxford, he won a fellowship at Magdalen College in 1925 and remained there through World War Two, then moved to Cambridge in 1954 as the first professor of Medieval and Renaissance English. The interwar decades bred skepticism fed by war graves and mechanized bureaucracy, and the postwar years elevated analytic precision through figures such as A. J. Ayer, whose Language, Truth and Logic in 1936 treated talk of God as nonsensical. Lewis lived inside that climate yet refused its narrowing of rationality. His own conversion unfolded across 1929 to 1931, through talk and walking with J. R. R. Tolkien and Hugo Dyson and through his recovery of an older Christian metaphysic that could make sense of wonder and duty. The BBC invited him to speak to the nation from 1941 to 1944; in bombed streets and on rationed tables his voice became a careful tutor in the grammar of belief. He wrote The Problem of Pain in 1940 at the outset of the Blitz to address the national cry of why God allows suffering. He debated at the Oxford Socratic Club, founded in 1941 with him as chair, and in February 1948 he met Elizabeth Anscombe, who challenged his argument against naturalism. Widowed in July 1960 when Joy Davidman died, he wrote A Grief Observed in 1961, and he died on 22 November 1963, the same day as the assassination of John F. Kennedy in the United States.

INTELLECTUAL FRAMEWORK
Lewis inhabited a synthesis of classical theism, moral realism, and romantic imagination. His Christian metaphysics is the old consensus of Augustine and Aquinas, the reality of a personal, necessarily existent God whose goodness grounds moral truths and whose Logos orders reason. Yet he learned this not by scholastic manuals but by literary apprenticeship. The Allegory of Love in 1936 and A Preface to Paradise Lost in 1942 show his craft of reading symbols as windows onto being, which becomes crucial in the apologetic essay Myth Became Fact in 1944, where he argues that myth is not falsehood but truth made radiant. The Abolition of Man in 1943 articulates a natural law framework, calling the shared moral grammar the Tao and insisting that emotivist reduction of value will unmake humans. This framework informs Mere Christianity in 1952, where the moral argument is set amid a claim that Christianity is the best map of the moral terrain we already walk. He drew also on Plato through Augustine to argue that desire can be pedagogical. In Surprised by Joy in 1955, the puncture of worldly satisfactions by a longing for something beyond becomes an argument that points to the transcendent. Lewis insisted that truth and meaning are joined, hence the necessity of narrative and image alongside syllogism. He stood nearer to G. K. Chesterton, whose Orthodoxy in 1908 celebrated the romance of truth, than to Karl Barth, whose Barmen Declaration in 1934 grounded theology in revelation alone and distrusted natural theology. Unlike Barth, Lewis thought public reason could be a prelude to faith.

EPISTEMOLOGICAL METHOD
His method is a cumulative case structured by inference to the best explanation. He argues from widespread human experience to the hypothesis that Christianity uniquely makes sense of reason, morality, and longing. In Miracles in 1947, he proposed that rational inference requires that not all causes are nonrational pushes, thus the very act of considering evidence points beyond naturalism to Reason with a capital R. After the 1948 encounter, he tightened the analysis in the 1960 revision, distinguishing causes that determine and grounds that justify, and seeking to show that if all beliefs are the product of nonrational causes, the category of justification dissolves. He pairs this with a common sense realism that trusts basic moral intuitions and the reliability of reason while allowing that these need a metaphysical home. He is not a strict evidentialist, since he values stories, symbols, and imagination as modes of apprehension. Yet he is not a presuppositionalist like Cornelius Van Til, who in 1939 argued that only Christian presuppositions make knowledge possible. Lewis aims at the common ground shared by believers and skeptics listening on the wireless in 1942. He treats testimony, conscience, and rational reflection as public evidence. The approach is pastoral in tone but analytic in structure, inviting a hearer to test whether Christianity yields a more coherent, deeper, and more liveable picture of reality than its rivals.

ARGUMENTATION AND CONTENT
Several core arguments organize his case. First, the moral argument in Mere Christianity, delivered in radio talks between 1941 and 1944 and gathered in 1952, begins from two facts, that people across times talk as if some moral standard is real, and that people often fail to keep it and feel they ought to. Lewis contends that such a law is better explained by a moral Lawgiver than by subjective feeling or mere social conditioning. He collides here with the emotivism that Ayer popularized in 1936, which treated moral claims as expressions of feeling and commands. Lewis replies that moral language behaves as if it aimed at truth, and education that denies objective value, as he argued in The Abolition of Man in 1943, erodes the formation of persons. He presses that moral obligation is not a projection but a participation in the reality of goodness.

Second, the argument from reason in Miracles asserts that naturalism undercuts the trustworthiness of reasoning. If every belief arose from nonrational causes, then no belief could be endorsed because of grounds. Elizabeth Anscombe, in February 1948, showed that Lewis conflated different senses of because and urged a sharper account of rational causation. Lewis revised the chapter in 1960 to meet that critique, and the debate improved the precision of his claim. The result is not a knockdown but a challenge to strict naturalism, and it places Lewis in conversation with later arguments about the reliability of cognitive faculties in Alvin Plantinga, whose God and Other Minds in 1967 and Warrant trilogy in 1993 would push that line within analytic terms.

Third, the argument from desire appears in The Pilgrims Regress in 1933 and is narrated in Surprised by Joy in 1955. Human beings experience a stab of Joy, a desire for something the world cannot supply. Such desire is not satisfied by pleasure or power. Lewis infers that natural desires have real objects, so this transcendent desire indicates a transcendent object. Critics have pressed that some desires are misfiring signals, yet his point is less deductive and more abductive, that the best explanation of this pattern is that humans are made for God.

Fourth, he engages evil in The Problem of Pain in 1940 and in A Grief Observed in 1961. The former offers a classical theodicy, that free will and soul making require a world with suffering, and that God is good in allowing pain that can be a megaphone to rouse a deaf world. The latter refuses glibness, recording raw sorrow after Joy Davidmans death in 1960. The pairing forms a humane apologetic, principle clarified in 1940, experience tested in 1961. He also explores judgment and mercy in The Great Divorce in 1945, where the doors of hell are locked on the inside, echoing the free refusal theme.

Fifth, Lewis developed a christological challenge often called the trilemma. In Mere Christianity in 1952 he argues that Jesus who claims divine status is either Lord, or a liar, or a lunatic. He urges readers to reject the comfortable option of Jesus as only a moral teacher. Later scholarship added a fourth option, legend, pressing that historical criticism could weaken the premise. Lewis thought the New Testament documents, read with the skills of a literary critic honed in 1936 and 1942, resist the legend thesis, since they bear the marks of reportage. He did not mount a full historical defense, yet he framed a crisp decision that has stirred countless readers.

Finally, he insisted that myth can convey truth. In Myth Became Fact in 1944 and in the Narnia sequence from 1950 to 1956, he dramatized doctrine. Aslan dies and rises in The Lion, the Witch and the Wardrobe in 1950, and readers meet substitution, atonement, and resurrection in images that bypass cynicism. This is apologetics by narrative, complementary to the arguments of Miracles and Mere Christianity.

INTERNAL COHERENCE AND LIMITS
Lewis writes with luminous clarity, yet his system bears limits. The moral argument presumes that shared moral intuitions indicate objective value, but evolutionary debunkers from the 1970s onward, influenced by E. O. Wilson in 1975, argue that moral feelings can be adaptive byproducts. Lewis anticipates this by insisting that moral claims aim at truth and that reductive accounts self erode, yet his argument does not prove a personal God, only a moral reality that fits better with theism. The argument from reason gains traction by exposing naturalisms tension, though Anscombe showed that careless language blurs the issue. Lewis improved the chapter in 1960, but some philosophers remain unconvinced that naturalism cannot host rational insight. His trilemma ignites decision yet overlooks complexities of christology and the development of doctrine, and modern textual criticism since 1947, the year of the first Dead Sea Scroll discoveries, broadened discussions he did not engage in technical detail. On evil, The Problem of Pain gives a classical account suitable for 1940 radio but seems thin against genocidal horror recorded in 1945. A Grief Observed partially answers by abandoning easy answers, though it now functions more as pastoral wisdom than argument. His method assumes that common human experience is a reliable starting point; in plural societies after 1960 the very description of common experience became contested. Even so, the breadth of his approach, joining analysis, story, and moral vision, gives it resilience.

SIGNIFICANCE AND COMPARATIVE INFLUENCE
Lewis stands as the twentieth centurys great translator of Christian metaphysics into public idiom. His BBC broadcasts in 1941 to 1944 and their 1952 consolidation into Mere Christianity shaped a generation recovering from war. He helped define a genre of cultural apologetics that takes beauty and morality as evidential. In comparison, G. K. Chesterton in Orthodoxy in 1908 delighted in paradox and civic common sense, while Lewis added analytic bite and wartime urgency. In contrast with Karl Barth, who rejected natural theology in 1934 and built from revelation, Lewis approached from below, building on reason and experience, then pointing to Christ. Relative to A. J. Ayer, who in 1936 condemned metaphysics as nonsense, Lewis defended the legitimacy of moral and religious discourse and exposed emotivisms costs. Relative to Alvin Plantinga, whose 1967 and 1993 works reconstructed belief in God as properly basic, Lewis remained outside technical analytic structures but anticipated the critique of naturalism and defended the rationality of theistic commitment. In a different register, Dorothy L. Sayers, with The Man Born to be King broadcast in 1941, also exemplified narrative apologetics, showing that story can carry doctrinal weight. Later apologists drew directly from Lewis. Tim Keller in The Reason for God in 2008 recast the moral argument and the imaginative appeal to meaning. Alister McGrath in 2013 interpreted Lewis as a master of rational imagination. University ministries worldwide still assign The Abolition of Man for its prescient analysis of education and desire. The 1948 Socratic Club debate became a myth of collapse among critics; careful historiography shows instead a sharpening of focus in the 1960 revision of Miracles, and his post 1948 fiction such as Till We Have Faces in 1956 displays the same apologetic energies transposed into mythic key. His long term significance lies in modeling an integral apologetic that is neither mere logic nor mere story but a disciplined conversation between them. One line sums his wager, that Christianity is true to the way the world is and truer still to the way we are. The result is a body of work that still teaches how to reason and how to imagine, and that continues to set the terms of debate for popular and scholarly apologetics.
