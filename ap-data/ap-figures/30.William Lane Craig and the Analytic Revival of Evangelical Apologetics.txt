### TITLE
William Lane Craig and the Analytic Revival of Evangelical Apologetics

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Time and setting, 1960s to 2010s, United States and United Kingdom campuses, a climate shaped by the analytic turn in philosophy of religion, Big Bang cosmology, and the polemics of New Atheism after 2001.
- Theological background, evangelical Protestant formation at Wheaton in 1967 to 1971, doctoral study under John Hick at Birmingham completed 1977 and under Wolfhart Pannenberg in Munich completed 1984, later aligned with Molinism drawn from Luis de Molina of the sixteenth century.
- Core apologetic aim, to show that belief in the Christian God is both rationally warranted and publicly defensible through natural theology and the historical case for the resurrection.
- Principal works, The Kalam Cosmological Argument 1979 for scholars, The Son Rises 1981 for pastors and students, The Only Wise God 1987 for evangelical readers, Reasonable Faith 1994 then revised 2008 as a seminary level handbook, Time and Eternity 2001 for philosophers and theologians, Philosophical Foundations for a Christian Worldview 2003 with J P Moreland as a graduate textbook.
- Method, analytic natural theology joined to a cumulative case that blends deductive, abductive, and historical arguments.
- Distinctive arguments or concepts, Kalam cosmological argument, a deductive case from beginning to cause and a personal creator; Hilberts hotel, a thought experiment against actual infinities applied to time; A theory of time, a defense of temporal becoming and divine temporal eternity; Middle knowledge or Molinism, divine providence and human freedom reconciled; Fine tuning, a probabilistic design inference; Resurrection minimal facts, a historically bounded inference to the best explanation.
- Interlocutors, Quentin Smith, atheist philosopher coauthor in 1993 on cosmology; Graham Oppy, critic of the simplicity and necessity of theism; Sam Harris, 2011 debate on moral foundations; Christopher Hitchens, 2009 debate on the existence of God; Sean Carroll, 2014 debate on cosmology and beginning; Wes Morriston, critic of kalam premises.
- Legacy cue, revived kalam as a live option in analytic philosophy, fused public debates with monograph level scholarship, and shaped evangelical pedagogy in apologetics.

### ESSAY
PERSONAL AND CULTURAL CONTEXT
William Lane Craig was born in 1949 in Illinois and came of age amid the social upheavals of the late 1960s. He studied at Wheaton College from 1967 to 1971, where evangelical faith and rigorous liberal arts training framed a lifelong drive to unite devotion with reason. His graduate formation bridged Anglo American analytic philosophy and continental theology. He earned a doctorate at the University of Birmingham in 1977 under John Hick, then a leading figure in philosophy of religion, and completed a second doctorate in Munich under Wolfhart Pannenberg in 1984, a theologian known for historical emphasis on the resurrection and for a strong engagement with the sciences.

Craig began publishing at the crest of the analytic revival in philosophy of religion that followed Alvin Plantingas work in the late 1960s. The decade after 1970 saw Big Bang cosmology widely accepted, while analytic philosophers renewed debates on arguments for God after a long positivist winter. By 1979, Craig published The Kalam Cosmological Argument, bringing a medieval Islamic line of thought into contemporary form. The 1990s and 2000s added a new public stage. Campus debates and media events became central venues, especially after the rise of New Atheism around 2006 with Richard Dawkins and 2007 with Christopher Hitchens. Craig founded Reasonable Faith in 2007 to disseminate scholarship, host podcasts, and train lay apologists. Key debates punctuate this era, including a widely viewed exchange with Christopher Hitchens in April 2009 at Biola University and a sustained engagement with scientific cosmology in a dialogue with Sean Carroll in 2014 in Los Angeles.

INTELLECTUAL FRAMEWORK
Craig draws on several strands. From natural theology he takes the conviction that the existence and nature of God can be argued from reason and evidence. From Plantinga and the Reformed epistemology movement, he accepts that belief in God can be properly basic, grounded in the inner witness of the Holy Spirit, yet he insists that public argument has a rightful place in persuasion and pedagogy. From Luis de Molina of 1588 he appropriates middle knowledge, building a Molinist doctrine of divine providence in The Only Wise God in 1987. From contemporary metaphysics he adopts an A theory of time, defending temporal becoming and divine temporality, a thesis elaborated in Time and Eternity in 2001. From the history of ideas he retrieves kalam, a medieval Islamic tradition worked by al Ghazali in the eleventh century, and retools it for analytic debate.

This framework is not merely a list of influences. It functions as an integrated architecture. Cosmological reasoning provides a metaphysical ground for a creator. Metaphysical time theory specifies how a temporal creation relates to God. Molinism articulates providence and libertarian freedom. Historical method anchors Christological claims in evidence. The threads are tied together in Reasonable Faith in 1994, which offers a unified toolkit for students and pastors. Philosophical Foundations for a Christian Worldview in 2003, coauthored with J P Moreland, extends the framework into a full course in analytic theology and apologetics.

EPISTEMOLOGICAL METHOD
Craig combines two sources of warrant. First, he affirms a form of internalist warrant through the Spirit, arguing that Christian belief can be rational without inferential support because of a self authenticating experience of God. He presents this as a pastoral and devotional grounding rather than as a premise in public argument. Second, he advances a cumulative case for the public square, weaving deductive, probabilistic, and historical arguments.

The kalam argument is deductive. It relies on a metaphysical principle that whatever begins to exist has a cause, and a second claim that the universe began to exist. Evidence for the second claim includes philosophical considerations about the impossibility of actual infinite collections of concrete things, illustrated by the Hilberts hotel paradox, and empirical inputs from cosmology, including the standard Big Bang model and the Borde Guth Vilenkin theorem announced in 2003.

For moral knowledge, Craig employs a moral argument that is quasi deductive. If objective moral values and duties exist, then God is their best ground. He asserts that moral realism is part of common human experience and is also affirmed by many contemporary philosophers. For design, he prefers a fine tuning argument presented in probabilistic terms. The delicate balance of physical constants is more expected on theism than on single universe naturalism, and a multiverse hypothesis requires auxiliary explanations and measure theory.

For the resurrection, Craig uses historical method shaped by contemporary New Testament scholarship. He isolates a set of facts that meet criteria like multiple attestation and early testimony, such as the 1 Corinthians 15 tradition that many date to the mid 30s, the burial by Joseph of Arimathea, the empty tomb tradition linked to Mark often dated around 70, and postmortem appearances reported by diverse witnesses. He then argues that the resurrection hypothesis explains these data better than hallucination or conspiracy.

ARGUMENTATION AND CONTENT
The body of Craigs work develops each strand with detailed engagement. The Kalam Cosmological Argument in 1979 set the pattern. It defends the causal principle with thought experiments and philosophical analysis, and it secures the beginning of the universe with two lines of argument. The first line appeals to the impossibility of actual infinities of successive events in time. The second line invokes scientific evidence, including cosmic expansion discovered in 1929 and the predictive success of Big Bang nucleosynthesis established in the 1960s. In later essays and debates, Craig cites the Borde Guth Vilenkin theorem of 2003 to claim that any universe with a positive average expansion is past finite. Critics such as Adolf Grunbaum challenged theological readings of Big Bang cosmology, arguing in the 1990s that a singularity is not a cause friendly beginning. Quentin Smith, who coauthored Atheism, Theism, and Big Bang Cosmology in 1993 with Craig, contended that a self contained cosmology undercuts the need for a creator. Graham Oppy has argued since the 1990s that theism is not a simpler or more explanatory hypothesis than naturalism, and that the kalam argument equivocates between metaphysical necessity and empirical generalization. Wes Morriston has pressed since 2002 that there is no incoherence in an actual infinite past, and that the Hilberts hotel analogy may not legitimately transfer to the real world.

Craig answers that the metaphysical principle is not a mere inductive generalization but a deep modal truth, and that cosmological evidence provides corroboration rather than sole foundation. He then extends the cosmological inference to the nature of the cause, arguing that a timeless, spaceless, immaterial, and personal cause is required to produce a temporal effect without prior determining conditions. That step moves from cosmology to philosophical theism.

His moral argument matured in public debate. In April 2011 at Notre Dame, Craig debated Sam Harris on whether God is the foundation of moral goodness. Craig pressed that Harris reduces moral facts to facts about well being, which he argued is a natural fact that cannot yield categorical moral obligation. Harris replied that well being has an objective structure that allows a science of morality. The exchange crystallized Craigs strategy. He shifts moral debate from moral epistemology to moral ontology, and he leverages the phenomenology of obligation to support theism.

Design reasoning features in campus events and texts such as On Guard in 2010. Craig frames fine tuning as a case where the hypothesis of a single universe without design renders the life permitting range astonishingly small, whereas theism predicts a fine tuned cosmos. In 2014, Sean Carroll challenged Craig by appealing to naturalistic accounts of apparent fine tuning, to the need for a prior probability function before speaking of improbability, and to cosmological models without a beginning. Craig replied that inflating the hypothesis space through multiverse proposals multiplies explanatory costs and does not remove the need for ultra fine tuning in the multiverse generator itself.

The historical case for the resurrection runs through The Son Rises in 1981 and Reasonable Faith in 1994. Craig emphasizes the early date of the 1 Corinthians 15 tradition, often placed within five years of the crucifixion in 30 or 33, and stresses that the reported appearances include group experiences and the conversion of hostile witnesses like Paul and James. In debate with Bart Ehrman in 2006, Craig argued that naturalistic hypotheses fail to account for the empty tomb and the breadth of appearances, while Ehrman disputed the reliability of the sources and the scope of what history can claim. Craig follows Pannenberg in asserting that historical reasoning can reach explanatory judgments that favor a miracle if a theistic worldview is defensible on independent grounds.

INTERNAL COHERENCE AND LIMITS
Craigs project aims to be architectonic. The cosmological argument points to a creator. The theory of time explains how a temporal world relates to a God who can be timeless sans creation and temporal since creation. Molinism unites divine sovereignty with libertarian freedom. Historical evidence then anchors the identity of this creator as the God who raised Jesus. This structure yields a coherence that many students find compelling.

Yet several limits press on the model. His defense of the A theory of time stands against a robust B theory tradition in both metaphysics and physics. Critics note that relativity theory since 1905 resists a privileged present, and that Craigs attempt to recover absolute simultaneity by positing a cosmic time rests on controversial interpretations. On kalam, mathematical set theory after 1874 permits actual infinities with internal consistency, and philosophers such as Morriston and Oppy argue that the inference from mathematical coherence to metaphysical possibility undercuts Craigs first philosophical argument. On cosmology, contemporary models such as eternal inflation and bouncing cosmologies provide scenarios without a global boundary, and Carroll in 2014 pressed that the Borde Guth Vilenkin theorem does not rule out past eternal models if the condition of positive average expansion fails.

The moral argument faces the Euthyphro style dilemma and the challenge of non theistic moral realism. Craigs answer that moral values are grounded in the nature of God avoids arbitrariness, yet it is contested by philosophers who hold that moral truths are necessary and stance independent without theism. The resurrection case faces the hard limit of historiography. Critics like Ehrman argue that miracle claims are intrinsically the least probable given uniform experience, though Craig replies that probability is conditional and can be high if the background case for theism is strong. Finally, integrating a self authenticating witness of the Spirit with public evidence raises questions. Plantinga allows that defeat conditions can undercut warrant, while Craig often emphasizes pastoral assurance more than formal defeat analysis.

SIGNIFICANCE AND COMPARATIVE INFLUENCE
Craig has been a principal architect of the late twentieth century and early twenty first century apologetic revival. Three works anchor his influence. The Kalam Cosmological Argument in 1979 made ancient reasoning newly salient. Reasonable Faith in 1994 and its 2008 revision became a standard seminary text. Philosophical Foundations for a Christian Worldview in 2003 equipped graduate students across disciplines. His public debates, including Hitchens in 2009, Harris in 2011, and Carroll in 2014, created a template for campus apologetics that blends analytic content with accessible rhetoric.

In comparison, Alvin Plantinga advanced warrant and modal ontological arguments, but avoided public debates, and emphasized epistemology more than natural theology. Richard Swinburne built a Bayesian cumulative case in 1979 and 2004, while Craig prefers crisp deductive forms that invite cross examination. Cornelius Van Til treated presuppositions as ultimate commitments and distrusted natural theology, while Craig embraces classical arguments and only secondarily invokes transcendental lines. John Lennox uses scientific exposition and biblical literacy in debates since 2007, while Craig relies more on formal analytic structure and metaphysical claims.

The long term impact is visible in two domains. In academia, kalam and Molinism are now standard topics in philosophy of religion syllabi, and Craig is directly cited in debates about time, infinity, and cosmology. In church education, Reasonable Faith chapters and study groups formed after 2007 have trained thousands in structured argument. Whether one agrees or not, Craig succeeded in making classical arguments audible in a secular age and in pairing them with historically disciplined Christology. The enduring test will be how his kalam survives future cosmology and how his Molinist synthesis navigates ongoing debates about freedom and foreknowledge. One sentence sums his project, analytic clarity allied to evangelistic purpose in defense of a philosophically thick Christian theism.
