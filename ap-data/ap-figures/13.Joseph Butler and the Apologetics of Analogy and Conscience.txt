### TITLE
Joseph Butler and the Apologetics of Analogy and Conscience

### PREVIEW NOTES
- England from 1692 to 1752, shaped by post 1689 Toleration politics and the Newtonian scientific mood, amid deist challenges peaking between 1700 and 1740.
- Raised in a dissenting household then ordained in the Church of England in 1718, influenced by Anglican moralism and by Samuel Clarke through letters in 1713 to 1714.
- He set out to answer deist claims that Christianity adds nothing to natural religion and that miracles and revelation are incredible.
- Principal works include Fifteen Sermons at the Rolls Chapel in 1726 for legal and educated Londoners, The Analogy of Religion in 1736 for the cultured despisers of revelation, and shorter pieces on personal identity and virtue in the late 1730s.
- Method in one phrase, cumulative probabilism through analogy with the course of nature.
- Distinctive moves, authority of conscience as a governing faculty, analogy of nature and revelation, probability the guide of life, critique of egoism, prudential rationality of faith.
- Interlocutors include Matthew Tindal the deist author in 1730, Anthony Collins the advocate of free thinking in 1713, Bernard Mandeville the egoist provocateur in 1723, and David Hume the skeptic of miracles in 1748.
- Legacy cue, a bridge from rational moral theology to later cumulative case apologetics seen in William Paley in 1794 and John Henry Newman in 1870.

### ESSAY
PERSONAL AND CULTURAL CONTEXT
Joseph Butler lived from 1692 to 1752, moving through an English landscape shaped by the Glorious Revolution of 1688 and the Toleration Act of 1689. The new settlement eased sectarian pressures yet opened public space for religious critique. The intellectual climate was Newtonian after 1687, empirically minded, and increasingly hospitable to writers like John Locke in 1690 and to deists who argued that religion should be confined to what reason alone may assert. Butler was born into dissent, educated among Presbyterians, and then entered Oriel College at Oxford in 1714 after gravitating to Anglicanism. Before ordination in 1718, he exchanged letters with Samuel Clarke in 1713 and 1714 about divine attributes and metaphysics, an early sign that he would not be a mere polemicist but a careful inquirer.

His public career placed him at the intersection of pastoral work and polite learning. From 1719 he served as preacher at the Rolls Chapel, where London lawyers and officials heard the sermons that became Fifteen Sermons in 1726. These were delivered amid the deist controversies that followed John Toland in 1696 and peaked with Matthew Tindal in 1730. After the death of Queen Caroline in 1737, whose salon welcomed theological discussion, Butler became Bishop of Bristol in 1738, Dean of St Pauls in 1740, and Bishop of Durham in 1750. By then David Hume had published the first Enquiry in 1748, sharpening the challenge on miracles and testimony that Butler had addressed in a different key. He died in 1752 at Bath, leaving a reputation as a patient moralist and a cautious defender of revelation.

INTELLECTUAL FRAMEWORK
Butlers intellectual framework joined a hierarchical account of human nature to a sober natural theology. In the Fifteen Sermons of 1726 he argued that we are a system of affections with a proper order. Self love is real but bounded, benevolence is real and directed toward others, and conscience is a governing faculty that judges the whole. This structure counters the reductionism of Thomas Hobbes in 1651 and Bernard Mandeville in 1714 and 1723, both of whom explained virtue as disguised self interest. Butler held that if there is a nature to humanity, it includes an authority in conscience that is not a mask but a rule.

In The Analogy of Religion in 1736 he widened his frame to include God and providence. He assumed, with Newtonian humility, that the course of nature exhibits settled laws and ends, though their reasons are often obscure. He used this to argue that objections to revelation frequently prove too much, since the same difficulties occur in nature. The God who orders nature may also order a revealed economy that unfolds through history, miracles, and particular covenants. Implicit here is a moderate teleology and a moral government doctrine, akin to Richard Hooker from 1594 yet articulated in post Locke terms. He does not attempt a geometrical metaphysics. Rather, he offers a lawyerly view of accumulation, where many modest considerations can sum to a serious presumption.

EPISTEMOLOGICAL METHOD
Butlers epistemology is probabilist and prudential. In a famous phrase, not tied to a single page but spread throughout the 1736 Analogy, probability is the very guide of life. He assumes the human condition is one of incomplete information where practical action cannot wait for strict demonstration. He draws from Locke the sense of degrees of assent and from Bacon the empirical patience that lets particulars add up. This means revelation need not be made certain by apodeictic proof. It need only be shown to be credible and more probable than its rivals, given the world we actually experience.

His core instrument is analogy. He asks how features of revelation that strike deists as arbitrary are mirrored in the constitution and course of nature. If nature already shows suffering, delayed justice, and a probationary character, then the moral government taught by Christianity fits that same pattern. If testimony is how we learn most of what we know in ordinary life, then testimony about miracles must be weighed rather than ruled out a priori. This approach anticipates later cumulative case reasoning, but Butler insists on intellectual chastity. He grants ignorance, admits objections, and then shows that the same objections would also overturn our ordinary confidence about nature and morality.

ARGUMENTATION AND CONTENT
The structure of the Analogy in 1736 has two parts, each developing the same strategy. Part one argues for natural religion. He maintains that the world is governed by a moral ruler who treats human life as a state of probation. Appeals to present disorder or the problem of evil do not defeat this claim, since the analogy of nature shows that delayed recompense and discipline through suffering are woven into the fabric of experience. Part two argues for revealed religion. He addresses objections to Christianity arising from supposed contradictions, miracles, and particularity. He replies that God already governs by means not fully disclosed, that historical processes necessarily involve selection and scandal, and that miracles cannot be ruled impossible since nature itself rests on regularities that only God sustains.

In the background stood Matthew Tindal, whose Christianity as Old as the Creation in 1730 argued that all true religion is eternal and purely rational, so any special revelation is superfluous or corrupting. Butler counters by insisting that while the moral core of religion is stable, humans need additional instruction and motivation that fit their condition, including positive institutions like sacraments. He also argues that the alleged superfluity vanishes once we see how little unaided reason actually yields for the many, a point aimed also at Anthony Collins and his 1713 defense of free thinking unbound by ecclesial authority. When he turns to miracles, Butler anticipates the later 1748 challenge from Hume by stressing prudence. Miracles are not antecedently impossible. Their credibility turns on the weight and coherence of testimony, the context of a revealed purpose, and their fit with the moral ends of God.

The Fifteen Sermons of 1726 supply the ethical core of his apologetic. Several sermons address self love, benevolence, and conscience. He denies the collapse of virtue into self interest by arguing that affections have distinct objects and that conscience is authoritative rather than merely strong. He contends that the approval of conscience is not a disguised pleasure. Rather, conscience approves because it has a governing role within our nature. This strengthens the case for a moral governor of the world, since the inward rule mirrors an outward rule by God. Bernard Mandevilles satire in 1723 had said private vices yield public benefits. Butler replies that such a calculus may explain certain economic effects, yet it does not transform vice into virtue or erase the authority of conscience.

Butler also contributed shorter pieces that matter for apologetics. In 1736 he wrote Of Personal Identity, a pointed critique of Lockes memory criterion from 1690. Butler argues that identity through time is grounded in the continued existence of the same living agent, and that memory presupposes, rather than constitutes, this identity. This matters for doctrines of responsibility and future judgment, both central to his Analogy. He also wrote a dissertation on virtue in the late 1730s, clarifying that virtue is not reducible to prudence and that benevolence has a real claim on us under the eye of conscience. His Durham Charge in 1751, delivered as Bishop of Durham, warned about practical atheism in the nation, noting that contempt for religion spreads less by argument than by fashion, a pastoral summary of his lifelong theme.

INTERNAL COHERENCE AND LIMITS
Butlers coherence depends on two hinges, his confidence in the authority of conscience and the symmetry he posits between nature and revelation. Critics have argued that the analogy can quickly become a mirror that merely reflects what he already believes. If nature is read as a theater of moral government, the reading may be influenced by Scripture rather than the other way round. He is vulnerable to the charge that a shared pattern does not establish truth, only fit. Furthermore, analogy handles objections by comparison, not by direct historical proof, so it may leave the historical evidences of Christianity under argued when faced with a skeptical empiricist like Hume in 1748.

His probabilism invites the worry that belief is left too precarious. Pastoral prudence can shade into epistemic minimalism. Critics from the Reformed tradition, such as later presuppositionalists, will say that Butler grants too much to autonomous reason and fails to challenge the no neutral ground claim. Others will fault his theodicy, since the appeal to probation may soften rather than solve the problem of evil that erupted during disasters like the Lisbon earthquake in 1755, which occurred after his death but tested similar arguments. Finally, his assurance in the voice of conscience may look like intuitionism lacking a deeper metaphysical anchor, a point made by those who prefer Thomist natural law or Kantian duty from 1785. Even so, the inner unity of his moral sermons and the apologetic of the Analogy remains evident.

SIGNIFICANCE AND COMPARATIVE INFLUENCE
Butlers significance lies in his consolidation of moral theology and apologetic method during a volatile moment. William Paley in 1794 and 1802 echoes Butler in his appeal to cumulative considerations and the language of prudence, yet Paley leans more on contrivance and design, while Butler leans on conscience and moral government. John Henry Newman in 1870 develops an illative sense and converging probabilities that recall Butler, yet Newman puts more stress on the personal act of assent and the grammar of certitude. Thomas Reid in 1764 champions common sense principles against skepticism, overlapping with Butlers refusal of hyperbolic doubt, yet Reid grounds this in the trustworthiness of faculties rather than in analogy to providence. Blaise Pascal in fragments released in 1670 offers wager and portraits of the divided self, which share Butlers prudential mood, yet Pascal is more existential and less ecclesially institutional.

For later debates on miracles, Butler prepares a path that Richard Whately and others would take in the early nineteenth century, namely to weigh testimony within a larger moral frame rather than isolate it as a bare probability. For ethics, his sermons seeded a tradition of Anglican conscience discourse that influenced the Broad Church and Victorian moralists after 1850. In debates over personal identity, his 1736 essay supplied a counterweight to Lockes influence and helped sustain a robust accountability doctrine for eschatology. In pastoral practice, his 1751 charge names a sociological truth, that belief is undermined by fashion and habit, a point revived by cultural apologists in the twentieth century.

Butlers apologetic is not a machine that produces certainty but a guide that shows why faith is a responsible venture within the actual conditions of human life. By insisting that the same God who orders nature might speak in history, and that conscience is a real authority, he fashioned a resilient strategy that survives changes in scientific detail. The final judgment is measured but affirmative, he made deism look less like hardheaded realism and more like selective skepticism, and he did so with a disciplined charity that still instructs.
