### TITLE
**The Politics of the Cross: A Christian Alternative to Partisanship** - Craig A. Carter

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context**
 - Published in the early 21st century amid heightened U.S. culture-war politics and post-9/11 security debates; written as a corrective within evangelical and broader Protestant conversation about Christian political identity.
 - Engages a milieu that includes activist evangelicals, Christian realists, post-liberal critics, and peace/justice Christians.

- **Central thesis**
 - Christians should adopt a politics shaped primarily by the cruciform reality of Christ (the cross) rather than by partisan loyalties or ideological capturings of the faith. The cross supplies theological categories for political judgment, posture, and practice.

- **Core argument / structure**
 - Diagnostic: contemporary partisanship (both conservative and progressive captures) distorts Christian witness and moral reasoning.
 - Theological foundation: Christology and atonement (cross) provide the normative lens for public life.
 - Ethical implications: cross-shaped virtues (humility, lament, mercy, non-domination) â†’ concrete political practices (advocacy that resists domination, solidarity with the vulnerable).
 - Ecclesial formation: church cultivates dispositions and practices that prepare Christians for faithful political engagement without becoming captive to party loyalty.
 - Public theology: proposes modest, neighbor-focused politics that attends to the common good while resisting utopian and triumphalist tendencies.

- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged**
 - Engages contemporary evangelical praxis and leaders; dialogues implicitly and explicitly with traditions represented by Kuyperian cultural engagement, Niebuhrian realism, Hauerwas/Yoder critiques of constantinianism, and broader Reformed resources on vocation and common grace.
 - Scriptural exegesis centers on Pauline and Gospel texts that articulate the paradox of the cross.

- **Doctrines treated**
 - **Christology** (the cross as formative for identity)
 - **Atonement** (political and ethical implications of reconciliation)
 - **Ecclesiology** (church as formative community and witness)
 - **Eschatology** (realized/inaugurated tension shaping political hope)
 - **Ethics / Political Theology** (virtue formation, practices, public reason)

- **Closing line**
 - Theologically, the work centers the cross as the primary criterion for Christian political life: a posture of redemptive humility and neighborly service that resists partisan domination and cultivates faithful public witness.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases**
 - Places the cross itself (rather than a particular social program or party platform) at the center of political theology.
 - Emphasizes formative practices and virtues over policy blueprints.

- **Corrections or contrasts**
 - Corrects **constantinian/triumphalist** models that equate national power with divine favor.
 - Pushes back on **ideological Christian Left and Right** by refusing to allow ideological ends to sacralize means contrary to cross-shaped ethics.
 - Dialogues critically with Kuyperian cultural engagement-affirming plural Christian influence but warning against cultural capture.

- **Conceptual innovations**
 - Treats the cross as a hermeneutical principle for public reasoning (a way to read political options, not merely a soteriological datum).
 - Frames political virtue as ecclesially formed practices (lament, hospitality, non-domination) rather than primarily as legislation or coercive power.

- **Reception and influence**
 - Received positively among scholars and pastors seeking alternatives to partisan alignments; cited in contemporary discussions on Christian nationalism and civic discipleship.
 - Critiques from some who view it as insufficiently programmatic for urgent policy decisions; praised as a corrective in theological ethics syllabi.

- **Enduring value**
 - Durable resource for reorienting Christian political formation toward ecclesial practices and theological categories rather than party loyalty.
 - Useful as a bridge between biblical-theological reflection and practical public theology.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Politics of the Cross**
 - The notion that the cross provides the primary interpretive and normative framework for political engagement.

- **Cross-shaped discipleship**
 - Personal and communal formation that imitates Christ's humility, self-giving, and solidarity with the marginalized.

- **Non-domination**
 - Political posture that resists domination and coercive power as contrary to the cross.

- **Lament**
 - Ecclesial and public practice recognizing suffering and refusing facile triumphalism; grounds compassionate policy.

- **Neighbor-love (agape in politics)**
 - Political decision-making oriented to the good of concrete others rather than ideological in-groups.

- **Eschatological restraint**
 - Hope that avoids utopian coercion; political action shaped by inaugurated but not consummated kingdom.

- **Common good (reoriented)**
 - Understood through cross-shaped service and solidarity, not merely civic order or national interest.

- **Ecclesial formation**
 - The church's role in cultivating dispositions for faithful political life (virtues, practices, worship).

- **Partisanship critique**
 - Analytical category diagnosing how party loyalty distorts Christian judgment and witness.

- **Witness vs. power**
 - Distinction between persuasive, sacrificial witness and pursuit of domination through state mechanisms.

- **Practical implication: advocacy without domination**
 - Specific policy engagement that seeks protection, care, and justice without mirroring oppressive tactics.

- **Hermeneutic of the cruciform**
 - Reading political realities through the themes of sacrifice, forgiveness, and reconciliation.

How this work is used across loci:
- As a **definition source** for "cross-centered political theology" in seminars and articles.
- As a **polemic** against Christian nationalism and partisan religious politics.
- As an **exegetical model** for applying Pauline and Gospel motifs (cross, kenosis) to public ethics and political formation.
