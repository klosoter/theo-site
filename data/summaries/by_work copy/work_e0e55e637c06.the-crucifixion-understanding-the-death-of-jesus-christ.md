### TITLE
The Crucifixion: Understanding the Death of Jesus Christ - Fleming Rutledge

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting / publication context**
 - Published 2015 by Eerdmans; written by Fleming Rutledge, an Episcopal/Anglican priest and preacher with roots in evangelicalism and classical liturgical practice.
 - Engages late 20th-early 21st century theological debates: postliberal and historical-critical readings that demythologize the cross, nonviolent atonement proposals, and renewed interest in Christus Victor and patristic readings.
 - Aimed at pastors, preachers, and theological students seeking an exegetical-theological account grounded in church life.

- **Central thesis / doctrinal problem**
 - The crucifixion is the decisive, theologically indispensable revelation of who God is - simultaneously the disclosure of divine love and divine judgment - and must be understood as both historically real and theologically revelatory against modern attempts to domesticate, soften, or secularize the cross.

- **Core argument / structure**
 - Part I: Scriptural and theological framing - the cross as the climactic act in salvation history; attention to biblical motifs of sacrifice, wrath, and suffering.
 - Part II: Detailed exegesis - passion narratives, Old Testament backgrounds (suffering servant, sacrificial imagery), Pauline and Johannine interpretations; close reading of the cry of dereliction and other marks of abandonment.
 - Part III: Theological synthesis and pastoral/liturgical implications - the cross and the Trinity, the meaning of atonement language (propitiation/expiation/substitution), pastoral care for suffering, preaching the cross in worship.
 - Major moves: affirm the historicity and scandal of the crucifixion; retrieve classical atonement language while resisting reductive caricatures; show how the cross discloses both wrath and love; apply to preaching, liturgy, and social ethics.

- **Figures, sources, interlocutors**
 - Biblical witnesses (Gospels, Paul, Isaiah, Psalms), patristic and medieval theologians, Reformers (especially Calvinian motifs), and contemporary theologians (critical engagement with N. T. Wright, Gustav Aul√©n, and various liberal and nonviolent atonement proponents).
 - Frequent allusions to liturgical texts, hymns, poets, and artistic representations to illumine theological claims.

- **Doctrines treated**
 - **Christology** (incarnation, two natures, the person and work of the Son)
 - **Atonement** (penal substitution, Christus Victor, propitiation/expiation)
 - **Trinity** (the Son's suffering in relation to the triune life)
 - **Hamartiology** (sin, divine wrath)
 - **Soteriology and eschatology** (reconciliation, defeat of evil)
 - **Pastoral theology and liturgy** (preaching, worship, care for sufferers)

- **Closing line**
 - The crucifixion, for Rutledge, is the nonnegotiable center of Christian truth: the cross is where God's holiness and love meet in decisive judgment and redemptive self-giving.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases**
 - Insistence on the coexistence of **divine wrath and divine love** in the cross; neither may be excised without theological incoherence.
 - Strong liturgical and pastoral immediacy - theology is practiced in preaching and worship, not merely abstracted.
 - Vivid attention to the **language of dereliction** (Jesus "forsaken") as theological index of theodicy and solidarity.

- **Corrections / contrasts to predecessors**
 - Pushes back against theological trends that neutralize the cross (e.g., moral influence-only models, some nonviolent atonement proposals).
 - Nuances rather than abandons **penal substitution**: defends it against caricature while integrating Christus Victor and sacrificial imagery.
 - Critiques overly rationalistic attempts to make the cross palatable to modern sensibilities by demythologizing or domestication.

- **Conceptual / terminological innovations**
 - Emphasizes the cross as a **dialectical revelation**: simultaneous disclosure of God's judgment and mercy (less an innovation than a clarifying retrieval).
 - Uses liturgical/poetic resources to reframe doctrinal categories for preaching contexts.

- **Reception and influence**
 - Widely praised across evangelical, Anglican, and Reformed circles for theological clarity and pastoral usefulness.
 - Criticized by some liberal and certain contextual theologians for reaffirming penal language and for perceived emphasis on divine wrath.
 - Has become a go-to resource in seminaries and preaching courses for integrating biblical exegesis with homiletic application.

- **Enduring value**
 - Model of how to do theological exegesis that is simultaneously pastoral and ecclesial.
 - Resource for reclaiming the cross as doctrinally central without lapsing into sentimentality or abstraction.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Crucifixion** - central historical event and theological locus; both enacted judgment and revealing love.
- **Atonement** - multifaceted: penal, sacrificial, victorious; Rutledge resists reduction to a single model.
- **Penal substitution** - affirmed in substantive terms but carefully qualified against moralistic or legalistic caricatures.
- **Christus Victor** - recovered as complementary: cross defeats cosmic powers, not an alternative to substitutionary language.
- **Divine wrath** - real and theologically necessary to account for holiness and judgment; not incompatible with love.
- **Propitiation / Expiation** - discussed exegetically; propitiation as appeasing divine wrath, expiation as cleansing sin - both brought into conversation.
- **Dereliction** - the cry of abandonment (e.g., "My God, my God") treated as theological center for suffering and identification.
- **Solidarity** - Jesus' participation in human suffering; the cross shows God's identifying with the forsaken.
- **Trinity** - cross is addressed theologically within Trinitarian relations: the Son's suffering is not a rupture in God but an intra-Trinitarian self-giving.
- **Sacrifice** - biblical sacrificial idioms used to explain substitution and covenantal restoration.
- **Holiness** - divine holiness as ground for divine wrath and for the necessity of atonement.
- **Redemption / Reconciliation** - outcomes of the cross: restoration of creation, defeat of evil, reconciliation of sinners.
- **Preaching / Liturgy** - Rutledge models how doctrinal claims about the cross are to be embodied and preached in worship contexts.
- **Witness / Exegesis** - close reading of passion texts as primary warrant for doctrinal claims.

- **How this work is used**
 - As a definitional source for reclaiming the cross in preaching and pastoral formation.
 - As a polemical resource against nonviolent-only or demythologizing accounts of atonement.
 - As an exegetical model for integrating Passion narrative study with systematic theology and liturgical practice.
