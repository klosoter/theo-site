### TITLE
Atonement: A Guide for the Perplexed - Adam J. Johnson

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context**
 - Contemporary introductory/advanced survey, part of the "Guide for the Perplexed" style literature aimed at students and pastors.
 - Situated in post-conservative theological conversation that seeks to recover ancient and Reformation patterns while engaging modern critiques (historical-critical, ethical, and philosophical).
- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem**
 - The book addresses the perennial question: what does Scripture mean by Christ's saving work? Johnson contends that a faithful doctrine of atonement must be historically informed, biblically grounded, and theologically integrated-resisting reduction to a single metaphor or mechanism.
 - He aims to navigate between caricatured alternatives (e.g., exclusive penal substitution vs. purely Christus Victor readings) to show how multiple motifs function together in Christian thought.
- **Core argument or structure**
 - Introductory orientation: method, theological commitments (canonical reading, Trinitarian and covenantal horizons).
 - Biblical survey: key OT sacrificial language, Suffering Servant, Psalms; New Testament emphases (Pauline forensic language, Johannine participation, Pauline participation, the Gospels' kingdom/eschatological motifs).
 - Historical trajectory: patristic recapitulation and Christus Victor emphases; medieval satisfaction and penal-thinking; Reformation developments (Calvinic forensic and covenantal notes); modern controversies (Anselmian readings; 19th-20th century developments; contemporary proposals).
 - Contemporary debates: critique of monolithic theories; assessment of strengths and limits of major models.
 - Constructive proposal: an **integrative pluralism**-a model that preserves the legitimacy and complementary roles of multiple atonement motifs (Christus Victor, satisfaction/substitution, participation/recapitulation, and ethical exemplarism) within a Trinitarian-covenantal frame.
- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged**
 - Patristic authors (Irenaeus, Athanasius), medieval (Anselm), Reformers (Calvin), moderns (Gustaf Aul√©n, John McLeod Campbell, J. H. Bavinck), and contemporary interlocutors (N. T. Wright, J. D. G. Dunn, C. S. Lewis critics, proponents of penal substitution like John Murray and modern critics).
 - Engagement with biblical scholarship (Pauline studies, Johannine theology) and systematic theologians (Barth, Moltmann to varying degrees).
- **Doctrines treated**
 - Soteriology (central), Christology, Trinity, covenant, sin and guilt, sacrificial typology, ecclesiology (union with Christ), eschatology.
- **Closing line**
 - Johnson's theological center: **atonement is multifaceted and must be read as the single, Trinitarian work of God in Christ that liberates, reconciles, substitutes, and unites-interpreted within covenantal and ecclesial horizons rather than reduced to one mechanism.**

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations**
 - Strong methodological insistence on **multimodal reading**: Scripture's metaphorical plurality is theological data, not a problem to be reduced.
 - Prioritizes **union with Christ** (participation) as the hermeneutical key that coheres other motifs into a single economy of salvation.
- **Corrections or contrasts to predecessors**
 - Corrects simplistic historiographies that portray Anselmian/penal models as the only historically significant approach.
 - Challenges exclusive proponents of either penal substitution or Christus Victor, arguing that each isolates biblical emphases to the detriment of a holistic soteriology.
- **Conceptual or terminological innovations**
 - Advocates the label **"integrative pluralism"** (or similar), emphasizing web-like relations among atonement motifs rather than hierarchy or strict synthesis.
 - Uses "matrix language" to describe how biblical images occupy different functional slots (legal, cosmic, participatory) within one salvific event.
- **Reception and influence**
 - Valued as a readable, student-friendly corrective to reductionist readings; often recommended in seminary courses.
 - Has modest traction in Reformed circles as a clarifying resource that does not abandon penal substitution but resists its isolation.
 - Cited in ecclesial teaching contexts where ecumenical attention to patristic motifs is desired.
- **Enduring value**
 - Useful for teaching: helps students hold complex data.
 - Serves as a constructive ecumenical bridge-helpful for dialogues between Protestant, Orthodox, and Catholic emphases on atonement.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Atonement** - Johnson treats it as the umbrella term for the work of Christ that includes liberation, satisfaction, substitution, and participation.
- **Penal substitution** - Recognized as a crucial Reformation and Protestant insight (legal-forensic language); Johnson affirms its importance but warns against making it exclusive.
- **Satisfaction (Anselmian)** - Explains moral/ontological dynamics of honor and satisfaction; placed historically and critiqued when read without covenantal context.
- **Christus Victor** - Emphasizes Christ's victory over powers; Johnson rescues its patristic merits and insists on its compatibility with substitutionary ideas.
- **Recapitulation/Participation** - Union with Christ; central organizing motif for Johnson that integrates other strands by positing salvation as incorporation into Christ.
- **Propitiation/Expiation** - Sacrificial language analyzed for both cultic and forensic senses; Johnson stresses semantic range in biblical usage.
- **Covenantal framework** - The lens that situates legal metaphors (forensic) and relational metaphors (participation) within God's faithful dealings with Israel and the church.
- **Trinitarian economy** - The insistence that atonement is the **work of the triune God**, not merely a transaction between Father and Son.
- **Moral exemplarism** - Treated as a secondary motif; Johnson acknowledges pastoral and ethical value but cautions against making it the primary atonement theory.
- **Forensic language** - Legal categories (justification, imputation) are affirmed as the NT's idiom for how sin and righteousness are dealt with.
- **Cosmic powers** - Language for spiritual and structural evil that Christus Victor addresses.
- **Union with Christ** - Key explanatory principle for sanctification, justification, and final glorification.
- **Ecumenical integration** - How varied traditions' emphases can be read as complementary, not antagonistic.

- How this work is used across loci:
 - As a **definition source** and orientation for students encountering diverse atonement theories.
 - As a **polemic** against reductionist monocausal models (either strictly penal or strictly Christus Victor).
 - As an **exegetical-model** recommending canonical, Trinitarian, and covenantal readings of key texts.
 - As a **conceptual pivot** in curricula when steering soteriology toward integrative, ecumenically aware constructions.
