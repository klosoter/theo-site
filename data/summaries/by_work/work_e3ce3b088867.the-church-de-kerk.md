### TITLE
**The Church (De Kerk)** 
Author: **Unknown** 
Tradition/Association: **Anonymous Reformed/Calvinist milieu (Dutch post-Reformation ecclesiology)** 
Site usage (this dossier): **Church And Kingdom; Church Government - Presbyterian Polity Offices Discipline; Reformation Ecclesiology**

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context**
 - Anonymous tract or treatise circulating within the Dutch/Reformed environment after the Reformation; addresses ecclesial order amid confessional consolidation.
 - Milieu: magisterial Reformation controversies (Roman Catholic episcopacy, Lutheran territorial churches, Anabaptist congregationalism) and Reformed efforts to define visible church polity and discipline.
- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem**
 - The church is the visible covenant community instituted and governed by Christ the King through ordained offices, sacramental means, and disciplinary practice; proper ordering of the church is essential to the advance of Christ's kingdom and to covenant faithfulness.
- **Core argument / structure**
 - Definition and marks of the church: visible vs. invisible distinction; preaching of the Word, sacraments rightly administered, and exercise of discipline as the certifying marks.
 - Authority and headship: Christ as sole head and king of the church; human offices act under his delegated authority.
 - Government and ministry: prescription of presbyterial (elder-based) structures - local elders/consistory, ministers, deacons, and assemblies (classis/synod) for mutual oversight.
 - Discipline and public witness: procedures for censure, excommunication, restoration; link between discipline and moral-political order (kingdom implications).
 - Ecclesial relation to state: differentiated roles of church and magistrate, with cooperative aims in promoting godliness while rejecting clerical domination of civil authority.
- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged**
 - Implicit engagement with Calvinist sources (Calvin, Reformed Consistories), polemics against Roman hierarchical claims and against radical Anabaptist ecclesiology; draws on Scripture (e.g., Matthew 16-18; 1 Corinthians 5; Acts) as juridical warrant.
- **Doctrines treated**
 - Ecclesiology, covenant theology (church as covenant community), Christology (Christ's headship and kingship), sacraments (baptism, Lord's Supper), church authority and keys, offices and ordination, discipline, church-state relations.
- **Closing line**
 - The work locates the church squarely within Christ's kingdom: as covenant society governed by delegated elders and sacraments, sustained by discipline, and ordered to witness under Christ the King.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations**
 - Strong integration of **kingdom language** with ordinary church governance: ecclesial order is not merely pragmatic but constitutive of Christ's rule on earth.
 - Emphasizes **presbyterial-synodal processes** (local consistory → classis → synod) as normative for both pastoral care and juridical decisions.
- **Corrections or contrasts**
 - Corrects Roman episcopal paternalism by denying a clerical hierarchy above presbyterial assemblies.
 - Distinct from congregationalism by rejecting sole congregational autonomy; from Lutheran territorialism by insisting on church courts and office-bearers distinct from civil magistracy.
- **Conceptual or terminological innovations**
 - Presents discipline as a **kingdom-safeguard**, not merely moral policing: excommunication functions as a public proclamation about covenant membership in Christ's kingdom.
 - Tightens the language of "marks" to include **discipline explicitly** as coequal with Word and Sacrament for recognizing the true church.
- **Reception and influence**
 - Resonated in Dutch Reformed circles and influenced practical church order in Reformed presbyterial polities; used as a resource for constructing consistorial discipline and regional synodal systems.
- **Enduring value**
 - Serves as a compact primer on Reformed ecclesial identity, especially useful for students of polity, discipline, and the theological relationship between church and kingdom.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Visible church**
 - The concrete congregation(s) where Word, sacraments, and discipline are enacted; contrasted with the invisible (elect) church.
- **Invisible church**
 - The elect known perfectly only to God; theological partner concept to justify discipline within the visible.
- **Marks of the church**
 - **Preaching**, **right administration of the sacraments**, **exercise of discipline** - all required to recognize a true church.
- **Covenant community**
 - The ecclesial self-understanding: membership grounded in covenant ties, familial and juridical obligations.
- **Christ the King / Head**
 - Central Christological term; all governance derives from his rule and keys.
- **Presbyterial polity (elders, consistory, classis, synod)**
 - Ordered system of local and regional oversight; elders as rulers, ministers as preachers/teachers, deacons as servants.
- **Discipline**
 - Public and pastoral procedures for admonition, suspension, excommunication, and restoration; portrayed as safeguarding covenant purity and public testimony.
- **Sacraments (Baptism, Lord's Supper)**
 - Means of grace tied to covenant identity and communal life; rightly administered within covenant community.
- **Keys (binding and loosing)**
 - Ecclesiastical authority to adjudicate membership and moral standing in the kingdom; exercised by church courts under Christ.
- **Church-State distinction**
 - Cooperative but distinct spheres; magistrate supports public order and piety but may not usurp ecclesial governance.
- **Communion of saints**
 - Social and spiritual bond among covenant members, actualized in worship and mutual oversight.
- **Public witness / testimony**
 - The church's external role in society rooted in covenantal fidelity and discipline.
- **Ordination / calling**
 - Ecclesiastical recognition of office-bearers as instituted agents under Christ.

How this work is used across loci:
- As a **definition source** for Reformed ecclesiology (identifying marks and polity).
- As a **polemic** against episcopacy and radical congregationalism, defending presbyterial-synodal order and discipline.
- As an **exegetical-practical model** for applying New Testament texts on discipline, offices, and the keys to concrete church structures.
