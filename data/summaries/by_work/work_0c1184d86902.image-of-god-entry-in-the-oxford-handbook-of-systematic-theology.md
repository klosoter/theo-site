### TITLE
Image of God - Michael S. Horton, entry in The Oxford Handbook of Systematic Theology

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context:** Horton writes from a contemporary Reformed-evangelical vantage within the early 21st century, contributing to a broad ecumenical Oxford Handbook (mid-2000s) that surveys central loci of systematic theology. His piece addresses longstanding debates shaped by modern biblical scholarship, analytic philosophy of personhood, and recent Trinitarian retrievals.
- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem:** The imago Dei should be read Christocentrically and relationally: it is not merely a set of capacities or a biological status but a vocation and pattern for human life rooted in the triune God and restored in Christ.
- **Core argument or structure:**
 - Critique of reductionisms: rejects purely **substantive** accounts (image = rational faculties) and purely **functional** accounts (image = dominion role) as insufficient alone.
 - Positive proposal: image as **relational-ontological + vocational**, grounded in the Trinity and concretized in Christ - which both locates human worth and frames human calling.
 - Anthropology and sin: explains how the image is wounded, disfigured, and distorted by sin (loss of original righteousness and vocational fidelity) but not entirely erased; restoration occurs through union with Christ (recapitulation and redemption).
 - Ecclesial and ethical implications: the church as community reflecting Trinitarian relations, and Christian vocation (stewardship, worship, service) as restoration of the image.
- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged:** engages classical sources (Genesis, Augustine, Calvin), modern and contemporary interlocutors (Barthian and neoâ€‘orthodox themes, relational Trinitarianists, analytic accounts of personhood), and touches on modern philosophers and ethicists when critiquing secular accounts.
- **Doctrines treated:** anthropology, Trinity (as the normative pattern), Christology (Christ as the definitive image), sin and grace (original righteousness, depravity, restoration), ecclesiology (church as locus of restored image), and practical ethics (vocation, stewardship).
- **Closing line capturing theological center of gravity:** The image of God is supremely realized and restored in Christ and is essentially relational and vocational - human dignity is rooted in being created for Trinitarian communion and redeemed into that communion by Christ.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations:**
 - Strong insistence that the imago Dei is primarily **relational** (reflecting Trinitarian life) and **vocational** (task-oriented), not simply a list of intrinsic capacities.
 - Christocentric hermeneutic: Christ is the ontological locus and telos of the image rather than merely its moral exemplar.
- **Corrections or contrasts to predecessors/rivals:**
 - Pushes back against Enlightenment/modernist accounts that privatize personhood (reducing image to autonomy or cognition).
 - Counters some evangelical trends that overemphasize either function (dominion only) or substance (rationality only), arguing both are integrated in a covenantal framework.
 - Dialogues critically with relational Trinitarianists who might underplay sin's disfiguring effect or the necessity of Christ's redemptive work.
- **Conceptual or terminological innovations:**
 - Uses a mediated vocabulary: image as "vocation-in-relation," tying metaphysical status to covenantal calling and ecclesial practice.
- **Reception and influence:**
 - Widely read among Reformed and conservative evangelical scholars as a succinct corrective to atomistic anthropology; influential in classrooms for bringing classic Reformed categories (original righteousness, federal headship) into conversation with modern Trinitarian emphases.
 - Engages ecumenically by appealing to shared concerns about human dignity and sin while preserving confessional distinctives.
- **Enduring value for study and teaching:**
 - Provides a compact, accessible synthesis that links doctrine (Trinity, Christology) to ethics and ecclesiology; useful as a teaching text for connecting biblical exegesis to systematic claims.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Imago Dei (image of God):** central category; Horton's relational-vocational reinterpretation locates it in Trinitarian patterns and Christic restoration.
- **Substantive model:** view that image equals inherent capacities (reason, will); critiqued as incomplete.
- **Functional model:** view that image equals role or task (dominion); accepted but integrated into a broader account.
- **Relational model:** Horton's preferred emphasis - image as constituted by interpersonal relations mirroring the Trinity.
- **Christocentrism / Christ as image:** Christ as the definitive and restorative image, the telos and repairer of human likeness to God.
- **Original righteousness:** pre-fall state of fitting relation to God; its loss explains moral and vocational impairment.
- **Original sin / disfigurement:** doctrine of the image's damage (not total annihilation), necessitating redemptive restoration.
- **Federal headship:** Adam as representative head whose fall affects humanity; Christ as representative head who restores.
- **Vocation / stewardship:** human calling to rule and care for creation as an aspect of the image, reoriented by redemption.
- **Trinitarian anthropology:** anthropology shaped by the doctrine of Trinity - persons in relation as normative for human personhood.
- **Ecclesial embodiment:** church as concrete community where restored image is displayed and cultivated.
- **Dignity and worth:** theological grounding for human value, resisting reductionist secular accounts.
- **Union with Christ:** mechanism of restoration - participation in Christ heals and fulfills the imago.
- **Ethical/communal implications:** public and social dimensions of image-bearing (justice, care, vocation).
- How this work is used across loci:
 - As a **definition source** for a Reformed, Trinitarian account of imago Dei in systematic theology.
 - As a **polemic** against reductionist substantive/functional accounts and secular concepts of personhood.
 - As an **exegetical-model** linking Genesis anthropology to Christology and ecclesiology for classroom discussion and doctrinal synthesis.
