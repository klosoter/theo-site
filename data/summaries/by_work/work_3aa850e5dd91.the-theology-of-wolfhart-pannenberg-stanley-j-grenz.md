### TITLE
The Theology of Wolfhart Pannenberg (Stanley J. Grenz) - concise research notes on Pannenberg's systematic project

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting / publication context**
 - Mid- to late-20th century German systematic theology; postwar theological reconstruction in dialogue with Barth, Bultmann, Hegelian and Kantian philosophy of history; Pannenberg seeks a public, historically accountable theology.
 - Grenz interprets and popularizes Pannenberg for an anglophone readership, emphasizing method and systematic implications.

- **Central thesis / doctrinal problem**
 - Theology must be a critical, historically informed science whose criterion of truth is the eschatological revelation of God in history-most decisively in the resurrection of Jesus. Dogmatic claims require historical and philosophical warrant.

- **Core argument / structure**
 - Epistemology: theology as a rational, public discipline that tests claims against historical evidence and philosophical coherence.
 - Revelation & history: history is the medium of revelation; eschatological events disclose God's self-giving.
 - Christology & resurrection: Jesus' resurrection functions as the decisive criterion and vindication of Christ's identity and the truth of Christian claims.
 - Systematic correlates: doctrine of God, Trinity, anthropology, salvation (including union with Christ and adoption), ecclesiology, and eschatology are reordered around historical eschatology.
 - Practical method: proleptic (anticipatory) eschatology-future consummation grounds present theological claims and ethics.

- **Figures / sources engaged**
 - Barth (revelation in Word), Bultmann (demythologizing and existential interpretation), Hegel (history and rationality), modern historiography and analytic philosophy of history.

- **Doctrines treated**
 - Revelation, Christology, Resurrection, Eschatology, Trinity, Anthropology, Soteriology (including union with Christ and adoption), Ecclesiology, Methodology of theology.

- **Closing line**
 - Pannenberg's theology centers on the claim that the eschatological disclosure of God in historical events-culminating in the resurrection-provides the rational and salvific grounding of Christian doctrine and life.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases**
 - Theology as an empirically responsible, public science; eschatology as the hermeneutical key for all doctrines; resurrection as criterion of theological truth.
- **Corrections / contrasts**
 - Against Barth: grounds revelation more decisively in public history rather than solely in the Word-event; against Bultmann: rejects purely existential re-interpretation detached from historical claims.
 - Against liberal historicism: insists on theological interpretation of history (eschatological orientation) rather than neutral positivism.
- **Conceptual innovations**
 - Proleptic eschatology as methodological principle: future consummation shapes present theology and ethical witness.
 - The resurrection as epistemic criterion-blends historical testimony with theological warrant.
- **Reception and influence**
 - Widely influential in continental and anglophone theology; shaped debates on resurrection, history, and the public character of theology; significant impact on evangelical and ecumenical discourse through interpreters like Grenz.
- **Enduring value**
 - Provides a robust model for integrating historical-historical-critical work with systematic theology; especially useful for doctrines that hinge on historical acts (union with Christ, adoption, justification).

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Revelation as historical disclosure** - God's self-giving disclosed in public history, not only in verbally propositional form.
- **Eschatology / Prolepsis** - future consummation that illuminates present reality; theology reads present as anticipatory of the end.
- **Resurrection as criterion** - the resurrection validates Christology and anchors theological truth-claims.
- **Theological science** - theology conceived as rational, disciplined, publicly accountable inquiry.
- **History as medium of revelation** - history is the arena where divine revelation becomes assessable.
- **Christology (eschatological Christ)** - Jesus understood primarily through his role in eschatological history; his person and work are determinative.
- **Trinity (relational/ontological correlate)** - doctrine located within the framework of historical revelation and eschatological selfâ€‘disclosure.
- **Union with Christ** - treated as historically grounded participation in the eschatological Christ; believer's incorporation is understood in light of the resurrection-event as the normative reality to which believers are joined.
 - Emphasis on historical event (resurrection) as the basis for real communion rather than merely juridical or purely mystical accounts.
- **Adoption** - soteriological status enacted and secured by the eschatological act of God in Christ; adoption is presented as an achieved, future-linked reality realized in the believer by participation in Christ's fate.
 - Adoption is read through Pannenberg's rubric of historical-eschatological incorporation rather than nominal category divorced from history.
- **Salvation / Justification (historicized)** - soteriology tied to concrete historic events and future consummation.
- **Church as eschatological community** - witness and participant in the public unfolding of God's purposes.

- **Uses across loci**
 - As a definitional source and methodological exemplar for grounding doctrines that depend on historical acts (e.g., union with Christ, adoption).
 - As a polemical resource against purely non-historical or existentialist readings of soteriology and revelation.
 - As an exegetical model for readings of New Testament texts that emphasize resurrection-centered Christology and participatory language (union/adoption).
