### TITLE
**Three Essays (1870) - Albrecht Ritschl**

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context**
 - Published in 1870 amid 19th-century German Protestant liberal theology; reaction to Hegelian metaphysics, Schleiermacherian subjectivism, and speculative dogmatics.
 - Ritschl situated theology as a historical, ethical science within the Protestant university milieu; formative for later Ritschlian school and liberal Protestantism.

- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem**
 - Theology must be re-founded on the moral revelation of God in history (the person and work of Jesus) rather than on metaphysical speculation; the chief theological task is to articulate the ethical-religious content and social consequences of Christian faith.

- **Core argument or structure**
 - Theology uses value-judgments grounded in practical religious experience and ecclesial affirmation rather than ontological proofs.
 - Christ is the historical revelation of God's will to establish the moral kingdom; atonement is reconciliation directed at the community and moral order.
 - Dogma functions as normative summaries of the church's practical faith, not as metaphysical descriptions of divine being.
 - Emphasis on the church and its practices as the primary context for revelation and salvation.

- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged**
 - Critical engagement with Kantian epistemology (limits of speculative reason), Schleiermacher (religious feeling), and Hegel (speculative system); dialogue with contemporary historical-critical scholarship.
 - Implicit polemic with traditional Lutheran scholasticism and later direct critique by Karl Barth.

- **Doctrines treated**
 - Revelation (historical and ethical), Christology (ethical-historical revelation), soteriology (reconciliation as moral restoration), ecclesiology (church as locus of revelation and moral community), limited engagement with Trinity and metaphysics (reduced to functional/ethical categories).
 - Eschatology reinterpreted in ethical-historical terms (kingdom of God as moral community to be realized).

- **Closing line**
 - Ritschl centers theology on the historical-moral revelation of God in Christ and the church: doctrine is serviceable to the church's ethical mission, not speculative metaphysics.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations**
 - Theology as a practical science of religious value-judgment; dogma as normative ecclesial statements grounded in the community's experience of God's redeeming will.
 - Atonement reframed from metaphysical substitution to moral reconciliation that restores relationships within the ethical community.

- **Corrections or contrasts to predecessors or rivals**
 - Rejects Schleiermacher's inward feeling as ultimate criterion and Hegelian speculative metaphysics; opposes scholastic accounts that treat dogma as metaphysical truth-claims.
 - Contrasts with penal-substitutional and forensic models by insisting on socially transformative reconciliation.

- **Conceptual or terminological innovations**
 - Use of "value-judgment" as epistemological category for theological claims.
 - Language of "kingdom as moral community" and "revelation as historical act" rather than static propositions about deity.

- **Reception and influence**
 - Major influence on late-19th-century liberal Protestantism, ethical theology, and methodological approaches to dogma.
 - Criticized and largely overturned by 20th-century neo-orthodoxy (Barth) for subordinating divine sovereignty to human ethical categories; still formative for constructive liberal and some social gospel projects.

- **Enduring value**
 - Clarifies how theology can remain historically responsible and ethically engaged; a pedagogical pivot for studies in theological method, ecclesiology, and social ethics.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Value-judgment**
 - Theological assertions rooted in evaluative religious experience and communal affirmation rather than metaphysical proof.
- **Historical revelation**
 - God reveals morally and redemptively in concrete historical acts (primarily in Jesus); history is normative for theology.
- **Moral kingdom / Kingdom of God**
 - The ethical community constituted by God's will; eschatology becomes communal realization rather than individualistic afterlife schema.
 - Relates to Individual and Cosmic Eschatology: Ritschl privileges communal, realized eschatology while allowing for future consummation in history.
- **Reconciliation (Vers√∂hnung)**
 - Social-moral restoration effected by Christ's work; focus on transformation of relations within the community.
- **Atonement - ethical model**
 - Atonement construed as influence and restoration, opposing metaphysical substitution; soteriology prioritizes renewed social life.
- **Church as locus of revelation**
 - The community's life, proclamation, and practices instantiate and judge theological truth.
- **Dogma as norm**
 - Doctrinal formulations are authoritative norms for communal faith and practice, not metaphysical descriptions.
- **Divine condescension / Incarnation**
 - God's self-limitation in Christ to disclose divine will; resonates with themes later discussed under Open Theism and Divine Condescension about divine relationality and historical responsiveness.
- **Prayer as response**
 - Prayer is moral and communal, expressing dependence and fostering ethical conformity rather than manipulating metaphysical causality.
- **Preaching as primary means of grace**
 - Proclamation is central to the church's formative and reconciling work; sacraments retain covenantal sign value without metaphysical channeling.
 - Connects to Means Of Grace: Ritschl privileges preaching and communal practices over sacramental ontologizing.
- **Eschatological realization**
 - Future consummation is foretold but the decisive mark is ongoing ethical realization in history.

- How this work is used across loci:
 - As a **methodological definition source** for theological epistemology (value-judgment, historical normativity).
 - As a **polemic** against metaphysical and forensic readings of atonement and dogma.
 - As an **exegetical/ecclesiological model** for locating revelation in the church's preaching, sacraments (sign-function), and prayerful moral life.
