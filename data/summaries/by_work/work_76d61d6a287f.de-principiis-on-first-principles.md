### TITLE
De Principiis (On First Principles) - Origen of Alexandria (c. 220s CE)

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context:** Early 3rd-century Alexandrian theology; Christian intellectual engagement with Hellenistic (Platonic/ Stoic) philosophy and Jewish exegesis. Origen writes as a systematic primer for Christian teachers, aiming to bring speculative reflection under scriptural authority.
- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem:** To articulate the "first principles" (principia) that ground Christian faith: the nature of God, the Logos and Trinity, creation and the soul, sin and free will, and the goal of salvation - integrating Scripture with rational argument and allegorical exegesis.
- **Core argument / structure:** 
 - Distinction of disciplines: what may be treated as doctrine, opinion, and scriptural allegory.
 - Theology of God and the Trinity: Father as monarch, Son (Logos) begotten eternally as mediator, Spirit proceeding but ontologically ordered beneath Father.
 - Cosmology and anthropology: created order of rational beings; souls preexist and are linked to moral states; material world as corrective pedagogy.
 - Soteriology and eschatology: moral purification through knowledge and free choice; eventual restoration (apokatastasis) as universal corrective.
- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged:** Scripture (extensive exegetical appeal), Clement of Alexandria, Platonic thought (esp. notions of preexistence and hierarchy), adversaries including literalist interpreters and philosophers skeptical of Christian claims.
- **Doctrines treated:** Revelation and hermeneutics (allegory vs literalism), Trinity and Logos-Christology (ordered/subordinationist terms), preexistence of souls, creation (cosmic and anthropic), free will and moral causality, theodicy (evil as privation and remedial), angelology/demonology, apokatastasis (universal restoration).
- **Closing line:** De Principiis aims to discipline Christian speculative theology by anchoring metaphysical speculation to scriptural exegesis, proposing a moral-cosmic economy in which the Logos mediates creation and the progressive restoration of rational beings.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations:**
 - Systematic use of allegorical exegesis as normative for speculative doctrine.
 - Integration of Platonic-style metaphysics (preexistence, hierarchy) with biblical themes of fall and redemption.
 - Emphasis on moral causality: sin as misuse of freedom remedied by knowledge and divine pedagogy.
- **Corrections or contrasts to predecessors or rivals:**
 - Rejects literalist genealogies and simplistic corporeal readings of Scripture.
 - Contrasts with traducianism by denying transmission of the soul through parents; affirms individual creation/preexistence of souls.
 - Adopts a monarchy of the Father model that departs from later Nicene equality language, placing Son and Spirit in ordered relation to Father.
- **Conceptual or terminological innovations:**
 - Treating theology as ordered "principia" with tiers (grounded belief, permissible opinion, and figurative interpretation).
 - Combining "preexistence of souls" with a corrective pedagogical purpose for embodiment.
- **Reception and influence in Reformed and wider theology:**
 - Widely read in patristic scholarship; shaped Alexandrian method and later mysticism.
 - Many doctrines (apokatastasis, preexistence, subordinationist trinitarian language) were controversial - later branded "Origenism" and largely rejected by mainstream postâ€‘Nicene orthodoxy; condemned elements in 6th-century controversies.
 - Reformed tradition generally values Origen's exegetical boldness but rejects his preexistence and universalism; his allegorical method influenced exegetical approaches but was critiqued by Reformers (e.g., Calvin).
- **Enduring value for study and teaching:**
 - Model of how to bring philosophical tools to bear on Scripture.
 - A test-case in limits of allegory and speculative theology.
 - Provocative resource for discussions on freedom, theodicy, and the anthropology of the soul.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Logos (Word):** Eternal divine reason/agent; mediator of creation and revelation; Son as Logos.
- **Monarchy of the Father:** The Father as ultimate principle; Son and Spirit eternally ordered to the Father (subordinationist language).
- **Hypostasis / Subsistence:** Early deployment to discuss persons of the Trinity; used in an ordered (not yet Nicene-equalizing) sense.
- **Preexistence of souls:** Souls exist prior to embodiment; their moral history influences embodied fate.
 - Contrasts with traducianism (rejected) and with simple creation-at-birth theories.
- **Creationism (soul origin):** Origen affirms individual souls are created by God (creationist stance), not propagated by parents.
 - *Site reference:* Traducianism vs Creationism - Origen explicitly argues against traducianism, defending creation (with preexistence).
- **Apokatastasis (universal restoration):** Final reconciliation of all rational beings to God; controversial and later condemned in orthodox councils.
- **Allegorical hermeneutic:** Scriptural sense beyond the literal; primary method for resolving difficult texts and deriving doctrine.
- **Free will / moral causality:** Central to explanation of sin and recovery; souls choose ignorance or knowledge.
- **Theodicy / evil as privation:** Evil is lack/deprivation, remedied by corrective divine pedagogy.
- **Angelology / hierarchy:** Ordered ranks of rational creatures; angels, demons, souls occupy moral-ontological places.
- **Salvation as theosis (deification):** Union with God through assimilation to Logos; restoration of rational beings.
- **Doctrine vs Opinion:** Methodological distinction Origen makes to delimit speculative theology from settled dogma.
- **Cosmic pedagogy:** Creation and history as educational means by which God trains souls toward restoration.

How this work is used across loci:
- As a primary source for early Christian **creationist** accounts of the soul and explicit polemic against **traducianism**.
- As a model for **allegorical exegesis** and a resource in debates on Christology, Trinity, and soteriology (often deployed polemically or critically by later theologians).
