### TITLE
Divine Will and Human Choice: Freedom, Contingency, and Necessity in Early Modern Reformed Thought - Richard A. Muller

### OUTLINE NOTES
- **Setting or publication context:** Early modern (16th-18th c.) Reformed scholasticism and post-Reformation confessional theology; modern publication collects historical-theological essays reconstructing Reformed treatments of will, necessity, contingency, and providence using primary Latin sources. A corrective to twentieth-century caricatures of Reformed determinism.
- **Central thesis or doctrinal problem:** Reformed theology develops a coherent compatibilist account of divine sovereignty and creaturely responsibility that resists both simplistic deterministic readings and later innovations (Molinism, Open Theism). The task: show how early modern Reformed thinkers handled divine foreknowledge, providence, and human freedom without incoherence.
- **Core argument / structure:**
 - Historicize Reformed categories (decree, providence, concurrence, contingency) and recover technical language from scholastic sources.
 - Demonstrate that many Reformed authors adopt a nuanced compatibilism: divine causation and secondary causality coexist; contingency is real but not random; necessity is typified (logical/metaphysical distinctions).
 - Compare Reformed treatments with Catholic Molinism (middle knowledge) and modern alternatives (Open Theism), arguing these are external frameworks that misread the historic Reformed locus.
- **Figures, sources, interlocutors engaged:**
 - Reformed: Calvin, Beza, Cocceius, Voetius, Turretin, Amyraut and other scholastics in the Reformed academies.
 - Catholic/Jesuit interlocutors: Luis de Molina, Francisco Su√°rez.
 - Modern interlocutors: proponents of Molinism, Open Theism, and contemporary analytic accounts of foreknowledge and providence.
- **Doctrines treated:** Divine will and decree, providence and concurrence, predestination, human freedom (compatibilist vs libertarian), contingency and necessity, revelation and covenantal implications for divine agency.
- **Closing line:** Muller situates Reformed theology as a historically specific, intellectually disciplined compatibilist tradition that secures divine sovereignty and genuine creaturely responsibility without recourse to middle knowledge or conceding the conditionality proposed by Open Theism.

### DISTINCTIVES
- **Unique emphases or formulations:**
 - Strong emphasis on recovering technical scholastic categories (concurrence, physical premotion, efficacious/preterition distinctions) rather than imposing modern analytic vocabulary.
 - Attention to semantic nuance: different senses of necessity (logical, metaphysical, modal, moral) and contingency used by Reformed theologians.
- **Corrections or contrasts to predecessors or rivals:**
 - Corrects historiographical tendencies that label Reformed doctrine as either naive determinism or proto-Molinism.
 - Contrasts Reformed compatibilism with Molina's scientia media (middle knowledge) - showing Molina solves a different problem with different assumptions.
 - Challenges Open Theism's reading of divine condescension and temporality by showing classical Reformed accounts preserve exhaustive divine knowledge and providential governance while affirming creaturely contingency.
- **Conceptual or terminological innovations:**
 - Methodological insistence on historical-linguistic retrieval: reading early modern texts on their own terms before applying modern analytic categories.
 - Clarifies "divine will" as multilayered: antecedent/mediate/secret will distinctions in Reformed usage.
- **Reception and influence:**
 - Widely influential among historians of doctrine and confessional Reformed theologians for rehabilitating scholastic resources.
 - Used by systematic theologians to resist both Molinist and Open Theist reconstructions of classical theism.
- **Enduring value:**
 - Model for historically informed doctrinal retrieval; supplies primary-source groundwork for constructive debates on foreknowledge, providence, and freedom.

### KEY TERMS & USES
- **Divine decree (decretum):** God's eternal plan ordering all things; distinguished from the execution of that plan in time.
- **Providence (providentia) & concurrence:** God sustains and concurs in secondary causes; concurrence is not mere foreknowledge but active sustaining causation.
- **Foreknowledge (scientia):** Exhaustive divine knowledge of future contingent events; Reformed authors defend compatibility of foreknowledge with human responsibility.
 - Related: **problem of evil** - Muller treats how providence and foreknowledge interact with theodicy in Reformed texts.
- **Compatibilism:** View that divine determinative causation and human moral responsibility are compatible; central to Muller's reading of the tradition.
- **Contingency vs Necessity:** Reformed distinctions between contingent events (not logically necessary) and various types of necessity (logical, metaphysical, providential).
- **Middle Knowledge (scientia media):** Molina's doctrine of God knowing counterfactuals of creaturely freedom; Muller contrasts this with Reformed presuppositions and rejects its historical equivalence to Reformed positions.
- **Libertarian freedom:** The alternative view (incompatibilist) often advanced by Arminians and moderns; Muller's recovered Reformed stance typically does not align with libertarianism.
- **Divine condescension:** The doctrine that God accommodates his revelation and action to creaturely capacities; discussed in relation to Open Theism's claims about divine temporality and self-limitation.
- **Secondary causality:** The genuine causal agency of creatures under God's primary causation.
- **Efficacious will vs antecedent/mediate will:** Distinctions used by Reformed authors to explain God's willing of ends and means, and the reality of human fallback.
- **Predestination & preterition:** How election and reprobation are articulated within sovereign decree.
- **Physical premotion / causal influence:** Scholastic terms for how divine causation orders secondary causes (treated cautiously by Muller when tracing Reformed usage).
- **Concursus ordinans & concursus cooperans:** Terms describing God's ordering concurrence and cooperating concurrence with creatures' actions.

How this work is used across loci:
- As a **historical-definition source** for Reformed categories of will, decree, and concurrence.
- As a **polemic resource** in debates with proponents of Middle Knowledge/Molinism and with Open Theism, showing historical alternatives and constraints.
- As an **exegetical and methodological model** for retrieving doctrinal concepts from primary early modern sources rather than importing modern analytic terms.
