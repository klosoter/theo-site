### TITLE
Christ's headship over the church expressed in jure divino presbyterian polity against Tudor episcopacy.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Elizabethan Settlement, Cambridge humanism, and the Admonition Controversy** frame Cartwright's Puritan ecclesiology amid **Tudor royal supremacy** and post-1559 **Anglican episcopacy**.
- Method: **biblical primacy shaped by Ramist logic**, scholastic disputation, and pastoral exegesis, pressing **sola Scriptura** toward **jus divinum** claims in **church polity**.
- Key works: **Second Admonition to the Parliament** 1572, **A Replye to an Answere made of M. Doctor Whitgifte against the Admonition** 1573, **The Second Replie of Thomas Cartwright** 1575, and the presbyterian **Book of Discipline** c. 1587.
- Central terms: **presbyterian parity**, **consistory and classis**, **synodical government**, **discipline as mark of the church**, **regulative principle of worship**, **adiaphora** contested by **ius divinum**.
- Main interlocutors: **John Whitgift** and later **Richard Hooker**, with formative influence from **John Calvin**, **Theodore Beza**, and **Martin Bucer**.
- Reformed reception cue: Bridge from **Genevan presbyterianism** to the English **Presbyterian movement**, prefiguring the **Westminster Assembly** and its **Directory for Publick Worship**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Context, formation, and method
Thomas Cartwright, born in 1535 and dying in 1603, matured within the conflicted world of the **Elizabethan Settlement** and the deep intellectual ferment of **Cambridge humanism**. As a rising scholar at **Cambridge**, eventually **Lady Margaret Professor of Divinity** in 1569, he absorbed and redeployed the exegetical priorities of **John Calvin** and **Martin Bucer** alongside the logical economy of **Ramist method**. His lectures on the **Acts of the Apostles** in 1570 pressed the question whether **New Testament polity** binds the church by **ius divinum**. Precisely here his method sharpened: a biblicist deployment of **sola Scriptura**, ordered by **Ramist dichotomies** and scholastic syllogisms, yielded concrete claims concerning **presbyterian parity**, **eldership**, and **synodical authority**. In the climate shaped by the **Vestiarian Controversy**, by **royal supremacy**, and by lingering **ceremonialism**, Cartwright’s ecclesiology was both reforming and polemical, oriented toward **discipline as a mark of the church** and toward a reconstituted **regulative principle of worship** that would purge what he called remnants of **papal error** in the **Book of Common Prayer**.

Removed from his Cambridge chair by **John Whitgift** in 1570 and deprived of his fellowship in 1571, Cartwright entered exile, encountering the **Genevan consistory** and continental **Reformed synods**. This apprenticeship to **Geneva’s discipline** fixed his conviction that the church’s ministry and courts are not matters of **adiaphora** but of **Christ’s headship** legislated in Scripture. The same conviction would animate his lifelong disputations, from his involvement in the **Admonition Controversy** to the later organization of English **classes** under an emergent **Book of Discipline**.

#### 2. The Admonition Controversy and the turn to jure divino polity
The crisis of 1572 crystallized these convictions. The **First Admonition to the Parliament** appeared that year from **John Field** and **Thomas Wilcox**; the **Second Admonition to the Parliament** 1572, widely associated with **Thomas Cartwright**, articulated a more elaborate vision for **presbyterian governance** and **reformed worship**. Against this volley, **Whitgift** fired back with his **Answer** 1572, defending **episcopacy**, the **Prayer Book**, and the crown’s **supremacy** by appeal to **antiquity**, **reason**, and the category of **things indifferent**. Cartwright responded with the programmatic **A Replye to an Answere made of M. Doctor Whitgifte** 1573, and then a more extensive **Second Replie** 1575, prosecuting the case that **apostolic polity** is of **ius divinum** and so obligatorily normative for all churches.

Two lines of argument dominate Cartwright’s **Replye** corpus. First, on the basis of **Acts 14:23**, **1 Timothy 5:17**, and **Ephesians 4:11**, he insists that **elders** and **ministers** exist in parity within local **consistories**, that **ruling elders** are a distinct office, and that broader **classes and synods** exercise binding **discipline** within the rule of Christ. Second, he tightens the **regulative principle of worship**. Ceremonies like **surplice**, **kneeling at communion**, and set forms in the **Book of Common Prayer** are not indifferent, he contends, because Scripture neither commands them nor indemnifies their **scandal**. Where **Whitgift** pleads **adiaphora** and the **prudentia** of governors, Cartwright asserts **Christ’s immediate sovereignty over the church** as revealed in Scripture, so that positive apostolic prescription, not civil ordinance, delineates ministerial **jurisdiction** and **rites**.

#### 3. Positive blueprint: offices, courts, worship, and discipline
If the **Replye** literature is largely polemical, it is also constructive. Cartwright’s ecclesial blueprint features **presbyterian parity** of pastors, **ruling elders** devoted to oversight, and **deacons** tasked with merciful administration. The local **consistory** governs worship and discipline; graded **synods** adjudicate appeals, preserve **catholic consent**, and guard doctrine. In a move indebted to **Calvin’s three marks**, he repeatedly identifies **discipline as the third mark of the church** alongside **Word** and **Sacraments**, the sinew that binds the body to its Head. Here the clarity of **Ramism** serves him: he organizes Scripture’s witness into binary contrasts, pressing the incompatibility of **diocesan episcopacy** with **apostolic parity**, and of **civil coercion** with **spiritual jurisdiction**.

In worship, Cartwright rearticulates the **regulative principle**. Only what **Scripture commands** for public worship may be imposed; everything else is to be weighed by **edification** and **order**. He targets the **Book of Common Prayer** as a vestige of **Romish liturgy**, arguing that **set forms** must not quench **preaching**, the central means of grace. He commends **lectio continua** preaching patterned on **Geneva**, a sober and frequent **communion**, and the eradication of **ceremonial vestiges** that he believes obscure **gospel simplicity**. The practical counterpart to this is the construction of **Articles for Discipline**, coalescing in the English **Book of Discipline** c. 1587, which codifies classes, election of elders, and synodical procedures for excommunication and restoration.

#### 4. Church and magistrate: a contested cooperation
Cartwright’s political ecclesiology is forged in appeals to **Parliament** as much as to **presbyteries**. The **Second Admonition** and later **supplications** request that Parliament abolish **episcopacy** and establish **presbyterian government**. Yet he is not **Erastian**. He distinguishes the **keys of the kingdom** as an essentially **spiritual power** lodged with **elders and synods**, while simultaneously maintaining the **magistrate’s duty** to promote the true religion, suppress **idolatry**, and assist the church’s peace in accord with **Deuteronomic** and **Pauline** warrants. The civil sword protects and orders; the ecclesial keys bind and loose. The English **classes** that emerged in the 1580s, influenced by Cartwright’s counsel, embodied this vision: semi-clandestine **presbyteries** operating within a hostile or divided establishment, with hopes for eventual legal establishment by **Parliament**.

This mixed posture created tension with the **royal supremacy** claimed by the Tudors. The **High Commission** and bishops regarded the presbyterian assertion of independent **jurisdiction** as a challenge to the crown. Cartwright, in turn, viewed **royal supremacy** as a civil headship limited to temporal affairs, incompatible with **Christ’s unique headship** over the church. The resulting impasse shaped late-Elizabethan religion, pitting a **jure divino** ecclesiology against a **magisterial, adiaphorist episcopacy**.

#### 5. Career turns: continent, captivity, and pastoral labor
Cartwright’s theoretical commitments were tested in itinerant service. After Cambridge and Geneva, he ministered among the English at **Antwerp** c. 1573 and in **Middelburg** in Zeeland, where **Reformed synods** modeled the **consistory** and **classis** he defended. In 1585 he accompanied the **Earl of Leicester** as chaplain to the English expedition in the **Netherlands**, moving within a theater where **Reformed church order** and **magistracy** cooperated amid war. Returning to England, he served at **Warwick**, became Master of the **Hospital of the Earl of Leicester**, and gave himself to catechesis, preaching, and the nurture of **classes** under surveillance.

His most painful chapter came with the **Martin Marprelate** controversy in 1588–89. Though the satirical tracts were not his work, Cartwright’s circle fell under suspicion. Arrested in 1590 by the **High Commission**, he endured imprisonment and examination until 1592. These trials did not alter his ecclesiology. He continued to mentor ministers, draft and refine **discipline articles**, and preach until his death in 1603. The pastoral consistency of these years shows that his **presbyterian polity**, **regulative worship**, and **discipline** were no mere academic scheme but a lived **Puritan** program.

#### 6. Scripture, tradition, and the structure of his reasoning
Cartwright’s method deserves attention. He organizes exegesis with **Ramist** clarity, yet he is not a mere proof-texter. He deploys the **fathers** and **canon law** critically, but subordinates them to **Scripture’s sufficiency**. His hermeneutic locates **ecclesial form** within the **apostolic pattern**: **Acts** supplies the narrative grammar of **eldership** and **synod**, the **Pastoral Epistles** prescribe offices and qualifications, and **Matthew 16** and **18** delineate the **keys** and **discipline**. From these loci he states his **ius divinum** thesis: Christ instituted a determinate polity by his apostles, which binds all ages. The theological premise is **Christ’s headship** operative through the ministry he ordained, which cannot be overwritten by **civil law** or **custom**.

Against the **adiaphorist** claim of **Whitgift**, Cartwright argues that what touches the essence of **worship** and **government** is not indifferent. He grants usage of **circumstances** according to **Christian prudence** in things common to human societies, time and place, but he refuses to class **offices**, **courts**, or **sacramental rites** as mere circumstances. His dialectical exactness also fuels his critique of **episcopacy**: he draws a categorical line between **presbyter-bishop** in the New Testament and the post-apostolic **diocesan bishop**, charging the latter with **canonical accretion** lacking scriptural warrant. Though relying on **Calvin** and **Beza**, he presses beyond them in rigidity at times, recasting **Genevan discipline** into a more comprehensive **normative system** for national church life.

#### 7. Interlocutors: Whitgift and Hooker as Anglican counterpoint
The running debate with **John Whitgift** frames Cartwright’s thought. **Whitgift’s Answer** 1572 and **Defense** 1574 anchor **Anglican episcopacy** in three cords: the lawful authority of the **crown**, the witness of **antiquity**, and the flexibility of **adiaphora** guided by **reason**. For Whitgift, Scripture provides sufficient doctrine of salvation and general rules, but not a fixed **institutional blueprint**; therefore episcopacy, liturgy, and ceremonies can be arranged by **prudent governors** for the peace of the church. Cartwright counters that such latitude surrenders **Christ’s positive legislation** of his church, making human ordinance co-legislator in **worship** and **government**. The question is not mere efficiency but **obedience**.

Later, **Richard Hooker’s Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity** 1593–1600 refined Whitgift’s position, articulating a robust account of **reason**, **law**, and **custom** in the church. Hooker criticizes presbyterian **jure divino** as a category error, confusing the **accidentia** of the early church for its **essentia**, and presents **episcopacy** as an ancient and fitting form consonant with Scripture. While Cartwright did not engage Hooker in formal print as **Walter Travers** did, Hooker’s polemic stands as the mature response to Cartwright’s school: the Bible does not specify **one necessary polity**, and to make discipline a **mark of the church** overreaches. The deep contrast concerns the material sufficiency of **Scripture** in **ecclesiology** and the norming role of **apostolic practice**: Cartwright sees binding **pattern**, Hooker permissive **example**. This clash would set the terms for Anglican–Presbyterian debate in the next century.

#### 8. Legacy: from English classes to Westminster
Cartwright’s legacy is disproportionate to the small number of printed volumes bearing his name. As architect and champion of **presbyterian polity**, he seeded networks of **classes** across England by the late 1580s, trained ministers in **discipline**, and drafted the **Book of Discipline** that circulated semi-clandestinely. His stress on **discipline as a mark of the church** and on **regulative worship** persist into the **Westminster Assembly** 1643–1649, where the **Form of Presbyterial Church-Government** and the **Directory for Publick Worship** encode presbyterian **parity**, **eldership**, and liturgical **simplicity**. The Assembly’s doctrine of the **power of the keys** and **synodical authority** resonates with Cartwright’s earlier theses, albeit within a broader **federal theology** and a more exact scholastic system.

Influence also runs through Cambridge. The pastoral and homiletical program of **William Perkins** and the casuistry of **William Ames** inhabit the same **Puritan** ecology of **Word-centered ministry**, **discipline**, and **piety**, though their center of gravity shifts from polity to **practical divinity**. In Scotland, the **Second Book of Discipline** 1578 converges with Cartwright’s vision, though by independent development through **Andrew Melville**. Later English presbyterians such as **Thomas Goodwin** and **Samuel Rutherford** revisit Cartwright’s coordinates of **Christ’s headship**, **Scripture’s sufficiency**, and **spiritual jurisdiction**, while differing over the reach of the **magistrate** and the articulation of **covenant**. In Anglican memory, by contrast, Cartwright appears as the foil to **Hooker**, the emblem of presbyterian **rigor** and **biblicism**. Either way, his impress upon the **ecclesiology** of the British Reformations remains clear.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and WTS vantage, Cartwright’s principal strengths are luminous. His insistence on **Christ’s headship over the church**, his defense of **Scripture’s sufficiency** for **worship** and **government**, his recovery of **discipline as a mark of the church**, and his articulation of **presbyterian parity** with graded **synods** cohere with the grain of **Reformed catholicity**. They anticipate the Westminster teaching that **church censures** are spiritual, that **synods and councils** serve for edification, and that **worship** is to be ordered by **divine institution**. Yet tensions appear. His **jure divino** posture sometimes collapses the distinction between Scriptural **precept**, **apostolic example**, and **prudential circumstance**, risking a narrowed catholicity and an under-theologized account of genuine **adiaphora**. His appropriation of **magistracy** leans toward a theocratic deployment of the **first table**, which can blur the Reformed twofold kingdom’s distinction between **spiritual** and **civil** administrations, even as he rejects **Erastianism**. A constructive alternative within Reformed orthodoxy would retain **presbyterian polity** as best consonant with **Scripture** and **Reformed practice**, uphold the **regulative principle** for essentials of worship, and affirm a principled **two-kingdoms** or **sphere sovereignty** distinction that protects the **spirituality of the church** while welcoming civil support without jurisdictional confusion. In sum, **Thomas Cartwright** bequeaths an enduring ecclesiological conscience for Reformed churches, one that must be received with gratitude and calibrated by broader **catholic** and **federal** commitments.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Elizabethan Settlement** and **Cambridge** formed Cartwright amid the **Vestiarian** and **Admonition** controversies under **Tudor royal supremacy**.
- Core problem: asserting **Christ’s headship** and **Scripture’s sufficiency** in **ecclesiology**, against **episcopacy**, **adiaphora**, and **Prayer Book** ceremonialism.
- Major works: **Second Admonition to the Parliament** 1572, **Replye** 1573, **Second Replie** 1575, and the presbyterian **Book of Discipline** c. 1587; all advanced **presbyterian polity** and **discipline**.
- Defining tools: **presbyterian parity**, **consistory-classis-synod**, **discipline as a mark**, **regulative principle of worship**, and **jure divino** claims for **church government**.
- Key interlocutors: **John Whitgift** and later **Richard Hooker**, with positive influence from **Calvin**, **Beza**, and **Bucer** on **polity** and **worship**.
- Reformed critique: affirm **Scripture’s normativity** and **spiritual jurisdiction**, but temper **jure divino** rigidity and clarify **magistracy** via **two kingdoms** to avoid **Erastian** or theocratic confusion.
- Legacy: catalyzed English **classes**, shaped **Presbyterianism**, and prefigured the **Westminster Assembly’s** **Directory** and **Form of Government**, while remaining Anglicanism’s defining foil on **polity**.
