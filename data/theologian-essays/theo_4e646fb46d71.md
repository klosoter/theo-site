### TITLE
Logic on fire: authoritative expository preaching as the ordinary means of revival under sovereign grace

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Early twentieth-century Britain**, **Welsh Nonconformity**, **interwar liberal Protestantism**, and **postwar neo-orthodoxy** frame his ministry from **Sandfields 1927** to **Westminster Chapel 1939–1968**.
- **Method of expository kerygma** called **logic on fire**, grounded in **Scripture’s self-authenticating authority** and the **Spirit’s testimonium internum**, skeptical of **natural theology** and **dialogical ecumenism**.
- Key works: **Studies in the Sermon on the Mount 1959–1960**, **Spiritual Depression 1965**, **Preaching and Preachers 1971**, **Great Doctrines of the Bible 1971–1973**, **Romans sermons 1955–1968 published 1985–1998**, **Joy Unspeakable 1984**, **The Sovereign Spirit 1984**, **Revival 1987**, **The Puritans 1987**.
- Central terms: **authority of Scripture**, **regeneration and assurance**, **baptism with the Spirit**, **revival not revivalism**, **penal substitution and propitiation**, **experimental Calvinism**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **John Calvin** and the **Puritans** including **John Owen**, **Jonathan Edwards**, and **George Whitefield**; debates with **Karl Barth** and **neo-orthodoxy**; evangelical disputes with **John Stott** and **J. I. Packer**; affinities with **B. B. Warfield** and **J. Gresham Machen**; resonance with **Cornelius Van Til** on **antithesis**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong **doctrine of God’s sovereignty** and **preaching of the Word as means of grace**, debated **pneumatology** on **Spirit baptism** and a lean **sacramentology** within a **Westminster Standards** frame.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
The life of **D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones 1899–1981** unfolded within the pressured modernities of **interwar Britain**, the **Welsh Calvinistic Methodist** legacy, and the collapse of **liberal Protestant** optimism. Trained at **St Bartholomew’s Hospital** under **Sir Thomas Horder**, he left a distinguished medical trajectory in 1927 to pastor **Sandfields, Aberavon**, before moving to **Westminster Chapel** in 1939. The biographer **Iain Murray** narrates this vocational rupture as a theological protest against **modernism** and a retrieval of **Reformed orthodoxy**. In London during the **Second World War** and ensuing reconstruction, Lloyd-Jones confronted **Anglican establishment**, **ecumenical diplomacy**, and the rise of **neo-orthodoxy**, judging them insufficiently anchored in **Scripture’s propositional revelation**. His program was simple and radical: the **authoritative exposition of the Word**, preached with **unction** under the **sovereign Spirit**, as the engine of **church renewal**. The method was encapsulated in his signature phrase **logic on fire**, a pairing of doctrinal **precision** with pneumatic **power**, framed by the conviction that **truth** must both convince the mind and arrest the conscience.

As a pastor-theologian steeped in **Puritan divinity**, he treated historical retrieval as theological method. The **Puritans**, **Edwards**, and **Whitefield** provided models of **experimental Calvinism**, where **doctrine** serves **experience** and **holiness**. He also drew on **Machen’s Christianity and Liberalism** for the antithetical stance of **evangelical orthodoxy** over against **theological liberalism**. Though not a technical apologist, his suspicion of **evidentialism** and reliance on **Scripture’s self-attestation** placed him near a **Van Tillian antithesis**, championing **revelation** over **autonomous reason**. At the same time his medical apprenticeship shaped a **diagnostic homiletic**, treating the pulpit as clinic and **sin** as both guilt and bondage, so that **Christ’s atonement** and **regeneration** operate as radical therapies rather than incremental improvements.

#### 2. Scripture, proclamation, and ecclesial stance
Lloyd-Jones’s doctrine of **Scripture** underwrote everything. In addresses gathered in **Authority 1958** and expounded across **Preaching and Preachers 1971**, he maintained the **verbal inspiration** and **inerrancy** of the Bible, insisting on **Scripture’s clarity** to the regenerate and the **Spirit’s testimonium internum** as the epistemic ground of assurance. Against **Karl Barth** and **neo-orthodoxy**, he rejected the separation of **Word-event** from **written Word**, preferring the older **propositional revelation** in which the **text** stands as God’s speech *ad extra*. Hence his reduction of church reform to the restoration of **expository preaching**, an ordinary means superior to techniques of **revivalism** or **liturgical novelty**. For Lloyd-Jones, **kerygma** was not bare announcement but **exegesis** harnessed to **doctrinal system**, so that series on **Ephesians** and **Romans** became a school in **dogmatics**.

His ecclesial posture followed from this bibliology. The 1966 address urging **evangelicals to separate** from **mixed denominations** rejected **ecumenical comprehensiveness** as a denial of **gospel clarity**. The confrontation with **John Stott** at the National Assembly of Evangelicals exposed fault lines over **church unity**, **ordained comprehensiveness**, and **mission strategy**. Lloyd-Jones chose **purity of confession** over **institutional breadth**, encouraging the **British Evangelical Council** as an alternative to the **World Council of Churches** and the **British Council of Churches**. The aim was not sectarian retreat but the creation of a platform where **evangelical doctrine** and **discipline** could flourish under **Word and Spirit**.

#### 3. Doctrinal center: God, Christ, cross, and salvation
Although he never composed a scholastic **systematics**, the sermons and lectures collated in **Great Doctrines of the Bible 1971–1973** display a classic **Augustinian-Reformed** core. On **the doctrine of God**, he affirmed **divine sovereignty**, **immutability**, and **holiness**, stressing **divine simplicity** in practical terms by refusing to let any divine attribute eclipse **holiness** or **love**. On **Christology**, he held a high **incarnation**, defended the **virgin birth**, opposed **kenoticism**, and preached the **two natures** with the communicatio described doxologically rather than scholastically. Most characteristic was his preaching of the **atonement** as **penal substitution** and **propitiation**, often expounding **Romans 3.25** to insist that the cross turned away **wrath** by satisfying **justice**. He pressed **justification by faith alone** as the believer’s external righteousness in **Christ extra nos**, warning against confounding **sanctification** with **acceptance** before God.

His **ordo salutis** was emphatically experiential. He stressed **total depravity** and **effectual calling**, with **regeneration** as sovereign act producing **repentance** and **faith**. Faith was analyzed as **knowledge**, **assent**, and **trust**, yet anchored in the **objectivity** of **Christ’s person and work**. He often explored **assurance** through a triad: resting on **promises**, observing **fruits**, and receiving the **direct witness of the Spirit**. Here his **experimental Calvinism** leaned toward a robust account of the **Spirit’s testimony** as an immediate sealing that elevates assurance from deduction to **consolation**.

#### 4. Pneumatology, assurance, and the baptism with the Spirit
No theme marks Lloyd-Jones more than his insistence on the **baptism with the Holy Spirit** as a distinct enduement of power. In **Joy Unspeakable 1984** and **The Sovereign Spirit 1984**, he argued that the **baptism with the Spirit** in Acts is not reducible to **regeneration** nor identical with **water baptism**. It is a post-conversion visitation that grounds heightened **assurance**, emboldens **witness**, and awakens **joy unspeakable and full of glory**. He disavowed **Pentecostal second-blessing perfectionism**, rejected making **tongues** normative, and cautioned against **techniques** that mimic **revival**. Yet he insisted that this **sealing** of **Ephesians 1.13** could and should be sought, not as a sacramental crisis but as **sovereign donation**. The result is a distinctive **pneumatology** that keeps **Calvinist monergism**, honors **ordinary means**, and nevertheless strains against a purely **cessationist** closure.

This account of the **Spirit** synchronized with his doctrine of **revival** in **Revival 1987**. Revival is the **sovereign intensification** of ordinary grace, a divine visitation that magnifies **preaching**, convicts of **sin**, and multiplies **conversions**. Against **revivalism**, he repudiated human management and urged **prayer** to the **Lord of the harvest**. Pastors were to labor faithfully, trusting the **Spirit’s freedom** rather than orchestrating **atmosphere**. Thus his famous exhortation to seek **unction** was not spiritual technology but a posture of **dependence** on **God’s sovereignty**.

#### 5. Bible, exegesis, and pastoral theology
Lloyd-Jones’s commentary-like sermon series treat **exegesis** as theological plumbing of revelation. The **Romans** sermons preached 1955 to 1968, published posthumously by **Banner of Truth** between 1985 and 1998, demonstrate his method. Rather than privileging **biblical theology** over **systematics** or vice versa, he allowed **text** to drive **dogmatic loci** in pastoral sequence. In **Studies in the Sermon on the Mount 1959–1960**, the **Beatitudes** became a ladder of **grace**, reshaping the **ethics** of the regenerate community. In **Spiritual Depression 1965**, he performed diagnostic pastoral care, treating despondency under headings like **false guilt**, **failure to grasp justification**, and **Satanic accusation**. The recurring grammar is **indicative** before **imperative**, **union with Christ** before **mortification**, **promise** before **command**.

His view of **preaching** in **Preaching and Preachers 1971** elevated the pulpit as a unique **means of grace**. He resisted **dialogue sermons**, **drama**, and **sociological analysis** as substitutes for **exposition**. Delivery was to be **lively** but not theatrical, **authoritative** without domineering, and above all **God-centered**. The measure of a sermon was whether **God had come**. To that end he prized **unction**, a term he filled with **Spirit-given liberty**, clarity, and authority. The pulpit for him was the ordinary point where **divine sovereignty** meets **human need** under **Word and Spirit**.

#### 6. Church, sacraments, and public theology
Lloyd-Jones remained a **Nonconformist** with **Congregationalist** commitments, though shaped by **Reformed confessions**. He practiced **paedobaptism** and affirmed two **sacraments** as institutions of **Christ**, yet his **sacramentology** was modest compared to his robust **homiletics**. The **Lord’s Supper** fed assurance by directing faith to **Christ crucified**, but he resisted **sacramental sacerdotalism** and any suggestion that grace could be dispensed through **clerical technique**. Ecclesial government was to aim at **discipline** and **holy fellowship**, with the **pastorate** charged above all with **preaching** and **prayer**.

In public theology he distrusted **politicized church programs**, declined **Kuyperian** projects of cultural transformation as strategic center, and instead located cultural hope in **revival** and **personal godliness**. He acknowledged **common grace** as restraint of evil and ground for civic goods, but exalted **special grace** as the decisive remedy for the **moral crisis** of modernity. This did not entail pietistic withdrawal. His preaching addressed **marriage**, **work**, **authority**, and **neighbor-love**, but always as entailments of **union with Christ** rather than autonomous ethical schemes.

#### 7. Interlocutors, controversies, and legacy
Directly or indirectly, Lloyd-Jones contended with **German theological modernity** and **Anglophone establishment**. He read **Karl Barth** appreciatively on **Christocentrism** yet charged **neo-orthodoxy** with ambiguity on **Scripture’s authority** and the **history of Jesus**. He admired **Warfield** on **inspiration** and **Machen** on the **antithesis** between **Christianity** and **liberalism**. In British evangelicalism he confronted **John Stott** and later **J. I. Packer** over **ecclesial strategy**, preferring **confessional separation** to **mixed-denominational reform**. The quarrel was not temperament but **principle** concerning **truth and fellowship**.

His positive interlocutors were the **Puritans**. The lectures published as **The Puritans 1987** portray **Owen**, **Goodwin**, **Baxter**, and **Edwards** as mentors in **experimental divinity**. Through his partnership with **Banner of Truth** and counsel to the **Westminster Fellowship** of pastors, he helped shape an evangelical recovery of **Reformed scholastic and practical theology**. The renewed confidence in **expository preaching**, the return to **penal substitution**, and the embrace of **sovereign grace** in British and global evangelicalism bear his imprint. Later preachers like **John Piper**, **Timothy Keller**, and many within the **Conservative Evangelical** world have acknowledged this debt to **logic on fire**, even where they diverge on **pneumatology** or **ecclesiology**.

#### 8. Distinctives under the microscope
Two distinctive emphases invite scrutiny. First, his strong account of the **Spirit’s sealing** and **baptism** as frequently post-conversion. Here Lloyd-Jones argued exegetically from **Acts** and **Ephesians**, descriptively from revival history, and pastorally from the need to ground **assurance** in more than inference. He closed no door to **critical testing** and warned against **excitability** and **imitation**, yet he refused to evacuate **experience** from **assurance**. Second, his ecclesial **separatism**. He believed that **truth** defines **fellowship**, that mixed communions confuse the **gospel**, and that a coherent **evangelical identity** demands structural expression. In both cases, he placed **divine sovereignty** and **Scripture’s clarity** above tactical expediency, refusing to trade **doctrine** for **influence**.

His strengths gather in a coherent profile. He integrated **dogma** and **doxology**, insisted on the primacy of **preaching**, and held together **objective atonement** with **subjective assurance**. He recovered **Puritan pastoral wisdom** and pressed it into a chastened modernity. He kept **revival** free from **revivalism**, defended **Scripture** against both **liberalism** and **neo-orthodoxy**, and raised generations of ministers to prize **expository faithfulness**. The weaknesses are the shadows cast by the same lights. A high view of **experience** risks elevating episodic **sealing** into a quasi-norm, and a high view of **purity** risks underestimating the penitential discipline of staying within **mixed bodies** for reform.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
Measured by **classical Reformed orthodoxy** and the ethos of **Westminster Theological Seminary**, Lloyd-Jones stands as an ally with selected tensions. On **Scripture**, **God**, **Christ**, **atonement**, and **sovereign grace**, he is resolutely within **confessional Reformed** boundaries, echoing **Westminster Confession** commitments to **inspiration**, **providence**, and **justification by faith alone**. His **preaching as means of grace** richly exemplifies **Word-centered ministry** cherished by **WTS**. On **apologetics**, his reliance on **Scripture’s self-attestation** and the **antithesis** comports with a **Van Til** trajectory, though he rarely theorized it. The tensions arise chiefly in **pneumatology** and **ecclesiology**. Reformed confessions commonly identify the **baptism with the Spirit** with the **once-for-all incorporation into Christ** at conversion, treating assurance as the fruit of **promises**, **means of grace**, and the **Spirit’s witness** without norming a post-conversion crisis. Lloyd-Jones’s accent on a distinct **sealing** can be received as an evangelical retrieval of **assurance** but should be subordinated to the confessional axiom that **Word and sacrament** are the ordinary conduits of **assurance**. On the church, a WTS-shaped evaluation will appreciate his refusal of **doctrinal indifferentism**, yet will also ask whether **confessional presbyterianism** offers a more robust **churchly discipline**, **sacramental catechesis**, and **connectional oversight** than his lean **Congregationalist** practice supplied. A constructive alternative tightens the bond between **redemptive-historical exegesis** and **ordo salutis**, integrates **assurance** more fully with **covenant promises** sealed in **baptism** and **the Supper**, and treats **Spirit baptism** as the **once-for-all pentecostal gift** shared by every believer while preserving room for **revival** as sovereign intensification. Within these guardrails, the legacy of **logic on fire** remains a tonic for ministers devoted to **truth on fire**, **holiness**, and **the glory of God in the face of Jesus Christ**.
