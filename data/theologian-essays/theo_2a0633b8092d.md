### TITLE
Analytic Theology as Ecclesially Located Metaphysics: from the critique of naturalism to a constructive account of divine hiddenness

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth and early twenty first century context of **analytic metaphysics**, **Reformed Epistemology**, and post-Quinean debates about **naturalism** at **Notre Dame** and the **Society of Christian Philosophers**.  
- Method marked by **analytic theology**, **stance commitments** in theory choice, and a retrieval of **classical theism** through rigorous **metaphysical modeling** tied to **Scripture**, **creeds**, and **liturgy**.  
- Key works: **World Without Design** 2002, **Material Constitution** reader 1997, **Analytic Theology** 2009 with **Oliver D. Crisp**, **The Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology** 2009 with **Thomas P. Flint**, and **The Hiddenness of God** 2018.  
- Central categories: **stance voluntarism**, **proper basicality**, **material constitution**, **numerical sameness without identity**, **divine hiddenness**, **liturgical epistemology**, **perfect being theology**.  
- Interlocutors and influences: **Alvin Plantinga**, **W. V. Quine**, **Peter van Inwagen**, **Jeffrey E. Brower**, **Eleonore Stump**, **Richard Swinburne**, **Aquinas** and **Augustine** on **divine simplicity** and **analogy**.  
- Reformed cue: strong alignment with **anti-naturalist epistemology** and **confessional starting points**, but tensions around **relative identity** in the **Trinity** and the evidential status of **liturgy** vis-a-vis **Sola Scriptura**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Michael C. Rea emerges within the late modern resurgence of **analytic philosophy of religion** shaped by **Alvin Plantinga** and the **Society of Christian Philosophers**, and within the broader analytic turn to **metaphysics** that followed Quinean critiques of **conceptual analysis**. Stationed at **Notre Dame**, and later serving as President of the **Society of Christian Philosophers**, Rea’s career unfolds at a crossroad where **Reformed Epistemology** meets a renewed Catholic and Protestant interest in **classical theism**. His earliest and most programmatic claim is methodological: both **naturalism** and **theism** are sustained by what he calls stance-like or basic **theory commitments** that are not licensed by neutral evidence. This methodological stance underwrites his later articulation of **analytic theology** as ecclesially located metaphysics in service of **Christian doctrine**. The approach is at once modest and ambitious: modest in acknowledging **stance voluntarism** about first principles, ambitious in recruiting the full arsenal of **metaphysical tools** to clarify **Trinity**, **Incarnation**, **divine simplicity**, and **divine hiddenness**.

The immediate intellectual background includes **W. V. Quine** on ontological commitment, **Peter van Inwagen** on **material beings**, and **Plantinga** on **proper basicality** and **warrant**, together with the practical ecclesial need to treat **Scripture** and **creed** not as targets for reduction but as authoritative data. Rea’s style is analytic and precise, yet overtly theological, resisting a merely generic **philosophy of religion** that stops with **perfect being theology**. His developing self-description is a hybrid: **philosophical theology** continuous with the metaphysical rigor of analytic philosophy, and **analytic theology** accountable to catholic consensuses of **Nicaea** and **Chalcedon**, to liturgical practice, and to the spiritual grammar of **Augustine** and **Aquinas**. The aim is a constructive metaphysics for **Christian doctrine** under authoritative constraints.

#### 2. World Without Design and the critique of naturalism
Rea’s **World Without Design: The Ontological Consequences of Naturalism** 2002 is his methodological cornerstone. Against the self-advertised austerity of **naturalism**, he argues that scientific practice implicitly relies on robust **metaphysical posits** that exceed a purely natural ontology. Quinean indispensability arguments push toward acceptance of **abstracta** like numbers and properties, while the norms that govern reasoning and theory choice invoke **epistemic** and **modal** standards that are not obviously natural. Thus, purportedly scientific constraints do not uniquely select **ontological naturalism**; rather, they show that the choice among large-scale pictures of reality is guided by **stance commitments**. Rea concludes that **naturalism** functions as a **research program** with faith-like basics no less than **theism** does. The claim is not a tu quoque simplification but a careful deployment of **metaphysical underdetermination**, designed to license parity for **theistic** starting points, including **proper basicality** and **theological testimony**.

This therapeutic critique aligns Rea with **Plantinga’s** anti-evidentialist turn, but it also secures independent space for **analytic theology**. If **naturalism** cannot claim rational monopoly, then **Christian doctrine** can be explored using **metaphysical resources** on its own terms, employing **Scripture**, **creeds**, and **tradition** as data. Rea’s emphasis on **stance voluntarism** does not level all viewpoints; it calls for transparency about **first principles** and for inferential rigor downstream. The upshot is methodological permission for robust **classical theism**, including **divine simplicity** and **aseity**, and for metaphysically rich accounts of **Trinity** and **Incarnation** without naturalistic vetoes.

#### 3. Constructing analytic theology
This methodological vision matures institutionally and literarily in the 2009 volumes **Analytic Theology: New Essays in the Philosophy of Theology** with **Oliver D. Crisp** and **The Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology** with **Thomas P. Flint**. Rea’s editorial leadership shapes a field that treats **dogmatic sources** as evidential and appropriates analytic tools for **doctrinal clarity**. The contrast between **philosophy of religion** and **analytic theology** is programmatic: rather than hovering over generic theism, contributors address specifically **Christian doctrines** like **atonement**, **incarnation**, and **Trinity**, drawing on **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Anselm**, and the **Reformers** with renewed precision. The operative norm is that catholic commitments found in **Nicaea** and **Chalcedon** function as constraints within which **metaphysical modeling** proceeds.

Rea’s own essays from this period and shortly thereafter display signature themes: a readiness to mine **mereology**, **constitution**, and **identity theory** for doctrinal payoffs; a preference for reconciling **classical theism** with strong metaphysical theses rather than deflating either; and a steady resistance to the claim that **scientific naturalism** sets the agenda for **theological ontology**. The result is a platform from which Rea and a cohort of analytic theologians argue that **clarity**, **logical rigor**, and **confessional accountability** need not be opposed.

#### 4. Trinity by constitution and numerical sameness without identity
Within the doctrine of the **Trinity**, Rea’s most influential contribution is developed with **Jeffrey E. Brower** under the constitution model. In essays such as **Material Constitution and the Trinity** 2005, they deploy the metaphysics of **material constitution** to model the unity and distinction of the **divine persons**. The central device is **numerical sameness without identity**, a relation known from the statue and clay case, now analogically repurposed for **Trinitarian** ontology. Each person of the **Trinity** is constituted by the same **divine nature**, so the nature is not an additional fourth thing but that in virtue of which the persons are each fully **God**. On this account, the persons are non-identical yet stand in a relation of numerical sameness to the one **Godhead**, thereby warding off **tritheism** and **modalism** and preserving **homoousios** and **perichoresis**.

The model engages critics such as **Brian Leftow**, **Richard Swinburne**, and proponents of **social trinitarianism**, and enters into conversation with **Aquinas** on **simplicity** and **processions**. Rea and Brower aim to show that the metaphysical repertoire of **constitution** and **relative identity** can illuminate the creedally fixed claims that the Father is **God**, the Son is **God**, and the Spirit is **God**, yet there is one **God**. The method is avowedly analogical and rule-governed by conciliar parameters, not a reductionist reinterpretation. The wager is that modern **mereology** can serve **Nicene** dogma in the way medieval **substance metaphysics** once did.

#### 5. Incarnation, natures, and metaphysical coherence
A parallel constructive impulse appears in Rea’s work on **Christology**, where the metaphysics of **constitution** is recruited to clarify **Chalcedon’s** one person in two natures. Alongside projects by figures like **Oliver D. Crisp**, Rea’s approach explores whether **hylomorphic** or **constitution** accounts can vindicate the unity of the **person of Christ** across **divine** and **human** predicates without contradiction. The aim is to avoid **kenotic** reductions while securing the genuine human cognitive and volitional life of the incarnate **Word**. Tools such as **relative identity**, fine-grained **property instantiation**, and distinctions between *ad intra* and *ad extra* operations underwrite a model where the one **hypostasis** bears nature-indexed properties under different dependency relations.

In these explorations Rea consistently prizes the **Chalcedonian** boundaries of without confusion, change, division, or separation. His models treat **natures** as metaphysically robust and seek to explain the cross-attribution of predicates without violating **immutability** or **impassibility** at the level of the **divine nature**. The instructive payoff is an analytically transparent itinerary from **creedal sentences** to **metaphysical structures**, encouraging clarity about what the council requires and what metaphysics can responsibly supply.

#### 6. The Hiddenness of God and liturgical epistemology
Rea’s **The Hiddenness of God** 2018 advances the debate with **J. L. Schellenberg** over **divine hiddenness** by reframing expectations about God’s presence and action. The thesis is twofold. First, given the aims of a perfectly loving **God** to form creatures for friendship and union, a degree of **hiddenness** is unsurprising and may be instrumentally good for non-coercive relationship and the cultivation of **virtue**. Second, the proper epistemic environment for acquaintance with God is not exclusively the detached assessment of public evidence but the life-shaping practices of **liturgy**, **prayer**, and **sacrament** that attune agents to divine presence. Rea thus deploys a robust notion of **liturgical epistemology** where participation is evidentially relevant, and where testimony from the **saints** and the **church** functions as properly basic within a theistic **stance**.

The argumentative posture remains analytic. Rea distinguishes **doxastic voluntarism**, calibrates the role of **pragmatic reasons**, and clarifies the evidential force of **religious experience** under conditions of ambiguity. Yet the constructive turn is unmistakable: he anchors **theological rationality** in ecclesial practices that shape perception of **grace** and presence. Themes from **Augustine’s** pedagogy of desire and **Aquinas’s** account of virtue and **sacramentality** are explicitly at work. The result is a chastened apologetics and a spiritually sensitive philosophical theology that acknowledges **divine silence** and spiritual **desolation** in the mode of **John of the Cross** while defending rational **theistic commitment**.

#### 7. Classical theism retrieved and analytically stated
Throughout, Rea signals charity toward **classical theism**, especially **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **eternity**, and **immutability**, even as he mobilizes contemporary **metaphysical** idioms. Perfect being considerations guide his construal of **God’s nature**, though always in dialogue with **Scripture** and the **creeds**. The appeal to **grounding**, **truthmaking**, and fine-grained **property theory** is not revisionist but clarificatory. He seeks to explicate how divine **attributes** relate to **Trinitarian** distinctions, how **inseparable operations** secure unity *ad extra*, and how the semantics of **theological predication** might defend **analogy** against both univocity and equivocity. The tone is confident that **analytic philosophy** can be a handmaid to **sacra doctrina** without capitulating to secular **naturalism** or to anti-metaphysical quietism.

Here the earlier critique of **naturalism** bears fruit. If **stance commitments** are inevitable at the base of inquiry, then theological sciences may openly declare their **principia** in **revelation** while pressing forward with exacting **argument**. Rea’s constructive voice legitimates the use of **ecclesial authority** as epistemic constraint, yet it also insists on transparent inference, dialectical fairness, and attention to rival views. In this way he models catholicity in form and content, moving between **Augustinian** introspection, **Thomistic** metaphysics, and contemporary **analytic** discipline.

#### 8. Interlocutors, debates, and institutional influence
Rea’s projects continuously engage the work of **Plantinga**, **van Inwagen**, **Quine**, **Swinburne**, **Leftow**, **Stump**, and a generation of analytic theologians such as **Crisp** and **Brower**. On **identity** and **constitution**, he navigates debates over **relative identity**, **mereological essentialism**, and **composition**. On **hiddenness**, he faces **Schellenberg’s** argument from nonresistant nonbelief and develops a spiritual theodicy of absence. On method, he debates the boundaries of **philosophy of religion** and **theology**, advocating for a porous border that honors **tradition** as data. His editorial work in the **Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology** gave the field a map of questions from **creation** and **providence** to **Christology**, while **Analytic Theology** 2009 presented a manifesto-like anthology for constructive engagement.

Institutionally, at **Notre Dame** and through leadership in the **Society of Christian Philosophers**, Rea helped normalize the presence of explicitly **dogmatic** projects in analytic venues. Students and junior scholars have followed his path in work on **divine attributes**, **sacramental theology**, **Trinity**, and **Incarnation** that reads **Aquinas** with the tools of **metaphysics** and takes **church practice** seriously. The legacy is not a single doctrine but a posture: metaphysically bold, confessional in scope, and candid about **stance commitments**.

#### 9. Theological upshot and integrated profile
Taken together, Rea’s oeuvre advances four theses with doctrinal reach. First, his anti-naturalist argument in **World Without Design** levels the field so that **theism** may reason from **revelatory** basics without guilt. Second, his editorial and constructive work defines **analytic theology** as a mode of **philosophical theology** normed by **creed** and **liturgy** rather than by secular **methodological naturalism**. Third, his **Trinity** and **Incarnation** projects exemplify the patience of **metaphysical modeling** under conciliar constraints, using **constitution** and **identity** theory to protect **monotheism** and **Chalcedon**. Fourth, his **Hiddenness of God** reframes evidential expectation by relocating much of the epistemic task within **ecclesial practices**, deepening the link between **spiritual formation** and **rational belief**. The cumulative effect is a theological metaphysics attuned to both the church’s confessions and the philosopher’s canons of clarity.

Rea’s approach has also catalyzed productive tensions. The recruitment of **numerical sameness without identity** for **Trinitarian** unity pushes analytic boundaries and invites scrutiny of its compatibility with **divine simplicity**. The turn to **liturgy** as evidentially salient challenges standard demarcations between **systematic theology** and **apologetics**, and it invites comparative work with **Augustinian** accounts of **love** and **illumination**. Still, the guiding vision is steady: a metaphysically serious, church-anchored, and philosophically candid **Christian theology**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **WTS** vantage, Rea’s critique of **naturalism** and embrace of **stance commitments** resonate strongly with the denial of **neutral reason**, the acknowledgment of the **noetic effects of sin**, and the principial role of **Scripture** as principium cognoscendi. His defense of metaphysical seriousness in **classical theism** also aligns with **aseity**, **immutability**, and **simplicity** as articulated by **Turretin** and retrieved by **Bavinck**. Yet two tensions remain. First, the **constitution** and **relative identity** strategies for the **Trinity** risk softening **simplicity** and **autotheos** by introducing quasi-structural relations that appear to presuppose metaphysical parts or constitutionally individuated hypostases, whereas a Reformed alternative would emphasize **simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, and the taxis of **processions** without importing the ontology of **material constitution**. Second, the evidential elevation of **liturgy** and **tradition** in **The Hiddenness of God** can blur **Sola Scriptura** by underwriting a quasi-sacramental epistemology that might be read as co-principial with **Scripture**; a WTS corrective would re-anchor the epistemic primacy of **canonical speech** within a **covenantal** account of knowledge where **Spirit**-given faith receives **Scripture** as sufficient and self-authenticating while still welcoming the ministerial role of **church** and **means of grace**. The constructive takeaway for Reformed theology is to appropriate Rea’s anti-naturalist method and analytic rigor while preferring models of **Trinity** that guard **simplicity** more strictly and an epistemology that subordinates **liturgy** to **Word** within the **covenant** economy.

### RECAP NOTES
- Setting: **Notre Dame** based leader in contemporary **analytic theology**, formed amid **Reformed Epistemology** and debates on **naturalism** in late twentieth and early twenty first century philosophy.  
- Core problem: how to justify **theistic** and **doctrinal** inquiry under conditions of **metaphysical underdetermination** and **divine hiddenness**.  
- Major works: **World Without Design** 2002 against **naturalism**; **Analytic Theology** 2009 and **Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology** 2009 consolidating the field; **The Hiddenness of God** 2018 on **liturgy** and evidence; essays on **Trinity** and **Incarnation** using **material constitution**.  
- Defining tools: **stance voluntarism**, **proper basicality**, **material constitution**, **numerical sameness without identity**, **perfect being theology**, **liturgical epistemology**.  
- Interlocutors: **Plantinga**, **van Inwagen**, **Quine**, **Brower**, **Swinburne**, **Leftow**, with retrieval of **Augustine** and **Aquinas** on **simplicity** and **analogy**.  
- Reformed critique: strong approval of anti-**naturalist** method and **classical theism**, with cautions about **relative identity** for the **Trinity** and the evidential status of **liturgy** relative to **Sola Scriptura**.  
- Legacy: architect of **analytic theology** as ecclesially located metaphysics, shaping debates on **divine attributes**, **Trinity**, **Incarnation**, and **divine hiddenness** for a generation of scholars.
