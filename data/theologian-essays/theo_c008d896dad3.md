### TITLE
One covenant, one church: Bullinger’s federal vision binding Word, sacrament, and polity in an irenic Swiss Reformation.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Reformation Switzerland**, post-Kappel consolidation in **Zurich** after **Zwingli** 1531; a humanist-pastoral leader shaping international **Reformed** identity.
- **Grammatical-historical exegesis** with patristic ressourcement, **Word and Spirit** epistemology, and a magistral model of **church polity**.
- **De testamento seu foedere Dei unico et aeterno** 1534; **The Old Faith** 1539; **Sermonum Decades quinque** 1549–1551; **Consensus Tigurinus** 1549; **Hundred Sermons on the Apocalypse** 1557; **Second Helvetic Confession** 1566.
- **Foedus Dei**, **unitas testamenti**, **spiritualis manducatio**, **magisterial Reformation**, **catholicity by continuity**, **pastoral predestination**.
- Dialogues with **Zwingli**, alliance with **Calvin** and **Vermigli**, engagement with **Lutherans** and opposition to **Anabaptists**, influence on **Cranmer** and **Jewel**.
- Reception through **Helvetic II** across Swiss and Central-European churches, English formation via the **Decades**, and later **Reformed federal theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Heinrich Bullinger, born 1504 in Aargau, emerged as the stabilizer of the **Swiss Reformation** after the catastrophe of Kappel in 1531 when **Zwingli** died on the field. Formed by northern humanism in Emmerich and at **Cologne** from 1519 to 1522, he appropriated **Erasmian philology** while moving decisively beyond scholastic disputation toward **biblical exegesis** as the sole theological foundation. His early lectures at the Cistercian house of **Kappel am Albis** from 1523 were marked by **grammatical-historical method**, patristic retrieval from **Augustine** and **Chrysostom**, and a deep conviction about **Scripture’s sufficiency** as norma normans. In December 1531 he became Antistes of the **Zurich church**, shaping a pastoral school anchored in **Word and Spirit**, where preaching, catechesis, and discipline ordered an entire civic community in the manner of a **magisterial Reformation**.

Bullinger’s intellectual posture fused humanist clarity with a catholic sense of continuity, a combination he called **vetus fides** or the **Old Faith**. Against both radical innovation and scholastic accretions he framed reform as the recovery of **apostolic doctrine** under the primacy of the **canon**. His epistemic stance foregrounded the simultaneity of the **externum verbum** and the **internum testimonium Spiritus**. Truth is given in the prophetic and apostolic text, illumined by the Spirit, and identifiable through the consensus of the **ancient church**. This yielded a method at once polemical and irenic: polemical toward **Anabaptist** biblicism detached from ecclesial order, irenic toward **Lutherans** where commonality could be confessed under the **catholic rule of faith**. The result was a distinctive Zurich habitus: a pastor-theologian presiding over a Reformed city and writing prolifically to knit **European Protestantism** into doctrinal and practical coherence.

#### 2. Programmatic works and the federal center
Bullinger’s earliest mature statement, **De testamento seu foedere Dei unico et aeterno** 1534, articulated the thesis that unifies his theology: the **foedus Dei** is one and eternal, administered diversely across redemptive history. He stresses **unitas testamenti** between Old and New, a single **covenant of grace** with Christ at its center, contra medieval discontinuities and **Anabaptist** dualisms. By appealing to prophetic promises and apostolic fulfilment, Bullinger argues that the church under Abraham and the church under the apostles share the same **justification by faith**, the same **Word and sacrament**, and the same parental obligation manifest in **infant baptism**. While he does not yet formalize a full **covenant of works** schema, his repeated delineation of Adam’s probation under a divine command anticipates later Reformed distinctions between **lex** and **evangelium** that would be codified in **Ursinus** and **Olevianus**.

In **The Old Faith** 1539, Bullinger advances a program of **catholicity by continuity**. The Reformation is not innovation but restoration, a claim substantiated by a dense collage of **patristic testimonia**. This apologetic strategy secured space for Zurich among humanist elites and civic magistrates by showing that the **Reformed** are heirs of **Augustine’s grace**, **Chrysostom’s exegesis**, and the **creedal tradition**. Here too the covenant sets the grammar: the fathers are read as witnesses to the one **divine economy** that educates and disciplines the church across ages. The **Decades** 1549–1551 then rendered this vision into pastoral architecture. Fifty extended sermons became a portable **Summa** of doctrine for clergy formation, ordered by **Scripture**, **creed**, **sacrament**, **prayer**, and **ethics**. The Decades functioned as catechetical scaffolding in Zurich and, through translation and royal injunctions, became standard reading for English ministers under **Edward VI** and later **Elizabeth I**.

#### 3. Word and sacrament under the consensus of Zurich
The Eucharistic controversy defined Bullinger’s mid-century diplomacy and doctrinal precision. In the **Consensus Tigurinus** 1549, drafted with **John Calvin**, he articulates **spiritualis manducatio** and the **real presence by the Spirit** without localizing Christ in the elements. The Supper is the Spirit’s instrument to unite believers to the **ascended Christ**, who remains **bodily in heaven** while truly feeding his people by faith. This formulation preserves **Zwingli’s** insistence on Christ’s heavenly session and the sign’s integrity while adopting **Calvin’s** robust language of **communio cum Christo**. It rejects both **transubstantiation** and **ubiquitarianism**, and it secures a shared **Reformed** identity capable of resisting **Lutheran** sacramental polemics and **Anabaptist** reductions. In short, Bullinger’s sacramentology is covenantal, pneumatic, and ecclesial, refusing a naked memorialism by anchoring the signs within the **foedus Dei** and the Spirit’s effective testimony.

Baptism receives parallel treatment keyed to **household inclusion** within the covenant. Because the **covenant of grace** embraces children as in **Genesis 17**, the sign of incorporation belongs to them as **promissio Dei** directed to the church’s seed. Bullinger insists that baptism obligates the entire community to **catechesis and discipline**, a thick ecclesiology in which sacramental grace is tethered to **preaching**, **prayer**, and **moral formation**. In this framework, **Word and sacrament** are not parallel rites but concentric instruments that reveal the covenant, pledge Christ, and bind saints into a disciplined **corpus Christi**.

#### 4. Predestination, assurance, and pastoral prudence
On **predestination**, Bullinger stands within the **Reformed** consensus but with a pastoral register distinct from later **Bezan** exactitude. He confesses **unconditional election** grounded in the divine **decretum**, yet resolutely ties assurance to the **evangelium** offered in the covenant. Election is for consolation, not speculation; its signs are **faith**, **love**, and persevering participation in the **means of grace**. He therefore employs a dialectic of promise and admonition within the **foedus Dei**, affirming that covenant membership is real but calls for persevering trust. The result is a rhetoric of **pastoral predestination** that curbs anxious introspection and refuses the fate-like constructions that enemies ascribed to the **Reformed**.

This pastoral prudence is matched by Bullinger’s handling of **law and gospel**. While he prefigures a later **covenant of works** by asserting that Adam stood under a law of obedience with the threat of death, he resists collapsing Christian life into antinomian ease. The **third use of the law** instructs the regenerate, and the Decades repeatedly exhort a civic holiness that adorns the **gospel**. His method remains exegetical and homiletical, eschewing speculative forays while drawing doctrinal lines firm enough to sustain **catechesis** and to inoculate the church against **Anabaptist** perfectionism and **Lutheran** antinomian charges.

#### 5. Church, polity, and catholic order
As Antistes, Bullinger constructed a durable **church polity** for Zurich under the city council. This **magisterial Reformation** underscored his conviction that God orders common life through the **twofold ministry of Word and sword**, distinct yet cooperative. Ministers preach and administer **discipline**, magistrates secure external peace, and both serve the **common good** under divine providence. His model resists sectarian withdrawal and integrates **catechesis**, **diaconal care**, and **education** into the city’s fabric. Bullinger’s letters show a statesman of the **Reformed international**, coordinating relief for refugees, exchanging counsel with **Cranmer**, **Hooper**, and **Jewel**, and guiding Swiss churches through famine, plague, and imperial pressure.

His anti-radical polemic aimed not at extinguishing zeal but at safeguarding **catholic order**. Against **Anabaptists**, Bullinger argued that rejection of **infant baptism** and **oaths** dissolved legitimate social bonds established under the **covenant**. Against **Lutheran** attacks, he defended Zurich’s worship as evangelical and ancient, stripping ceremonies to the essentials while maintaining **creedal orthodoxy**. The upshot was a disciplined, liturgically spare, and catechetically robust **Reformed** church whose pedagogical spine was the **Decades** and whose public confession would climax in the **Second Helvetic Confession**.

#### 6. Confessing the faith: Helvetic I and Helvetic II
Bullinger participated in the **First Helvetic Confession** 1536, a cooperative Swiss platform designed to express common doctrine with measured tone. Its irenic strategy foreshadows his later achievement in the **Second Helvetic Confession** 1566, the most widely received Reformed symbol of the sixteenth century after the **Genevan Catechism** and the **Belgic Confession**. Composed initially as a personal apologia during a time of plague and then elevated to public confession, **Helvetic II** offers a comprehensive and lucid statement of **Scripture**, **Trinity**, **Christology**, **justification**, **sacrament**, **church**, and **magistracy**. It enshrines his commitments to **Scripture’s sufficiency**, the **ancient creeds**, **unitas testamenti**, **spiritual presence** in the Supper, **infant baptism**, and a cooperative **magistracy**. Its reception in Switzerland, the **Grisons**, **Hungary**, **Transylvania**, and parts of **Poland** secured a shared doctrinal platform that could accommodate local church orders while maintaining **Reformed catholicity**.

The confession’s idiom is notably pedagogical and patristic. Bullinger routinely cites **Augustine** and **Chrysostom** to root **Reformed** teaching in the great tradition, while he avoids the sharper polemics of some contemporaries. The formulation of **Christ’s presence** and the sacraments bears the stamp of the **Consensus Tigurinus** and reflects a self-conscious **Zurich-Geneva concord**. The confession also clarifies his view of **images**, **worship**, and **ceremonies**, insisting on adherence to the **regulative principle** by Scriptural warrant while permitting **adiaphora** that serve edification and order. Helvetic II thus canonizes the **Zurich school** as a leading voice of measured Reformed orthodoxy.

#### 7. Exegesis, apocalyptic, and the pedagogy of preaching
Bullinger’s biblical work undergirds his system. The **Sermonum Decades quinque** 1549–1551 remain the capstone, but his **Hundred Sermons on the Apocalypse** 1557 display the pastoral exegesis that nourished Zurich and beyond. He adopts a **historicist** reading of **Revelation**, opposing **chiliastic literalism** and identifying the **papacy** with antichristian corruption in terms that align with broader **magisterial Protestantism**. Yet even here the tone is catechetical rather than sensational, aimed at cultivating perseverance under the **covenant** and trust in the **sovereignty of Christ**. His commentaries and homilies on **Pauline epistles**, **Jeremiah**, and **Daniel** echo the same method: philological rigor, typological sensitivity under **unitas testamenti**, and a constant draw back to the **means of grace** as the Spirit’s chosen instruments for building the church.

The pedagogical strategy of the **Decades** deserves notice for its international effect. In England the **Edwardian Injunctions** and later episcopal directives required clergy to study the Decades, making Bullinger a de facto professor to an entire episcopate. His theology thus mediated **Zurich** to **Canterbury**, shaping **English Reformed** identity on **covenant**, **sacrament**, and **discipline**. The constant theme is the tying of doctrine to practice, so that **justification by faith** produces a people marked by catechized minds, ordered loves, and civic responsibility under the **magistracy**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, networks, and the art of concord
Bullinger’s genius lay as much in correspondence as in composition. He cultivated alliances with **Calvin**, receiving and shaping a shared **Reformed** profile, while offering refuge and counsel to **Vermigli**, whose scholastic precision complemented Zurich’s homiletic clarity. With **Melanchthon**, he explored avenues of rapprochement across the **Lutheran** divide, though the **Interim** controversies and the **ubiquity** debate revealed limits imposed by sacramentology. With **Cranmer**, **Hooper**, and **Jewel**, his letters on **liturgy**, **articles**, and **discipline** entered the bloodstream of the English church, aiding the formation of the **Forty-two** and later **Thirty-Nine Articles**, especially on **Scripture**, **sacraments**, and **church order**.

Theologically, these networks fostered a habit of **irenic firmness**. Bullinger would not budge on **spiritual presence**, **infant baptism**, or the unity of the **covenant**, yet he framed common causes with care to avoid needless rupture. His famous clarity, brevity, and balance prepared the way for later **Reformed orthodoxy** to codify federal categories and confessional standards without renouncing the pastoral accent of **Zurich**. The stability he created allowed the **Zurich school** to hand off to figures like **Gualter** and to converse fruitfully with **Heidelberg** on the maturing **federal theology**.

#### 9. Doctrinal synthesis and enduring contribution
Bullinger’s synthesis can be summarized as a federal and ecclesial construal of **Reformed catholicity**. The arch doctrines are **Scripture’s sufficiency**, **unitas testamenti** in the single **covenant of grace**, **spiritual presence** in the Supper, **pastoral predestination**, and a disciplined **church polity** in concert with the **magistracy**. The covenant functions as architectonic principle, coordinating **Christology** and **soteriology** with **ecclesiology** and **ethics**. Doctrinal loci are read along the axis of divine **promise**, human **response**, and communal **formation**, yielding a Christianity that looks simultaneously backward to the fathers and forward to public witness.

His achievement is also institutional. The **Second Helvetic Confession** codified a moderate, comprehensive **Reformed** voice used across languages and lands. The **Decades** trained clergy in a homiletical dogmatics, shaping pastoral habits that outlived doctrinal controversies. The **Consensus Tigurinus** rescued Swiss unity and aligned **Zurich** and **Geneva** around a shared **sacramental** grammar. Through these instruments Bullinger made the **foedus Dei** the hinge of doctrine and the heart of piety for the Reformed tradition as it moved from early reform to confessional stability.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and WTS perspective, Bullinger’s strengths are considerable. His **unitas testamenti** secures the continuity of **redemptive history**, his account of **Word and Spirit** grounds epistemology in revelation rather than speculation, and his **spiritual presence** avoids both sacramental minimalism and materialism. His **pastoral predestination** rightly refuses curiosity and directs assurance to **Christ in the gospel**. Yet tensions remain. The **magisterial** tightness of church and council risks **Erastian** confusion regarding **Christ’s kingship** and the spiritual jurisdiction of the **keys**, inviting a corrective from a more developed **two-kingdoms** or sphere sovereignty account. His covenantal rhetoric sometimes flows toward conditional formulations that, if not carefully hedged by **effectual calling** and the **perseverance of the saints**, can weaken assurance or blur the nature of **visible and invisible church**. Relative to **Calvin**, his Eucharistic idiom can sound cautious, leaving some room to strengthen language about **Spirit-wrought communion** without compromising Christ’s ascension. The maturation of a bicovenantal **works-grace** schema, as articulated in **Westminster Confession of Faith 7** and **19**, clarifies law-gospel relations in ways only adumbrated by Bullinger’s Adamic testing. Still, within classical Reformed orthodoxy his federal vision, scriptural method, and confessional labor remain exemplary, and his synthesis aligns substantially with the WTS commitments to **biblical authority**, **covenant theology**, and the centrality of **Christ** and the **means of grace**.
