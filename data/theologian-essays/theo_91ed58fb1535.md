### TITLE
Unity in Covenantal Progress: a redemptive-historical, canonical reading of law, prophecy, and Psalms in service of one people of God

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth-century evangelicalism**, with **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** as a hub, amid debates on **Dispensationalism**, **Theonomy**, and the rise of the **canonical** and **redemptive-historical** approaches.
- **Grammatical-historical exegesis** disciplined by **Hebrew philology** and enriched through **redemptive-historical** and **canonical** sensibilities; cautious openness to *sensus plenior* under **divine authorship** and **apostolic hermeneutics**.
- **The Progress of Redemption** 1988; **Interpreting the Prophetic Word** 1990; **Psalms** in the **Expositor's Bible Commentary** 1991; general editor of **NIDOTTE** 1997; editor of **A Guide to Old Testament Theology and Exegesis** 1999; contributor to **Five Views on Law and Gospel** 1999.
- Core categories: **covenant**, **kingdom of God**, **people of God**, **law and gospel** with the **third use of the law**, **prophecy and fulfillment**, **canonical shape of the Psalter**, **Hebrew lexical semantics**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **John Calvin**, **Geerhardus Vos**, **Herman Bavinck**, **James Barr**, **Meredith Kline**, **O. Palmer Robertson**; debaters include **Dispensationalists**, **Theonomists** like **Greg Bahnsen**, and evangelical exegetes such as **Douglas Moo** and **Walter Kaiser**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong alignment with **covenant theology** and **typology**; debates persist on the **tripartite law**, the clarity of **covenant of works** categories, and the handling of **Israel-church** continuity within **classic Reformed orthodoxy**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and methodological posture
Willem A. VanGemeren emerged as a leading voice in late twentieth-century evangelical biblical theology, teaching for decades at **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** where his work in **Old Testament and Semitic Languages** shaped a generation of evangelical pastors and scholars. His scholarly milieu was defined by the waning dominance of **classic Dispensationalism**, the ferment around **redaction criticism** and **canonical criticism**, and a renewal of interest in **redemptive-historical** reading through the legacy of **Geerhardus Vos** and related **Reformed** currents. VanGemeren positioned himself as a mediator: he insisted on **grammatical-historical exegesis** and rigorous **Hebrew philology**, yet he located exegesis within the unified economy of **covenant** and **kingdom of God**, thus resisting both atomistic proof-texting and purely diachronic fragmentation. Theologically, his instincts are **Calvinian** and **Bavinckian**, stressing the organic unity of **Scripture** and **progressive revelation**, while his lexical work shows close attention to insights from **James Barr** on **semantics** and the dangers of illegitimate totality transfer.

Methodologically, VanGemeren integrates *analogia fidei* and *analogia Scripturae* with careful genre analysis, allowing typology to arise from the **canonical** context rather than from allegory. He emphasizes that the **divine authorship** of Scripture warrants a controlled *sensus plenior*, particularly where **apostolic hermeneutics** disclose Christological fulfillment. His stance is neither an uncritical adoption of **childsian canonical criticism** nor a retreat to precritical assumptions; it is a confessional and philological approach seeking to hear **Torah**, **prophets**, and **writings** as a coherent witness to the **Messiah** and the **new covenant**.

#### 2. Key works and scholarly arc
Several volumes chart VanGemeren's trajectory. In 1988, **The Progress of Redemption** presented a whole-Bible storyline in covenantal and kingdom terms, narrating creation to new creation with attention to **Abrahamic**, **Mosaic**, and **Davidic** administrations and their Christological telos. In 1990, **Interpreting the Prophetic Word** offered an introduction to **prophetic literature** and established his hallmark emphases: prophetic **genre**, **eschatological foreshortening**, **the day of the Lord**, and the movement from historical judgment-oracles to **messianic** and **new covenant** hope. His 1991 **Psalms** contribution in the **Expositor's Bible Commentary** consolidated his reputation as an exegete-theologian, integrating **Hebrew poetry**, canonical ordering, and **Davidic kingship** into a theological reading of Israel's prayer book. Serving as general editor of the **New International Dictionary of Old Testament Theology and Exegesis** in 1997, VanGemeren convened a reference project that refined evangelical practice in **Hebrew lexicography**, with methodological attention to **synchronic** and **diachronic** semantics and the use of **cognate languages**. Finally, his essays collected in **A Guide to Old Testament Theology and Exegesis** 1999 and his chapter representing the **Reformed view** in **Five Views on Law and Gospel** 1999 display his hermeneutical convictions at the interface of exegesis and dogmatics.

These works share a common grammar. The biblical story advances by **promise and fulfillment**, by **type and antitype**, and by the **kingdom of God** advancing through covenantal administrations toward the **Christ** who enacts the **new covenant**. Israel and the church are one **people of God** across the centuries, such that the **Torah** is not abrogated but reaches its telos in **Jesus Christ**, who writes the **law** on the heart by the **Spirit**.

#### 3. Law and gospel within a Reformed framework
VanGemeren's most programmatic dogmatic interface appears in **Five Views on Law and Gospel** 1999, where he articulates a **Reformed** account of the law's unity and diversity over time, responding to **Dispensationalism**, **Theonomy** represented by **Greg Bahnsen**, and the **Lutheran** law-gospel dichotomy. He affirms the **moral law** as perpetually binding according to the classic **tripartite division** while urging readers to see the law under the **law of Christ** and the **Spirit** in the **new covenant**. He deploys the **third use of the law** as the Spirit-enabled rule of life for the justified, echoing **Calvin** and **Westminster** while distancing himself from any civil imposition of **Mosaic judicials** as if they were normatively binding on modern polities. Against antinomian misreadings, he argues for **Christ's fulfillment** rather than abolition, invoking Romans and Galatians in conversation with contributors like **Douglas Moo** and **Walter Kaiser**.

Theologically, VanGemeren situates **law and gospel** within **redemptive-historical** development. The **Mosaic covenant** is a gracious administration embedded within the broader **Abrahamic** promise, not a works arrangement that stands in polar antithesis to the **new covenant**. He resists both the **Theonomic** claim that civil-judicial law remains directly normative and the **Dispensational** claim that the law belongs to a superseded dispensation. The continuity he stresses is teleological: the **Messiah** embodies Israel and internalizes **Torah**, so that obedience arises from union with Christ in the **Spirit**. His preferred idiom is Pauline: the **righteousness of the law** is fulfilled in those who walk according to the **Spirit**, a claim that safeguards the **third use** while maintaining the primacy of **grace**.

#### 4. Israel, the church, and the people of God
In dialogue with **classic** and **progressive Dispensationalism**, VanGemeren argues for the unity of the **people of God** and the typological integration of **Israel** and **church**. The **land promise** functions as a type of the broader **inheritance** realized in **Christ** and consummated as **new creation**, such that territorial particularity yields to cosmic scope. He aligns with **covenant theology** in rejecting a hard Israel-church dualism, yet he avoids collapsing the historical particularities of Israel's vocation. Influenced by **Vos** and covenantal readings, he sees the **Abrahamic covenant** as determinative for the inclusion of the **nations**, and the **Davidic covenant** as mediating the **kingdom of God** through the **Messiah**.

In this frame, **prophetic promises** of restoration and a renewed **Zion** are not denied but expanded: the prophetic idiom is retained while the referent is enlarged in Christ. His approach engages **progressive Dispensationalists** like **Craig Blaising** and **Darrell Bock** by recognizing an already-not-yet **kingdom** and a robust future hope, yet he insists the controlling hermeneutic is Christological, canonical, and covenantal rather than a promise-fulfillment scheme that re-centers on national Israel. The upshot is ecclesiological: the **church** as Jew and Gentile in one body is the historical manifestation of the **people of God**, awaiting the resurrection and the **new earth** in which the land promise is magnified, not marginalized.

#### 5. Prophecy, genre, and eschatology
In **Interpreting the Prophetic Word** 1990, VanGemeren offers a compact hermeneutics of **prophecy** that is neither reductionist historicism nor speculative futurism. He treats **genre** and **form** as conduits of theology, highlighting **covenant lawsuit**, **woe oracle**, **vision report**, and **salvation oracle** as literary vehicles for the **prophetic** disclosure of God's purposes. He commends the classic notion of **prophetic foreshortening** or telescoping to account for the near-far horizons of **the day of the Lord**, and he contends that **apocalyptic** symbolism operates within the same covenantal grammar as the **classical prophets**.

Crucially, VanGemeren integrates **apostolic hermeneutics**. The New Testament's rereading of the **Old Testament** arises from the conviction that **Christ** is the telos of the **Torah and Prophets**, authorizing a judicious use of *sensus plenior* within the bounds of the **canon** and the **Spirit's** inspiration. He resists a wooden single-sense literalism that fences off prophetic language from typological and eschatological reach, while also rejecting free-form allegory. The result is an eschatology of **inaugurated kingdom**, Israel-in-Christ, and a **new covenant** that transforms rather than terminates the prophetic hope.

#### 6. Psalms as canonical theology
VanGemeren's **Psalms** commentary in the **Expositor's Bible Commentary** 1991 models his synthesis of exegesis and theology. He reads **parallelism**, **metaphor**, and **Hebrew poetics** with philological care, yet he constantly draws the reader back to the **Davidic covenant**, **kingship**, and the **Torah** frame of piety. Canonically, he tracks the fivefold division of the **Psalter**, the prominence of **Psalms 1â€“2** as an entrance liturgy of **Torah** and **Messiah**, and the editorial shaping that foregrounds **Davidic** hope amid exile and restoration. He engages the canonical-shape scholarship associated with figures like **Gerald Wilson** while retaining a conservative view of authorial claims and a theological reading that anticipates the **Messiah**.

This canonical approach bears fruit in theologically charged topics. **Imprecatory psalms** are not embarrassments to be silenced, but eschatological prayers that are transfigured by union with the **Messiah** who bears the curse and will judge with righteousness. Lament and praise articulate a covenantal spirituality grounded in the **name of the LORD**, the gifts of **Torah**, and the promise of **kingdom**. For VanGemeren, praying the **Psalms** is ecclesial catechesis: the church learns **law and gospel**, **repentance and faith**, and participation in the **people of God** across the ages.

#### 7. Lexical semantics and theological method
As general editor of **NIDOTTE** 1997, VanGemeren championed methodological discipline in **Hebrew lexicography**. Building on **James Barr's** critique of etymologizing and semantic totality, he encouraged articles that distinguished **diachronic** from **synchronic** usage, mapped **semantic fields**, and evaluated **cognate Semitic** data without overreach. The dictionary's structure fosters a movement from **word study** to **conceptual theology**, helping readers avoid the fallacy that a concept equals a lexeme and guiding them into textual patterns that disclose **covenant**, **holiness**, **chesed**, and **wisdom** within their literary and historical contexts.

VanGemeren's essays in **A Guide to Old Testament Theology and Exegesis** 1999 reiterate this program. Exegesis must submit to the text's **genres**, respect the **canon**, and confess the **unity of Scripture** as God's speech. The theological payoff is clarity: **law** participates in the story of **grace**, **prophecy** works within the **covenant**, and **wisdom** belongs to the same piety as **Torah** and **temple**. His method thus resists both biblicist atomism and speculative system, advocating a chastened **systematic theology** fed by **biblical theology** and answerable to the **church's** confessional heritage.

#### 8. The Progress of Redemption and the whole-Bible arc
In **The Progress of Redemption** 1988, VanGemeren narrates the economy of grace as the historical outworking of **covenant** under the **kingdom of God**. The architecture moves from **creation** and **fall**, through **Noahic** stability, into **Abrahamic** promise, **Mosaic** formation, **Davidic** kingship, **prophetic** critique and consolation, and then to **Christ**, **church**, and **new creation**. He emphasizes the **presence of God** as a through-line: from **Eden** to **tabernacle**, **temple**, **Immanuel**, and **Spirit**, culminating in the **New Jerusalem**. The story is not a flat recital but a drama of escalation and resolution, governed by **typology** in which **Israel**, **sacrifice**, **priesthood**, **kingship**, and **land** are shadows of the **Messiah** and his **kingdom**.

This storyline corrals contested topics. The **law** becomes a gift within grace, the **people of God** widen to the nations in fulfillment of **Abraham**, and the **prophets** become the heralds of a **new covenant** whose internalization of **Torah** realizes what earlier administrations promised. VanGemeren thereby supplies pastors and students with a scaffold for preaching **Christ** from **all Scripture**, urging the church to inhabit the canonical narrative rather than fragmenting it along confessional or critical lines.

#### 9. Interlocutors, debates, and disciplinary influence
VanGemeren's interlocutors are many. With **Dispensationalism**, he disputes a rigid Israel-church separation, while appreciating the call to read **prophecy** seriously. With **Theonomy** and **Greg Bahnsen**, he shares a high view of **law** but rejects direct transposition of **civil** code into modern governance, preferring the **moral law** mediated through **Christ** and the **Spirit**. He converses with **Lutheran** law-gospel paradigms by affirming forensic **justification** without evacuating the **third use**. His lexical and hermeneutical cautions owe much to **James Barr** and to evangelical discussions mediated by figures like **D. A. Carson** on **biblical theology** and **semantics**.

The institutional mark of his work is felt in classrooms and reference shelves. At **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School**, alongside colleagues like **John Woodbridge** and **John Feinberg**, he resisted the drift toward both skeptical **higher criticism** and parochial proof-texting, and he built bridges between **Old Testament theology**, **exegesis**, and **systematics**. As editor of **NIDOTTE**, he curated an evangelical benchmark that trained readers in responsible **lexical** and **conceptual** work. As commentator on **Psalms**, he gave pastors a model of canonical, Christ-centered exposition that respects **Hebrew poetry** and **canonical shaping**.

#### 10. Reformed and Westminster evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage, VanGemeren's achievement is substantial: he articulates a robust **covenantal** unity, practices disciplined **grammatical-historical** exegesis, advances **canonical** reading, and gives constructive accounts of **law and gospel**, **prophecy**, and **Psalms** that equip the church. Two tensions invite refinement. First, while his defense of the **tripartite division of the law** and the **third use** is pastorally apt, its exegetical grounding can appear more confessional than textual, and his integration of **Mosaic** within **Abrahamic** grace may understate the prelapsarian **covenant of works** and the pedagogical-legal function emphasized by classic **Westminster** categories. A clearer account of the law as a republication or heightened administration of the original works principle, as argued by some within the **WTS** stream, would sharpen the **law-gospel** contrast without lapsing into Dispensational discontinuity. Second, his controlled openness to *sensus plenior* under **apostolic hermeneutics** could be more fully normed by the explicit typological patterns modeled in Scripture, lest canonical readings outpace textual signals. Still, judged by Reformed principles of **revelation**, **covenant**, **grace**, and **history**, VanGemeren offers a faithful, capacious, and churchly hermeneutic whose alternatives within classical Reformed orthodoxy are largely matters of calibration rather than contradiction.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Evangelical OT scholar at **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** in the late twentieth century, mediating between **critical** and **confessional** approaches amid debates on **Dispensationalism** and **Theonomy**.
- **Core focus**: A **redemptive-historical**, **canonical** reading of the Old Testament that integrates **law**, **prophecy**, and **Psalms** for one **people of God** under the **kingdom of God**.
- **Major works**: **The Progress of Redemption** 1988; **Interpreting the Prophetic Word** 1990; **Psalms** in **EBC** 1991; general editor of **NIDOTTE** 1997; **A Guide to Old Testament Theology and Exegesis** 1999; **Reformed view** in **Five Views on Law and Gospel** 1999.
- **Distinctives**: Emphasis on **tripartite law** with **third use**, **typology** and **promise-fulfillment**, cautious *sensus plenior* under **divine authorship**, and canonical **Psalter** theology centered on **Torah** and **Messiah**.
- **Interlocutors**: Engages **Greg Bahnsen**, **Dispensationalists**, **Lutheran** law-gospel advocates, and semantic critiques of **James Barr**; influenced by **Calvin**, **Vos**, **Bavinck**, **Kline**, and **O. Palmer Robertson**.
- **Reformed critique**: Strong covenantal unity and **canonical** method; questions persist on exegetical grounding of the **tripartite law**, articulation of the **covenant of works**, and guardrails for *sensus plenior* within **Westminster** categories.
- **Legacy**: Durable impact through **NIDOTTE**, pastoral-resourced **Psalms** exegesis, and a widely adopted **redemptive-historical** hermeneutic that equips evangelical and **Reformed** preaching and teaching.
