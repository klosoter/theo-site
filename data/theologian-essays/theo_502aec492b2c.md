### TITLE
Rational trust re-centered: knowledge, morality, and authority unified by intellectual virtue, exemplarist motivation, and a theistic account of conscience.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth-century **analytic philosophy** after **Gettier**, revival of **virtue ethics** and **virtue epistemology**, Catholic engagement with **Aquinas**.
- Method: **responsibilist virtue epistemology**, **exemplarism**, and a theory of **epistemic authority** built from **conscientious self-trust** and the **preemption** of reasons.
- Key works: **The Dilemma of Freedom and Foreknowledge** (1991), **Virtues of the Mind** (1996), **Divine Motivation Theory** (2004), **On Epistemology** (2009), **Epistemic Authority** (2012), **Exemplarist Moral Theory** (2017), essays on **omnisubjectivity** (2010s).
- Central terms: **intellectual virtues**, **epistemic luck**, **credit thesis**, **conscientiousness**, **testimony and tradition**, **preemptive authority**, **divine motivation**, **exemplarist reference-fixing**, **omnisubjectivity**, **foreknowledge and freedom**.
- Interlocutors: **Aristotle**, **Thomas Aquinas**, **Alvin Plantinga**, **Ernest Sosa**, **John Greco**, **William Alston**, **Joseph Raz**, **Robert Adams**, **Duncan Pritchard**.
- Reformed cue: weigh **virtue epistemology** and **authority of testimony** against **sola Scriptura**, **noetic effects of sin**, **divine simplicity** versus **omnisubjectivity**, and **covenantal epistemology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and context
Linda T. Zagzebski emerges within late twentieth-century **analytic philosophy**, where the post-**Gettier** crisis and the rehabilitation of **virtue ethics** created space for rethinking knowledge in terms of **character**. A Catholic philosopher trained in the analytic tradition, she reads **Aristotle** and **Thomas Aquinas** as live resources for **epistemology** and **moral theory**, locating rational life within a teleological account of human flourishing. Already in **The Dilemma of Freedom and Foreknowledge** 1991 she displayed a taste for precise modal analysis in theology, engaging **Boethius**, **Ockham**, and **Molina** with argumentative care. The decisive turn arrives in **Virtues of the Mind** 1996, where she offers a fully developed **responsibilist virtue epistemology**, contrasting with reliabilist models associated with **Ernest Sosa** and **John Greco**. Across subsequent decades she integrates a theory of **divine motivation** 2004, a robust account of **epistemic authority** 2012, and an **exemplarist** moral theory 2017, crafting an architectonic in which **intellectual virtue**, **authority**, and **theism** are mutually interpretive.

#### 2. Method: intellectual virtue and conscientiousness
The methodological center is the claim that intellectual agency is governed by **intellectual virtues** that are deep and stable excellences, paralleling moral **virtues**. Against thin deontic models of justification, she argues that the primary normative category is **conscientiousness**, an excellence of motivation to believe responsibly and to pursue truth fittingly. This **responsibilist virtue epistemology** treats knowledge as success because of virtue, articulating a **credit thesis** that addresses the **value problem** beyond reliabilist truth-tracking. The concepts of **epistemic luck** and **Gettier** failures are analyzed as cases where the agent does not deserve credit in the right way. By embedding the aim of truth within a eudaimonist **telos**, she aligns knowing with **Aquinas's** account of *habitus* and **Aristotelian phronesis**, while engaging contemporary figures like **Alvin Plantinga** and **William Alston** on the relation between **warrant**, **perception**, and **justification**.

#### 3. Virtues of the Mind 1996: knowledge through intellectual virtue
In **Virtues of the Mind** she offers a systematic account of **knowledge** as cognitive contact with reality arising from **intellectual virtue**, framed by a robust analysis of **motivation**, **reliability**, and **success**. Her distinction between **reliabilist** and **responsibilist** strands within **virtue epistemology** leads her to champion the latter, insisting that the agent's motives and aims are essential in converting true belief into knowledge. The **credit thesis** supplies an answer to the **value problem**, since what is valuable in knowledge beyond true belief is the exercise of **excellence** that merits credit. She resists strict **evidentialism** in the style of **Richard Feldman**, while rejecting a purely externalist reliabilism that effaces agency. By drawing on **Aquinas** to construe the virtues as perfected **habitus**, she embeds belief-formation within moral-spiritual formation, integrating analysis of **epistemic luck** with a taxonomy of **virtues** like intellectual humility, courage, and **conscientiousness**.

#### 4. Divine Motivation Theory 2004: virtue ethics under theism
With **Divine Motivation Theory** she shifts from deontologic or command models toward an account of morality grounded in **God's motives** as the supreme exemplar of goodness. The thesis is that the fundamental normative standard is the pattern of **divine motivations**, particularly **agapic** love of the good, rather than bare **divine commands**. Engaging **Robert Merrihew Adams** on the metaphysical primacy of the **Good** and the nature of **excellence**, and drawing heavily on **Aquinas's** doctrine of **charity**, she proposes that human moral virtue imitates **God's motivational structure**, a theistic **eudaimonism** where love fixates the moral saliences we ought to perceive. This framework explains the unity of **character**, grounds reasons for action in the attractiveness of the **Good**, and promises to reconcile moral particularism with principle through the perception of what the good person would be moved by. The theory attempts to avoid voluntarist arbitrariness while maintaining a robustly **theocentric** normativity.

#### 5. Freedom, foreknowledge, and omnisubjectivity
Earlier in **The Dilemma of Freedom and Foreknowledge** 1991, Zagzebski pressed the problem of **theological fatalism**, analyzing modalities of necessity and the temporal status of **divine knowledge**. She rigorously surveys **Boethian** timeless knowledge, **Ockhamist** distinctions of hard and soft facts, and **Molinist** middle knowledge, aiming to show the contours of a solution in which **divine omniscience** does not collapse human freedom. In later work she extends the doctrine of God with the distinctive attribute of **omnisubjectivity**, arguing in 2010s essays that beyond knowing all truths, God perfectly knows all first-person perspectives from the inside. This proposal, influenced by phenomenological insights on empathy and consistent with a theistic perfectionism, redescribes **omniscience** to include exhaustive access to subjective states. It has implications for **incarnation**, **divine compassion**, and debates on **impassibility**, since a God who knows our experiences as such appears maximally attuned to creaturely subjectivity while remaining the **Creator**.

#### 6. Epistemic Authority 2012: trust, autonomy, and preemption
In **Epistemic Authority** 2012 Zagzebski develops a theory of rational **authority** grounded in **conscientious self-reflection**. Starting from the inescapable need to trust our own faculties, she argues that if I have reason to think my conscientious judgment is less reliable than another person's or a community's judgment on some domain, then rationality requires that I treat the other's judgment as a **preemptive** reason, displacing some of my own reasons. She adapts **Joseph Raz's** service conception of practical authority to the **epistemic** sphere, yielding a principled account of **testimony**, **tradition**, and institutional **authority**. The model explains why deference to experts, peers, or ecclesial bodies can be rational without surrendering **autonomy**, because conscientiousness itself issues in the authority-recognizing stance. This framework has explicit theological consequence: a conscientious believer may rationally submit to the **authority of tradition** and the **Church** when conditions for competence and identification are met, while still honoring the primacy of truth.

#### 7. Exemplarist Moral Theory 2017: admiration and reference-fixing
Her **Exemplarist Moral Theory** 2017 continues the turn to **exemplarism**, arguing that moral concepts are fixed by reference to **exemplars** known through **admiration**. Instead of defining goodness by properties, we ostend to paradigmatic persons, much as we fix the referent of natural-kind terms. The morally serious agent is trained to admire what is truly admirable, calibrating **perception** and **motivation** by sustained attention to saints and sages. This theory cohere with **intellectual virtue** by treating epistemic exemplars as well, shaping **habitus** of attention, courage, and humility in the pursuit of truth. The role of **narrative** is central: stories of **exemplary agents** furnish thick ethical salience conditions that principles alone cannot supply. Theologically, the approach resonates with **Aquinas's prudence** and the formation of **charity**, while practically underwriting pedagogy that inculcates **virtue** through liturgy, community, and disciplined admiration of holy lives.

#### 8. Integration: epistemology, ethics, and community
Across **On Epistemology** 2009 and numerous essays, Zagzebski locates the unity of rational life in the interplay of **knowledge**, **understanding**, and **wisdom**. By stressing **intellectual virtue** and **exemplarism**, she links the value of knowing to the agent's formation, addresses the **value problem** beyond entitlement to belief, and reframes **disagreement** as a site of conscientious deference or steadfastness depending on **authority** conditions. Her approach intersects with **Reformed epistemology** in **Alvin Plantinga's** sense, since both reject classical evidentialism and honor **proper basicality** or **self-trust**, yet she pushes further toward communal **authority** and **tradition** as rationally preemptive. Her contribution to **testimony** studies aligns with **William Alston** on the permissibility of doxastic reliance, but she provides a more complete blueprint for when **autonomy** should yield. The result is a theistically inflected picture where **virtue**, **exemplar**, and **authority** entangle to shape responsible faith seeking understanding.

#### 9. Internal tensions and clarifications
Zagzebski anticipates objections that **self-trust** verges on circularity or that **authority** licensed by **preemption** risks **authoritarianism**. She replies that all normative systems face a reflective stopping-point, and conscientiousness is the least arbitrary because it is the virtue that governs all deliberation. Likewise, the preemption of one s own reasons is conditional on identified **competence**, proper **identification** with the community or expert, and ongoing **conscientious review**. On **peer disagreement**, she balances steadfastness with rational **deference** by testing the comparative reliability of parties and the weight of institutional **tradition**. Regarding **omnisubjectivity**, she tries to safeguard **divine transcendence** by specifying that God knows first-personal contents without sharing creaturely limitations. In ethics, the **exemplarist** appeal to **admiration** invites worries about projection or cultural bias, to which she answers with the need for virtues of **humility** and **openness** that recalibrate admiration under the light of the **Good** revealed supremely in God.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a Reformed vantage, several strengths stand out. Her **virtue epistemology** recovers the agentive texture of knowing that classical **Reformed orthodoxy** locates in the **habitus fidei**, the work of the **Spirit** forming **wisdom** through **Scripture**, **church**, and *usus*. Her account of **testimony** and **epistemic authority** coheres with **catholicity**, warning against an atomized **autonomy** foreign to the body of Christ. Yet principled tensions remain. First, the grounding of rational **preemption** in **conscientious self-trust** risks underweighting the **noetic effects of sin**, whereas **sola Scriptura** makes the external **Word** the principium that normatively preempts all human judgments, including ecclesial **tradition**. A **covenantal epistemology** would subordinate admiration of **exemplars** to the analogical authority of **revelation**, receiving **Christ** as the archetypal exemplar by the **Spirit** rather than calibrating admiration by largely natural conscience. Second, **omnisubjectivity** leans toward a maximalist empathy that may pressure **divine impassibility**, **aseity**, and **divine simplicity**; classical **Reformed** theology would preserve God s perfect knowledge of creaturely acts via **omniscience** and **analogia** without positing first-personal participation in creaturely states, lest Creator and creature be confused. Third, **divine motivation theory** rightly resists voluntarism, but a Reformed alternative grounds moral order in the triune **nature** as simple, holy love and in the revealed **law** as covenantal gift, integrating motives with **commands** under **Christ s** mediatorship. In sum, Zagzebski offers powerful tools for sanctified **intellectual character**, communal **authority**, and **moral formation**; a Reformed appropriation will receive these under the primacy of **Scripture**, the **Spirit s** renewing grace, and a classical doctrine of **God** that safeguards transcendence while affirming intimate providence.

### RECAP NOTES
- Late 20th-century **analytic philosophy** context, Catholic engagement with **Aristotle** and **Aquinas**, responding to **Gettier** and the revival of **virtue** traditions.
- Core problem: unify **knowledge**, **morality**, and **authority** via **intellectual virtue**, **exemplarism**, and the rationality of **trust**.
- Major works: **The Dilemma of Freedom and Foreknowledge** (1991), **Virtues of the Mind** (1996), **Divine Motivation Theory** (2004), **On Epistemology** (2009), **Epistemic Authority** (2012), **Exemplarist Moral Theory** (2017), with essays on **omnisubjectivity**.
- Distinctives: **responsibilist virtue epistemology**, **credit thesis** against **epistemic luck**, **conscientiousness**, **preemptive** **epistemic authority**, **divine motivation**, **exemplarist reference-fixing**, and **omnisubjectivity** as a divine attribute.
- Interlocutors and debates: against strict **evidentialism**; alongside **Plantinga**, **Sosa**, **Greco**, **Alston**; drawing on **Raz** for **authority**, **Adams** for **theistic ethics**, and classical debates on **foreknowledge**.
- Reformed evaluation: affirm **virtue** and **testimony**, yet reassert **sola Scriptura**, **noetic effects of sin**, and classical **impassibility** and **simplicity** over **omnisubjectivity**; commend a **covenantal epistemology** centered on **Christ** and the **Spirit**.
- Legacy: a leading voice in **virtue epistemology** and **philosophy of religion**, shaping discussions of **authority**, **tradition**, and **moral exemplarism** in contemporary theology and analytic philosophy.
