Bridging Word and World through expository preaching and the self-substitution of God in the cross

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Born in 1921 and educated at **Eton** and **Trinity College Cambridge**, **John R. W. Stott** emerged from mid-twentieth-century **British Anglican** contexts marked by the decline of establishment religion and the rise of postwar **evangelical renewal**. Ordained in 1945 and installed as Rector of **All Souls Langham Place** in 1950, he ministered in London while cultivating a global platform that culminated in his drafting of the **Lausanne Covenant** in 1974. His method was neither scholastic nor programmatic but pastoral and hermeneutical, summarized by his famous call to **double listening** to the **Word of God** and to the **world**. That maxim governed his **expository preaching**, his engagement with culture through **Issues Facing Christians Today**, and his reform of mission in **Christian Mission in the Modern World**. Theologically he was a broad **evangelical**, indebted to **Augustinian** and **Reformed** soteriology, yet Anglican in ecclesiology and temper, displaying an irenic catholicity that shaped his approach to **authority**, **mission**, and **ethics**.

Stott's ministry unfolded within contested ecclesial landscapes where **liberal Anglicanism**, resurgent **Pentecostalism**, and ecumenical **missio Dei** proposals pressured evangelical identity. His 1966 exchange with **D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones** crystallized his method, as he resisted separatist calls and advocated evangelical renewal within mixed denominations anchored in **Scripture** and the **Gospel**. The Langham Trust founded in 1969 and later the **Langham Partnership** and the **London Institute for Contemporary Christianity** in 1982 institutionalized his strategy to form scholar-preachers in the Majority World and to embed **expository preaching** within global evangelicalism. Stott's hermeneutic prized historical-grammatical exegesis disciplined by churchly application, captured in **Between Two Worlds** and **I Believe in Preaching**, where the preacher is a bridge bringing the **kerygma** across the cultural gap without loss of **biblical authority**.

#### 2. Scripture, preaching, and ecclesial posture
The axis of Stott's method is an unwavering conviction regarding the **authority and infallibility of Scripture**. In **Understanding the Bible** and **Evangelical Truth**, he argued that God has spoken with clarity and reliability, so that the church must submit intellect and life to the written **Word**. Though sympathetic to the Chicago tradition on **inerrancy**, Stott consistently preferred the British idiom of **infallibility**, insisting that Scripture, rightly interpreted according to genre and intent, is wholly trustworthy in all it affirms. This commitment generated his characteristic **expository preaching**, where the text governs the sermon's structure and aim, and where application is framed by the **lordship of Christ**. He urged preachers to reject both liberalism's reduction of the **kerygma** to moral uplift and fundamentalism's isolation from culture, commending instead a disciplined attentiveness to authors, contexts, canonical scope, and contemporary questions of justice, sexuality, and public life as elaborated in **Issues Facing Christians Today**.

Stott's ecclesial posture remained Anglican and catholic in scope. He sought evangelical unity across denominational lines, a stance that brought him into tension with **Lloyd-Jones** in 1966 when the latter called evangelicals to withdraw from mixed denominations. Stott pressed for reformation from within, insisting that fidelity to the **Gospel** and the **Scriptures** is not safeguarded by institutional separatism but by the ministry of **expository preaching** and the formation of local churches under the **Word**. This posture animated his cooperative leadership in the **Lausanne Movement**, where he championed confessional clarity and missional humility in the **Lausanne Covenant**, balancing the primacy of **evangelism** with the necessity of **social responsibility**.

#### 3. Atonement and the self-substitution of God
Stott's central dogmatic achievement is **The Cross of Christ** published in 1986, a sustained exposition of **penal substitution** as the heart of the New Testament witness to salvation. He framed the atonement as the **self-substitution of God**, integrating classic insights from **Anselm** and **Calvin** with biblical exegesis of **Isaiah 53**, **Romans 3**, and **2 Corinthians 5**. Against **C. H. Dodd's** reduction of **hilasterion** to mere expiation, Stott contended for both **propitiation and expiation**, arguing that the cross satisfies the holy wrath of God against sin while removing guilt. Engaging **Gustaf Aulen's** Christus Victor thesis, he acknowledged victory motifs yet insisted that victory is achieved through penal substitution rather than as an alternative. His rhetoric about divine initiative emphasized Trinitarian agency, so that the Father does not punish an innocent third party, but in the incarnate Son God bears the penalty that God alone can bear and remove.

This Trinitarian framing serves pastoral and apologetic ends. In a climate critical of **divine wrath**, Stott argued that the holy love of God necessitates judgment and that the cross uniquely manifests both divine **holiness** and divine **mercy**. He dismantled caricatures that associate penal substitution with cosmic child abuse by emphasizing the unity of will in the **homoousios** communion of Father and Son. He located ethical entailments in cruciform discipleship, so that justification by the **blood of Christ** yields a community of reconciliation and service. The book stands as a classic of evangelical dogmatics, marrying **biblical theology** with doctrinal clarity and pastoral urgency, and it set the standard for subsequent debates on atonement within global **evangelicalism**.

#### 4. Mission, culture, and the Lausanne vision
A second major contribution lies in Stott's recalibration of **mission**. In **Christian Mission in the Modern World** from 1975 and in his leadership at Lausanne in 1974 and subsequent consultations, he advanced a vision of **integral mission**. He maintained the theological primacy of **evangelism** as verbal proclamation of the **Gospel**, while arguing that **social action** is both a consequence of and a partner to evangelism. He critiqued ecumenical models that equated **missio Dei** with sociopolitical liberation and conservative models that restricted mission to soul-saving, pressing instead for a holistic response to human need that flows from the lordship of the risen **Christ**. The resulting **Lausanne Covenant** articulated a balanced theology that has governed evangelical networks ever since, endorsing both proclamation and justice, both church planting and creation care.

Stott's ethical commitments reinforced this missional synthesis. In **Issues Facing Christians Today** he addressed war, wealth, abortion, sexuality, and ecology with careful exegesis and principled reasoning. He promoted a **simple lifestyle**, arguing that affluent Christians should embrace economic restraint for the sake of global equity, an emphasis later integrated into Lausanne documents on lifestyle and poverty. His book **The Contemporary Christian** in 1992 sharpened his call to **double listening**, urging believers to exegete newspapers as well as Scripture without surrendering to the spirit of the age. Here Stott's burden was formation of the mind under **biblical authority**, captured in **Your Mind Matters** and institutionalized through the **London Institute for Contemporary Christianity**, which equipped laity for faithful presence in work and public life.

#### 5. Pneumatology and charismatic renewal
Stott's **pneumatology** is concisely set forth in **Baptism and Fullness** from 1964. He affirmed the person and work of the **Holy Spirit** in regeneration, sanctification, and mission, while offering a careful critique of the classical **Pentecostal** doctrine of a second baptism. For Stott, **Spirit baptism** is coextensive with conversion and incorporation into the body of **Christ**, as confirmed by **1 Corinthians 12** and **Romans 8**. He encouraged the exercise of **spiritual gifts** under biblical order and insisted on the primacy of the Spirit's fruit in Christlike character. His Anglican moderation aimed to guard against experientialism untethered from the **Word**, while avoiding a hard **cessationism**. This stance allowed him to welcome renewal where submissive to **Scripture**, even as he sustained a strong case for **expository preaching** as the ordinary means by which the Spirit edifies the church.

His ecclesial prudence was evident in dialogues with charismatic leaders in Britain and in the pastoral tenor of his writings. He urged churches to test all claims by the **canon** of Scripture and to cultivate congregational life that is both prayerful and biblically saturated. This commitment to ordered freedom reflects Stott's broader conviction that the **Gospel** liberates under lordship rather than through autonomy, a controlling theme of his discipleship works culminating in **The Radical Disciple** published in 2010, where he summarized marks of mature Christian life shaped by the cross, the **Spirit**, and the Scriptures.

#### 6. Creation, Adam, and eschatological judgment
On questions of creation and human origins, Stott represented a cautious evangelical openness to scientific discovery under **biblical authority**. In **The Message of Romans** from 1994 he explored the **Adam** of Romans 5 with sympathetic reference to **Derek Kidner's** proposal that a historical Adam could be a representative chosen from a wider hominid community. Stott did not insist on a sole-genetic progenitor model, but affirmed the theological headship of **Adam** and the historicity of sin's entrance through one man under covenantal arrangement. This posture exemplifies his method of **double listening**, seeking to hear both the exegetical voice of **Paul** and the credible findings of the natural sciences without capitulating to materialist reductionism.

On the doctrine of **hell**, Stott stirred debate by expressing a tentative preference for **annihilationism**, or conditional immortality, in an essay published within **Evangelical Essentials** in 1988 in dialogue with **David L. Edwards**. He appealed to texts that speak of destruction rather than endless conscious torment and raised moral concerns about proportionality, yet he avoided dogmatism and subordinated his view to the authority of **Scripture**. His treatment of final judgment elsewhere retained classic evangelical emphases on the return of **Christ**, resurrection, and accountability. The proposal on annihilationism revealed both his willingness to revisit traditional formulations through biblical exegesis and his loyalty to the overarching justice and mercy of God as revealed in the **cross of Christ**.

#### 7. Dialogue, controversy, and catholicity
Stott's career involved sustained dialogue with alternative Christian visions. His engagement with **liberal Anglicanism** is exemplified in **Evangelical Essentials**, a conversation with **David L. Edwards** defined by clarity without rancor. He defended **evangelical truth** as the historic faith grounded in Scripture and centered on **Christ crucified**, while conceding areas of legitimate openness such as creation questions and certain ethical applications. In mission debates he responded to **World Council of Churches** trends that identified **missio Dei** almost entirely with political liberation, arguing that without the **Gospel's** verbal proclamation and call to faith, social change loses its eschatological orientation. Conversely, he pressed conservative evangelicals to embrace justice and compassion as intrinsic to discipleship, not as optional add-ons to evangelism.

The 1966 disagreement with **D. Martyn Lloyd-Jones** exposed divergent ecclesiologies inside British evangelicalism. Stott resisted building a new evangelical denomination and insisted that unity must be doctrinal rather than sociological, with evangelical clergy remaining in historic churches to witness and reform. His model of catholicity was confessional and missional, centered on the **Scriptures** and the **Lausanne Covenant**, which he regarded as a basic evangelical confession for global partnership. This posture made Stott a bridge between North Atlantic evangelicals and emerging Majority World leaders, a bridge further strengthened by the **Langham Partnership**, which funded doctoral training and literature development to deepen **expository preaching** in Asia, Africa, and Latin America.

#### 8. Legacy, institutions, and moral vision
Beyond books and conferences, Stott's legacy lies in institutions and habits. The **Langham Partnership** championed indigenous theological leadership and supplied expository resources through the **Bible Speaks Today** series, for which Stott wrote multiple volumes including **The Message of the Sermon on the Mount** in 1978 and **The Message of Romans** in 1994. The **London Institute for Contemporary Christianity** incubated lay discipleship for public life, embodying his conviction in **The Contemporary Christian** that cultural engagement must be biblically normed. Stott's global itinerancy, humble lifestyle, and refusal to monetize his platform underscored his ethic of **simplicity**, featured in Lausanne statements on simple living and echoed in **The Radical Disciple**. He served as Chaplain to the Queen and remained Rector Emeritus at **All Souls**, yet he deliberately centered the ministry of the **Word** rather than personality or power.

His moral vision is thoroughly shaped by **the cross of Christ**. For Stott, the cross is not only atonement but also paradigm, summoning the church to servanthood, peacemaking, and truth telling. His critique of nationalism, violence, and greed drew from **Sermon on the Mount** ethics and from apostolic patterns of holiness. He mentored a generation of evangelical leaders who found in his combination of exegesis, charity, and courage a model for global evangelical maturity. As debates over **atonement**, **Scripture**, and **mission** continue, Stott's work remains a standard for evangelical dogmatics that seeks to be at once confessional, pastoral, and publicly engaged.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **Westminster Theological Seminary** perspective, Stott is a deeply allied yet occasionally divergent partner. His centering of **penal substitution**, his insistence on **biblical authority**, his pastoral commitment to **expository preaching**, and his high view of **Lordship** commend him strongly. His Trinitarian articulation of the **self-substitution of God** tracks with **Reformed orthodoxy** on satisfaction and imputation, and his critique of both liberal reduction and pietist withdrawal resonates with covenantal commitments to the lordship of **Christ** over all of life. Tensions arise, however, in several domains. On **Scripture**, Stott's preference for **infallibility** over the stricter idiom of **inerrancy** may strike confessional Reformed readers as insufficiently precise, where Westminster standards affirm verbal plenary inspiration and the total truthfulness of the autographic text. On **Adam** and origins, his openness to representative models that allow for hominid ancestry presses against **federal headship** as historically received in **covenant theology**, which typically insists on a single historical progenitor grounding original sin. On **hell**, his tentative **annihilationism** conflicts with the doctrine of eternal conscious punishment as confessed in classic **Reformed confessions**. On **mission**, his **integral mission** ethic is salutary yet requires careful calibration so that works of mercy flow from and bear witness to proclamation without collapsing into a transformationalist soteriology, a concern voiced by some **two-kingdoms** interpreters. On **pneumatology**, his cautious non-cessationism may be debated among stricter **cessationists**, though his practical subordination of gifts to the **Word** aligns with **means of grace** piety. The Reformed alternative would press more explicit **covenant** categories, a firmer confessional account of **inerrancy** and **federal headship**, and a sharper distinction between the church's primary ministry of **Word and sacrament** and the penultimate goods of social action, while gladly receiving Stott's towering exposition of the **cross**, his formation of **preachers**, and his global service to the church.
