### TITLE
Designing technologies to embody and advance moral values through Value Sensitive Design.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Historical context**: Late twentieth and early twenty-first century rise of **HCI**, **STS**, and debates over **ethics in computing** in the shadow of the **internet**, **ubiquitous data**, and **algorithmic governance**.
- **Method**: A tripartite **Value Sensitive Design** method that integrates **conceptual**, **empirical**, and **technical investigations** toward normative uptake in artifacts.
- **Key works**: **Human Values and the Design of Computer Technology** 1997, **Bias in Computer Systems** 1996, **Value Sensitive Design and Information Systems** 2004, **Envisioning Cards** 2012, **Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination** 2019.
- **Central categories**: **human values in design**, **direct and indirect stakeholders**, **value tensions**, **value dams and flows**, **multi-lifespan design**, **privacy**, **trust**, **accountability**, **dignity**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Helen Nissenbaum**, **Peter Kahn Jr.**, **Alan Borning**, **David Hendry**, **Don Norman**, **Langdon Winner**, **Bruno Latour**, **Peter-Paul Verbeek**, **Katie Shilton**.
- **Reformed cue**: Opens space for **common grace** engagement with technology while pressing questions about **teleology**, **sin**, **antithesis**, and **covenantal normativity** before God *coram Deo*.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and the emergence of a method
Batya Friedman’s enduring contribution is the articulation of **Value Sensitive Design**, a field-shaping method that insists technologies are never value-neutral but always already embed **moral values** through their architectures, interfaces, and infrastructures. Her work arises within the late twentieth century maturation of **Human-Computer Interaction** and **Science and Technology Studies**, where scholars like **Don Norman** emphasized usability and **Langdon Winner** famously argued that artifacts have politics. In this context Friedman positioned a constructive path between technological determinism and naive neutralism by making **normative analysis** intrinsic to design practice. The University of Washington Information School became a crucible for this agenda, linking **conceptual ethics**, **empirical studies**, and **technical prototyping**. The social horizon included the commercialization of the **web**, the spread of **databases** and **surveillance**, and nascent debates about **algorithmic bias**, all of which demanded a rigorous account of how **values** travel in code.

Friedman’s early formation drew on and challenged adjacent movements. **Participatory Design** foregrounded worker and user agency yet often lacked explicit ethical categories. **Privacy by Design** advanced a single value while sidelining trade-offs among **privacy**, **accountability**, and **security**. **Actor-Network Theory** and **postphenomenology** explored mediation, with figures like **Bruno Latour** and **Peter-Paul Verbeek** pressing on artifact agency, but with contested normative footing. Against these partialities, Friedman proposed **Value Sensitive Design** as a rational, iterative, and cumulative method that aims to incorporate **moral imagination** upstream in technology lifecycles. The thesis was simple and radical: design must be answerable to **human dignity**, **justice**, and other core values, not only to performance metrics.

#### 2. The VSD method as epistemic stance
At the methodological core of **Value Sensitive Design** lies a tripartite process. First, **conceptual investigations** identify and refine the relevant **values**, analyze how they may conflict, and clarify key distinctions such as **direct stakeholders** who interact with a system and **indirect stakeholders** who are affected by it without direct contact. Second, **empirical investigations** gather data on stakeholder experiences, social practices, and situated norms that bear on those values, often through interviews, surveys, and ethnography. Third, **technical investigations** prototype and evaluate design features that materialize the values, crafting constraints, affordances, and system properties that instantiate **privacy**, **trustworthiness**, or **accountability** in practice. Across all three, Friedman emphasizes **iterative alignment** and the norm that values should be treated as more than preferences, a claim elaborated in **Value Sensitive Design and Information Systems** 2004 and consolidated in **Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination** 2019.

Two further innovations stabilize the stance. The first is the formal handling of **value tensions**. Competing goods such as **privacy** and **accountability** or **autonomy** and **care** call for design judgments that neither collapse into utilitarian calculus nor retreat into ad hoc compromise. Friedman’s method operationalizes such tensions through tools like **value scenarios**, **value source analysis**, and **value hierarchies**, naming the relevant goods and mapping how interface and infrastructure choices impact them. The second innovation is the procedural device of **value dams and flows**, introduced to prioritize features based on stakeholders who cannot accept a value compromise and those who would benefit significantly. By attending to dams and flows, VSD offers a principled yet pragmatic way to adjudicate contested design choices within plural communities.

#### 3. Bias and the moral topology of sociotechnical systems
A watershed statement of Friedman’s perspective arrives in **Bias in Computer Systems** 1996 with **Helen Nissenbaum**. The article reframed the then vague charge that systems are biased by distinguishing **preexisting bias** rooted in social institutions and designers, **technical bias** emerging from constraints and formalizations, and **emergent bias** arising as systems interact with dynamic contexts. The analysis further differentiated **representational harms** that stereotype or denigrate from **allocational harms** that distribute opportunities and resources unfairly. That taxonomy has proven remarkably durable in later debates over **algorithmic fairness** and **machine learning**, anticipating the layered causality of bias across data, models, interfaces, and institutions. By revealing a moral topology of sociotechnical systems, the paper provided the diagnostic spine for **Value Sensitive Design** and set the agenda for integrating **justice** into the technical development process.

This bias framework also crystalized Friedman's insistence that reengineering is not merely a matter of tuning parameters but of reframing problem definitions and system boundaries. Consider **credit scoring**, **predictive policing**, or **content moderation**, where training data, optimization targets, and feedback loops all embed social imaginaries. VSD places those imaginaries under **normative scrutiny** and returns design responses that may range from transparency tools to structural redesign. In this, Friedman refused both cynicism that declares technology intrinsically corrupt and techno-utopianism that promises automatic redemption. She instead offered a morally serious path that binds **engineering judgment** to the task of **goods-realization**.

#### 4. Tools and artifacts for moral imagination
Friedman’s theory turned concrete through a suite of methods and artifacts. The **Envisioning Cards** 2012 created a tactile, portable toolkit to provoke reflection on **Stakeholders**, **Time**, **Values**, and **Permeability**, seeding conversations about long-term and wide-field effects. Team practices like **stakeholder analyses**, **value sketches**, and **design patterns for values** became staples for those who sought to move beyond checklist ethics. Within system development, her collaborations produced examples such as privacy-sensitive public transit information systems, value-integrated urban planning tools linked to **UrbanSim** with **Alan Borning**, and digital archives designed for contested histories where **accountability**, **authenticity**, and **respect for affected communities** needed to be designed in from the start.

A distinctive emphasis in this toolkit is attention to **indirect stakeholders** who often bear the externalities of platforms and infrastructures. By shifting the unit of analysis from user interface to **sociotechnical ecology**, Friedman seeded design that considers bystanders recorded by cameras, residents affected by data-centric policymaking, and the long tail of communities shaped by platform governance. This widening lens tracks with her signature interest in **public values**, where **trust**, **civility**, and **deliberation** are understood as design targets, not accidental byproducts.

#### 5. Multi-lifespan design and the ethics of time
Later work on **multi-lifespan design** pressed VSD beyond short innovation cycles toward problems that outlast any single human life. Friedman and collaborators argued that issues like **climate change**, **landmine remediation**, and **transitional justice** require information systems with **temporal resilience**, **intergenerational accountability**, and **institutional memory**. The design response included principles for data curation, stewardship, and governance that can survive leadership turnover and cultural shifts, and that cultivate values such as **hope**, **patience**, and **responsibility** over extended horizons. This turn complemented the VSD lens on stakeholders with a lens on **time**, placing the moral life of artifacts within a longue durée where **path dependency** and **legacy effects** demand careful moral foresight.

Theologically minded readers will notice the implicit eschatological register in this work. By urging designers to anticipate harms and goods across generations, Friedman gestures toward a moral telos of technology beyond immediate utility. Without invoking theological doctrine, **multi-lifespan design** resonates with traditions that speak of **stewardship**, *cura mundi*, and the cultivation of **common goods** that are neither privatized nor consumable on demand.

#### 6. Values catalogues and the normativity of value talk
A recurring criticism of design ethics is that value talk can degrade into preference aggregation. Friedman rejected this collapse by arguing in **Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination** 2019 for a catalog of **prima facie values** that have moral weight independent of desire or market pull. Common entries include **human well-being**, **dignity**, **privacy**, **autonomy**, **trust**, **accountability**, **informed consent**, **justice**, and **environmental sustainability**. While she avoids a single comprehensive moral theory, the stance is decidedly normative and has affinities with **rights discourse**, **Kantian respect**, and neo-Aristotelian articulations of **flourishing**. This strategic ecumenism supports coalition building within interdisciplinary teams while enabling concrete design directives such as data minimization for **privacy**, explanation facilities for **accountability**, and equitable default settings for **justice**.

To prevent drift into abstraction, VSD insists on binding these values to implementable requirements and measurable proxies, while acknowledging that proxies are always partial. The insistence that code can carry **ethical affordances** returns agency to the designer engineer who is responsible not only for what a system can do but for what a system makes easy, hard, or impossible to do. In this regard Friedman reframes the craft of computing as a venue for **virtue** and **practical wisdom**, where disciplined attention to **value tensions** is a marker of professional excellence.

#### 7. Interlocutors and debates across HCI and STS
The breadth of **Friedman’s interlocutors** marks VSD’s catholicity and its contested status. With **Helen Nissenbaum** she sharpened the account of bias and the place of **contextual integrity** in privacy. Engagements with **Bruno Latour** and **Peter-Paul Verbeek** drew a line between strong claims of artifact agency and the VSD approach that treats artifacts as mediators with moral import without displacing human responsibility. Comparative dialogue with **Participatory Design** underscored VSD’s complementarity as it champions stakeholder voice while retaining an explicit normativity that participatory methods sometimes bracket. Exchanges with **Katie Shilton** and values in design frameworks clarified methodological differences and shared aspirations. Conversations with **Don Norman** and the usability community pressed VSD to show that value advances can align with or fruitfully constrain usability goals rather than oppose them.

Critiques have been instructive. Some argue that VSD lacks a thick, unified theory of value, which can dull its edge when confronting deep **structural injustice**. Others worry about corporate capture, where procedural value talk can be domesticated within product cycles. Friedman’s replies, implicit and explicit, emphasize practice-tested tools, stakeholder inclusion, and the steady work of **institutionalizing ethics** in engineering education and industry. Whatever one’s stance, the debates themselves have advanced the field by forcing clarity regarding the **normative sources**, **scope**, and **limits** of design ethics.

#### 8. Theological traction and horizons for public theology
Though not a theologian, Friedman’s work carries substantial theological traction. The insistence that technologies mediate **moral relations** and can be oriented toward **human dignity** invites dialogue with Christian accounts of the **imago Dei** and the duty of **neighbor love**. The handling of **bias** as preexisting, technical, and emergent aligns with doctrines of **sin** as both personal and structural, making **repentance** and **reparative design** intelligible at scale. The distinction between **direct and indirect stakeholders** echoes the biblical attention to the **poor**, **stranger**, and those at the margins who are nonetheless harmed by the gates of the city. The **multi-lifespan** horizon resonates with *ordo creationis*, the created order, and with stewardship that spans generations. One can thus translate VSD’s **value tensions** into theological virtues and norms, treating design as a practice where **prudence**, **justice**, **temperance**, and **fortitude** guide engineering choices under the claim of **truth** and **love**.

This traction does not collapse VSD into theology. Rather, VSD provides an interlingua by which religious and secular actors can collaborate on **public goods** without settling deep metaphysical disputes. Its posture is procedural and dialogical, seeking principled accommodation where possible and honest naming of conflict where necessary. For theologians concerned with **technology and ethics**, Friedman’s corpus sketches a way to participate in design conversations with specificity, moving from sermons about **idolatries of technique** to code, interfaces, and governance structures that better serve the **common good**.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** standpoint, Friedman’s **Value Sensitive Design** is a gift of **common grace** that recovers the moral weight of artifacts and calls engineers to **responsible dominion** under God. Its attention to **indirect stakeholders** honors **neighbor love**, its analysis of **bias** squares with the **noetic effects of sin**, and its **multi-lifespan** sensibility reflects the creational rhythms of time and responsibility. The method’s procedural seriousness resonates with **sphere sovereignty** and principled pluralism, where differentiated institutions seek the **shalom** of the city. Yet several tensions remain. First, VSD’s ecumenical catalog of values lacks an explicit **teleology** ordered to God, which can flatten **justice** into negotiated equilibrium rather than covenantal faithfulness before God *coram Deo*. Second, absent a doctrine of **antithesis**, VSD may underestimate how **idolatry** and disordered loves corrupt even well-structured design processes, especially where profit logics rule. Third, VSD’s stakeholder model risks anthropocentrism, whereas Reformed theology would widen moral consideranda to include the **nonhuman creation** as a neighbor to be kept and served. A constructive alternative is **covenantal design** that receives VSD’s tools within a thicker normativity drawn from the **Decalogue**, the **lex charitatis**, the law of charity, and the creational mandate. This reframing would specify design ends by vocations and spheres, measure **accountability** not only by auditability but by truthfulness, and treat **privacy** and **transparency** as ordered to covenantal fidelity and justice. In practice, Reformed communities can adopt VSD’s **conceptual empirical technical** triad while submitting value catalogs to Scripture and confessional standards, naming sin where value tensions mask moral evasion, and orienting **moral imagination** to the hope of renewal in Christ that sustains patient **multi-lifespan** work for the life of the world.

- **Core problem**: How to embed **moral values** in technology, rejecting both **value-neutrality** and **technological determinism** through **Value Sensitive Design**.
- **Major works**: **Bias in Computer Systems** 1996, **Human Values and the Design of Computer Technology** 1997, **Value Sensitive Design and Information Systems** 2004, **Envisioning Cards** 2012, **Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination** 2019.
- **Defining tools**: **conceptual empirical technical** investigations, **direct and indirect stakeholders**, **value tensions**, **value dams and flows**, **multi-lifespan design**.
- **Key interlocutors**: **Helen Nissenbaum** on bias and privacy, **Alan Borning** on urban systems, **David Hendry** on moral imagination, debates with **Latour** and **Verbeek** on mediation.
- **Reformed evaluation**: Strong **common grace** resource for **neighbor love** and **stewardship**, yet needs explicit **teleology**, account of **sin and antithesis**, and inclusion of **creation care** within **covenantal design**.
- **Legacy**: Institutionalized **design ethics** practices, durable taxonomies of **bias**, widely used **VSD toolkits**, and an enduring template for value-oriented technology in service of **public goods**.
