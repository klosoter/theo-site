### TITLE
Baptized in the Spirit: An ecumenical, eschatological, and ecclesial re-centering of soteriology around the gift and mission of the Holy Spirit

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary Pentecostalism**, emerging from **Azusa Street** to global Christianity, informs Macchia's work within late modern **ecumenical theology** and **renewed Trinitarian dogmatics**.
- A **pneumatological method** that is **eschatological**, **ecclesial**, and **participatory**, reframing **justification** and **sanctification** through **Spirit baptism** and the **missio Dei**.
- Key works: **Baptized in the Spirit**, 2006; **Justified in the Spirit**, 2010; **The Spirit-Baptized Church**, 2020; **Tongues of Fire: A Systematic Theology of the Christian Faith**, 2023.
- Central categories: **Spirit baptism**, **initial evidence**, **glossolalia**, **Kingdom eschatology**, **perichoresis**, **kenosis**, **sacramentality**, **communion ecclesiology**, **participatory justification**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **William J. Seymour**, **John Wesley**, **Karl Barth**, **Jürgen Moltmann**, **Wolfhart Pannenberg**, **Yves Congar**, **John Zizioulas**, and Pentecostal peers **Amos Yong**, **Simon Chan**, **Gordon Fee**.
- Reformed reception cue: tensions around **sola fide and forensic justification**, **union with Christ and the ordo salutis**, **Word and sacrament as ordinary means**, and the **redemptive-historical uniqueness of Pentecost**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Frank D. Macchia belongs to the first generation of constructive **Pentecostal systematic theologians** who matured after the movement’s explosive twentieth-century growth and its complex **ecumenical encounters**. Teaching long term at **Vanguard University** and serving as a past president in the **Society for Pentecostal Studies**, he operates in the space where **classical Pentecostal spirituality** is interrogated and refined by **modern Trinitarian theology** and **global ecclesial dialogues**. The backdrop is a post-Azusa Street world in which **William J. Seymour’s** radical emphasis on the **Spirit’s outpouring** meets the intellectual aftershocks of **Barth’s christocentrism**, **Moltmann’s eschatology**, and **Pannenberg’s history of revelation**. Macchia’s signature proposal is that **Spirit baptism** functions not as a marginal revivalist motif but as the most fitting symbol of the **gospel’s eschatological fullness** in the **people of God**.

Methodologically, Macchia advances a **pneumatological soteriology** that is programmatically **ecclesial** and **eschatological**. He resists a merely experiential or individualistic **charismatic emphasis**, recuperating **patristic theosis** motifs and **communion ecclesiology** while insisting on the **Pentecostal praxis of charisms**. He invokes **perichoresis** as a grammar for divine communion and **kenosis** as the humility of the triune missions, integrating **Spirit Christology** with the **missio Dei**. The result is an approach that treats **Spirit baptism** as a theological master trope for **initiation**, **empowerment**, and **sanctification**, reshaping **justification** without abandoning the **forensic dimension**.

#### 2. Sources, interlocutors, and constructive stance
Macchia’s interlocutors are ecumenically broad and doctrinally weighty. From **John Wesley**, he receives a vocabulary of **holiness** and **perfecting love**; from **Barth**, an insistence on **God’s sovereign grace** and the primacy of **revelation**; from **Moltmann**, a **social Trinitarian** and **eschatological** horizon; from **Pannenberg**, a vision of truth as **proleptic** and historically **vindicated**; from **Yves Congar** and **John Zizioulas**, a **communion ecclesiology** that binds **charism** and **office**, **Spirit** and **sacrament**. He is also in sustained conversation with **Pentecostal exegetes** such as **Gordon Fee**, constructive theologians like **Amos Yong** and **Simon Chan**, and the early Pentecostal witness of **Seymour**, whose multiracial **Azusa Street revival** testified to the **eschatological reconciliation** that Macchia now renders dogmatically as **Spirit-baptized ecclesiology**.

Theologically, Macchia employs an **analogy of gift** rather than an **analogia entis** or a narrowly **analogia fidei**. The **Spirit** as gift and giver becomes the key to **creation’s gratuity**, **reconciliation’s renewal**, and the **church’s charismata**. This yields a hermeneutic that privileges **Acts 2** as the **Basileia**-inaugurating event and reads **glossolalia** as the **Babel reversal** and sign of **inclusion of the nations**. His program seeks to expand **justification by faith** into **justification in the Spirit**, a participatory and communal act of God that does not eliminate **forensic declaration** but enfolds it within **pneumatological incorporation** and **missional vocation**.

#### 3. Baptized in the Spirit, 2006
Macchia’s pivotal work, **Baptized in the Spirit** published 2006, reframes **Spirit baptism** as the ecumenical symbol for the **gospel’s fullness**. Rather than defending **subsequence** and **initial evidence** as dogmatic shibboleths, he proposes **Spirit baptism** as an integrating motif that encompasses **conversion-initiation**, **sanctification**, and **empowerment for witness**. He reads **Luke-Acts** as narrating the **eschatological outpouring** whereby the **church** is constituted as the **Spirit-immersed body**. The language of baptism evokes **sacramentality** without collapsing into **ritualism**; the **sign** lies in the **charismatic overflow** whose paradigmatic token is **glossolalia**, understood as a symbolic eruption of the **age to come** within the **present age**. 

Key to this reframing is a widened ecclesial lens. **Spirit baptism** is fundamentally corporate, creating a **priestly-prophetic people** whose **charisms** serve **edification** and **mission**. The sign of tongues, argued as **initial sign** rather than a universal mandatory **evidence**, bespeaks the **democratization of the Spirit** and the **mission to the nations**. The work appeals to **patristic pneumatology**, to **Wesleyan holiness**, and to **Moltmann’s theology of hope**, but remains tethered to **classical Pentecostal instincts** about **power for witness**. The outcome is an ecumenically generous yet distinctly **Pentecostal pneumatology** that relocates debates over **subsequence** into a broader **eschatological ecclesiology**.

#### 4. Justified in the Spirit, 2010
In **Justified in the Spirit** published 2010, Macchia presses the thesis that **justification** must be construed as **pneumatological participation** in addition to **forensic pardon**. He receives from **Luther** and **Calvin** the priority of **divine judgment graciously rendered**, yet argues via **Barth** and **Moltmann** that the **Spirit’s unitive agency** in **Christ** grounds a more capacious vision of **righteousness**. Justification becomes the **eschatological verdict** of God, announced ahead of time by the **Spirit** who unites believers to **Christ’s faithfulness**, reshapes their **agency in love**, and enfolds them within a **people made righteous**. 

Macchia thus refuses to isolate **imputation** from **incorporation**. He speaks of **participatory justification**, in which the **Spirit** not only applies but also **enacts** the verdict by regenerating people into **Christ’s new humanity**. This is not a denial of **forensic justification** but an insistence that **δικαίωσις** as **eschatological gift** has **transformative** dimensions. He extends the scope to **creation**, proposing a cosmic breadth that resonates with **Romans 8**, where the **Spirit** groans toward **new creation**. Engagements with the **Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification**, **Roman Catholic** accounts of **grace**, and **Wesleyan** themes of **holiness** mark the book as a model of **ecumenical dogmatics** within a **Pentecostal frame**.

#### 5. The Spirit-Baptized Church, 2020
In **The Spirit-Baptized Church** published 2020, Macchia delivers a full **ecclesiology** ordered by **Spirit baptism**. The church is the **sacrament of the Kingdom**, formed by the **Pentecostal effusion** that mediates **Christ’s exalted presence**. He correlates **Word**, **sacrament**, and **charism** as the triadic grammar of ecclesial life, resisting dichotomies that pit **charismatic spontaneity** against **sacramental order**. The **charisms** are not optional extras but constitutive manifestations of the **Spirit’s economy**, oriented to **koinonia**, **mission**, and **holiness**. He retrieves **Yves Congar’s** emphasis on the **Spirit’s role in ecclesial structures**, yet maintains a **prophetic edge** by championing the **democratization** and **discernment** of gifts through the **whole body**.

A pivotal motif here is **glossolalia as sign**. The phenomenon is treated as a **sacramental symbol** of **eschatological inclusion**, an audible doxology of **creation’s praise**, and an enacted **Babel reversal** signaling the **gathering of the nations**. Macchia locates **tongues** within a theology of **prayer and praise**, a pedagogy of **desire**, and an ethic of **hospitality** to the **other**. The book presses toward a **communal holiness** shaped by **justice** and **mercy**, contending that **Spirit baptism** inherently disrupts **social hierarchies** and empowers **mission from the margins**. The result is a vision of the **church** as the **Spirit’s temple**, gifted for **witness** and **service** under the **eschatological Lordship of Christ**.

#### 6. Tongues of Fire, 2023, and the integrative system
With **Tongues of Fire: A Systematic Theology of the Christian Faith** published 2023, Macchia expands his **pneumatological thesis** into a full **dogmatic system**. The work is doxologically driven, tying **theology proper**, **creation**, **Christology**, **atonement**, **salvation**, **church**, and **eschatology** to the **Pentecost event** as hermeneutical key. He develops a **Trinitarian theology of gift**, portraying the **Father** as fount of **gratuity**, the **Son** as the **gift given** in **kenotic love**, and the **Spirit** as the **giver who indwells** and leads creation into the **Sabbath of God**. **Perichoresis** functions to safeguard divine **unity** and **communion**, while a nuanced approach to the **filioque** proposes the **Spirit’s procession from the Father through the Son**, keeping Eastern **monarchia** and Western **communio** in dynamic tension.

Throughout the system, **Pentecost** frames the **economy of salvation** as a **doxological movement** from **creation** to **new creation**. **Christology** is rendered in **Spirit Christology** accents without compromising **homoousios**, and **atonement** is described as **participatory reconciliation** that opens to **Spirit-filled transformation**. The **ordo salutis** is recast as **event and process** within **Spirit baptism’s** encompassing grace, while **ethics** is approached as the **fruit of the Spirit** ordering communal **holiness** and **public witness**. The system makes **glossolalia** emblematic of a **praise-saturated epistemology**, where knowing God is a **prayerful participation** in the **triune life** rather than a mere **propositional mastery**.

#### 7. Specific doctrinal developments: Spirit, sacraments, and mission
Macchia’s treatment of **subsequence and initial evidence** is pastoral and constructive. He honors classical **Pentecostal testimony** to **post-conversion empowerment**, yet integrates this within a broader account where **Spirit baptism** names the **eschatological fullness** of **Christian initiation**. Hence he prefers the language of **initial sign** to **initial evidence**, holding that **glossolalia** is paradigmatic but not universal, and that the **Spirit’s sovereignty** governs the economy of **charisms**. This recalibration aims to preserve the **Acts-pattern** emphasis on **witness** and **mission** while disarming sectarian claims that **tongues** is mandatory proof of **Spirit reception**.

On **sacramentality**, Macchia proposes a mutual indwelling of **sacrament** and **charism**, arguing that **baptism** and **Eucharist** are intensified by **charismatic worship**, and that **charisms** are ordered by the church’s **sacramental life** under **Word and Spirit**. He warns against a **bare memorialism** that mutes **pneumatological presence** and against a **charismatic antinomianism** that despises **ecclesial form**. In **mission**, he adopts a **missio Dei** posture where **Spirit baptism** is empowerment for **witness**, **justice**, and **mercy**, pressing the church toward **reconciliation** across **ethnic**, **economic**, and **gendered** divides as an outflow of **eschatological peace**.

#### 8. Scripture, tradition, and ecumenical dialogues
For Macchia, **Scripture** retains normative authority within a **traditioned logic** of **Spirit-led reception**. The **Pentecostal hermeneutic** highlights **narrative** and **testimony**, especially **Acts**, yet he anchors charismatic experience in a **canonical rule of faith** derived from **patristic** and **conciliar** trajectories. He participates in **World Council of Churches Faith and Order** conversations and **International Catholic-Pentecostal Dialogue**, pressing for convergence on **Trinity**, **salvation**, and **church** while preserving **Pentecostal distinctives** of **charisms** and **witness**. The constructive outcome is a **communion ecclesiology** that embraces **diversity of gifts** as essential to the **church’s catholicity**, an insight he draws from **Zizioulas** and **Congar** and renders Penteco-stally through **Spirit baptism**.

This ecumenical posture also shapes his stance on the **filioque** and **pneumatological language**. He proposes the procession of the **Spirit** from the **Father through the Son** as a way to honor **Eastern pneumatology** while maintaining Western insights into **Trinitarian communion**. The practical implication is a church that is both **ordered** and **open**, faithful to **apostolic doctrine** while expectant of **new charisms**, united by **Word and sacrament** yet enlivened by **prophetic praise**. Here **glossolalia** serves, again, as a **sign** of the Spirit’s unmasterable freedom and the church’s **eschatological vocation** to be a **house of prayer for all nations**.

#### 9. Evaluation and influence
Macchia’s influence within **Pentecostal theology** is considerable. He has helped to shift the center of gravity from defensive **evidence doctrine** debates toward a constructive **pneumatological dogmatics** that can enter the **ecumenical mainstream**. His comprehensive articulation across **Baptized in the Spirit**, **Justified in the Spirit**, **The Spirit-Baptized Church**, and **Tongues of Fire** gives Pentecostalism a mature **systematic voice**, marked by **Trinitarian depth**, **eschatological scope**, and **ecclesial concreteness**. He models a **doxological epistemology** where **praise**, **prayer**, and **charism** are not add-ons to doctrine but its liturgical heart. By reframing **justification** and **church** through **Spirit baptism**, he provides a platform for future work on **sacramental-charismatic integration**, **mission from the margins**, and **creation’s renewal**.

Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation
From a confessional **Reformed** perspective, Macchia’s work invites both gratitude and critique. Positively, his **Christ-Spirit** focus underscores **union with Christ**, the **Spirit’s indwelling**, and the **missionary nature** of the church, aligning with a **redemptive-historical** reading that sees **Pentecost** as the gift of the **age to come**. His retrieval of **Word and Spirit** together and his concern for **ecclesial holiness** and **justice** resonate with a robust **covenant theology**. Yet tensions remain. First, his **participatory justification** risks blurring the **forensic priority** of **sola fide**, where **imputation** grounds all **sanctifying participation**; a classical alternative would insist on **forensic justification** as the exclusive ground, with **Spirit-wrought union** applying that verdict without constituting it by inherent renewal. Second, his **Spirit baptism** as ecumenical master symbol can obscure the **redemptive-historical uniqueness** of **Pentecost**, which Reformed theology locates as a once-for-all epochal advance, not a repeatable **subsequence**; a covenantal alternative centers **Spirit reception** at **effectual calling** through **Word and sacrament**, with any extraordinary **charisms** judged by **Scripture** and not normed for every congregation. Third, his **sacramental-charismatic** convergence and **glossolalia as sacramental sign** challenge the **ordinary means of grace** paradigm, where **preaching**, **baptism**, and the **Lord’s Supper** are sufficient and central; a Reformed corrective would subordinate all **charisms** to the **ministry of the Word**, cautioning against experiential criteria that might unsettle **assurance**. Finally, his **kenotic** and **social Trinitarian** accents risk attenuating **divine simplicity** and **aseity**; a classical **Reformed dogmatics** would retrieve **simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, and **autotheos** of the Son to guard the **immanent Trinity** while celebrating the **Spirit’s missions**. In sum, Macchia’s constructive Pentecostalism presses Reformed theology to deepen its **pneumatology** and **ecclesiology**, even as Reformed orthodoxy offers a principled framework of **revelation**, **covenant**, and **means of grace** to test and refine his proposals.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: A leading voice in **contemporary Pentecostal theology**, shaped by **Azusa Street’s legacy**, global **ecumenical dialogues**, and renewed **Trinitarian dogmatics**.
- **Core problem**: How to render **Spirit baptism** as the central, ecumenically credible symbol for **soteriology** and **ecclesiology** within an **eschatological** horizon.
- **Major works**: **Baptized in the Spirit** 2006, **Justified in the Spirit** 2010, **The Spirit-Baptized Church** 2020, **Tongues of Fire** 2023, all advancing a **pneumatological system**.
- **Distinctive positions**: **Participatory justification**, **glossolalia as initial sign**, **sacramental-charismatic integration**, **Spirit Christology**, and **perichoretic Trinitarianism** with **kenotic missions**.
- **Interlocutors**: Engagement with **Wesley**, **Barth**, **Moltmann**, **Pannenberg**, **Congar**, **Zizioulas**, alongside **Pentecostal** peers **Yong**, **Chan**, **Fee**, and the heritage of **Seymour**.
- **Reformed critique**: Guard **forensic justification** and **union with Christ** via **Word and sacrament**, affirm **Pentecost’s uniqueness**, and test **charisms** by **Scripture** within **ordinary means of grace**.
- **Legacy**: Consolidated an ecumenically conversant **Pentecostal dogmatics**, repositioned **Spirit baptism** at the center, and catalyzed ongoing work on **church**, **mission**, and **eschatological hope**.
