### TITLE
Technology makes selves: from simulation culture to the ethics of conversation as a humanizing counter-technique.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Cold War to platform capitalism**, MIT **AI culture** and postmodern theory form the backdrop to Turkle’s **contemporary humanism**.
- **Psychoanalytic ethnography**, qualitative **clinical interviews**, and **co-constructionist sociology of technology** govern her method.
- Key works: **The Second Self** 1984, **Life on the Screen** 1995, **Evocative Objects** 2007, **Simulation and Its Discontents** 2009, **Alone Together** 2011, **Reclaiming Conversation** 2015, **The Empathy Diaries** 2021.
- Central terms: **the second self**, **identity play**, **simulation culture**, **sociable robots**, **the Goldilocks effect**, **the flight from conversation**, **empathy crisis**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Seymour Papert**, **Winnicott**, **Freud**, **Weizenbaum**, with conversation partners like **Goffman**, **Baudrillard**, **Heidegger** and **Ellul** in the background of **technology critique**.
- Reformed cue: her defense of **embodiment, presence, limit, and sabbath-like spaces** overlaps with **imago Dei** and exposes **idolatry of technique**, yet lacks **revelatory grounding**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Sherry Turkle emerges within the late twentieth-century ecology of **MIT**, where the optimism of **artificial intelligence** meets the sobering witness of **humanistic psychoanalysis**. Born in 1948, she came of age academically as **ARPANET** matured into the internet, and she watched the **AI Lab** debate between engineering hubris and critique modeled by **Joseph Weizenbaum’s Computer Power and Human Reason**. Her intellectual formation marries **Freud** and **Winnicott** to **Seymour Papert’s constructionism**, giving her a method that is neither technological determinism nor naive instrumentalism. She calls computers not just tools but **evocative objects**, artifacts that solicit stories and reveal **the second self**, the self mirrored and shaped through digital engagement. Her method is a psychoanalytically attuned **ethnography of human-computer interaction**, a clinical interviewing posture that listens for transferences, defenses, and desire. The setting is crucial, because **Cold War cybernetics**, the turn to **postmodern theory**, and the emergence of **platform capitalism** generate both the ideology of **simulation culture** and the hunger for **presence** that her work will thematize.

Turkle’s epistemic stance is interpretive and reparative, a **co-constructionist** claim that technologies and subjects constitute each other within concrete practices. She rejects simple **technological instrumentalism**, arguing that media have affordances that invite particular **forms of selfhood**, while also resisting substantive technological fatalism. Her norm is a humanist telos of **empathy, conversation, and attention**, defended through thick description and clinical judgment. This is neither a theology of culture nor a moral philosophy of first principles, but a **practical anthropology** of everyday life under the sign of the screen. The keywords are **identity play**, **tethered selves**, **the Goldilocks effect**, and eventually **the flight from conversation**, each naming a structural temptation made ordinary by digital devices. The outcome is a distinctive American voice that brings **psychoanalytic listening** to **technology studies**, and positions **presence** as the criterion for the good life.

#### 2. The Second Self and identity as technology
In **The Second Self** 1984 Turkle announced the thesis that computers are mirrors in which persons discover and fashion **selves**, not merely calculators for external tasks. Observing early personal computing and children programming with **LOGO**, she depicts the machine as a laboratory for **epistemological styles**, contrasting **planner** and **bricoleur** approaches that echo **Papert’s constructionism**. The computer becomes an object to think with, an **evocative object** that reveals how people understand agency, causality, and intimacy. The conceptual anchor is **the second self**, the notion that human identity takes a technological doubling that feeds back into first-order subjectivity. Identity thus appears as iterative and plastic, a set of scripts performed in dialogue with artifacts, anticipating later work on **identity play** and **presentation of self**.

Theologically read, this early book unsettles thin accounts of **personhood** by showing how practices with **tools** shape the formation of **habitus**. Her case studies demonstrate that skill acquisition is never merely technical but moral, cultivating postures of attention and responsibility. While Turkle does not invoke **virtue ethics** or the **imago Dei**, her thick descriptions imply that use generates character, and that artifacts are **liturgical** in effect even when secular. The key contrast is between **instrumental mastery** and **relational engagement**, with Turkle preferring modes of interaction that encourage reflection and dialogue. Already the seeds of her later critique of **distraction** and **empathy erosion** are present, though here framed as possibilities latent in **playful construction** rather than as culture-wide crises.

#### 3. Life on the Screen and the postmodern self
By **Life on the Screen** 1995 the horizon shifts to networked worlds, from MUDs and chat rooms to the early **web**. Turkle’s analysis incorporates **Goffman’s dramaturgical self** and **Baudrillard’s simulation**, portraying identity as **multiple, distributed, and playful** across online venues. The screen does not simply represent reality, it becomes a site where reality is constructed through **simulation** and performance. The book refuses moral panic, instead lingers over **identity play** as a potentially liberating exploration of self, especially for those constrained by offline roles. She coins, and then problematizes, the idea of being **alone together**, tethered to others yet buffered by screens, a phrase that will become the emblem of her later ethical unease.

Two things are noteworthy theologically. First, the book raises a constructive challenge to confessional accounts of **unity of person** by documenting experiential multiplicity without collapsing into relativism. Second, it heightens the stakes of **presence and absence**, since digital mediation blurs proximity and distance, rendering virtual co-presence a rival to face-to-face encounter. The emerging norm is the cultivation of **self-reflection** and **empathic conversation** across media, yet already the affordances of **asynchronicity** and **curated exposure** invite what she later names the **Goldilocks effect**, connection at a personally optimized temperature. Thus the promise of postmodern play is shadowed by the danger of moral convenience.

#### 4. Simulation and Its Discontents: epistemology under the screen
In **Simulation and Its Discontents** 2009 Turkle turns from identity to knowledge. Her ethnography of architects, engineers, and scientists documents a shift from **modeling with materials** to **screen-based simulation**, and the educational and professional consequences of that shift. She tracks the erosion of what practitioners call **feel for the material**, a tacit knowledge once acquired through contact with **resistance, friction, and failure**. The book narrates disorientation among students who excel at simulation but falter when confronting physical reality, prompting anxiety about **verification and validation** and about diminished capacity for **judgment** under complexity.

Theologically, this is a meditation on **epistemic humility** and **creaturely limits**. The simulation turn promises **omni-competence**, a quasi-gnostic escape into a world without splinters. Turkle registers a humanist protest, insisting on the pedagogy of **resistance** for any truthful knowledge of the world. This resonates with doctrines of **creation** as stubbornly given, and of **wisdom** as trained discernment within limits. Her critique of **frictionless technics** sits adjacent to classic Christian warnings against **hubris**, and to critiques by **Heidegger** and **Ellul** of **enframing** and **la technique**. Turkle remains empirical and local, but her judgment names a civilizational temptation toward false transcendence through simulation.

#### 5. Alone Together and the robotic moment
**Alone Together** 2011 consolidates Turkle’s darker turn. The first half studies **sociable robots** such as **Furbies**, **AIBO**, **Paro**, and MIT’s **Kismet**, probing the **robotic moment** when people ask not what machines think but what relationships they want with them. She argues that robots offer **cheap empathy**, engineered responsiveness without the burdens of reciprocity. The second half diagnoses **device culture** and the **tethered self**, where smartphones and social media reconfigure attention, intimacy, and solitude. The **Goldilocks effect** names our preference for relationships that are not too close and not too distant, curated to our comfort. The upshot is an **empathy crisis**, especially among the young, who trade **conversation** for **connection** and **performance** for presence.

This is Turkle’s moral center. She defends **solitude** as the condition for true togetherness, an anthropological claim later summarized as you are what you **attend** to. The book functions as a secular homily for practices of **presence**, testifying to the costs of ambient anxiety and continuous partial attention. Though she writes without appeal to **soul** or **sacrament**, her portrait of **hunger for recognition** and the simulacra that answer it invites theological translation into **desire**, **idolatry**, and **neighbor-love**. The contrast is stark: **encounter with a Thou** or **management of It**, echoing **Buber’s I-Thou** against the commodification of relation. Turkle thus calls for re-humanizing limits, places where persons are not optimized but received.

#### 6. Reclaiming Conversation and the ethics of attention
With **Reclaiming Conversation** 2015 Turkle articulates a practical program. The telos is **empathy**, and the chief means is **face-to-face conversation** safeguarded by **solitude** and mindful **attention**. Drawing on school, workplace, and family case studies, she argues that conversation builds **capacities for perspective-taking**, moral imagination, and civic trust that cannot be replaced by text threads or emoji. She proposes **device-free zones**, **Sabbath-like intervals**, and what she names the **seven-minute rule**, leaving enough unfilled time for real talk to begin. The book is anti-ascetic only in appearance. It is in fact a call to intentional **discipline**, the formation of **habitus** through ordinary practices that resist the logic of **interruptibility**.

What grounds this ethic is a vision of the person as vulnerable and relational, dependent on **attention** as the medium of love. Turkle’s prescription aligns with broader worries about **surveillance capitalism** and the commodification of **time** and **care**, yet her emphasis remains the moral interior of **empathy** rather than structural regulation. She does not deny design or policy levers, but she prioritizes practices by which households, classrooms, and teams re-inscribe **presence**. In theological idiom, her proposals resemble a secular **rule of life**, trading in small renunciations for the sake of communion. The contrasts are **conversation vs connection**, **presence vs performance**, **formation vs frictionless ease**.

#### 7. Evocative Objects, memoir, and the listening stance
Between her major arguments, Turkle curated a set of volumes, **Evocative Objects** 2007, **Falling for Science** 2008, and **The Inner History of Devices** 2008, that instantiate her method. These projects practice **object relations** in cultural key, treating things as companions to thought. The essays exhibit her pedagogical conviction that artifacts carry **biographies** that, when narrated, disclose the **self**. Later, **The Empathy Diaries** 2021, a memoir, renders explicit the psychoanalytic core of her stance, tracing how early experiences of secrecy, naming, and belonging trained her ear for **ambivalence** and **desire** in technological life. Her memoir does not revise her theses so much as root them in a vocation of **listening**.

This listening is the through-line from **The Second Self** to **Reclaiming Conversation**. It explains her faith in **clinical interviewing**, her preference for the **case study** over abstraction, and her confidence that telling one’s story in the presence of an attentive other can be reparative. In effect, Turkle proposes a modest **cure of souls** in the clinic of ordinary life, with the screen as symptom and setting. While she sometimes gestures to policy and design, she keeps returning to **presence**, as if to say that in a culture of simulation the most countercultural act is to pay attention. The theological resonance is obvious: the grammar of **presence**, **attention**, and **confession** stands at the heart of many traditions of **pastoral care** and **spiritual direction**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, contrasts, and developing judgments
Turkle’s work sits among several canons. With **Papert**, she shares a constructivist faith in learning by making, yet she is more alert to the psycho-social risks of **simulation**. With **Weizenbaum**, she shares suspicion of **AI** as a moral provocateur, but she does not dramatize the threat as machine autonomy so much as human abdication of **difficult empathy**. In the broader discourse of technology critique, her judgments harmonize with **Heidegger’s enframing** and **Ellul’s la technique**, yet she refuses metaphysical pronouncement in favor of **clinical description**. Across media studies, she diverges from **techno-optimism** by arguing that design affordances structure the **self**, even while denying hard determinism. Her argument is thus a distinct **humanistic substantivism**, an empirically grounded claim that technologies are not neutral because humans are not neutral users.

Her trajectory also displays development. **Life on the Screen** entertained a playful postmodern multiplicity under the rubric of **identity play**, while **Alone Together** and **Reclaiming Conversation** voice a crisis of **empathy** and a prescriptive ethic of **attention**. This is neither a reversal nor a contradiction. It is a judgment that the ecology of platforms, smartphones, and **sociable robots** has tipped the balance from possibility to harm. The phrase **alone together** thus functions first as description of a new condition, then as indictment of a refusal of **presence**. Throughout, the criterion remains **human flourishing** as measured by the health of **conversation**, a standard she defends through listening rather than through first principles.

#### 9. Theological resonances and constructive uses
Although Turkle writes as a secular humanist, her corpus invites explicit theological appropriation. Her defense of **face-to-face presence** dovetails with Christian claims about **incarnation** and **embodied communion**, and her insistence that **solitude** is the seedbed of togetherness resonates with the disciplines of **prayer**, **Sabbath**, and *coram Deo*. The anthropology implied by **empathy** and **attention** can be rendered as a retrieval of the **imago Dei** as relational and receptive, tied to the created goodness of **limits**. Her critique of **cheap empathy** from machines exposes what classical theology names **idolatry**, treating artifacts as responsive gods that demand no conversion of the heart. In moral theology, her prescriptions read as small **ascetic** rules, ordered to **virtue** through habitual renunciation of distractions for the sake of **neighbor-love**.

In ecclesial ethics, her thesis supplies cautions for **digital liturgies** and the pastoral use of **mediated presence**. It commends guarding the **means of grace** from dilution by constant **interruptibility**, specifying zones of silence and attention as integral to **preaching**, **prayer**, and **sacrament**. Her analysis of **simulation** also speaks to theological education, where overreliance on frictionless tools can sap **judgment**. Finally, her ethnography of youth and devices grounds the pastoral diagnosis of **acedia**, the noonday demon of restless inattention, in the concrete **affordances** of platforms. Without becoming a theologian, Turkle offers the church a vocabulary of **presence**, **conversation**, and **limit** with which to resist the liturgies of **optimization**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical Reformed perspective, Turkle’s work is a rich account of **general revelation** in the domain of cultural practice, yet it is finally governed by a **humanist norm** rather than **special revelation**. Positively, her defense of **embodiment**, **limit**, and **face-to-face conversation** aligns with **creation order**, the **imago Dei**, and a covenantal vision of life together ordered by **speech-acts** that bind. Her warning against **cheap empathy**, **simulation hubris**, and the **Goldilocks effect** reads like a practical commentary on **Calvin’s idol-factory**, wherein the heart curates reality to its measure. Her call for **Sabbath-like spaces** accords with the Decalogue’s rhythm of work and rest and with **means-of-grace** piety that privileges undistracted **Word** and **Table**. Yet her remedies lack theological depth, because she does not account for the **noetic effects of sin**, nor does she mount a doctrine of **grace** that can heal disordered loves. Empathy as telos is thin without **neighbor-love** under the **lordship of Christ**, and conversation as method requires regulation by **Scripture** as final norm. A Reformed alternative would affirm her practices as **common-grace** wisdom while subordinating them to a thicker account of **worship**, **catechesis**, and **vocation**. Engineers and designers would be called to **covenantal stewardship** rather than mere humanism, and households would adopt not only device-free zones but also a **rule of life** ordered to **Sabbath**, **prayer**, and **hospitality**. In sum, Turkle rightly names the disorder of **technological desire** and prescribes salutary disciplines of **presence**, but Reformed theology would root these in **revelation**, situate them within **churchly practices**, and interpret their failure through **sin and grace** rather than through culture alone.

### RECAP NOTES
- **MIT culture**, **AI optimism**, and late twentieth-century **postmodernism** frame Turkle’s **humanistic critique of technology**.
- Core problem: how **screens, simulations, and sociable robots** co-construct the **self**, erode **conversation**, and threaten **empathy**.
- Major works: **The Second Self** 1984 on identity and tools, **Life on the Screen** 1995 on online **identity play**, **Simulation and Its Discontents** 2009 on **epistemology**, **Alone Together** 2011 on **sociable robots** and the **Goldilocks effect**, **Reclaiming Conversation** 2015 on **attention** and **presence**, **The Empathy Diaries** 2021 on **listening**.
- Distinctive tools: **psychoanalytic ethnography**, **evocative objects**, **co-constructionism**, and an ethic of **solitude for the sake of conversation**.
- Interlocutors: **Papert** and **Winnicott** as formative, alongside **Weizenbaum**, with background debates from **Goffman**, **Baudrillard**, **Heidegger**, and **Ellul** on **technology**.
- Reformed critique: affirm **embodiment, Sabbath, and presence** while supplying **revelatory grounding**, accounting for **sin and idolatry**, and situating practices within **means of grace**.
- Legacy: a durable vocabulary of **the second self**, **alone together**, and **reclaiming conversation** shaping academic, pastoral, and design debates about **digital life**.
