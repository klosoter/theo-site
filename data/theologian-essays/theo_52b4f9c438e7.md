### TITLE
Unmasking secular modernity: the immanent frame, moral sources, and the fate of transcendence.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Cold War to late-modern West**; Quebec Quiet Revolution, postwar **analytic-continental debates**, and the **communitarian turn** against **liberal neutrality**.
- **Hermeneutic-phenomenological method** joined to **historical genealogy**; anti-reductionist **interpretive social science** guided by the **best account principle** and **strong evaluation**.
- **The Explanation of Behaviour (1964)**; **Hegel (1975)**; **Sources of the Self (1989)**; **The Ethics of Authenticity (1991)**; **Modern Social Imaginaries (2004)**; **A Secular Age (2007)**; **The Language Animal (2016)**.
- Central lexicon: **moral sources**, **hypergoods**, **horizons of significance**, **social imaginaries**, **exclusive humanism**, **immanent frame**, **buffered self** and **porous self**, **cross pressures**, **nova effect**.
- Key interlocutors and influences: **Augustine** to **Hegel**, **Wittgenstein**, **Merleau-Ponty**, **Herder**, dialogue with **Rawls**, **Berlin**, **MacIntyre**, **Habermas**, **Weber**, **Durkheim**, **Ricoeur**, **William James**.
- Reformed reception cue: leverage **plausibility structure** analysis and critique of **subtraction stories**, while querying his **nature-grace schema**, the vagueness of **fullness**, and the absence of **covenantal revelation**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Born in 1931 in Montreal, **Charles Taylor** emerges from a Catholic, bilingual Canada that would soon undergo the **Quiet Revolution**, a setting that sharpened his sense of **religious disenchantment** and public pluralism. Educated at **McGill** and **Oxford**, he absorbed **Wittgensteinian** sensitivity to forms of life and **Merleau-Ponty’s phenomenology** of embodiment, even as he studied **Hegel** as a grand narrator of modern spirit. Early work in **The Explanation of Behaviour (1964)** contested reductionist accounts of action, laying the groundwork for an anti-naturalist, interpretive anthropology. Against scientistic social theory, Taylor proposes **interpretive social science**, where human action is grasped by recovering the **self-interpretations** that make it intelligible. His method is best summarized by two recurring commitments: **strong evaluation**, the claim that we weigh our desires by qualitative discriminations of better and worse anchored in **moral sources**; and the **best account principle**, a hermeneutic norm that privileges the thickest, least reductive articulation that makes the most sense of experience. In place of proceduralism, he insists on **horizons of significance** that constitute selves and societies.

Taylor’s Catholic humanism funds his resistance to **exclusive humanism** and his appreciation for **sacramental ontology**, yet he writes as a philosopher of culture rather than dogmatic theologian. Historically, he inhabits late modernity’s contest between **liberal neutrality** and **communitarian particularity**, engaging **Rawls**, **Dworkin**, and **Berlin** while conversing with **MacIntyre** over the recovery of goods internal to practices. From **Weber** he inherits a narrative of **disenchantment** and rationalization; from **Durkheim**, an attunement to the social genesis of meaning; from **Ricoeur**, a hermeneutics of narrative identity. The upshot is a methodological profile: genealogical retrieval plus phenomenological description in the service of diagnosing the **conditions of belief**.

#### 2. Sources of the Self: moral ontology and modern identity
The benchmark statement of Taylor’s anthropology is **Sources of the Self (1989)**, a genealogy of **modern identity** that refuses both **subtraction stories** and crude progress narratives. Taylor argues that moderns are defined by orienting frameworks and **hypergoods** that command allegiance, even when unacknowledged. The modern affirmation of **ordinary life** and the dignity of labor, the interiorization of conscience since **Augustine**, the **disengaged reason** of **Descartes**, the punctual self of **Locke**, and the **expressivist turn** of **Rousseau** and **Herder** conspire to generate a moral topography where the self is simultaneously agent, observer, and respondent to depth. The point is not to lament modernity but to articulate its **moral sources** so that our valuations are not thin, emotivist gestures but responsibly narrated commitments.

Taylor distinguishes **qualitative distinctions** that rank goods by worth from mere preference sorting, thereby correcting **positivist** and **behaviorist** flattenings. He contends that one cannot make sense of courage, benevolence, or dignity except within a **horizon of significance** that bestows meaning. His capacious history rescues **transcendent reference** from Nietzschean genealogies by showing how Christian and post-Christian strands have nourished modern moral aspirations. The diagnosing question is whether our modern **strong evaluations** can remain coherent if cut off from the **theistic** or **transcendent** sources that birthed them.

#### 3. Authenticity, recognition, and the communitarian debate
In **The Ethics of Authenticity (1991)**, Taylor sifts the modern ideal of **authenticity**, distinguishing a noble aspiration to be faithful to one’s **originality** from a corrosive **subjectivism** that collapses normativity into preference. He argues that authenticity requires **dialogical recognition** in communities that articulate standards, a rejection of **soft relativism**, and realist reference to **strong goods**. Here he engages **liberal neutrality** in **Rawls** and **Dworkin**, contending that the state cannot but presuppose views of the good life through its **social imaginaries** of the person, public reason, and rights. His position is not statist moralism but a call to articulate the **moral sources** that fund liberal commitments, lest they empty into proceduralism.

Underneath the political exchange lies a philosophical anthropology: selves are **embodied, situated, and dialogical**, not **punctual**, disengaged minds. The **recognition** debates, later expanded in discussions of **identity politics**, presuppose that misrecognition can inflict harm by distorting a person’s self-understanding. Taylor’s insistence that the **horizon of significance** is communal and historical pushes public reason to move beyond abstract neutrality without collapsing into authoritarianism. This anticipates his later account of **social imaginaries**.

#### 4. Social imaginaries and the public sphere
With **Modern Social Imaginaries (2004)**, Taylor theorizes the taken-for-granted pictures that organize modern social life. A **social imaginary** is more than a theory; it is the implicit map by which people imagine the **economy**, **public sphere**, and **self-governing people**. He traces the emergence of the **public sphere** from early modern Europe through **print capitalism**, voluntary associations, and the rise of an impersonal **market economy**, building on the work of **Habermas** and **Weber** yet resisting one-dimensional rationalization accounts. The modern West’s self-understanding as a society of equal persons, mutual benefit, and instrumental reason depends on this imaginary, which confers legitimacy.

For theology, the payoff is immediate: evangelization and moral persuasion interact with **pre-theoretical pictures** of agency and community. Taylor argues that altering practices and institutions recalibrates the **conditions of belief** because imaginaries organize plausibility. This framework prepares the way for **A Secular Age**, where he narrates the transformation from a world in which belief is virtually unchallenged to one in which unbelief is equally viable. The story is not subtraction but **recomposition** of imaginaries.

#### 5. A Secular Age: immanent frame, buffered self, and cross pressures
In **A Secular Age (2007)**, Taylor famously reframes the **secularization** debate by distinguishing **Secularity 1** as institutional differentiation, **Secularity 2** as decline of religious practice, and **Secularity 3** as the **conditions of belief** under late modernity. The heart of his thesis is the rise of the **immanent frame**, a constructed space of meaning in which explanations and aspirations are confined to natural flourishing. Within this frame the modern **buffered self** replaces the **porous self** of an enchanted cosmos, shielding the person from spirits, sacrament, and cosmic meanings that once penetrated experience. The result is a field in which **exclusive humanism** becomes a moral possibility and often a moral ideal.

Yet Taylor resists triumphalist narratives. He deconstructs **subtraction stories** that imagine modernity as the shedding of superstition, arguing that new **moral sources** such as the **affirmation of ordinary life** and humanitarian universalism are positive inventions. He maps the **nova effect**, a proliferation of spiritual and moral options, and the **cross pressures** that churn within the immanent frame, where haunting experiences of **fullness** threaten to break the spell of closure. Along the way he traces the **Reform** dynamic that sought to purify Christian practice, the emergence of **providential Deism**, and the new disciplinary regimes described by **Weber** and echoed by **Foucault**. The conclusion is not a systematic theology but a phenomenology of faith and doubt amid late-modern fragilization.

#### 6. Language, articulation, and Hegelian inheritance
Taylor’s earlier volumes, **Hegel (1975)** and **Hegel and Modern Society (1979)**, interpret **Hegel** as a theorist of recognition and historical embodiment rather than an absolutist metaphysician. He receives from Hegel the conviction that selves and societies are constituted through **self-interpretation**, that freedom matures through **Sittlichkeit** or ethical life, and that modernity’s gains and pathologies must be told in a narrative of dialectical struggle. This Hegelian inheritance underwrites Taylor’s wager that **articulation** can recover **moral sources** that lie implicit within experience.

That wager expands in **The Language Animal (2016)**, where Taylor retrieves **Herder** and **Humboldt** to argue that language is constitutive, not representational. Humans are **language animals** whose speech discloses new possibilities of meaning through **constitutive expressivism**. Articulation is not mere description but an ontological opening of the space of reasons and goods. This directly serves his **best account principle**: as we articulate, we not only report but reshape the **horizons of significance** that make agency and moral life intelligible. The critic of **naturalist linguistics** and **reductive cognitive science** thus doubles as a theologian’s ally in defending the irreducibility of meaning and the sacramental density of speech.

#### 7. Religion, conversion, and Catholic modernity
Although a philosopher, Taylor’s **Catholic** commitments surface in **A Catholic Modernity? (1999)** and in chapters across **A Secular Age**, where he portrays Christian faith as a lived response to **fullness** rather than assent to isolated propositions. He engages **William James** to commend attention to varieties of religious experience, while insisting on **strong evaluation** against relativizing reductions. Taylor’s portraits of **conversion**, crisis, and everyday practices show how **social imaginaries** either occlude or invite a sense of transcendence. His sympathies lie with a **sacramental ontology** that resists the flattening of the **immanent frame** by re-hallowing ordinary life through **agape** and liturgy.

Taylor does not, however, deliver **dogmatic content** so much as a phenomenology of believing and doubting. His prominence stems from giving late modern people a grammar to name **cross pressures**, to detect the itch of transcendence, and to see how **exclusive humanism** contests and feeds upon Christian inheritances. This is why theologians mine his categories of **porous self**, **buffered self**, and **nova effect** for pastoral and apologetic practice, even as they debate his construal of **grace** and **history**.

#### 8. Interlocutors and disputes over secularization
Taylor’s account of secularity unfolds in argument with sociologists like **Peter Berger** and **José Casanova** and with anthropologist **Talal Asad**. Against linear decline theories, he argues that **Secularity 3** is the key to understanding modern belief, while acknowledging **global differentiation** where secularization does not entail de-Christianization. He sides with critics of **rational choice** religion models that treat faith as consumer preference, insisting that **moral sources** and **hypergoods** order desire more deeply than utility. In political theory, exchanges with **Rawls** and **Habermas** contest whether **public reason** can be religiously articulate without violating fairness. Taylor proposes that a mature **pluralism** requires porous borders between religious and secular reasons within a democratic **social imaginary**.

He also disputes the catechism of **disenchantment**. While indebted to **Weber**, he corrects Weber’s one-way tale with the **nova effect**, arguing that modernity produced rival theisms, spiritualities, and secularisms. At stake is whether transcendence is a live option within the **immanent frame**, and whether the experience of **fullness** can be spoken without theological foreclosure. His dialogue with **MacIntyre** keeps open the question of traditions as carriers of **rationality** and of practices as schools of **virtue** within late-modern fragmentation.

#### 9. Doctrinal payoffs and theological uses
Theologically, Taylor’s value lies in rendering the **conditions of belief** with unprecedented granularity. His language of **plausibility structures**, **social imaginaries**, and **cross pressures** equips pastors and theologians to see why proclamation can sound implausible to the **buffered self**. Liturgical theology can hear his plea for recovering a **porous self** under **sacramental ontology**, while moral theology can draw upon **strong evaluation** to resist emotivism. Public theology can deploy **social imaginaries** to ask how institutions and practices catechize loves. Apologetics can apply the critique of **subtraction stories** to unmask the borrowed capital of **exclusive humanism**, showing how appeals to human dignity, rights, and compassion remain parasitic on **moral sources** that transcend immanence.

At the same time, Taylor’s choice of **genealogical narrative** and **phenomenological description** rather than doctrinal confession leaves crucial questions underdetermined. The term **fullness** hovers as a phenomenological index, not a christological claim, and the account of **grace** is more aesthetic and ethical than covenantal and soteriological. His sympathy with **Catholic modernity** and rehabilitation of **authenticity** risk blurring the antithesis between revelation and autonomous self-legislation, even as he refuses relativism through **strong goods**. These tensions are the price of a project aimed at cultural diagnosis rather than dogmatic specification.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
Reformed readers can gratefully receive Taylor’s analysis of the **immanent frame**, **buffered self**, and **subtraction stories** as clarifying the **noetic environment** in which proclamation must sound. His **moral sources** resonate with **Calvin’s sensus divinitatis** and **common grace**, and his **best account principle** echoes a Reformed insistence on honoring the irreducibility of personhood against **naturalism**. Yet from a classical Reformed and WTS vantage, several cautions follow. First, Taylor’s phenomenology of **fullness** remains theologically indeterminate, while Reformed orthodoxy insists that the knowledge of God is covenantally specified by **special revelation** in **Word and sacrament** under the authority of **Scripture**. Second, his account of **authenticity** and **recognition** treats identity formation as dialogical within **social imaginaries** but underplays the **noetic effects of sin** and the **antithesis** of belief and unbelief, which **Van Til** highlights as a structural suppression of truth within the **immanent frame** described by **Romans 1**. Third, Taylor’s sympathy for **sacramental ontology** risks a nature-grace continuum that Reformed theology recasts as **covenantal** rather than ontic, safeguarding **divine aseity** and **Christ’s mediatorial exclusivity**. The Reformed alternative welcomes Taylor’s diagnosis yet answers his indeterminate **fullness** with **Christ’s lordship over every sphere**, frames **social imaginaries** within **creation-fall-redemption-consummation**, and locates durability against **cross pressures** in the regenerating work of the **Spirit** who discloses reality through **Scripture**, reshapes the **habitus** by **Word and sacrament**, and reopens the world as truly **porous** to the Triune God.
