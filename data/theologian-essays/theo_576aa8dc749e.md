### TITLE
God speaks: the self-revealing triune God in Jesus Christ as the sole criterion, content, and norm of theology.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Swiss Reformed pastor-professor** formed amid **liberal Protestantism** and the trauma of **World War I**, turning to **dialectical theology** and the **Confessing Church** against **German nationalism**.
- **Revelational actualism** and **christocentric method**: theology arises from *Deus dixit*, the **threefold Word of God**, and the **analogia fidei** rather than the **analogia entis**.
- **Key works**: **Epistle to the Romans** 1919, 2nd ed. 1922; **Fides Quaerens Intellectum** 1931; **Nein!** 1934; **Barmen Declaration** 1934; **Church Dogmatics** I–IV, 1932–1967; **Gospel and Law** 1935; **The Humanity of God** 1956; **Evangelical Theology** 1962.
- **Central categories**: **revelation and Word**, **Trinity and election**, **covenant and creation**, **reconciliation and objective atonement**, **actualism and event**, **witness and Scripture**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Wilhelm Herrmann**, **Adolf von Harnack**, **Emil Brunner**, **Rudolf Bultmann**, **Erich Przywara**, **Anselm of Canterbury**, and the **Reformed confessions**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: esteem for **God-centeredness** and **Trinitarian dogmatics**, reservations about **Scripture’s inerrancy**, **double predestination**, **natural theology**, and the **sacraments**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, rupture, and the birth of dialectical theology
**Karl Barth** was born in 1886 in Basel into a **Swiss Reformed** setting marked by catechetical seriousness and nineteenth-century **liberal Protestantism**. He studied under **Wilhelm Herrmann** at Marburg and heard **Adolf von Harnack** in Berlin; both embodied the post-Kantian confidence that the essence of Christianity was **ethical religion** rather than **dogmatic revelation**. The catastrophe of **World War I** and the 1914 manifesto signed by many German professors, including his mentors, shattered Barth's trust in a theology captive to **culture-protestantism**. As pastor in **Safenwil** from 1911 to 1921 he turned to the Bible with a preacher's urgency and produced the explosive commentary **Epistle to the Romans** first in 1919 and then radically reworked in 1922. There he announced the **wholly other God**, the **divine judgment** that relativizes all human religion, and the sheer event of *Deus dixit*. The result was **dialectical theology**, a rhetoric of **No and Yes** in which **revelation** stands over against **religion**, **God's act** against **human possibility**, and **grace** against **nature**.

Barth's pastorate gave way to an academic calling: **Göttingen** 1921–1925, **Münster** 1925–1930, **Bonn** 1930–1935, and then back to **Basel** after his expulsion by the Nazi regime. During these years his dialectic matured into a **revelational actualism** whose center is **the triune God who speaks**. Methodologically he broke with classic prolegomena and began his dogmatics from **revelation, Trinity, and Word of God**, insisting that the **object** determines the **method**. Theology for Barth became a churchly science disciplined by **Scripture** and **confession**, yet ever responsive to the freedom of the **living Lord**.

#### 2. Revelation, Word of God, and the rejection of natural theology
The early dogmatic turn took shape in **Church Dogmatics I/1** in 1932 and **I/2** in 1938. Here Barth articulated the **threefold form of the Word of God**: **Jesus Christ as the Word**, **Holy Scripture as the normative witness**, and **church proclamation** as *Verkündigung* that God may graciously use. Revelation is not a deposit beneath or behind the text but the **free self-presentation of the triune God** who, in an **ever fresh act**, renders human words his **Verbum Dei**. The axiom *revelatio est revelata* resists all capture by religious consciousness or metaphysical inference. Hence the **analogia fidei** governs all theological predication: we speak as God gives us himself to be known.

Barth sharpened this stance in his **Nein!** of 1934 against **Emil Brunner** on **natural theology**. He affirmed the **point of contact** only as an event created by the **Holy Spirit**, not as a **residual capacity** of fallen nature. The same year he drafted the **Barmen Declaration**, which asserted that **Jesus Christ**, not the **Volksgeist**, is the one Word of God the church has to hear. Against **Erich Przywara's analogia entis**, Barth insisted that **Creator-creature distinction** requires not an ontological ladder but a **Christologically mediated analogy**. The theological method is therefore **Trinitarian and actualistic**: doctrine moves with the **movement of God**, never with autonomous nature or reason.

#### 3. The doctrine of God and the Trinity: perfection as free love
In **Church Dogmatics II/1** in 1940 Barth expounded the **doctrine of God** and the **divine perfections** in a manner that braided **freedom** and **love**. He retrieved **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **aseity**, yet recast them as the living perfections of the **covenant Lord** rather than inert attributes. The **Trinity** is not an appendix to revelation but the **grammar of revelation itself**: the God who reveals himself is the God he is. The **immanent Trinity** and **economic Trinity** coincide in identity of subject, not in identity of mode, under the rule of the *homoousios*. This led Barth to the programmatic emphasis on **God's self-determination** in the **eternal decision** to be God for us, a decision that threads through his later **doctrine of election**.

Barth's **actualism** has methodological consequences. God is not a set of metaphysical properties but **his act** in **self-revelation** and **reconciliation**. Still, the divine constancy is not denied but affirmed as the faithfulness of the **covenant God**. Thus Barth resists both **process mutation** and **classical impassibility without qualification** by redefining the **immutability of love** as the unwearying readiness to engage the creature. In this framework, **providence** and **prayer** are coherent because the **One who rules** is the **One who speaks** and binds himself to his **promises**.

#### 4. Election, covenant, and creation: the primacy of Jesus Christ
The crescendo of the dogmatics regarding the divine decision comes in **Church Dogmatics II/2** in 1942, where Barth famously **reconfigures election**. The basic thesis is **christological concentration**: **Jesus Christ is both the electing God and the elected man**. Election is the **inner content** of the **Gospel**, unveiling the **triune will** to be gracious. The corresponding **reprobation** falls upon **Jesus Christ** himself as the **covenant judge judged in our place**. The doctrine, once a decree behind Christ, is now an **identical movement** in and as **Christ**. The effect is a universal expansion of the **scope of grace** without a dogmatic assertion of **apokatastasis**. Barth leaves unresolved the quantitative scope, but the qualitative claim is clear: **grace is triumphant and particular in Christ**.

This vision reframes **creation** in **Church Dogmatics III**. God creates as the **one who wills covenant fellowship**. In Barth's lapidary formulation, the **covenant is the internal basis of creation**, and **creation is the external basis of the covenant**. Creation is not a neutral stage but the ordered sphere for **reconciliation**. The **doctrine of providence**, the **doctrine of man**, and the **orders of creation** are thus read through the **covenantal telos**, rejecting both **natural law foundationalism** and secular sovereignties. Barth employs the **analogia relationis** to describe human being in correspondence to God, especially in the **I-Thou** relation, yet he will not ground ethics in any **intramundane ontology** independent of **revelation**.

#### 5. Christology and reconciliation: the objectivity of grace
Barth's **Christology** lives at the center of **Church Dogmatics IV** on **reconciliation**. Using the classical grammar of *anhypostasis* and *enhypostasis*, he safeguards both the **full divinity and full humanity** of **Jesus Christ** while giving the incarnation an **elective depth**: the **Logos** takes humanity into personal union in the concrete history of **Jesus of Nazareth**. Barth resists a speculative **Logos asarkos** detached from the economy, insisting instead that **election** determines who the **Son** is in eternity. The **extra Calvinisticum** is respected insofar as the **eternal Son** remains **Lord** beyond the flesh, yet the emphasis falls on **the Son for us**, not on metaphysical distance.

In **reconciliation**, Barth describes the threefold work of **Christ's offices**: **prophet**, **priest**, **king**, unfolded as **the Judge judged in our place**, **the Servant who justifies the ungodly**, and **the Lord who sanctifies his people**. The atonement is **vicarious and objective**; the sinner's history is taken up into **Christ's cross and resurrection**. While he employs **penal** and **substitutionary** idioms, he reframes **penal substitution** within the **primacy of grace** and the **covenant narrative**, resisting any **transactional** model. The result is a strong **objectivism**: **God's Yes** precedes and grounds all human response, and **faith** is the awakening to what is already true in **Jesus Christ**.

#### 6. Scripture, witness, and ecclesial proclamation
Barth's **doctrine of Scripture** follows his threefold Word. **Scripture is normative** as the **divinely commissioned witness** whose authority is singular and irreplaceable. Yet its **inspiration** means being **taken up** into the **event of God's speaking**, not an inherent property of **inerrant texts**. The Bible can err in **historical, scientific, or textual detail**, yet God infallibly accomplishes his **self-witness** through these **fallible human words** when and where he pleases. Preaching as *Verkündigung* is subordinate yet real participation in the **Word of God** as the Spirit gives **illumination**.

This stance marks his exegesis from **Romans** to **1 Corinthians** and frames the ecclesiology of **CD IV/3**, where the **church** is the **community of witness** ordered to **mission**, **sacrament**, and **discipline**. Barth stands within the **Reformed** stream in rejecting **sacramental ex opere operato**, yet in his 1943 and 1967 treatments of **baptism** he controversially argues against **infant baptism**, pressing for **believers' baptism** as the **human response** to the **divine act** already accomplished. The **Lord's Supper** is a **means of witness** to **reconciliation**, not a metaphysical conveyor of **substance**, and so the **Word** retains priority.

#### 7. Ethics, politics, and the humanity of God
In ethics Barth develops a pattern of **divine command** grounded in **revelation** rather than **natural law**. The **Gospel and Law** lecture of 1935 famously inverts the traditional order to protect the **priority of grace**. Moral discernment is concrete obedience to the **living Christ**, not deduction from **orders of creation**. The **analogia relationis** is pressed into service in his accounts of **male and female**, **marriage**, and **community**, though always subordinated to the **covenantal Lordship** of God. Barth's political theology was forged in the heat of the **Confessing Church**, where the **Barmen Declaration** opposed the **German Christians** and their idolatry of **nation and race**.

Later, in **The Humanity of God** in 1956, Barth stressed that the **divine majesty** is manifest precisely as **humility and nearness**. This was not a revision of **Trinitarian immutability** but a deeper insistence that **God's freedom** is the freedom to be **for us**. He never abandoned the **gospel's objectivity** or the **priority of revelation**, but he gladly re-centered his rhetoric on the **Yes** of the **covenant God** rather than merely the **No** of crisis.

#### 8. Anselmian logic, academic interlocutors, and Catholic reception
Barth's 1931 **Fides Quaerens Intellectum** demonstrated his affinity with **Anselm of Canterbury**. He read Anselm's argument not as **rationalist proof** but as a paradigmatic case of *faith seeking understanding*, where **conceptual exposition** follows **revealed content**. This Anselmian logic suffuses the **Church Dogmatics**: theological reasoning is **a posteriori** to **revelation**, even as it is internally coherent and rigorous. In debate with **Rudolf Bultmann**, Barth rejected **demythologization** that would disjoin **kerygma** from **history**, insisting on the **historical particularity** of **Jesus Christ** as the site of **God's act**. With **Erich Przywara**, he sparred over the **analogia entis**, preferring the **analogia fidei** as the only adequate correspondence.

His influence reached **Roman Catholic** circles. **Hans Urs von Balthasar's The Theology of Karl Barth** praised Barth's **Christocentrism** while probing the tensions with **Catholic ontology**. Barth's robust **Trinitarianism**, his rehabilitation of **doctrine**, and his re-centering of **grace** shaped ecumenical theology beyond **Protestantism**, and his student and interpreter **Thomas F. Torrance** extended Barth's **incarnational and trinitarian** emphases in conversation with **patristic theology**. Figures like **Eberhard Jüngel** deepened Barth's **actualism** with attention to **God's being in becoming**.

#### 9. Architecture and incompletion: the Church Dogmatics as unfinished cathedral
The **Church Dogmatics** is an unfinished cathedral composed of **twelve part-volumes** across four main loci: **Word of God** I/1–2, **God** II/1–2, **Creation** III/1–4, **Reconciliation** IV/1–3 with fragments of **IV/4**, and the unwritten **Redemption and consummation**. Its architecture is scaffolding for a single claim: **God's self-revelation in Jesus Christ** is the **criterion and content** of all theology. The **Trinity** organizes the **prolegomena**; **election** organizes the doctrine of **God**; **covenant** organizes **creation**; **vicarious humanity** organizes **reconciliation**. Throughout, **actualism** insists that **being** is known in **act**, and **objectivism** insists that **grace** precedes and grounds **faith**. The incompletion of **eschatology** is striking, though the direction is clear: the **Lord who is coming** is the **Lord who has come**, and **hope** is participation in **Christ's resurrection**.

By design, Barth's work is a sustained **reformation** of dogmatics in the twentieth century. He re-centers **theology** in the **church**, rehabilitates **doctrine** after **historicist skepticism**, and expels **natural theology** from the heart of **Christian speech** about God. He is not a restorationist of **Protestant orthodoxy**, nor a modernist, but a **church theologian** pressing **revelation** to its christological limit.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation: principled convergences and abiding tensions
From a **Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary** vantage, Barth's achievements command respect even as they compel critique. Positively, his **God-centeredness**, explicit **Trinitarian dogmatics**, retrieval of **election** through **Jesus Christ**, and insistence on **revelation's primacy** align with classical **Reformed** instincts to honor **divine sovereignty**, **Christ's mediatorship**, and **Scripture's sufficiency**. His **Barmen** stance models **confessional courage**, and his **objectivist gospel** resists anthropocentric religion. Yet principled tensions remain. First, on **revelation and Scripture**, Barth's **threefold Word** and **witness doctrine** undercut a robust **autopistia Scripturae** and **inerrancy**, blurring the difference between **revelation** as God's speech and **illumination** in the church. A classical **Reformed** alternative would affirm **Scripture's plenary inspiration**, the **self-authenticating authority of the text**, and the **Spirit's inward testimony**, securing both objectivity and certainty. Second, on **nature and grace**, the rejection of **natural theology** collapses **general revelation** into **redemption**, jeopardizing the **catholic insight** that the heavens declare the **glory of God** and correlative **natural law** accessible to conscience, as attested in **Romans 1–2**. Third, on **covenant and election**, Barth's **christological reconfiguration** threatens to erase **reprobation** as the just outworking of **divine justice**, introduces a practical **universalism**, and weakens the **law-gospel** dialectic that grounds **justification by faith** in a clear **forensic** frame with **penal substitution** at its core. Classical **federal theology** would retain a historical **covenant of works** with Adam, **federal headship** in **Christ**, and a twofold decree that magnifies both **mercy** and **justice** without dissolving either into the other. Fourth, on **Christology and sacraments**, Barth's narrow use of *anhypostasis* and the tendency to collapse **election** into **incarnation** risk diminishing the **extra Calvinisticum** and the communicative integrity of natures, while his rejection of **infant baptism** departs from **Reformed covenant practice** that sees the sacrament as **God's promise** to households. Finally, in epistemology, Barth's **actualism** may tilt toward **subjectivism** in the event of the Word and has been judged by **Cornelius Van Til** to be a species of modern theology in ecclesial dress, because it eschews **propositional revelation** as the principled form of **divine speech**. A constructive Reformed pathway would retain Barth's **Christological concentration** and **Trinitarian prolegomena**, yet re-anchor them in the **self-attesting Scripture**, a robust **twofold knowledge of God** in nature and grace, a clear **penal-substitutionary atonement**, and the **confessional** articulation of **law and gospel** within the **covenant of works and grace**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: **Swiss Reformed** theologian shaped by **liberal Protestantism**, the crisis of **World War I**, and the **Confessing Church** against **German nationalism**.
- **Problem**: How to ground theology in **God's self-revelation** rather than **religion**, securing **Trinitarian** and **christocentric** dogmatics.
- **Works**: **Epistle to the Romans** 1919/1922; **Church Dogmatics** I–IV 1932–1967; **Fides Quaerens Intellectum** 1931; **Nein!** 1934; **Barmen Declaration** 1934; **Gospel and Law** 1935; **The Humanity of God** 1956.
- **Distinctives**: **Threefold Word of God**, **analogia fidei** over **analogia entis**, **election in Christ**, **covenant as basis of creation**, **objective reconciliation**, **actualism**.
- **Debates**: Against **natural theology** and **Emil Brunner**, in conversation with **Rudolf Bultmann**, sparring with **Erich Przywara**, retrieved **Anselm** for *fides quaerens intellectum*.
- **Reformed critique**: Concerns about **Scripture's inerrancy**, **general revelation**, **double predestination**, clarity of **penal substitution**, **infant baptism**, and potential **universalism**.
- **Legacy**: Reoriented twentieth-century **dogmatics** toward **Trinity** and **Christ**, influenced **ecumenical** and **Catholic** theology, and remains a touchstone for debates on **revelation**, **grace**, and **church confession**.
