### TITLE
Retrieving classical theism and catholicity through evangelical sola Scriptura for an Anglican theology of love.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth to early twenty-first century Anglican evangelicalism**, shaped by **postwar Anglican realignments**, **biblical studies revolutions**, and **patristic ressourcement**.
- Method grounded in **sola Scriptura** with a ministerial role for **creed and tradition**, a historical-theological synthesis that privileges **canonical exegesis** and **Augustinian realism**.
- Key works: **Creeds, Councils and Christ** 1984; **The Doctrine of God** 1993; **Biblical Interpretation: Past and Present** 1996; **Documents of the English Reformation** 1994, rev. 2004; **God Is Love** 2012; **God Has Spoken** 2014; **Doing Theology with the Reformers** 2014; **Augustine on the Christian Life** 2015; **The Church** 2016; **The Attributes of God** 2021.
- Central categories: **divine simplicity**, **aseity and impassibility**, **Trinitarian orthodoxy with the filioque**, **canonical interpretation**, **Reformed catholicity**, **Thirty-Nine Articles**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Calvin and the Reformers**, **Richard Hooker**, moderns such as **Barth**, **Rahner**, **Pannenberg**, and debates with **process theology** and **open theism**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong alignment on **Scripture and classic theism**, with discussion points around **love as organizing attribute**, **Anglican ecclesiology**, and the articulation of **sacramental presence**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Anglican context, and evangelical method
Gerald Bray, born in 1948, emerges as a principal voice of contemporary **Anglican evangelical theology**, working at the intersection of **historical theology**, **biblical interpretation**, and **systematics**. His intellectual itinerary passes through the English university world and the evangelical study center movement, notably the research culture around **Latimer House, Oxford**, before long tenure as Research Professor at **Beeson Divinity School**. Bray’s formation is marked by the fissures of postwar Anglicanism, where **liberal revisionism**, **Anglo-Catholic retrieval**, and **global evangelical renewal** contended for the soul of the communion. Against this backdrop he advanced a method of **sola Scriptura** that honors **creeds and councils** as faithful witnesses rather than rivals to the biblical canon. Early studies such as **Creeds, Councils and Christ** 1984 and work on **Documents of the English Reformation** 1994, rev. 2004 positioned him as an interpreter of **conciliar orthodoxy** and the **Thirty-Nine Articles**, setting the methodological template that would govern his approach to **the Trinity**, **Christology**, and **ecclesiology**.

Bray’s method is a disciplined retrieval of **catholic dogma** under the judgment of **Scripture**, marked by a double fidelity to **patristic consensuses** and **Reformation clarity**. He treats **tradition** as ministerial, not magisterial, and he accents the Anglican triad of **Scripture, reason, and tradition** by insisting that only **Scripture** is principial while reason and tradition serve as handmaids. His evangelical posture appears in the conviction that the rule of faith is generated by the **canonical economy**, and in the corollary that **dogma** is exegetical result rather than speculative architecture. This sets Bray at some distance from speculative **social trinitarianism** and from postcritical hermeneutics that eclipse the sacred text, even as he draws gratefully on **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, and **Calvin** as exegetes of **divine simplicity** and **Trinitarian processions**. The animating thesis is that **sola Scriptura** secured by **creedal boundaries** yields a stable framework for theological reasoning across loci.

#### 2. Hermeneutics and historical theology as scaffolding
The massive **Biblical Interpretation: Past and Present** 1996 constitutes Bray’s cartography of the Christian mind at work on the **canon**, from the **fathers** through the **Reformation** to modernity. He tracks the interplay between the **literal sense** and polyvalent readings, showing how the patristic fourfold schema could be integrated with a disciplined **sensus literalis**. Against reductive **historical criticism** he argues that the **canonical form** of Scripture, received within the **church’s rule of faith**, is the proper hermeneutical horizon. Yet against undisciplined **allegory** he maintains the priority of the literal sense in the **Reformed** tradition. This balanced program undergirds his later systematic work, where **Trinitarian doctrine**, **atonement**, and **sacramentology** are rigorously exegetical and historically conscious.

Complementary to this is **God Has Spoken: A History of Christian Theology** 2014, a wide-angle account of doctrinal development in which Bray narrates **Nicaea**, **Chalcedon**, the **Augustinian settlement** on grace, and **Reformation** realignments as successive acts in the reception of **Scripture**. The leitmotif is **ressourcement** without romanticism, using **Augustine** and **Hooker** to model how Scripture governs tradition. Related monographs such as **Doing Theology with the Reformers** 2014 and **Augustine on the Christian Life** 2015 further specify Bray’s intellectual lineage: he is an **Augustinian** on **original sin** and **grace**, a **Reformed catholic** on the **supper** and **ministry**, and an evangelical Anglican in his appeal to the **Articles of Religion** as concise distillations of biblical teaching. These projects collectively supply the hermeneutical scaffolding for his constructive doctrine of **God**.

#### 3. The doctrine of God and classical theism rearticulated
Bray’s **The Doctrine of God** 1993, in the Contours of Christian Theology series, is a landmark in late twentieth-century retrieval of **classical theism**. He expounds **aseity**, **simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility**, not as metaphysical hangovers but as exegetically warranted summaries of the biblical God who is **YHWH**, the I AM. He reaffirms the **Creator-creature distinction** and frames all divine acts *ad extra* as free expressions of God’s perfect life *ad intra*. On the **Trinity**, Bray upholds **homoousios** and the Western **filioque**, treating **eternal generation** and **spiration** as the grammar of personal distinctions. He engages **Karl Rahner’s rule** by insisting that the **economic Trinity** truly reveals the **immanent Trinity**, yet without collapsing ontology into history or reducing **simplicity** to a social model. The result is a biblically driven exposition that resists both **process theology** and thin **deistic theism**, offering instead a thick account of **providence** as the wise and loving governance of the one **simple God**.

Bray’s critique of modern alternatives is careful rather than polemical. He distances himself from **open theism** for compromising **foreknowledge** and **sovereignty**, and he finds **process theology** deeply at odds with **immutability** and **impassibility**, the very conditions for covenantal fidelity. While sympathetic to the **Barthian** recovery of Trinity and revelation, he judges certain modern moves toward **immanent-economic identity** and **actualistic ontology** precarious if they unsettle **eternal processions** or undermine **aseity**. In these debates Bray functions as an evangelical interpreter of the classical consensus, tethering **metaphysics** to the **canon** and refusing to choose between the **God of the philosophers** and the **God of Abraham**, because rightly construed they are one.

#### 4. God Is Love as organizing grammar for the loci
The constructive culmination of Bray’s mature work appears in **God Is Love: A Biblical and Systematic Theology** 2012, where the Johannine confession that God is love supplies an architectonic across the loci. This is not sentiment but a dogmatic thesis coordinated with **divine simplicity**. Love names the fullness of God’s **goodness** as the form of all God’s attributes and acts. Creation is love’s generous overflow, **providence** is love’s wise ordering, **law** is love’s moral shape, and **gospel** is love’s redemptive act in **Christ**. The doctrine of **atonement** is treated with robust **penal substitution** within a broader account of **satisfaction**, **victory**, and **recapitulation**, all qualified by the one divine love that is holy and just. In **Christology**, **homoousios** and **hypostatic union** safeguard that love’s history is the history of God with us, not merely divine empathy.

Crucially, Bray secures **impassibility** while refusing to mute the language of divine compassion. Because of **simplicity**, divine love is not a fluctuating affection but the immutable plenitude of God that acts in time through the Word and Spirit. As such, **impassibility** is reconceived as the condition for trustworthy passion, the real and efficacious turning of God to us without mutability. In **pneumatology**, the Spirit as the bond of love applies redemption in the **church**, producing **sanctification** and **mission**. This love-structured dogmatics aims to unify the loci, arguing that the same **triune love** that justifies also reorders creation, worship, and hope in **eschatology**.

#### 5. Anglican identity, ecclesiology, and sacramental theology
Bray’s Anglican studies tie his systematics to an ecclesial profile of **Reformed catholicity**. His editions of **Documents of the English Reformation** 1994, rev. 2004 and **The Anglican Canons 1529–1947** 1998, along with **The Faith We Confess: An Exposition of the Thirty-Nine Articles** 2009, present **Anglicanism** as a reforming movement committed to **Scripture’s supremacy**, the classic **creeds**, and a restrained **sacramental realism**. For Bray, the **Articles** codify a Protestant consensus on **justification by faith**, the rejection of **transubstantiation**, and a spiritual presence in the **Eucharist** compatible with **Calvin’s** account. He values **episcopacy** historically and pastorally but argues it is not of the essence of the **church**, thereby distinguishing **polity** from marks such as the pure preaching of the **gospel** and right administration of the **sacraments**.

In **The Church: A Theological and Historical Account** 2016, Bray canvasses the **visible-invisible distinction**, the nature of **ministry**, and the rhythms of **liturgy** as catechesis. He treats **baptism** as the sign and seal of incorporation into Christ, rooting efficacy not in the rite ex opere operato but in the **Word and Spirit**. He reads **Hooker** as an ally in defending order and reason while refusing the inflation of **tradition** into a parallel magisterium. Against **Anglo-Catholic** sacramental maximalism and **liberal** doctrinal minimalism, he proposes an evangelical Anglican median anchored in **Scripture** and the **Articles**, committed to catholic breadth without dogmatic dilution.

#### 6. Engagements with modern theology and contested doctrines
Bray’s dialogue with modern theology clarifies his constructive options. He welcomes **Barth’s** critique of natural theologies that bypass **revelation**, yet he insists on a properly ordered **natural theology** as Romans 1 requires, in which **creation** witnesses to the Creator without salvific sufficiency. He appreciates **Pannenberg’s** historical sensibility while resisting any subordination of **ontology** to **history** that would jeopardize **simplicity** or **eternity**. He recognizes insights in **Gunton’s** relational ontology but critiques social readings of **Trinity** that erode **one will** and **divine unity**. Regarding **Rahner**, Bray qualifies the axiom concerning the identity of the **economic** and **immanent Trinity** by defending **eternal processions** as the grammar that makes the economy truly revelatory.

In contemporary debates, Bray rejects **open theism** as incompatible with **foreknowledge** and covenantal **providence**, and he demurs from **process** accounts that make God co-evolver with the world. He resists **feminist** re-namings of God that sever dogmatic language from **revelation**, while admitting that biblical metaphors are rich and analogically multiform. On Scripture, he is functionally **inerrantist**, arguing that **authority** and **truthfulness** are correlative properties of the inspired text, even if he often prefers the broader language of **trustworthiness** and **clarity** used in the **Articles**. Across these engagements, Bray is a conservative ecumenist, open to catholic breadth but unbending on **Trinity**, **Christology**, and the **gospel** of grace.

#### 7. The pedagogy of ressourcement and the Reformers
Several works exhibit Bray’s pedagogical DNA, especially **Doing Theology with the Reformers** 2014. He profiles **Luther**, **Calvin**, **Cranmer**, and **Hooker** as exegetical theologians whose loci emerge from **catechesis** and **liturgy**. Bray contends that the **Reformation** is a renewal of **patristic** insight under **Scripture**, not a rupture, and that **Anglican** formularies transmit **Reformed** soteriology and a classic doctrine of **God**. His **Augustine on the Christian Life** 2015 practices an evangelical moral psychology grounded in **grace**, **ordered love**, and the sanctifying work of the **Spirit**. This pedagogical emphasis explains Bray’s investment in training pastors and laity who can read **Scripture** with the **fathers** and confess **creeds** with conviction.

His long editorial and teaching service, including leadership associated with the journal **Churchman** and work at **Beeson Divinity School**, models a vocation of retrieval for the church. He contributes to the wider movement of patristic and Reformation commentary that made resources like the **Ancient Christian Commentary** and the **Reformation Commentary on Scripture** common goods, and he cultivates trans-Atlantic networks of **evangelical Anglicanism**. The net effect is an integrated program where **historical theology** is not antiquarian but serves the renewal of **preaching**, **sacramental practice**, and the **mission** of the church.

#### 8. Integrating the loci: love, holiness, and the economy of grace
Taken together, Bray’s corpus proposes a unifying grammar for the loci in which **divine love** is the form of all God’s perfections. By tethering **love** to **simplicity**, he refuses the false choice between love and **holiness**. Creation witnesses to gratuitous generosity, **providence** to meticulous care, **sin** to disordered love, and **salvation** to the reordering of love through the cross where **penal substitution** and **victory** coinhere. The **church** is the fellowship of love formed by the **Word and sacraments**, and **eschatology** is the consummation of love’s communion with the **triune God**. Within this frame, Bray maintains **classical immutability** and **impassibility**, articulates **eternal generation** and **spiration**, preserves the **Creator-creature distinction**, and refuses both historicist collapse and metaphysical abstraction.

The repeated refrain across topics is **Reformed catholicity**. Bray argues that Anglican identity, rightly read through the **Articles of Religion** and **Book of Common Prayer**, is a reforming catholicism aligned with the core of **Reformed orthodoxy**. In **The Attributes of God** 2021 he returns to classical themes for a new generation, presenting **aseity**, **omnipotence**, **omniscience**, **holiness**, and **love** as exegetical perfections that explain the economy. He is consistently a biblical divine who insists that **dogmatics** serves **catechesis** and **doxology**, and that the church’s future requires confidence in the **canon**, sustained by the **creeds**, and energized by the **gospel** of grace.

#### 9. A single integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage, Bray’s project is a welcome instance of evangelical **ressourcement**. His anchoring of **sola Scriptura** within **creedal orthodoxy**, his defense of **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **impassibility**, and **eternal generation**, and his robust **penal substitution** place him in deep alignment with the **Westminster Standards** on **God**, **Scripture**, and **salvation**. His **Anglican** exposition of the **sacraments** coheres substantially with a Reformed spiritual presence, and his nuanced view of **episcopacy** avoids jure divino claims that would trouble Presbyterian polity. Two tensions deserve notice. First, while centering **God is love** as an organizing axiom yields pastoral fruit, a Westminster accent would prefer the formal primacy of **God’s glory** and a covenantal architecture, lest the attribute of love, even safeguarded by **simplicity**, exert hermeneutical pressure that softens the rhetoric of **wrath** or dilutes the decretal backbone of **providence**. Second, Bray’s Anglican breadth sometimes speaks of **Scripture’s authority** more than technical **inerrancy**, which in Westminster usage guards the same reality but with sharper confessional precision. These are calibrations within a shared classical theism and evangelical commitment. A WTS alternative would organize the loci explicitly through **covenant theology** and the **history of special revelation** in a Vosian line, while affirming Bray’s central achievement: a Scripture-governed retrieval of **catholic doctrine** that equips the church with durable grammar for **Trinity**, **gospel**, and **church**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Context**: Canadian-born, late twentieth to early twenty-first century **Anglican evangelical** theologian working amid **postwar Anglican** upheavals and **patristic ressourcement**.
- **Core focus**: Rearticulation of **classical theism** and **Reformed catholicity** through **sola Scriptura** and historically informed **canonical exegesis**.
- **Major works**: **The Doctrine of God** 1993; **Biblical Interpretation** 1996; **Documents of the English Reformation** 1994, 2004; **God Is Love** 2012; **God Has Spoken** 2014; **Doing Theology with the Reformers** 2014; **Augustine on the Christian Life** 2015; **The Church** 2016; **The Attributes of God** 2021.
- **Distinctives**: Defense of **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, **impassibility**; affirmation of **filioque**; organization of loci around **God is love** within **simplicity**; robust **penal substitution** and **spiritual presence** in the **Eucharist**.
- **Interlocutors**: Retrieval of **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Calvin**, **Hooker**; critical engagement with **Barth**, **Rahner**, **Pannenberg**; opposition to **process theology** and **open theism**.
- **Reformed evaluation**: Strong convergence with **Westminster** on **Scripture** and **God**, with cautions about love as the architectonic and preference for explicit **inerrancy** and **covenant** structuring.
- **Legacy**: A leading voice for **evangelical Anglican** theology, shaping pastors and scholars through **historical theology**, **systematics**, and a durable model of **Reformed catholicity** anchored in **Scripture** and **creed**.
