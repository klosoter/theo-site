### TITLE
Middle knowledge as the key to meticulous providence with libertarian freedom.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth-century **analytic philosophy of religion** at **Notre Dame** meets early modern **Jesuit scholasticism**; retrieval of **Luis de Molina** under the influence of **Alvin Plantinga** and against **open theism**.
- Method: **analytic philosophical theology** using **possible worlds semantics**, **counterfactuals**, and a robust defense of **libertarian free will** within a classical doctrine of **divine providence** and **omniscience**.
- Key works: **Divine Providence: The Molinist Account (1998)**; editor with **Michael C. Rea** of **The Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology (2009)**; influential essays on **middle knowledge**, **prayer**, **incarnation**, and the **grounding objection** across the 1990s–2010s.
- Central terms: **middle knowledge**; **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**; **natural and free knowledge**; **feasible worlds**; **Ockhamism** about the past; **providential control** with **libertarian freedom**.
- Main interlocutors: historical **Luis de Molina** and **Dominican Bañezians**; contemporary **William Hasker**, **Robert Adams**, **Peter van Inwagen**, **Eleonore Stump**, **William Lane Craig**, and the broader **open theism** debate.
- Reformed reception cue: tensions around **divine decree**, **compatibilism vs libertarianism**, **divine simplicity** and the loci of **election and grace**, yet appreciation for Flint's rigorous defense of **meticulous providence** and critique of **open theism**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Thomas P. Flint, born in 1955 and teaching for decades at the University of **Notre Dame**, emerged as a leading architect of a revived **Molinism** within the resurgence of late twentieth-century **analytic philosophical theology**. His intellectual formation was shaped in conversation with the **Plantinga** circle, where the tools of **modal logic**, **possible worlds semantics**, and meticulous argument about **freedom and foreknowledge** were brought directly into classical doctrinal loci. The institutional context of Notre Dame, with its distinctive Catholic identity, brought him into living contact with early modern **Jesuit scholasticism**, especially **Luis de Molina** of the 1588 Concordia. Flint situated that sixteenth-century debate between **Molina** and the **Dominican Bañezian** tradition within contemporary disputes over **open theism**, **theological determinism**, and **Ockhamist** responses to fatalism. His method was not antiquarian retrieval but systematic construction: a programmatic proposal for **divine providence** that attempts to secure both comprehensive **sovereignty** and genuine **libertarian freedom**, using the conceptual machinery of **middle knowledge** and **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**.

Flint's prose and classroom presence were marked by the clarity and charity characteristic of the post-**Plantinga** analytic turn. He regularly foregrounded the metaphysical architecture behind doctrinal claims, distinguishing **natural knowledge** of necessary truths, **middle knowledge** of subjunctive conditionals, and **free knowledge** of God's decreed world. His philosophical stance was realist about modal truth, cautious about **truthmaker maximalism**, and sympathetic to **Ockhamism** regarding the fixity of the past and **accidental necessity**. Because Flint was a Roman Catholic philosopher engaging ecclesial doctrine without clerical office, he exhibited a theologian's instinct for the grammar of **Chalcedonian Christology**, **Trinitarian** boundaries, and **Augustinian** accounts of grace, even while he reinterpreted these in a Molinist key. The continuity he sought was to show that analytic precision can deepen rather than replace the classical Christian grammar of **providence**, **incarnation**, and **soteriology**.

Flint's intellectual temperament made him a constructive interlocutor across the spectrum. With **William Lane Craig** he shared advocacy for **Molinism** and a robust account of **divine foreknowledge**, yet his tone and aims were more ecclesially catholic and academically patient. In engagement with critics like **William Hasker** and **Robert Adams**, he pressed hard philosophical objections but always returned to the doctrinal payoffs of **middle knowledge** for prayer, mission, and the rational life before God. The anchoring claim throughout his career, sharpened in debates through the 1990s and 2000s, was that a God endowed with **middle knowledge** can rationally and lovingly orchestrate a world with meticulous **providence** that does not collapse **libertarian freedom**.

#### 2. Major works and the Molinist architecture
Flint's magnum opus, **Divine Providence: The Molinist Account (1998)**, is the definitive contemporary defense of **Molinism** in English. The book offers a systematic treatment of **God's knowledge** in three logical moments, the nature of **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**, and the selection of **feasible worlds** compatible with those counterfactuals. He argues that prior to any divine decree God knows not only necessary truths by **natural knowledge** but also what any possible free creature would do in any possible set of circumstances by **middle knowledge**. On that basis, God then chooses to create a world that secures his purposes by relying on those subjunctive truths, and only then acquires **free knowledge** of what will in fact be. The Molinist claim is that this sequence underwrites both comprehensive **providential control** and undetermined **libertarian choices**. Flint painstakingly distinguishes logical priority from temporal sequence, thereby safeguarding **divine eternity** and **immutability** while ensuring that the content of **middle knowledge** is prevolitional.

A central challenge to this schema is the **grounding objection**, pressed by **Robert Adams** and developed by **William Hasker**, which questions what makes the **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom** true if not the creature's actual free act. Flint's reply is multi-pronged. He resists **truthmaker maximalism**, arguing that not all truths require robust ontological grounds of the truthmaking sort, and he suggests that the truth of a conditional may be secured by the creature's libertarian essence together with the totality of circumstances described in the antecedent. He is also content to treat the truth of some counterfactuals as primitive, insisting that the explanatory gain in **providence** justifies accepting them as basic facts about modal reality. Flint extends the case by arguing that **counterfactuals** populate ordinary reasoning and prudence, so it is not special pleading to admit them into **theology**. As a result, he holds that **middle knowledge** retains its status as knowledge that is both independent of the decree and yet informative enough to guide the decree toward a specific **feasible world**.

In his treatment of **foreknowledge** and **fatalism**, Flint deploys **Ockhamism** to handle the **accidental necessity** of the past. He argues that God’s beliefs about future contingents are **soft facts** whose truth-makers include future events and so do not impose necessity. This Ockhamist strategy complements **Molinism** by ensuring that while God infallibly foreknows what will happen, such foreknowledge neither determines nor entails the acts in question. Together, **Ockhamism** and **middle knowledge** yield a coherent picture where divine **omniscience** is both comprehensive and non-coercive. Flint's account thereby seeks to coordinate the logic of divine knowledge with the grammar of **human responsibility**.

Flint's contribution extends beyond the metaphysics of knowledge to the practical face of **providence**, particularly **petitionary prayer**. In a widely noted chapter of the 1998 book, he argues that **Molinism** can model the efficacy of prayer without reducing it to causal overdetermination. Because God knows via **middle knowledge** how any creature would freely pray in any envisaged situation, God can factor those would-prayers into his selection of a **feasible world** in which his purposes are accomplished through and in answer to free petitions. This yields both a robust motive to pray and a satisfying account of answered prayer that neither manipulates nor marginalizes creaturely agency. The doctrine also supplies resources for handling **miracles** and **special providences**, where **counterfactuals** about human and natural systems allow God to thread the needle between regularity and intervention.

#### 3. Soteriology, Christology, and doctrinal development
Although primarily a philosopher of **providence**, Flint addresses **grace** and **predestination** through a Molinist lens, in continuity with **Jesuit** trajectories. He contends that God, armed with **middle knowledge**, may choose to actualize a world in which his prevenient graces are extended in ways that he knows will be freely accepted by some and freely rejected by others, thus preserving both the gratuity of grace and **libertarian freedom**. Flint explores forms of what later literature calls **transworld depravity**, drawing on **Plantinga**, to illuminate why some might fail to freely respond salvifically in any set of circumstances God could actualize. On such a view God’s election is unconditional at the level of world choice but not coercive at the level of human response. The result is a conditional soteriology operationally, yet nested within an unconditional divine volition at the level of **feasible worlds**. Flint is aware of the pastoral and doctrinal stakes here, and he shows how **Molinism** intends to honor the breadth of divine universal salvific will while acknowledging the reality of fixed creaturely trajectories across modal space.

Flint also contributed to **Christology**, defending a version of the **two minds** account of the **incarnation** that builds on **Thomas V. Morris** while addressing medieval concerns about **communicatio idiomatum** and **unity of the person**. On his view, the incarnate **Logos** possesses both a divine range of awareness and a human cognitive life, with asymmetric access structuring their relation so that kenotic worries are avoided and **Chalcedonian** constraints are observed. The same analytic rigor he brought to **providence** appears in his Christological essays, where he uses distinctions about **de re** and **de dicto** predication, **qua** operators, and **primary vs secondary properties** to articulate how one subject, the Son, can truly have attributes of both **divine** and **human** natures without confusion. Flint employs that apparatus to handle scriptural moments of apparent limitation in Jesus while maintaining **immutability** and **impassibility** at the level of the divine nature *ad intra*.

In debates over **divine freedom**, Flint defended the coherence of God's free choice among **feasible worlds**. He argued against the charge that **Molinism** makes God hostage to a modal landscape that fixes what is achievable, by insisting that divine **aseity** is not compromised by the existence of abstract modal truths any more than it is by the existence of necessary truths of logic. God remains supremely free and wise in choosing which **feasible world** to actualize, even if the set of feasible options is constrained by the truths of **counterfactuals of freedom**. Flint thus navigates between libertarian creaturely freedom and a robustly free divine will, contending that both can be coherently affirmed under **middle knowledge**.

Flint's editorial and pedagogical leadership amplified his constructive proposals. As co-editor with **Michael C. Rea** of **The Oxford Handbook of Philosophical Theology (2009)**, he helped consolidate the field and made room for debates about **divine simplicity**, **eternity**, and **Trinity** to be pursued with the technical precision of analytic philosophy. His essays and seminars often returned to the same knot: can one keep the classical theistic package of **omnipotence**, **omniscience**, and **providence** while respecting the libertarian intuitions many find morally and phenomenologically compelling. Flint's answer, consistently, was yes, and **Molinism** is the key.

#### 4. Interlocutors, debates, and legacy
Flint's main contemporary critic was **William Hasker**, whose **open theism** denies exhaustive divine foreknowledge of future contingents. Flint's replies insist that open theism sacrifices too much of classical **providence**, rendering God's governance risk-laden in ways incompatible with biblical depiction and doxological trust. He argued that **Molinism** keeps divine providence meticulous without lapsing into **theological determinism**. Against **Robert Adams**, he developed the anti-maximalist stance about **truthmakers**, contending that it is philosophically respectable to affirm true counterfactuals without thick ontological grounds. With **Peter van Inwagen**, who sympathized with libertarian intuitions but was wary of modal metaphysics, Flint shared concerns about avoiding fatalism while diverging on the need for **middle knowledge**. With **Eleonore Stump**, who favors **Bañezian** concurrentism and a Thomist account of **causality**, Flint debated whether **Molinism** is compatible with **divine simplicity** and whether foreknown response to grace introduces Pelagian anxieties. Flint positioned himself as faithful to **Aquinas** on divine eternity and primary causality, yet closer to **Molina** on the mechanics of freedom.

Flint was also a friendly co-belligerent with **William Lane Craig** in public defenses of **Molinism** against **open theism** and **process theism**, though he tended to emphasize systematic theologoumena rather than apologetic utility. He used **possible worlds** discourse with care, speaking of **feasible worlds** rather than merely possible ones to highlight how **middle knowledge** restricts divine options. His account of **petitionary prayer** and missionary success made contact with ecclesial practice, showing that **Molinism** can explain why God might call his people to pray and evangelize without suggesting that their actions are dispensable. Flint's arguments also crossed into philosophy of science, where he offered ways to think about **quantum indeterminacy** and **randomness** as compatible with **providence** once **counterfactuals** are admitted.

Historically, Flint set **Molinism** on firmer analytic footing than it had enjoyed since the early modern controversies. He clarified how **middle knowledge** is a distinct epistemic category, not reducible to divine conjecture or to post-decretal **free knowledge**, and he formalized the selection of **feasible worlds** as the site of providential wisdom. His treatments of **Ockhamism**, **fatalism**, and **prayer** are now standard points of reference. Even critics grant that after 1998 one must meet the Flintian version of the view to reject **Molinism** responsibly. With his death in 2019, a generation of students and colleagues marked the loss of a measured voice whose substantive charity made space for debate about the most basic matters of **God and freedom**.

In the end, Flint's constructive proposal yields a portrait of divine **providence** that is both meticulous and morally serious. By distinguishing **natural**, **middle**, and **free knowledge**, and by positing true **counterfactuals of creaturely freedom**, he maintains a robust **libertarian** picture of human agency without diminishing **divine sovereignty**. That synthesis, rather than any single argument, is his enduring contribution to **philosophical theology**. The structure of his thought offers clean lines along which further work on **grace**, **sin**, **incarnation**, and **prayer** may proceed, and it sets a standard for how analytic tools can be applied to doctrinal construction without losing sight of the Church's classical commitments.

From a Reformed or Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, the Flintian achievement is both impressive and problematic. Positively, his defense of meticulous **providence**, critique of **open theism**, and careful affirmation of God's exhaustive **omniscience** resonate with **Reformed orthodoxy**. Yet the **Molinist** partitioning of divine cognition into logical moments oriented by prevolitional truths about creaturely acts appears to threaten **divine simplicity** and **aseity**, insofar as God's knowledge seems conditioned by truths not identical to the simple divine act. Moreover, the insistence on **libertarian freedom** sits uneasily with **Reformed compatibilism** and a decretal account of **concursus** that grounds responsibility in the moral nature of agents under God’s comprehensive decree. Flint's soteriological deployment of **middle knowledge** strains against **unconditional election**, appearing to smuggle in foreseen faith and frustrating Reformed emphases on the asymmetry of **grace**. A Reformed alternative would commend a doctrine of **exhaustive divine decree**, **compatibilist freedom**, and **primary and secondary causality** under **providence**, appealing to **Calvins autotheos** and classical **simplicity** to avoid the ontological intermediacy posited by **middle knowledge**. Still, the Reformed theologian should be grateful for Flint's lucid articulation of the field of dispute and for his demonstration that the moral seriousness of agency can be preserved without retreating from robust **sovereignty**.
