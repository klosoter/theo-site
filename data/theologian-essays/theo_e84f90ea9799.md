### TITLE
Bridging historical precision and evangelical conviction: Calvin retrieval and justification clarified for contemporary ecumenical theology.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth and early twenty-first century **British evangelical** setting, long tenure at **London School of Theology**, working within a post-Vatican II **ecumenical** and post-critical **historical theology** environment.
- Method marked by **primary-source historical retrieval**, careful lexical analysis, and a restrained **evangelical hermeneutic** that distinguishes description from prescription while guarding **sola fide**.
- Key works: **A Concise History of Christian Thought** 1984, rev. 2006; **John Calvin: Student of the Church Fathers** 1999; **Justification by Faith in Catholic-Protestant Dialogue: An Evangelical Assessment** 2002.
- Central categories: **forensic justification**, **imputed righteousness**, **infused grace**, **simul iustus et peccator**, **Calvin’s duplex gratia**, **patristic ressourcement**.
- Main interlocutors: **John Calvin**, **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **the Council of Trent**, the **Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification**, and contemporary **Catholic-Protestant dialogue**.
- Reformed reception cue: appreciated for clarifying **Calvin** and defending **forensic justification**, yet queried on ecumenical rapprochement where **imputation** and **assurance** remain non-negotiables.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Anthony N. S. Lane emerged as a distinctive voice in contemporary **Reformed** historical theology by combining patient archival work with an evangelical commitment to **sola fide**. As a long-time professor at the **London School of Theology**, he matured within a post-Vatican II horizon where Protestant-Catholic discussion on **justification** and **authority** reopened after centuries of polemic. The climate after the 1960s encouraged careful rereadings of **Trent**, newly translated sources from **Aquinas** and **Augustine**, and the rediscovery of sixteenth-century **Reformation** nuances beyond confessional stereotypes. Lane’s formation reflects this moment: he reads broadly, cites generously, and insists that historical judgments be warranted by primary texts rather than inherited slogans. His method is descriptive first and then evaluative, trained on the close semantics of **Latin** and **Greek** doctrinal terms, especially around **imputation**, **infusion**, **merit**, and **assurance**.

Lane’s intellectual project is animated by two intertwined questions: how to locate **John Calvin** within the longer patristic and medieval tradition, and how to adjudicate the extent of convergence and divergence on **justification** between contemporary **Catholic** and **Protestant** voices. The first task requires precise mapping of **Calvin’s** engagement with **the Fathers** and the second demands meticulous comparisons of definitions within **Trent**, later Catholic manuals, and modern ecumenical statements such as the **Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification**. In both tasks Lane deploys a consistent epistemic stance: he distrusts grand theses that outrun textual demonstration, prefers restrained causal attributions to ideas, and differentiates doctrinal identity from rhetorical accent. The resulting portrait is historically thick yet theologically clear on the dividing lines of **forensic justification** and **assurance**.

#### 2. Calvin as patristic heir and Protestant reformer
Lane’s best-known monograph, **John Calvin: Student of the Church Fathers** 1999, reframes **Calvin** not as iconoclast but as a discerning heir of **patristic ressourcement**. Against portrayals of **Calvin** as a purely biblical primitivist or a systematic innovator akin to **Aquinas**, Lane shows a multi-textured use of **Augustine**, **Chrysostom**, **Cyprian**, and **Tertullian**. The analysis moves beyond citation counts to track functional roles: **Chrysostom** as an exegetical ally, **Cyprian** in debates on **discipline** and **the church**, and **Augustine** as the normative voice on **grace** and **original sin**. For Lane, **Calvin’s duplex gratia** union with Christ in justification and sanctification appears as Augustinian in substance while decisively rearticulated in a juridical key that centers **forensic justification**.

A hallmark of Lane’s study is its refusal to flatten **Calvin’s** diversity of patristic uses into a single ideology. Instead, he shows how **Calvin** negotiates doctrinal continuity and reform by leveraging **patristic consensus** against late medieval developments on **merit** and **penance**. By analyzing how **Calvin** handles contested loci such as **the Eucharist**, **baptism**, and **ecclesial authority**, Lane demonstrates that appeal to **the Fathers** is neither window dressing nor anti-scholastic impulse but a disciplined strategy to locate **Reformed** teaching within the great tradition while contesting specific medieval accretions. This framing supports Lane’s broader thesis: **Reformed orthodoxy** draws from catholic sources to articulate the evangel of **grace alone**, not as novelty but as faithful retrieval shaped by Scripture.

#### 3. Justification, imputation, and ecumenical precision
Lane’s most influential constructive work, **Justification by Faith in Catholic-Protestant Dialogue: An Evangelical Assessment** 2002, stands at the center of post-1990s discussions around the **Joint Declaration on the Doctrine of Justification** 1999. Here Lane acts as both historian and analyst, comparing **Trent’s Decretum de iustificatione** with twentieth-century Catholic exegesis, Lutheran and Reformed confessions, and the emergent language of ecumenical consensus. He privileges careful definition of terms such as **iustificatio** as declaration, **iustitia imputata** as reckoned righteousness, and **iustitia infusa** as interior renewal, showing how these are configured differently in the **magisterial Reformers** and in **Trent**. The centerpiece argument asserts that while modern Catholic documents attenuate older polemical condemnations and adopt more robust **grace** language, decisive differences remain regarding the constitutive role of **imputed righteousness** and the status of **simul iustus et peccator**.

Lane’s evaluation of the **Joint Declaration** is characteristically nuanced: he welcomes convergence on the priority of **grace**, the non-meritorious character of faith, and the repudiation of Pelagianizing tendencies, yet he judges the statement insufficiently explicit on the forensic nature of **justification** and the unrepeatable, definitive reckoning of the sinner as righteous in Christ. He argues that without an unambiguous affirmation of **imputation** and of the believer’s ongoing sinfulness coram Deo captured by **simul iustus et peccator**, the agreement risks operating at the level of compatible slogans rather than doctrinal identity. His preferred path forward is not withdrawal but lexical clarity: ecumenical texts must bind themselves to the grammar of **forensic declaration**, lest **sanctification** be smuggled into the article by which the church stands or falls.

#### 4. Trent, merit, and assurance
Across essays and lectures, Lane returns to **Trent** to probe its teaching on **merit**, **condignity** and **congruity**, and the relation of **baptism** to initial justification. He recognizes internal complexity in **Trent’s** canons, noting the simultaneous insistence on the gratuity of justification and the maintenance of a real increase of **righteousness** that bears on final salvation. Lane’s contribution is to disentangle these strands without caricature. He argues that **Trent** safeguards the necessity of grace against crude Pelagianism but embeds renewal into the definition of **justification** in a way that blurs the Lutheran and Reformed separation of **justification** and **sanctification**. The result, he contends, is real divergence on what counts as the formal cause of the justified status, that is, **iustitia Christi imputata** versus **iustitia inhaerens**.

Lane ties this doctrinal configuration to spiritual experience by treating **assurance**. Drawing on **Calvin** and the **Reformed** tradition, he defends assurance grounded in Christ’s alien righteousness and the Spirit’s **testimonium internum**, arguing that probabilistic or contingent assurance characteristic of some Catholic streams follows from their inclusion of moral renewal within justification itself. He neither denies the Catholic possibility of confidence nor caricatures pastoral practice, but he insists that certainty rests on the objective **forensic verdict** apart from works, while evidential fruits belong to **sanctification**. This insistence preserves the Reformation’s Christocentric consolation.

#### 5. Surveying the tradition: teaching as retrieval
Lane’s pedagogical vocation issues in the widely used **A Concise History of Christian Thought** first published 1984 and revised 2006. The book exemplifies his method: lucid summaries organized around key figures and doctrines, with special attention to **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Luther**, **Calvin**, **Edwards**, and modern critics. The volume functions not as a neutral encyclopedia but as a guide to reading the church’s past with informed sympathy and doctrinal discrimination. For Lane, historical literacy serves theological wisdom; knowing how debates over **the Trinity**, **Christology**, and **grace** were framed gives contemporary readers the conceptual tools to avoid anachronism when approaching contested topics like **justification**. His survey refuses teleology yet makes clear that the **Reformation** codified lasting insights about **Scripture**, **grace**, and **faith** that cannot be traded away in ecumenical commerce.

Within this teaching horizon, Lane often highlights the lexical precision of doctrinal controversies. He shows how small shifts in terms such as **meritum**, **caritas**, **fides formata**, or **habitus** altered trajectories in medieval soteriology, and how **Luther’s** redefinition of **fides** as receptive trust rather than a formed virtue undergirds **forensic justification**. This sensitivity to language is a theological method: doctrinal settlement requires shared semantics. Without agreement on the meaning of **iustificare**, apparent consensus is rhetorical. Lane’s students thus learn that theology is an exercise in historical philology disciplined by canonical exegesis.

#### 6. Calvin’s doctrine in ecumenical perspective
Lane’s Calvin studies extend to loci where ecumenical implications surface, notably **sacraments**, **church order**, and **sanctification**. He reads **Calvin** on **Eucharist** as mediating between **Lutheran** real presence and **Zwinglian** memorialism, deploying **pneumatic presence** and **spiritual feeding** categories that remain catholic in breadth. Yet he underlines that **Calvin’s** sacramental realism never softens the decisive juridical moment of **justification** by **imputation**. By holding together **duplex gratia** and **forensic declaration**, Lane presents **Calvin** as a resource for constructive proposals that resist the either-or of bare legalism or moral infusion. This portrait supports Lane’s call for ecumenical patience: Reformed theology can speak robustly of transformation while guarding the article of **justification**.

On the doctrine of **sanctification**, Lane traces how **Calvin’s** union with Christ frames both **mortification** and **vivification**, while refusing to make progress in holiness feed back into the ground of acceptance. He notes strategic convergences with Catholic language of **renewal** and **charity** but insists that these belong to a different locus. The point for ecumenical dialogue is methodological: set right the loci, and genuine commonality appears without compromising the **forensic** grammar. Lane thus offers a way to affirm moral seriousness without theological confusion.

#### 7. Dialogues and debates in the contemporary scene
Beyond the **Joint Declaration**, Lane engages broader currents such as the **New Perspective on Paul** and debates on **Roman Catholic** retrievals of **Augustine**. While not a polemicist against the **New Perspective**, he presses its exponents to clarify the courtroom metaphor and the status of **imputation** in Pauline texts like 2 Corinthians 5 and Romans 4. His argument is not that covenantal readings are wrong but that they must not eclipse the legal reckoning at the heart of **justification**. On Catholic Augustinianism, Lane welcomes the ressourcement that centers **grace** while observing that the decisive sixteenth-century turn was not the priority of grace alone but the nature of the justifying act as **forensic declaration**. The enduring Protestant protest, in Lane’s account, is focused and non-redundant.

Lane’s contribution is as much tone as thesis. He models generous critique, acknowledging Catholic corrections of past Protestant caricatures while asking for equally specific Catholic guarantees about **imputed righteousness** and **assurance**. He resists both false irenicism that trades away essentials and sectarianism that refuses to see progress. In this he stands as a **Reformed** scholar who prizes catholicity ordered by the gospel’s juridical core.

#### 8. Style, virtues, and limits of the approach
Lane’s writing displays virtues that commend his work for seminar rooms and ecumenical tables alike: transparency about sources, economy of thesis, and a near forensic attention to doctrinal definitions. The hallmark terms recur for good reason: **forensic justification**, **imputation**, **infusion**, **assurance**, **duplex gratia**, **simul iustus et peccator**. He avoids grand metaphysical syntheses and cautious speculation about **divine simplicity** or **nature and grace**, preferring focused disputes where textual adjudication is feasible. This limitation is strategic; it makes his verdicts portable across traditions, because they are tethered to the same primary documents that both sides claim.

One cost of this focus is a comparative reticence about larger dogmatic systems and their internal pressures. Lane rarely reconstructs full accounts of **covenant theology**, the **ordo salutis**, or **ecclesiology** in their confessional detail, even when these would clarify how **justification** coheres with **sanctification** and **assurance** within **Reformed** orthodoxy. Yet even here the restraint is instructive. By leaving space between historical description and confessional exposition, he invites theologians to complete the dogmatic work on the firm footing of shared texts and sharpened terms.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a confessional **Reformed** and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Lane’s project exhibits exemplary strengths and a few tensions. His retrieval of **Calvin** as patristically conversant aligns with classical **Reformed catholicity**, and his insistence on **forensic justification**, **imputed righteousness**, and **simul iustus et peccator** reflects core Westminster commitments. His critique of the **Joint Declaration** for its ambiguity on **imputation** is judicious, and his account of **assurance** rightly grounds consolation in Christ’s finished work and the Spirit’s witness rather than in inherent renewal. The tensions emerge not in doctrine but in ecumenical strategy: by prioritizing lexical clarification within shared texts, Lane can appear to understate the deeper principial contrasts between a Scripture-normed **covenant of grace** and a nature-grace schema that tends to reconfigure **justification** through **infused charity**. A WTS analysis would press further on revelation and authority, urging that convergence on **grace** cannot be safely assessed apart from principial commitments to the sufficiency and perspicuity of **Scripture**, the law-gospel distinction within redemptive history, and the covenantal frame that secures the extra nos of **imputation** and the definitive, non-augmentable status of the justified. Within those parameters, Lane’s careful mapping is a valuable instrument, provided it is joined to explicit confessional delimitation that resists any synthesis in which **sanctification** seeps into the ground of acceptance or **assurance** is rendered probabilistic by sacramental or moral contingencies.
