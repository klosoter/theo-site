### TITLE
Nonreductive physicalism as a Christian anthropology: emergent mind, responsible agency, and ecclesial formation grounded in resurrection hope.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late 20th–early 21st century neuroscience** meets **philosophical theology** in the American integrationist milieu of **Fuller Theological Seminary** and the Templeton-era **science–theology dialogue**.
- **Interdisciplinary method** marked by **critical realism**, **explanatory pluralism**, and **levels-of-analysis** reasoning against both **reductive physicalism** and **Cartesian dualism**.
- **Whatever Happened to the Soul?** 1998; **Did My Neurons Make Me Do It?** 2007; **Neuroscience, Psychology, and Religion** 2009; **The Physical Nature of Christian Life** 2012.
- **Nonreductive physicalism**, **emergence**, **supervenience without reduction**, **downward or constraint causation**, **personal agency**, **imago Dei as relational-capacitative**, **resurrection-centered eschatology**.
- Interlocutors include **Nancey Murphy**, **Malcolm Jeeves**, **Michael Polanyi**, **Roger Sperry**, **Jaegwon Kim**, **David Chalmers**, **J. P. Moreland**, **Richard Swinburne**, **Kevin Corcoran**, **Lynne Rudder Baker**.
- Reformed cue: affirm **compatibilist responsibility** and **resurrection primacy**, scrutinize **intermediate state**, **concursus and providence**, and **imago Dei** within **classical Reformed anthropology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and scientific-theological setting
Warren S. Brown is a contemporary figure whose enduring contribution is the articulation of a **nonreductive physicalist** anthropology that is both scientifically credible and theologically constructive. Working from the institutional base of **Fuller Theological Seminary** and directing the **Lee Travis Research Institute**, Brown has long stood at the intersection of **neuropsychology** and **philosophical theology**. His empirical research, including sustained work on **corpus callosum** function, **callosal agenesis**, and cognitive integration, grew in the wake of **Roger Sperry's split-brain** revolution, the rise of **cognitive neuroscience**, and the maturation of **complexity theory**. That scientific context met an ecumenical Protestant theological environment shaped by **Templeton-funded science–religion programs**, creating space for Brown to propose an anthropology that rejects both **reductive physicalism** and **Cartesian substance dualism** while remaining attentive to **biblical holism** and **resurrection hope**.

Brown's historical location is defined by late twentieth-century debates over **mind–body** relations, the **soul**, and **free will**. These debates were reshaped by advances in **neural plasticity**, **distributed processing**, and the systems-level organization of the brain, as well as by analytic philosophy's work on **supervenience**, **multiple realizability**, and **causal exclusion**. Brown entered this conversation with a commitment to **empirical rigor** and **theological responsibility**, insisting that any Christian view of the **imago Dei** and of human **agency** must be accountable to what **neuroscience** actually shows about the embodied mind.

#### 2. Method, epistemic stance, and conceptual tools
Methodologically, Brown embodies an **interdisciplinary integration** that prioritizes **critical realism** over both naïve biblical concordism and scientistic reduction. He argues for **explanatory pluralism** and **levels-of-analysis** reasoning, claiming that phenomena such as **intentional action**, **virtue**, and **worship** require descriptions and explanations different from neuronal microdynamics while remaining wholly consistent with them. Influenced by **Michael Polanyi's tacit knowledge**, **emergent organization**, and **top-down constraints**, as well as by the cybernetic insights of **Donald MacKay**, Brown advances **downward causation** in a non-mysterian key, naming it more precisely as **constraint causation** in complex systems.

Against **reductive physicalism**, Brown holds that higher-level patterns possess genuine **causal efficacy** as constraints on lower-level degrees of freedom, thereby defending **mental causation** and **personal agency**. Against **Cartesian dualism**, he rejects positing an immaterial **substance soul** as explanatorily superfluous in light of **neural embodiment**. His **nonreductive physicalism** is therefore a metaphysical thesis about the human being as a single, fully physical organism, together with an epistemic thesis about the necessity of multi-level accounts of **human powers**. He uses **supervenience without reduction** to mark dependence without identity, citing work in **dynamical systems** and **complexity** to situate intentionality within nested hierarchies of organization. This method is deployed in service of Christian doctrines such as the **imago Dei**, **moral responsibility**, and **resurrection**, which he interprets through a biblically attuned **holism** in conversation with **Hebrew nephesh** language rather than Hellenistic **psyche** metaphysics.

#### 3. Major works and constructive arc
The 1998 volume **Whatever Happened to the Soul? Scientific and Theological Portraits of Human Nature**, co-edited with **Nancey Murphy** and **H. Newton Malony**, crystalized Brown's project. The book gathers scientific, philosophical, and theological essays to argue that Christians can relinquish **substance dualism** without abandoning **immortality hopes** or **moral agency**, re-centering eschatology on **bodily resurrection** rather than on the **immortal soul**. In 2007, **Did My Neurons Make Me Do It? Philosophical and Neurobiological Perspectives on Moral Responsibility and Free Will**, co-authored with **Nancey Murphy**, engaged analytic debates on **free will**, offering a **nonreductive physicalist compatibilism** underwritten by **constraint causation** and a **reasons-responsive** account of agency. The 2009 book **Neuroscience, Psychology, and Religion: Illusions, Delusions, and Realities about Human Nature**, co-authored with **Malcolm Jeeves**, serves as a lucid mapping of contested boundary lines between **neural explanations** and **religious experience**, defending a chastened realism about **spiritual life** in light of **neural correlates**.

A particularly ecclesial turn arrives in **The Physical Nature of Christian Life: Neuroscience, Psychology, and the Church** 2012, co-authored with **Brad D. Strawn**. There Brown extends his nonreductive framework into **spiritual formation**, arguing that **practices**, **liturgy**, and **community** leverage **neuroplasticity** to shape **virtue**, **attention**, and **desire**. Throughout these works Brown engages cognate positions such as **Kevin Corcoran's constitution view** and **Lynne Rudder Baker's constitution of persons**, situating his own stance as a straightforward **Christian materialism** with robust emergentist commitments rather than a metaphysics of constitution by non-identical bodies.

#### 4. Anthropology: biblical holism, imago Dei, and eschatology
At the core of Brown's anthropology is the claim that the human being is a **unified physical organism** whose **mental life** is an emergent set of capacities realized by the brain's systemic organization. He reads Scripture's **nephesh**, **basar**, and **ruach** as indexing a **holistic** account of the person rather than a **dualist** anthropology, and he sees **Paul's soma pneumatikon** in 1 Corinthians 15 as a **resurrection-transformed body** rather than as a **dual substance**. Accordingly, Brown relocates Christian hope from **immortality of the soul** to **bodily resurrection**, grounding personal identity in **God's covenantal fidelity** and **narrative continuity** rather than in an incorporeal **anima**.

On the **imago Dei**, Brown emphasizes **relational-capacitative** features such as **language**, **moral deliberation**, **sociality**, and **openness to God**, all realized in and through **embodied neural networks**. He is wary of locating the image in a separable **rational soul**, preferring a **functional-relational** account that can be mapped onto **developmental neurobiology** and **social cognition**. Sin is rendered not as a defect in a detachable soul but as a corruption of **habitus**, **attention**, and **desire** within embodied persons embedded in **fallen social systems**. Eschatologically, Brown acknowledges the problem of the **intermediate state**, often proposing a view of **divine memory** and **preservation of identity** in God between death and **resurrection**, thereby attempting to honor biblical witness and pastoral realities without positing a **separable soul-substance**.

#### 5. Agency, freedom, and moral responsibility
Brown's mature position on **free will** and **moral responsibility** is elaborated in **Did My Neurons Make Me Do It?**, where he and **Nancey Murphy** defend a **nonreductive physicalist compatibilism**. They adapt **John Martin Fischer's reasons-responsiveness** and complex systems insights to contend that human choices are caused by the agent as a whole through **top-down constraints**, not merely by micro-level neural events. Against **Jaegwon Kim's causal exclusion** argument, Brown argues that higher-level properties and patterns can be causally efficacious in virtue of their **organizational form** and **constraint relations** that channel lower-level causal flows. The upshot is that human agents are responsible because their actions issue from **stable character**, **formed intentions**, and **sensitive responsiveness** to reasons within a suitably **open** causal structure.

This account leans on **multiple realizability**, **dynamical systems**, and the **supervenience** of mental states on neural states without reduction. Brown holds that **neural plasticity** underwrites **self-regulation** and **virtue cultivation**, opening a space for robust **moral formation**. Rather than a libertarian power of **contrary choice**, responsibility is linked to **selfhood** as an emergent unity whose **volitional power** is exercised through embodied practices. Such compatibilism aims to be faithful both to **neuroscientific evidence** and to Christian commitments concerning **guilt**, **praise**, and **the call to holiness**.

#### 6. Divine action and providence within an emergentist frame
While Brown does not offer a full-blown doctrine of **providence**, his nonreductive framework implies a view of **divine action** that honors both **regularity** in nature and God's **personal governance**. Following **Polanyi** and resonating with **John Polkinghorne**, he suggests that God can act through **top-down or constraint causation**, shaping boundary conditions, social systems, and personal narratives in ways that are not **violative** of lower-level regularities. Brown tolerates the presence of **stochastic indeterminacy** in neural processes but does not rest divine action on gaps. Rather, God's **providence** is seen in the emergence and sustenance of **order**, the **teleological grain** of human capacities for **truth**, **goodness**, and **worship**, and the **ecclesial forms** that shape embodied life for **love of God and neighbor**.

This reframing of divine action is meant to secure both **scientific integrity** and **theological confession**. It relocates many traditional claims about **sanctification**, **guidance**, and **gifts of the Spirit** into domains where **levels-of-explanation** logic can operate. Thus prayer, sacrament, and communal discipline have **neurobiological correlates** without being reduced to them. The order is one of **concurrence** articulated with **emergence**, such that God is active in and through secondary causes understood as **multi-level causal structures**.

#### 7. Church, formation, and the physical nature of Christian life
In **The Physical Nature of Christian Life**, Brown and **Brad D. Strawn** explicate how **ecclesial practices** shape the brain and, by extension, **character**. They connect **virtue theory** with **neuroplasticity**, arguing that habits like **gratitude**, **confession**, **scripture meditation**, and **communal worship** are not merely spiritual but also **embodied** training regimens. This resonates with broader currents in **practical theology** and **philosophical anthropology**, where **habitus** language mediates between **Augustinian** accounts of desire and contemporary **psychology** of attention. Brown here extends **nonreductive physicalism** into a pastoral theology that sees the **church** as a community of **embodied formation**, thereby integrating **neuroscience**, **virtue ethics**, and **ecclesiology**.

Importantly, Brown resists the temptation to claim that neuroscience can validate theology. Instead he adopts **critical realism** to insist that **neural mechanisms** are correlative to, not constitutive of, the **graces** of **sanctification**. The contribution is a pedagogy for **holiness** that appreciates **embodied limits** and **creaturely means**. By emphasizing **distributed processing** and the social embeddedness of cognition, Brown highlights how **community norms**, **narratives**, and **liturgy** function as **top-down constraints** that guide **personal agency** toward **Christlikeness**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, debates, and philosophical traction
Brown's position sits within a crowded field of **philosophy of mind** and **theological anthropology**. He engages **David Chalmers** on the **hard problem of consciousness**, acknowledging the explanatory gap while arguing that the gap is not a license for **dual-substance** metaphysics. With **Jaegwon Kim**, he wrestles over **supervenience** and **exclusion**, proposing **constraint causation** as a way to secure **mental causation** for emergent properties. Against **J. P. Moreland** and **Richard Swinburne**, Brown denies that Christian doctrine requires a **substance dualist soul**, contending that **resurrection** and **divine faithfulness** suffice to ground **personal identity**. In conversation with **Kevin Corcoran** and **Lynne Rudder Baker**, he appreciates **constitution views** yet prefers a more straightforward **material unity** that avoids positing non-identical but colocated entities.

On **free will**, Brown aligns partly with **Daniel Dennett's compatibilism** while resisting Dennett's tendency toward **deflationary reduction** of agency. He draws on **virtue ethics** and **reasons-responsiveness** to avoid both **hard determinism** and **metaphysical libertarianism**. In the theology of **divine action**, he is proximate to **noninterventionist accounts** in the **Zygon** and **Vatican Observatory** dialogues, yet insists that **providence** can be robustly affirmed within an **emergentist metaphysic**. Throughout, Brown maintains fidelity to **biblical holism**, regularly returning to **1 Corinthians 15** and **Hebraic anthropology** to regulate speculative moves.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed and WTS** vantage point, Brown's **nonreductive physicalism** garners real commendation for its insistence on **creaturely embodiment**, its refusal of **reductive naturalism**, and its re-centering of Christian hope on **bodily resurrection** in continuity with **Pauline eschatology**. His **compatibilist account of agency** resonates with Reformed emphases on **providence**, **secondary causes**, and **responsibility** under divine **sovereignty**. His ecclesial turn in **The Physical Nature of Christian Life** accords with a **means-of-grace** understanding of formation through **Word and sacrament**, now explicated in **embodied** terms. Yet several tensions remain. First, classical Reformed confessions uphold a real **soul–body distinction** ordered to a conscious **intermediate state** with Christ, as seen in **Calvin's** rejection of **psychopannychia** and in the **Westminster Confession**. Brown's appeal to **divine memory** and identity preservation between death and **resurrection** lacks the metaphysical resources to explicate **beatific presence** apart from the body, potentially unsettling pastoral assurances regarding the dead in Christ. Second, the **imago Dei** as merely **relational-capacitative** risks thinning robust **ontological accounts** tied to rational and spiritual powers that ground **knowledge of God**, **federal headship**, and **regeneration**. Third, while **constraint causation** is a promising way to articulate **secondary causality**, it may under-describe **divine concursus** and **particular providence**, especially in prayer and **effectual calling**, where Reformed theology confesses a more intimate **operation of the Spirit** than general **top-down** descriptions capture. Finally, any adoption of Brown's framework must reckon with **Christology**. The tradition affirms a true human **rational soul** in the **anhypostatic–enhypostatic** union. A strict **materialism** could complicate that grammar unless supplemented by a **hylomorphic duality** of **form and matter** that preserves **soul** as the organizing principle of the body without positing a separable substance. A constructive Reformed alternative would combine Brown's **holistic embodiment**, **multi-level causality**, and **resurrection priority** with **Thomistic hylomorphism** or **holistic dualism**, thereby securing the **intermediate state**, a thicker **imago Dei**, and a more precise **concursus** while retaining the genuine insights of **neuroscience** for sanctification and pastoral care.
