### TITLE
Inerrancy by Genre: Evangelical redaction criticism that reads Matthew as midrashic theological art

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar evangelicalism**, British critical scholarship, and the **ETS inerrancy crisis** frame Gundry in late twentieth-century North American debates.
- **Evangelical redaction criticism**, **genre-sensitive inerrancy**, and canonical authority form his method, with stress on **authorial intention** and Second Temple **Jewish midrash**.
- **The Use of the Old Testament in St. Matthew's Gospel** 1967, **The Church and the Tribulation** 1973, **Soma in Biblical Theology** 1976, **Matthew: A Commentary on His Literary and Theological Art** 1982, **Mark: A Commentary on His Apology for the Cross** 1993, **Matthew: A Commentary on His Handbook for a Mixed Church under Persecution** 1994, **Jesus the Word according to John the Sectarian** 2002, **The Old Is Better** 2005, **The Jesus of the Gospels** 2009, **Commentary on the New Testament** 2010, **Peter: False Disciple and Apostate according to Saint Matthew** 2015.
- **Midrashic composition**, **redaction criticism**, **Markan priority**, **apology for the cross**, **posttribulationism**, **Pauline soma**, **Johannine sectarianism** are central categories.
- Interlocutors and influences include **F. F. Bruce**, **Rudolf Bultmann**, **Norman Geisler**, **D. A. Carson**, **Craig Blomberg**, **James D. G. Dunn**.
- Reformed reception cue: appreciation for **authorial intent** and **redemptive history**, yet caution regarding claims of **non-historicity** within inspired **Gospel narratives**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Robert H. **Gundry** emerged within postwar **evangelical** scholarship that sought academic credibility without surrendering **biblical inerrancy**. Trained in Britain under **F. F. Bruce** at Manchester, he absorbed the strengths of historical criticism while retaining a confessional fidelity to the **canon**. His decades at **Westmont College** positioned him at the nexus of churchly instruction and scholarly exchange, a location that shaped his writing of widely used textbooks like **A Survey of the New Testament** 1970 and fostered his apologetic posture toward both academy and church. The larger context included the **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** 1978 and the rise of the **Evangelical Theological Society** as a boundary-setting guild, through which questions of **genre**, **truth**, and **authorial intention** came to a head.

Gundry’s signature method is **evangelical redaction criticism** joined to **genre-sensitive inerrancy**. He argues that truth claims are indexed to **literary form**, so that if a Gospel writer employs **midrashic** elaboration or figurative compression, inerrancy is preserved when readers render judgments according to that form rather than by modern historiography. His practice blends close **synoptic comparison**, **Second Temple Jewish intertextuality**, and the recognition of **Markan priority**, while carefully tracing how evangelists employ the Old Testament to articulate **messianic fulfillment**. Against both naive harmonization and skeptical reduction, he contends for a robust **authorial intention** that uses artistry to intensify theology.

#### 2. Early work on Matthew, Paul, and eschatology
Gundry’s first major publication, **The Use of the Old Testament in St. Matthew’s Gospel** 1967, established his attention to **Matthew’s formula quotations**, midrashic syntheses, and the way the evangelist rereads **Israel’s Scriptures** to present Jesus as **Messiah**. The study argued that Matthew’s hermeneutic is both faithful and innovative, weaving typological textures in which **Jesus as Emmanuel** recapitulates Israel’s story. This emphasis on **typology**, **fulfillment**, and literary intentionality became the platform for his later claim that Matthew’s mode includes theological embellishment.

His **Soma in Biblical Theology** 1976 confronted the then widespread claim, derived in part from **Rudolf Bultmann**, that **Pauline anthropology** is holistic in a way that evacuates **soma** of its concrete bodily sense. Gundry contended that **soma** in Paul ordinarily denotes the physical body, sometimes as the person embodied, and that resurrection hope is unequivocally **bodily** rather than a mythic cipher for existential selfhood. This reading aims to secure **resurrection realism**, guarding against a docetic anthropology while maintaining Paul’s ethical summons for embodied holiness.

In **The Church and the Tribulation** 1973, Gundry broke with popular American **pretribulationism**, arguing exegetically for a **posttribulational** rapture. He grounds this in the unity of the **people of God**, the sequence of events in **Matthew 24** and **1 Thessalonians 4**, and a critique of dispensational gap theories. The proposal situates the church within eschatological suffering, emphasizes endurance, and underwrites a theology of **discipleship** that is cruciform rather than escapist.

#### 3. Matthew as literary and theological art, and the ETS crisis
Gundry’s most controversial book, **Matthew: A Commentary on His Literary and Theological Art** 1982, set forth his now-famous thesis that Matthew composes with **midrashic freedom** to expound theology to a church under pressure. He highlights episodes that, in his judgment, display signs of Christian **haggadah**, such as the **Magi**, the **coin in the fish’s mouth**, **Peter’s walking on water**, the **resurrection of the saints** in Matthew 27, and the **guard at the tomb**. He concludes that Matthew sometimes intensifies or reshapes tradition to preach Christ, presenting a mode of truth that is more **figural** and theological than strictly stenographic. This does not deny **resurrection historicity** or the fundamental factuality of Jesus, but it reconfigures the boundary between event and exposition.

The work provoked sharp reaction within the **Evangelical Theological Society**, culminating in 1983 with a vote that led to Gundry’s resignation. Critics such as **Norman Geisler** argued that his reading violated the ETS affirmation of **inerrancy**, understood to entail the factual historicity of narrated events. Defenders contended that inerrancy should be defined by **authorial intent** and **literary genre**. The episode functioned as a referendum on whether evangelicalism could integrate **redaction criticism** and **genre criticism** while retaining a classical doctrine of Scripture. Gundry later produced a shorter sequel, **Matthew: A Commentary on His Handbook for a Mixed Church under Persecution** 1994, clarifying pastoral stakes and sustaining the thrust that Matthew writes artfully to stabilize a community under duress.

#### 4. Mark and an apology for the cross
Turning to Mark, **Mark: A Commentary on His Apology for the Cross** 1993 argues that Mark is constructing a sustained **apologia** for the scandal of a crucified Messiah. Gundry reads Mark’s **narrative irony**, **messianic secrecy**, and the persistent misunderstanding of the **disciples** as rhetorical strategies to commend the cross as God’s wisdom. The disciples, and preeminently **Peter**, come across not as models but as foils who resist the way of the cross until after the resurrection. Gundry’s portrait of **Peter’s apostasy** functions to call the church to a cruciform **discipleship** that repudiates triumphalism.

This theme generated fresh controversy. Gundry’s later monograph, **Peter: False Disciple and Apostate according to Saint Matthew** 2015, extends the thesis to **Matthew**, proposing that the evangelist portrays Peter as paradigmatically compromised until restoration. The cumulative effect is to cast apostolic failure as the stage on which the **gospel of grace** displays its power, yet it also tests traditional readings that emphasize **apostolic authority** and ecclesial stability.

#### 5. Johannine sectarianism and evangelical identity
In **Jesus the Word according to John the Sectarian** 2002, subtitled a **paleofundamentalist manifesto**, Gundry engages **Johannine high Christology** as boundary-drawing proclamation. He contends that the Gospel of **John** delineates the identity of Jesus as **ho Logos** in ways that necessarily exclude rival christologies. The label sectarian is not pejorative in his usage. It names the way **canon** fortifies the church against assimilation by setting confessional lines, not least in the claim that the **Word** who became flesh is the only way to the Father. Here Gundry addresses contemporary **evangelical elites**, urging a recovery of doctrinal specification and a wariness of ecumenical vagueness.

This Johannine strand complements his earlier insistence on **midrashic truth** by relocating the debate from mere historicity to **confessional verity**. For Gundry, the Gospel imposes allegiance, and the church’s task is to herald that allegiance with clarity. Yet the same stress prompts questions about how **sectarian identity** interfaces with catholicity, especially in contexts where the church aspires to articulate **mere Christianity** while honoring the precision of **creedal orthodoxy**.

#### 6. Later syntheses and pedagogical footprint
Gundry’s **The Old Is Better** 2005 gathers essays that defend traditional exegesis against fashionable novelty, pressing for readings that privilege **canonical coherence**. **The Jesus of the Gospels** 2009 and the **Commentary on the New Testament** 2010 display his mature voice, a blend of philological detail, historical awareness, and theological punch. Through successive editions of **A Survey of the New Testament**, ending with a fifth edition in 2012, he educated evangelical students across decades, embedding a habit of careful reading of **Gospels**, **Pauline letters**, and **Apocalypse**. These pedagogical works train readers to weigh **Semitic backgrounds**, textual features, and the freight of **Old Testament intertextuality**.

Across this corpus, the motif of **authorial artistry** remains central. Gundry urges readers to savor narrative devices, irony, inclusios, and typological fabric. His synthesis resists a thin historicism just as it resists corrosive skepticism. The aim is a chastened **critical realism** in which the **Holy Spirit’s** inspiration does not flatten literature into transcript but breathes theology into history with literary elegance.

#### 7. Doctrinal profile through exegetical commitments
Gundry’s doctrinal accents can be mapped through his exegesis. In **Christology**, he affirms **incarnation** and a robust **resurrection realism**, especially against existential reductions of **soma** that would evacuate the body. In **soteriology**, his Markan and Matthean emphases center the **cross** and **discipleship**, opposing any ethic that would mute suffering or endorse a prosperity scheme. In **ecclesiology**, the motif of a **mixed church under persecution** underwrites pastoral admonition and speaks to the church’s pilgrim status. In **eschatology**, his **posttribulationism** keeps the church within the purview of tribulation, correlating with an ethic of endurance and hope.

His **bibliology** is the most debated. Gundry advances **inerrancy by genre**, insisting that **truth-telling** occurs according to **literary form**. Consequently, passages like **Matthew 27** that feature the resurrection of the saints, or the **guard at the tomb**, may be read as midrashic intensifications that proclaim the victory of **Messiah** without requiring a one-to-one historical referent for every narrative detail. He extends the same point to **Petrine** narratives that function parabolically to teach the church. The intention is to defend **Scripture’s authority** while honoring **authorial intent** and **Jewish narrative conventions**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, debate, and influence
Gundry’s proposals drew fire and fostered alliances. **Norman Geisler** and other defenders of maximal **historical inerrancy** judged the midrashic thesis incompatible with the ETS affirmation, leading to the 1983 rupture. **D. A. Carson** and **Craig Blomberg** engaged more sympathetically with **redaction criticism** while questioning the extent of proposed **non-historicity**. Against older German criticism, **Rudolf Bultmann** particularly, Gundry’s **Soma** volume pushed back on anti-bodily tendencies, finding common cause with scholars like **James D. G. Dunn** at points yet disagreeing on the semantics of **soma**. Through these debates, Gundry helped normalize careful use of **synoptic theory**, **Second Temple backgrounds**, and **genre analysis** within evangelical guilds.

The lasting effect is twofold. He emboldened evangelicals to practice **critical exegesis** without embarrassment, and he sharpened the terms of **bibliological** discourse by shifting attention to **genre** and **authorial intention**. His students and readers, influenced through **Westmont** and his textbooks, have populated classrooms and pulpits, bringing with them a habit of careful reading of **Gospel artistry** and a willingness to debate the boundaries of **inerrancy**. The controversies have not ended, but they have deepened the conversation about how **Scripture** means what it says.

#### 9. Integrated assessment and theological coherence
Taken as a whole, Gundry’s work exhibits a fierce devotion to **Jesus Christ**, a careful tracking of **Old Testament fulfillment**, and a principled account of **Scripture’s truthfulness**. The strength of his approach lies in refusing to reduce **Gospels** to thin reportage, insisting instead on **literary theology** grounded in the canon of **Israel’s Scriptures**. His reading of **Mark** as an apology for the cross intensifies a biblical **theologia crucis**, and his defense of **bodily resurrection** strengthens confessional orthodoxy against demythologizing currents. His critique of **pretribulationism** restores gravity to **eschatological discipleship**. These virtues commend his method as a serious evangelical bid to unite academic rigor with churchly confession.

Yet the same method introduces tensions. The claim that Matthew’s artistry entails instances of **intentional non-historicity** presses hard on the link between **redemptive history** and narrated events. The portrayal of **Peter** as an apostate on whom the church should not model itself disturbs traditional accounts of **apostolic authority**. And the deployment of **Johannine sectarianism** can risk narrowing catholic sympathy. Gundry aims to hold together **literary form** and **divine inspiration**, but debate remains over whether he concedes too much to **midrashic freedom**, given the canonical commitment to the deeds of **God in history**.

In a concise Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation, one can affirm Gundry’s stress on **authorial intent**, **typology**, and **redactional theology**, as well as his vigorous defense of **bodily resurrection** and a cross-shaped **discipleship**. Classical Reformed orthodoxy, however, ties **inerrancy** to the unity of **revelation and history** in the covenantal economy, so that **Gospel truth** is irreducibly bound to what God did in space and time. A Reformed alternative would deploy **figural realism** and the **analogia fidei** to account for Matthew’s artistry without positing non-historical invention, treating phenomena like **Matthew 27** as apocalyptic description that is symbolically thick yet historically anchored, and reading **Peter** through the unity of **apostolic foundation** and penitential restoration. On that path, genre is honored, **Scripture’s authority** is upheld, and the **economy of salvation** remains historically concrete.
