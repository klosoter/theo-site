### TITLE
Union with Christ as the grammar of grace: a confessional, pastoral retrieval for evangelical holiness and assurance.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Scottish Presbyterianism**, late twentieth-century **evangelical Reformed renewal**, and the retrieval of **Puritan divinity** within the institutions of **Ligonier Ministries** and confessional Presbyterian churches.
- A methodological blend of **confessional dogmatics** under the **Westminster Standards** with **redemptive-historical exegesis** and pastoral application, privileging **analogia fidei** and the **ordinary means of grace**.
- Key works: **John Owen on the Christian Life** 1987; **Children of the Living God** 1989; **The Holy Spirit** 1996; **From the Mouth of God** 2014; **The Whole Christ** 2016; **Devoted to God** 2016; **Some Pastors and Teachers** 2017; **Maturity** 2019.
- Central categories: **union with Christ**, **adoption**, **duplex gratia** justification and sanctification, **definitive sanctification**, **law and gospel** in the **Marrow controversy**, **free offer**, **Word and sacrament**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **John Calvin**, **John Owen**, **Thomas Boston** and the **Marrow tradition**, **Geerhardus Vos** and **John Murray**, alongside conversations with **Pentecostal-charismatic** and **neonomian** trajectories.
- Reformed reception: warmly received in **confessional Presbyterian** circles, seen as a faithful steward of **Westminster orthodoxy**, with debates around **law–gospel calibration** and **cessationism**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Born in 1948, **Sinclair B. Ferguson** emerged from the matrix of **Scottish Presbyterianism**, educated at the University of Aberdeen, and ordained to a ministry that would span pulpit and lectern. His vocational pattern is ecclesial first and academic second, with long pastorates in Glasgow at **St George’s Tron** and later at **First Presbyterian Church, Columbia, South Carolina**, complemented by teaching in the 1980s and 1990s at **Westminster Theological Seminary** and other Reformed institutions. This dual location informs his method: a confessional and pastoral **dogmatics** ordered by the **Westminster Confession and Catechisms**, fueled by **redemptive-historical exegesis** and aimed at the holiness and assurance of ordinary believers. When Ferguson co-edited the **New Dictionary of Theology** in 1988 with **J. I. Packer** and **David F. Wright**, he modeled a catholic Reformed retrieval that is as concerned with doctrinal integrity as with ecclesial catechesis. His theological center is **union with Christ** as the grammar that holds together **duplex gratia**, the **ordo salutis** with the *historia salutis*, and the **ordinary means of grace**. Within a late modern evangelical climate fractured by **moralism**, **therapeutic religion**, and **antinomian reaction**, his constructive burden is to re-present classic **Reformed soteriology** as deeply **Trinitarian**, churchly, and transformative.

Ferguson’s epistemic stance is resolutely **Scripture-principled** and **confessional**, favoring **analogia fidei** over speculative correlational methods. In **From the Mouth of God** 2014, he articulates the authority, clarity, and sufficiency of Scripture, pressing a *Christus totus* reading calibrated by **apostolic exegesis** and the **canonical context**. His hermeneutic resists both historicist flattening and allegorical excess, disciplining Christocentric preaching by the texts literary structure and the economy of the **covenants**. A Vos-Murray line is evident: the center of Scripture is the **kingdom of God** realized in the **historia salutis**, and the believer’s participation flows by the **Spirit** through **unio mystica** into sanctified obedience. This yields a practical dogmatics that prioritizes the **indicative–imperative** order and the **ordinary means of grace** Word, sacrament, and prayer as Spirit-wrought conduits of **communion with the Triune God**.

#### 2. Pneumatology and the eschatological Spirit
In **The Holy Spirit** 1996, Ferguson offers a full-scale **pneumatology** in the Contours of Christian Theology series. He advances a **Spirit-Christology** without collapsing the persons, arguing that the incarnate **Son** lives in the fullness of the **Spirit** so that Pentecost, as the eschatological gift, mediates the exalted **Christ** to the church. At the level of *historia salutis*, Pentecost is not a repeatable addendum but the once-for-all inauguration of the **new covenant** age, while at the level of **ordo salutis** the **Spirit** effects **regeneration**, **adoption**, **sanctification**, and **assurance** in union with Christ. Ferguson engages **Keswick perfectionism** and **second-blessing** schemes, arguing that they misread **Acts** as normative sequence rather than redemptive-historical transition. He articulates a careful **cessationism**, not as denial of the Spirit’s sovereignty, but as an affirmation of the sufficiency of **Scripture** and the foundational nature of apostolic **sign-gifts**. This theological line secures the primacy of the **Word and sacrament** as the **means of grace**, embeds experiential **assurance** in Christ’s work rather than in episodic ecstasies, and locates sanctification in the **definitive** and **progressive** lifework of the **Spirit** within the church.

Pastorally, this pneumatology renders the Christian life ecclesial and covenantal rather than voluntarist or self-styled. Ferguson’s exposition of **Romans 8** grounds **assurance** in the Spirits *Abba*-cry and in the believers **adoption**, themes he had already distilled in **Children of the Living God** 1989. The Spirits ministry centers on conforming Christians to the image of the **Son** by cultivating the habits of **mortification** and **vivification** through the **means of grace**. By integrating **Trinitarian theology** with the **ordo salutis**, Ferguson pushes back against depersonalized accounts of spirituality while retrieving the Puritan emphasis on **communion with God** framed by **divine simplicity** and the undivided operations of the **Trinity** *ad extra*.

#### 3. Union with Christ, adoption, and the shape of salvation
From his early **John Owen on the Christian Life** 1987 to the essay collection **In Christ Alone** 2007, Ferguson’s soteriology revolves around **unio cum Christo**. He appropriates **Calvin’s** **duplex gratia** justification and sanctification as twin fruits of union, refusing both their confusion and their separation. In Ferguson’s synthesis, **justification** is forensic and alien, grounded in the imputed righteousness of **Christ** and received by faith alone, while **sanctification** is inherent and transformative, yet inseparably tethered to the same union. He frames Christian identity through **adoption**, arguing that the filial status of believers is not a peripheral benefit but a controlling center for **assurance** and **obedience**. This aligns with the Puritan tradition in **Owen** and **Boston**, but is recast for contemporary discipleship where identity politics and performance anxieties mingle to undermine **assurance**.

This union-centered soteriology appears in his exposition of the **indicative–imperative** structure of Pauline ethics. The imperatives of holiness depend on the reality that believers have died and risen with **Christ**, an emphasis echoed in the concept of **definitive sanctification** popularized by **John Murray** and warmly embraced and extended by Ferguson. Theologically, definitive sanctification marks a once-for-all breach with the dominion of sin, while progressive sanctification is Spirit-enabled conformity over time. By distinguishing and uniting these terms, Ferguson addresses common pastoral distortions: **perfectionism** that denies ongoing sin, and **defeatism** that denies the power of the **resurrection** life. The practical upshot is a spirituality of gratitude and reverent fear, rather than servile fear or self-sufficiency.

#### 4. The Whole Christ: Law, gospel, and assurance
Ferguson’s most influential constructive work, **The Whole Christ** 2016, retrieves the **Marrow controversy** of early eighteenth-century Scotland to diagnose perennial evangelical maladies: **legalism** and **antinomianism**. Through the lens of **Thomas Boston** and the contested book **The Marrow of Modern Divinity**, he insists that legalism and antinomianism are not opposites but mirror images of the same Christ-deprived piety. Both mislocate the place of the **law** and the **gospel** by detaching the person of **Christ** from the offer and demands of salvation. The cure is the **free offer of the gospel** Christ himself offered to sinners without prior conditions in the **covenant of grace** and received by faith that unites to a **whole Christ** in whom **justification** and **sanctification** are distinct yet inseparable benefits.

A key doctrinal move is Ferguson’s reassertion of **assurance** as an ordinary privilege of faith, rooted in **Christ’s sufficiency**, the **promises of God**, the internal **witness of the Spirit**, and the evidences of **grace** in sanctification. Against **neonomian** schemes associated historically with **Richard Baxter** and some later Scottish critics, he resists any reframing of the gospel as a softer **law** that turns faith and repentance into meritorious conditions. Yet Ferguson also overturns **antinomian** claims by showing how **union with Christ** necessarily yields new obedience, so that the **law** as **lex Christi** becomes a friend in the hands of the **Mediator**. The Marrow lens allows him to unify **free grace**, **assurance**, and **holiness** without eroding **sola fide**. The argument is not a museum tour but a pastoral program for preaching, counseling, and sacramental practice.

#### 5. Sanctification, holiness, and spiritual formation
In **Devoted to God** 2016 and **Maturity** 2019, Ferguson develops a theology of sanctification that is explicitly **Trinitarian** and **ecclesial**, charting a catechetical pathway into holiness. The doctrinal architecture remains **union with Christ** and **adoption**, but he expands on the classic Puritan dynamics of **mortification** and **vivification**, structured by **Romans 6–8** and **Colossians 3**. Definitive sanctification is an accomplished act by which the believer is set apart in Christ, while progressive sanctification is an ongoing **Spirit**-energized life of putting sin to death and cultivating the **virtues** of the new man. He retrieves **Thomas Chalmers’s** motif of the **expulsive power of a new affection**, showing how the beauty of the **Son** displaces rival loves.

Ferguson is explicit about the **means of grace** as the Spirit’s appointed instruments for this growth: **preaching**, **sacrament**, **prayer**, and the **communion of saints**. He resists modern programs of spiritual technique by returning to the **ordinary** yet potent rhythms of Lord’s Day worship and catechesis. He also emphasizes the **indicative–imperative** order to secure the dignity of obedience as that of adopted children, not hired servants. Ethical exhortation arises from the **new identity** granted in Christ, reorienting the motivations of the heart. The pastoral result is a robust yet non-anxious piety: neither presuming on grace nor attempting to earn divine favor.

#### 6. Ministry, ecclesiology, and pastoral theology
In the large collection **Some Pastors and Teachers** 2017, Ferguson gathers decades of reflection on the **pastoral office**, **preaching**, and **church life**, drawing deeply from **Calvin**, **Owen**, and **the Westminster Directory**. He defends the **ordinary means of grace** pattern and warns against managerial or therapeutic reductions of ministry. The **minister** is a servant of the **Word**, charged to preach **Christ** from all Scripture with **redemptive-historical** awareness and doctrinal clarity. The gathered church is the locus of **discipleship**, and the **sacraments** seal the promises of the **covenant of grace**. He urges pastoral practice ordered by **catechesis**, **discipline**, and **prayer**, shaping a people who live under the **lordship of Christ**.

Ferguson’s ecclesiology maintains a strong connection between **confession** and **communion**. The **Westminster Standards** are not straitjackets but guardrails that secure catholic fidelity in doctrine and worship. His expository works, such as **Let’s Study Ephesians** 1994 and sermons on the **Sermon on the Mount**, display how **union with Christ** structures the identity and mission of the **church** as the **body of Christ**. He resists parachurch displacement of the congregation by relocating the fulcrum of spiritual formation to the Lord’s Day assembly. The tone is more retrieval than innovation, yet the retrieval is capacious, integrating **Puritan** pastoral wisdom with late modern pastoral challenges.

#### 7. Scripture, doctrine, and the task of theology
Ferguson’s **From the Mouth of God** 2014 distills his doctrine of **Scripture**: inspiration, inerrancy, authority, clarity, and sufficiency within the **sola Scriptura** principle. He urges a hermeneutic that is **canonical** and **Christological** without dissolving textual integrity. The **analogia fidei** mediates the interplay of **doctrinal synthesis** and exegesis, with the **Westminster Confession** serving as a doctrinal map rather than a master. He affirms the **regulative principle of worship** in spirit if not in detailed polemic, by locating transformation within **Word and sacrament** rather than in spectacle or technique. The theologian’s task is ecclesial service, not academic game; doctrine exists for **doxology** and **discipleship**.

Though not a specialist in **natural theology** or the metaphysics of **divine simplicity**, Ferguson’s Trinitarian piety assumes the classical attributes and undivided operations of God. His doctrine of **assurance** leans on the threefold ground taught in the **Westminster Confession**: the **promises**, the **Spirit’s testimony**, and the **evidences of grace**. His language of *simul iustus et peccator* recognizes the ongoing conflict of the Christian life without surrendering the cheerful boldness of **adoption**. In an evangelical landscape tempted by either **doctrinal minimalism** or speculative maximalism, Ferguson occupies a middle path of **confessional catholicity**, tethering exegesis to worship and practice.

#### 8. Polemics and pastoral apologetic
Ferguson’s polemical edge remains pastoral. He contends with **antinomianism**, **legalism**, **neonomianism**, and **second-blessing** sanctification, but he refuses caricature. His treatments of **Pentecostal-charismatic** claims in **The Holy Spirit** offer appreciative exegesis while defending the finality of **apostolic foundation** and the sufficiency of **Scripture**. He resists reductionist **law–gospel** antitheses that mute the **imperatives** of the New Testament, yet he is equally alert to moralistic homiletics that subvert **sola gratia**. His alignment with **Geerhardus Vos** and **John Murray** keeps biblical theology and systematic theology in symbiosis rather than rivalry.

In his editor and teacher roles, Ferguson has contributed to shaping a generation of ministers inclined toward **confessional Presbyterianism**, **Banner of Truth** spirituality, and **Ligonier** catechesis. The goal is not to produce speculative theologians but **shepherd-theologians** whose preaching of **Christ** animates holy living. Where he enters contemporary debates on **justification** and the **New Perspective on Paul**, he reaffirms **imputation** and the covenantal structure of **Reformed orthodoxy** without mounting novel architectures. His influence, then, is integrative and stabilizing, re-centering evangelical energies on **union with Christ**, **adoption**, and the lived coherence of **doctrine and discipleship**.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Ferguson’s theology displays a remarkable consonance with the **Westminster Standards**, the **Vos–Murray** tradition, and the pastoral ethos of **Owen** and **Boston**. Strengths are clear: a richly **Trinitarian** soteriology anchored in **union with Christ**; a compelling rearticulation of **assurance** within the **Marrow** framework; a robust **pneumatology** that secures the **ordinary means of grace**; and a method that binds **systematic theology** to **exegesis** and **ecclesial practice**. Possible tensions appear at points of calibration. First, in stressing the pastoral friendship of the **law in Christ**, some might urge clearer dogmatic accent on the prelapsarian **covenant of works** so that the **law–gospel** antithesis in the order of justification remains sharply stated even as **lex Christi** guides sanctification. Second, his **cessationism**, though carefully framed, can seem under-engaged with the global **Pentecostal** challenge and the doctrine of **illumination** across cultures. Third, because his project is pastoral rather than metaphysical, the articulation of **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** appears assumed more than elaborated, which invites complementary work from classical **Reformed scholasticism**. None of these tensions undercut his central achievement. Within **classical Reformed orthodoxy**, Ferguson stands as a principal contemporary voice whose retrieval of **union with Christ**, **duplex gratia**, and the **ordinary means of grace** has recalibrated evangelical piety toward **assurance** and **holiness** in the church, a model well suited to the confessional aims of **Westminster Theological Seminary** while inviting continued sharpening in covenantal and theological prolegomena.
