### TITLE
Evangelical **Arminianism** as generous orthodoxy — a postconservative retrieval of the **Great Tradition** against **divine determinism**

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth to early twenty-first century **evangelicalism** under pressure from **fundamentalism**, **postliberalism**, and the **Young, Restless, Reformed** resurgence frames Olson’s project of pietist, Baptist, free-church **theology**.
- Method: **postconservative evangelicalism** marked by fallibilism, communal hermeneutics, and appeal to the **Great Tradition** under *sola Scriptura* rather than **foundationalism** or **biblicism**.
- Key works: **20th-Century Theology** 1992, **The Story of Christian Theology** 1999, **The Mosaic of Christian Belief** 2002 and 2016, **Arminian Theology: Myths and Realities** 2006, **Reformed and Always Reforming** 2007, **Against Calvinism** 2011, **The Journey of Modern Theology** 2013, **Reclaiming Pietism** 2015, **Essentials of Christian Thought** 2017.
- Central categories: **prevenient grace**, **libertarian freedom**, **simple foreknowledge**, **relational theism**, **consensual orthodoxy**, **unlimited atonement**, **resistible grace**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Jacob Arminius**, **John Wesley**, **Pietism**, **Stanley J. Grenz**, **Clark Pinnock**, **Wolfhart Pannenberg**, **Karl Barth**, and critics such as **Michael Horton** and **John Piper**.
- Reformed cue: affirmations of Nicene **Trinitarianism** and evangelical **authority of Scripture** yet strong critique of **unconditional election**, **meticulous providence**, **divine impassibility**, and rigid **inerrancy**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and the evangelical field
Born in 1952 and formed within free-church and **Pietist** streams of American **evangelicalism**, Roger E. Olson came of ecclesial age as the late twentieth century negotiated the fallout of the **fundamentalist-modernist** conflict and the consolidation of a new evangelical center. Educated in historical and modern **theology** and earning a doctorate in 1984, his early teaching at Bethel in Minnesota and later at Baylor’s **Truett Theological Seminary** placed him at key institutions of postfundamentalist evangelical identity. His collaborative work with **Stanley J. Grenz** on twentieth-century theology situated him within constructive evangelical engagement with **Barth**, **Pannenberg**, and **Yale postliberalism**, resisting both sterile **propositionalism** and dissolving relativism. Biographically and ecclesially, Olson’s vocation has been to articulate a warm-hearted **orthodoxy** — a Pietist Baptist retrieval of the **Great Tradition** — against hard forms of **divine determinism** and culture-war **biblicism**.

Olson narrates evangelical theology as a reformist movement that must remain accountable to **Nicene-Chalcedonian** consensus while refusing captivity to any one late-modern method. He appeals to **Vincent of Lerins** and the pattern of consensual catholicity as a way of naming the boundaries of legitimate evangelical diversity. The animating context is a field strained by the rise of the **Young, Restless, Reformed** constellation, by the consolidation of **Chicago-style inerrancy**, and by the marginalization of **Arminian** and **Pietist** voices. Olson’s distinctive contribution is to argue that evangelical identity need not entail **Calvinist** soteriology or confessional **determinism**, and that doctrinal seriousness can be joined to affective piety and ecclesial charity.

#### 2. Method — postconservative evangelicalism, Scripture, and tradition
Olson’s methodological centerpiece is the proposal for **postconservative evangelicalism** in Reformed and Always Reforming 2007, a constructive stance that takes the **Bible** as the supreme norm while rejecting **foundationalism** and one-to-one **inerrantist** propositionalism as the essence of evangelical orthodoxy. He distinguishes *sola Scriptura* from **nuda Scriptura**, insisting that Scripture is the norma normans non normata yet read within the **Great Tradition** and in conversation with the lived experience of the Spirit-indwelt community. This yields a principled fallibilism — all postcanonical doctrine is revisable in light of Scripture and catholic consensus — and a preference for the **analogia fidei** of consensual orthodoxy rather than the **analogia entis** of natural theology.

In The Mosaic of Christian Belief 2002 and 2016, Olson codifies this method through the metaphor of a mosaic, a patterned unity-in-diversity that centers the **Trinity**, **incarnation**, **atonement**, and **salvation by grace**, while permitting legitimate options on secondary matters. He pushes back against reductionist **biblicism** that confuses confession with a concordance and against top-down **magisterialism** that freezes a single post-Reformation confessional platform into evangelical dogma. This method is indebted to **Pietism** and to the narrative sensibilities of **postliberal** theology, yet it seeks thick doctrinal anchors in **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** grammar.

#### 3. Mapping the corpus — history, taxonomy, and retrieval
Olson’s scholarly arc moves between historical narration and constructive taxonomy. With **Stanley J. Grenz**, 20th-Century Theology 1992 maps modern theology around God and world, placing **Barth** and **Pannenberg** as pivots for renewal beyond **liberal Protestantism**. The Story of Christian Theology 1999 retells doctrinal development as a global drama of reception and debate, centering **ecumenical councils** and the dynamic of heresy and orthodoxy. The Westminster Handbook to Evangelical Theology 2004 offers concise entries that reflect Olson’s generous but bounded evangelical vision.

Constructively, Arminian Theology: Myths and Realities 2006 clarifies **Arminianism** as evangelical synergism anchored in **grace** rather than as **Pelagian** or **semi-Pelagian** deviation. Against Calvinism 2011, paired with **Michael Horton**’s For Calvinism, becomes Olson’s most public polemic against what he calls **divine determinism** and **meticulous providence**. The Journey of Modern Theology 2013 and Essentials of Christian Thought 2017 frame a biblical-theological metaphysic in which the **triune God** of love grounds a personalist ontology over against impersonalist or pantheist options, while Reclaiming Pietism 2015 recovers **Pietist ethos** — warm-hearted orthodoxy, conversion, and activism — as a permanent evangelical good.

#### 4. The Arminian soteriological proposal — grace, freedom, and love
Olson’s account of **salvation** is the center of his theological identity. He affirms **total depravity** as radical inability, but insists that **prevenient grace** universally heals the will sufficiently for genuine response to the **gospel**. Election is **conditional** in its final referent to persons, though Olson often highlights the **corporate election** of the church in Christ as the locus of God’s gracious choosing. The **atonement** is **unlimited**, offered for all and efficacious for those who believe, with no restriction by a decretal **limited atonement**. **Grace** is **resistible**, and **perseverance** is conditional participation in Christ, rather than an indefectible decree.

This Arminianism is framed, polemically and positively, by the truth that the **character of God is love**. Olson argues that any soteriology that implicates God as the author of sin or that damns unconditionally violates the revealed **agape** of God in Christ. He rejects **Pelagianism** and **semi-Pelagianism** explicitly, locating Arminian synergism within the Augustinian stream in which grace precedes, accompanies, and enables every act of faith. By clarifying that **libertarian freedom** need not be autonomy from grace but freedom healed and moved by **prevenient grace**, Olson positions Arminianism as fully evangelical and fully dependent on God.

#### 5. Providence, foreknowledge, and theodicy — against meticulous determinism
Olson’s doctrine of **providence** rejects **meticulous providence** and **compatibilist** freedom as incompatible with moral responsibility and the goodness of God. He defends a model of divine governance that includes specific interventions and a real divine permission of evil, while affirming God’s ultimate victory through **providence** that works all things toward redemption without predetermining every creaturely act. On **foreknowledge**, Olson is not an **open theist**. He defends **simple foreknowledge** — God knows the future exhaustively as a settled reality — while rejecting **Molinism** as speculative and unnecessary and resisting the determinist entailments he sees in **decretal** Calvinism.

The theodicy corollary is central. In Against Calvinism 2011, Olson argues that meticulous **divine determinism** makes God morally indistinguishable from evil by collapsing permission into ordination. The Arminian free will defense becomes, for him, a confessional safeguard of God’s **holiness** and **goodness**. He grants mystery in the concurrence of divine and human action, but he denies that mystery can be invoked to reconcile unconditional **reprobation** with divine love. The burden of his polemic is that the biblical God of **agape** disclosed in **Jesus Christ** cannot be the ultimate determiner of sin and unbelief.

#### 6. Scripture, authority, and the Great Tradition
Olson’s view of **Scripture** is high yet not strictly **Chicago-inerrantist**. He prefers to speak of **infallibility** in all that Scripture intends to teach pertaining to faith and practice, and he resists enshrining a philosophical theory of errorlessness as a boundary marker of evangelical identity. He frames biblical interpretation within the **Great Tradition**, which functions as a hermeneutical guardrail that keeps evangelical theology catholic without becoming a second magisterium. The rule of faith — the **Trinitarian** and **Christological** dogmas of **Nicaea** and **Chalcedon** — shapes the *analogia fidei* by which Scripture interprets Scripture.

This approach invests heavily in consensual dogma as canon within the canon of theology, while granting space for intramural variance on secondary matters. Olson’s allergy is to a thin **biblicism** that pretends to read the Bible with no creed, and to a thick **confessionalism** that elevates one post-Reformation system to untouchable norm. His mediating stance draws on **Pietist** humility and **postliberal** community-sense while maintaining evangelical **conversionism** and **crucicentrism**.

#### 7. Evangelical identity, Pietism, and public polemics
A hallmark of Olson’s voice is his retrieval of **Pietism** as evangelical habitus. In Reclaiming Pietism 2015 he commends **warm-hearted orthodoxy** — rigorous in dogma yet centered on new birth, discipleship, and mission — as medicine for culture-war evangelicalism. His blog and public writings intervened repeatedly in controversies over **inerrancy**, **atonement models**, **open theism**, and the **Young, Restless, Reformed** movement. He defends the evangelical bona fides of **Clark Pinnock** and other non-Calvinist voices, even when demurring from **open theism** itself, and warns against boundary-setting by **TULIP** rather than by **Nicene** faith and the gospel.

Against Calvinism 2011 engaged **Michael Horton**, **John Piper**, and likeminded Reformed advocates, not as enemies but as siblings whose soteriology needs reform for the sake of the **gospel**’s moral coherence. Olson’s polemics are anchored in a pedagogy of myths and realities — especially in Arminian Theology 2006 — that aims to de-weaponize labels like **Pelagian** and **semi-Pelagian**. The goal is a chastened, historically informed, and generous evangelical **orthodoxy**.

#### 8. Atonement, doctrine of God, and constructive trajectories
Olson’s atonement theology commends a **mosaic** of motifs rather than a single exhaustive theory. He affirms **penal substitution** as biblically warranted yet resists making it the hermeneutical totality, advocating integration with **Christus Victor**, **moral influence**, and **recapitulation** themes. This integrative instinct follows from his mosaic method and his insistence that the **New Testament** witnesses present a surplus of atonement metaphors. In eschatology and judgment he maintains traditional warnings about **hell**, while granting that **annihilationism** may be evangelically legitimate, though he refrains from endorsing universalism.

On the doctrine of God, Olson promotes a **relational theism** that prizes divine **love** and **faithfulness** while expressing skepticism about strong **impassibility** and certain uses of **divine simplicity** that seem to evacuate reciprocity between God and creatures. He upholds classical **Trinitarianism** and divine **aseity**, yet favors biblical depictions of God’s suffering love in Christ as revelatory of God’s eternal disposition toward the world. Essentials of Christian Thought 2017 amplifies a biblically shaped metaphysic — reality is personal because ultimate reality is the triune personal God — over against impersonal **naturalism** and pantheistic collapse.

#### 9. Historiography and evangelical catholicity
As a historian-theologian, Olson’s impulse is ecumenical retrieval. The Story of Christian Theology 1999 and The Journey of Modern Theology 2013 model an evangelical **ressourcement** that believes in providential guidance of doctrine through time, even as it calls for ongoing **reformation** according to the Word. He treats **heresy** and **orthodoxy** as historically discerned realities, not mere power plays, and he urges evangelical students to own the **creeds** as their home. The Mosaic of Christian Belief 2002 thereby functions as a catechetical map for evangelical **catholicity**, where unity is secured by the center — **Trinity**, **incarnation**, **grace** — and diversity is permitted at the periphery.

Olson’s pedagogical style is to unmask caricatures, display conceptual options, and argue for an Arminian reading of **salvation** that preserves divine **goodness** and human accountability. The through-line is a theocentric and Christocentric commitment to **God’s love** as the hermeneutical light, which for Olson is not a reduction but the biblical summum of divine self-revelation. His project is thus simultaneously critical of hard **Calvinism** and corrective of thin **liberalism**, aiming at a generous but bounded evangelical **orthodoxy**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation — points of tension and possible corrections
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster Theological Seminary angle, Olson’s strengths include his steady appeal to **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** dogma, his resistance to **nuda Scriptura**, his reforming critique of culture-war **biblicism**, and his pastoral concern that the doctrine of **God** secure divine **goodness** and the moral seriousness of **sin**. Yet significant tensions remain. First, his methodological **postconservatism** risks a canon within the canon by elevating the divine **love** as hermeneutical axiom in ways that can mute texts that reveal the terror as well as the tenderness of the Holy One and that, within **covenant** history, disclose both mercy and fearful **judgment**. Second, his rejection of **meticulous providence** and **compatibilism** strains against the Reformed grammar of exhaustive **sovereignty** and concursus, in which God is first cause without being the author of sin and in which **effectual calling** safeguards the gratuity of grace. Third, his preference for **infallibility** over **inerrancy**, coupled with a consensual **Great Tradition** hermeneutic, may be read as softening the self-attesting, self-interpreting authority of **Scripture** as the principium cognoscendi of theology. Fourth, his hesitation about **impassibility** and strong **simplicity** may, in Reformed eyes, imperil the transcendence that underwrites the reliability of divine **promises**. Within confessional Reformed orthodoxy, alternative construals would retain catholic **love** while grounding it in God’s simple and immutable **aseity**, would explicate **reprobation** within a covenantal frame that preserves divine justice and creaturely blameworthiness, and would uphold monergistic **grace** as the heart of the gospel to which the regenerate freely and gladly respond.

- Setting: late modern **evangelicalism** negotiating **fundamentalism**, **postliberalism**, and the **Young, Restless, Reformed** moment.
- Core focus: defense of evangelical **Arminianism** and **Pietist** warm-hearted **orthodoxy** against **divine determinism**.
- Major works: **20th-Century Theology** 1992, **Story of Christian Theology** 1999, **Mosaic of Christian Belief** 2002 and 2016, **Arminian Theology** 2006, **Reformed and Always Reforming** 2007, **Against Calvinism** 2011, **Journey of Modern Theology** 2013, **Reclaiming Pietism** 2015, **Essentials of Christian Thought** 2017.
- Distinctives: **prevenient grace**, **libertarian freedom**, **simple foreknowledge**, **unlimited atonement**, **resistible grace**, **relational theism**, **consensual orthodoxy** within the **Great Tradition**.
- Interlocutors: engagements with **Grenz**, **Pinnock**, **Barth**, **Pannenberg**, and critics like **Michael Horton** and **John Piper** over **providence** and **election**.
- Reformed critique: discomfort with rejection of **unconditional election**, **compatibilism**, strong **inerrancy**, **impassibility**, and robust **simplicity**, while valuing Nicene **Trinitarianism** and anti-**biblicism**.
- Legacy: a leading contemporary voice for evangelical **Arminianism**, shaping debates on **grace** and **providence**, and modeling a generous **evangelical catholicity** grounded in the **Great Tradition**.
