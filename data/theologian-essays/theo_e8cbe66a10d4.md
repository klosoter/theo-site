### TITLE
**Private platforms as the new governors of speech**: securing rule-of-law legitimacy for **content moderation** in the **digital public square**.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Contemporary milieu of **platform capitalism**, **First Amendment exceptionalism**, and post-2016 crises; draws on **administrative law** and **socio-legal** inquiry to parse **platform governance**.
- Empirical-legal method joining **doctrinal analysis** and **interviews**; normative emphasis on **procedural legitimacy** and **due process for content moderation**.
- **The New Governors: The People, Rules, and Processes Governing Online Speech** (2018); longform reporting on the **Facebook Oversight Board** in the **New Yorker** (2021).
- Key categories: **private administrative law**, **platform rulemaking and adjudication**, **trust and safety**, **transparency and accountability**, **digital public square**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Jack Balkin**, **Julie Cohen**, **Tarleton Gillespie**, **Daphne Keller**, **Evelyn Douek**, **Danielle Citron**, **Jürgen Habermas**.
- Reformed cue: weigh **proceduralism** against **lex naturae** and **two-kingdoms** prudence; analogize platform **discipline** to **Calvins third mark of the church** with **ministerial not magisterial authority**.

### ESSAY
The contemporary legal scholar **Kate Klonick** has become a leading voice in analyzing how **private platforms** govern **online speech** through rules, enforcement, and appeals that resemble **administrative law**. Her enduring thesis is that platforms like **Facebook** and **YouTube** function as **new governors** whose **content moderation** exercises real power over the public sphere, and thus must be legitimated by **rule-of-law** norms such as notice, transparency, and reasoned explanation. Though not a theologian, her account bears directly on public theology: if the digital square is mediated by **private governance**, then any account of **speech, authority, and community** must grapple with these institutions and their normativity. Klonick’s work supplies an anatomy of power and process that invites theological judgments about **auctoritas**, **lex**, and the **moral ends** of communication under *coram Deo*.

#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Situated in the post-2010 ascent of **platform capitalism** and the post-2016 reckoning over **disinformation**, **harassment**, and **democratic legitimacy**, Klonick trained as a **U.S. law scholar** attentive to how platforms organize speech at scale. Her formation bears marks of the **law-and-technology** community shaped by **Jack Balkin** on the **information fiduciary** and **Julie Cohen** on **networked subjectivity**, alongside **Tarleton Gillespie** on **custodians of the internet**. She joined these programmatic accounts with the practical world of **trust-and-safety** teams, bringing **empirical-legal** interviews to bear on conceptual frameworks. The methodological wager is that understanding **governance-in-practice** is prerequisite to prescribing **normative legitimacy**. This is a jurisprudence of institutions: interrogate rule texts, enforcement discretion, and appeals, then ask whether **due process** principles can migrate into the **private law** of platforms.

Klonick’s epistemic stance is both **positivist** in mapping the rules and **constructivist** about legitimacy. She tracks how **platform rules** emerge from internal deliberations, external pressures, and crisis response. She is skeptical of court-centric **First Amendment** romance and instead embraces **administrative law** analogies: speech governance at scale requires **rulemaking**, **adjudication**, **reason-giving**, and **review**. Her interviews with policy staff and content moderators function as a kind of *thick description* of **institutional rationality**. The implicit anthropology is that of **liberal pluralism**: no comprehensive doctrine governs the internet; legitimacy is built procedurally by ensuring **predictability**, **participation**, and **accountability** within and across **platform governance** regimes.

#### 2. The New Governors thesis and its architecture
Klonick’s signature contribution, **The New Governors: The People, Rules, and Processes Governing Online Speech** (2018), frames platforms as **governors**, not merely conduits or publishers. The essay catalogs the actors who make and apply rules, from **policy teams** to **moderators** and **outside experts**, and it analyzes the resulting **governance stack**: high-level **community standards**, granular **enforcement guidelines**, automated **ranking and removal**, and escalating **appeals**. The central claim is that this system already constitutes a form of **private administrative law** with recognizable features of **rule of law** and **discretion**. The article’s analytic categories borrow from **law of rulemaking** and **adjudication**, suggesting that **notice-and-comment** analogues, explanatory **reason-giving**, and robust **appeals** can translate into platform practice.

Two normative pivots emerge in the **New Governors** argument: **procedural legitimacy** and **functional accountability**. Procedural legitimacy demands **clarity** of rules, **foreseeability** of application, and **access** to remedy. Functional accountability acknowledges that democratically unaccountable actors regulate speech with **massive externalities**, so private processes must absorb public-law sensibilities. Klonick engages **Jürgen Habermas** on the **public sphere**, noting that mediation by **private intermediaries** upends classical assumptions. She also weighs **Jack Balkin’s** call for **new school speech regulation**, where the state steers platforms through **transparency mandates** and **systemic audits** rather than direct content controls, situating procedural norms as a middle path between state censorship and laissez-faire.

#### 3. Oversight, adjudication, and constitutionalization by design
Klonick’s longform reporting on the **Facebook Oversight Board** in the **New Yorker** (2021) extends the administrative analogy by examining purpose-built **appellate institutions**. She chronicles how **Facebook** sought to generate **institutional independence** through trust funding, selection processes, and judicialized procedure, producing a forum for **rights-like adjudication**. Here the conceptual vocabulary shifts to **constitutionalization by design**: platforms can harden **constraints** on their own power by building **independent review**, producing **jurisprudence** that binds future decisions and educates the public. Klonick’s analysis spotlights the Board’s **reasoned opinions**, **precedent-like referencing**, and **policy recommendations**, anticipating how adjudication feeds back into **rulemaking**.

This institutional turn exposes a tension between **scale** and **legalism**. Klonick acknowledges critiques by **Evelyn Douek** on **content moderation at scale**, which warns against case-by-case romanticism in favor of **systemic oversight** and **transparency of error rates**. Yet she maintains that adjudicative **reason-giving** cultivates **public trust** and **internal discipline**, the *ethos* of rule-of-law. The **Oversight Board** becomes a laboratory for **gradated scrutiny**: not every removal merits full review, but archetypal disputes can anchor **doctrine** and signal **policy reform**. These dynamics suggest an emerging **constitutional pluralism** within platform governance, where **community norms**, **advertiser constraints**, **state laws**, and **civil society pressure** interact in a multi-source **normative order**.

Klonick’s toolkit therefore includes design levers familiar to **administrative law**: ex ante **rule clarity**, ex post **explanation**, internal **audits**, and external **ombuds**. She examines **transparency reports**, **appeals dashboards**, and **third-party audits** as mechanisms of **accountability**. There is no metaphysical doctrine of truth animating her proposals, but a **prudential jurisprudence** aimed at stabilizing expectations and enabling contestation, a secular echo of *lex naturalis* without commitment to **natural law** as such.

#### 4. Interlocutors, debates, and critical refinements
Klonick’s interlocution with **Julie Cohen**, **Daphne Keller**, **Danielle Citron**, and **Frank Pasquale** refines her account of power. **Cohen’s** critique of **infrastructural power** and **subject formation** presses beyond proceduralism, warning that **surveillance capitalism** shapes preferences before rule-application. **Keller** emphasizes conflicts of law and **collateral censorship** through intermediary liability, complicating the neatness of **due process** when cross-border **legal asymmetries** bite. **Citron** foregrounds **dignitary harms** and the need for **intimate privacy** protections that procedural neutrality can miss. **Pasquale** pushes for **accountable algorithms** and the **black box society** critique, urging substantive limits on what platforms may optimize.

Against the backdrop of **First Amendment exceptionalism**, where state action doctrine insulates platforms from constitutional claims, Klonick offers a **third way**: build **rights-like procedures** voluntarily or via **soft law** mandates. She does not collapse **public** and **private**, but she insists on **functional equivalence** whenever private actors wield **public-facing power** over speech. These debates converge on a shared insight: the moral center of content moderation is not only the line between **free speech** and **harm**, but the quality of **institutional processes** by which that line is drawn and redrawn. This re-centers **prudence** and **phronesis**, aligning rule-of-law **virtues** with the messy practice of governance.

In theological register, this conversation resonates with **Habermasian** concerns about the **public sphere**, yet faces the Augustinian worry about **curved-inward loves** that proceduralism cannot heal. Klonick does not propose an account of **teleology** or **common good** in classical terms; her project is to keep **authority** answerable to **reasoned explanation** and **contestability**. That limitation is programmatic: when no substantive **summum bonum** commands agreement, thicken **process**. Whether that can bear the moral weight of the **digital commonweal** is the open question left for theologians.

#### 5. Theological interface: speech, discipline, and common life
Transposed into ecclesial categories, Klonick’s map of **platform governance** clarifies analogies to **church polity**. Platforms’ **community standards** function like a secular **rule of faith** for speech, their **moderation** resembles **discipline** in the **forum externum**, and **appeals** echo **synodal review**. Theologically, **Calvin’s third mark of the church**, namely **discipline**, insists that communal holiness and truthful speech require **forms** of correction. Klonick supplies non-theological but richly institutional insight into how **forms** work at scale: how **rules** gain authority, how **explanation** proceeds, how **remedy** inculcates trust. Her secular **procedural virtues** mirror the classical juridical virtues needed for *cura animarum*, even as the **ends** diverge.

Further, her account of **constitutionalization by design** aids Christian reflection on **ministerial authority**. In Reformed terms, **auctoritas ministerialis** is rule-bound, **ministerial not magisterial**, constrained by **Scripture** and **confessiones**. Platforms, too, can bind themselves through **independent review**, limiting executive **discretion**. The analogy is imperfect because the **telos** of the church is the **communio sanctorum** under **Word and sacrament**, while platforms pursue **engagement** and **market share**. Yet the structural insight holds: legitimacy grows where **power** submits to **known norms**, gives **reasons**, and welcomes **appeal**. In Latin terms, the reconciliation of **ius** and **lex** is as important in Silicon Valley as in Geneva.

At the same time, Klonick’s turn to **procedural legitimacy** is an implicit confession of pluralism: without a shared **substantive common good**, the best we can do is to discipline **decision-making**. Theological voices must therefore supply substantive ends for speech: **truth-telling**, **neighbor love**, and **justice** framed by **divine law**. Klonick’s map does not provide those ends, but it does show where in the **governance stack** they would need to be inscribed, whether as **non-derogable values**, **heightened scrutiny categories**, or **design constraints** on algorithmic amplification.

A key contribution is her insistence on **reason-giving**. Reformed theology has long emphasized **perspicuity of Scripture** and the **responsibility of office-bearers** to teach and defend publicly. Translating to platforms, **transparency**, **explanation**, and **retrospective review** exhibit the same humble submission of authority to **verbum publicum**. The formal virtue of reasoned decisions is not redemptive, but it is **creationally fitting** under **general revelation**, acknowledging the image-bearing capacities for **understanding** and **accountability**.

The doctrinal friction lies in anthropology. Klonick assumes that **procedures** can sufficiently counter incentives baked into **surveillance capitalism**. Theologically, an Augustinian account of **concupiscentia** would warn that design choices will bend toward **disordered loves** unless **ends** are reformed. Here **Frank Pasquale’s** call for **substantive limits** aligns more closely with a norm of **moral law** than proceduralism alone. Yet even this critique presupposes institutional capacity for **self-limitation**, which Klonick’s work cultivates by blueprinting how limits can be built.

In sum, the **new governors** thesis discloses a moral field in which theologians must labor. **Authority**, **speech**, **truth**, and **community** have become entangled with **platform governance**. Klonick’s categories equip the church to evaluate that entanglement, to discern where **prudential process** can support **virtue**, and where **substantive doctrine** must challenge the defaults of the **digital public square**.

The Reformed and WTS evaluation: From a classical **Reformed** standpoint, Klonick’s emphasis on **procedural legitimacy** is a salutary enactment of **lex naturae** under **common grace**, constraining arbitrariness in the **forum externum**. Her analogies to **administrative law**, **rulemaking**, and **reasoned adjudication** commend themselves to a **two-kingdoms** prudence that distinguishes the **civil** from the **spiritual** kingdom while insisting that both are under **Christ’s lordship**. Yet her project presumes a **neutral proceduralism** at odds with **Van Tilian antithesis**: there is no religiously neutral zone, and governance inevitably encodes **nature-grace** judgments about truth and harm. A Reformed alternative would integrate her **due-process** architecture with substantive norms ordered to **neighbor love** and **truthfulness**, limiting amplification of **false witness** and protecting **the weak** as matters of **natural law** not mere policy preference. Practically, the Reformed tradition’s **ministerial not magisterial authority**, **Calvin’s discipline**, and **Kuyperian sphere sovereignty** together counsel platforms to adopt ministerial self-limitation, transparent **jurisdictio**, and differentiated **competences** rather than totalizing control. Where Klonick’s framework risks elevating **procedure** into **telos**, Reformed theology would receive it as a penultimate good that must be normed by **special revelation**, **covenantally-ordered** ends, and the **common good** of truthful speech within God’s providential **ordo**.
