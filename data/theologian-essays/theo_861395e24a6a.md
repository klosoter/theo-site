### TITLE
God the Most Moved Mover: an open, relational, Spirit-centered evangelical theology of love and risk

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar evangelicalism**, late twentieth-century North America, inerrancy controversies and the **Evangelical Theological Society**, analytic philosophy of religion and debates with **process theology** set the stage.
- Method shifts from **evidentialist foundationalism** to **critical realism** and a **pneumatological, canonical, postfoundational** stance, with a **cumulative-case apologetic** and experiential criteria.
- Key works: **Set Forth Your Case** 1967; **Biblical Revelation** 1971; **Grace Unlimited** ed. 1975; **Reason Enough** 1980; **The Scripture Principle** 1984; **The Grace of God and the Will of Man** ed. 1989; **Tracking the Maze** 1990; **A Wideness in God’s Mercy** 1992; **The Openness of God** 1994; **Unbounded Love** 1994; **Flame of Love** 1996; **Most Moved Mover** 2001.
- Central terms: **openness of God**, **dynamic omniscience**, **divine self-limitation**, **libertarian freedom**, **prevenient grace**, **corporate election**, **universal atonement**, **soteriological inclusivism**, **conditional immortality**, **Scripture principle**, **pneumatological imagination**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **F. F. Bruce**, **Arminius** and **Wesley**, **Molina** and **middle knowledge**, **Karl Barth**, **Karl Rahner**, **Wolfhart Pannenberg**, **Charles Hartshorne** and **process thought**, **John Hick** on pluralism, the **Openness circle** of **Rice, Sanders, Hasker, Basinger**.
- Reformed reception cue: contested by **classical Reformed orthodoxy** on **divine immutability, impassibility, decree, exhaustive foreknowledge**, and **inerrancy**, with limited convergence on **mission, Trinity, and Scripture’s authority**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Clark H. Pinnock, born in 1937 in Toronto and deceased in 2010, matured within Canadian Baptist life yet worked at the heart of **North American evangelicalism** as it wrestled with **inerrancy, pluralism, and analytic philosophy of religion**. Trained in New Testament under **F. F. Bruce** at the University of Manchester, receiving his doctorate in 1963, Pinnock learned historical exegesis and evangelical restraint from a mentor who combined critical tools with **biblical authority**. Early teaching posts at **New Orleans Baptist Theological Seminary**, then at institutions such as **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** and ultimately **McMaster Divinity College** from 1977, placed him near the nerve centers of the **evangelical renaissance** and the **ETS debates**. His early posture was conservative, aligned with **inerrancy** and evidentialist defense of **revelation**, yet his trajectory, spanning five decades, models the move from midcentury **foundationalism** to late-modern **postfoundational, pneumatological, relational theism**.

Pinnock’s methodological development began with a robust **apologetic foundationalism**. In **Set Forth Your Case** 1967 and **Biblical Revelation** 1971 he argued for the cognitive credibility of **Christian theism**, using historical evidences for **resurrection** and a propositional model of **inspiration** that comported with the rising **Chicago tradition** on inerrancy. By the 1980s, however, he embraced **critical realism** and a **cumulative-case** approach in **Reason Enough** 1980 and **Tracking the Maze** 1990, trading Cartesian certainty for fallibilist confidence shaped by **community, Scripture, and the Spirit**. This methodological pivot was codified in **The Scripture Principle** 1984, where he sought a via media between **biblicist rationalism** and **neoorthodox kerygma**, advocating a **canonical, Spirit-given authority** that is trustworthy without rigid harmonization. The movement from **foundationalism** to **postfoundational coherence** prepared the ground for his later **open theism** and **pneumatology**, where divine-human reciprocity and narrative shape the epistemic field.

#### 2. Doctrine of God and the openness proposal
The leitmotif of Pinnock’s mature work is a thoroughgoing **relational theism** that reconfigures **divine attributes** around **love** and **freedom**. His signature statement appears in **The Openness of God** 1994, a collaborative volume with **Richard Rice, John Sanders, William Hasker, and David Basinger**, and in his later monograph **Most Moved Mover** 2001. There he argues that the Bible portrays God as genuinely interactive, temporally engaged, and responsive, a God who enters covenantal give-and-take rather than a static, impassible Absolute. Against a synthesis he associates with **Augustine** and **Aquinas**, he contends that **divine immutability and impassibility**, rightly understood, must be qualified by covenantal dynamism, for the God of **Abraham, Moses, and Jesus** suffers, rejoices, and grieves. The central construct is **dynamic omniscience**. God knows all reality perfectly, but future free choices are not yet determinate, hence are known by God as open possibilities rather than fixed certainties. This **open futurism** secures the genuineness of **libertarian freedom** and makes sense of **petitionary prayer, prophecy as conditional, and divine regret** without collapsing into **determinism**.

Pinnock’s account of **divine self-limitation** is not an ontological reduction but a free covenantal decision grounded in **love**, an *ad extra* disposition by which God grants creaturely otherness and risk. He engages **process theology** and **Hartshorne** appreciatively for their stress on divine relatedness, yet refuses metaphysical dualism or the denial of **creatio ex nihilo** and **omnipotence**. Similarly, he dialogues with **Molinism**, exploring **middle knowledge** as a possible route to foreknowledge and freedom, but ultimately judges it to reintroduce determinacy and to be less faithful to the **biblical drama** than openness. In both **The Openness of God** and **Most Moved Mover**, he deploys narratives of **Abraham interceding for Sodom, God relenting with Nineveh, and Jesus’ tears at Lazarus** as exegetical anchors, claiming that **classical theism** has allegorized away the reality of divine pathos. The upshot is a **risk-taking God** whose **providence** is flexible and whose **governance** is responsive, a model he believes honors **Trinitarian personalism** and eschews stoic metaphysics.

#### 3. Soteriology, election, and the wideness of grace
Pinnock’s soteriology underwent a marked transition from earlier sympathy with **Reformed apologetics** to a settled **Arminian-Wesleyan** stance. In **Grace Unlimited** 1975 and the later anthology **The Grace of God and the Will of Man** 1989, he advocates **prevenient grace**, **universal atonement**, and **synergistic conversion**, opposing **unconditional election** and **reprobation** as incompatible with the **character of divine love** revealed in Christ. His exegesis of Romans 9 through 11 and Ephesians 1 favors **corporate election** in Christ, where election is fundamentally ecclesial and missional rather than an intratrinitarian decree selecting particular individuals for salvation and perdition. He deploys **libertarian freedom** as a metaphysical correlate of personalist love, arguing that coerced love is a contradiction and that resistible grace best reflects **covenant** as mutual giving.

The most controversial extension of his soteriology is his defense of **soteriological inclusivism** in **A Wideness in God’s Mercy** 1992 and **Unbounded Love** 1994 with **Robert Brow**. Here he contends that the **Spirit of Christ** is universally active, mediating prevenient light to the unevangelized, such that those who respond in faith to this light may be saved through Christ without explicit historical knowledge of the **gospel**. He resists **pluralism** of the **John Hick** type, affirming the unique and normative **incarnation** and atonement, yet refuses a strict **restrictivism** that would consign all unevangelized to damnation. He flirts with a **postmortem evangelization** hope and endorses **conditional immortality** or **annihilationism**, contending that **eternal conscious torment** misreads biblical imagery and impugns **divine justice**. The energy of this soteriology is doxological and missional, a bid to magnify the **magnanimity of grace** while preserving human responsibility.

#### 4. Pneumatology and the Spirit’s mission
The integrative center of Pinnock’s later theology is his **pneumatology**. In **Flame of Love** 1996 he offers a biblically and ecumenically rich account of the **Holy Spirit** as the bond of **Trinitarian love** and the agent of **creation, new creation, and mission**. Drawing on **Yves Congar**, **Eastern Orthodox** themes of *theosis*, and **Pentecostal-charismatic** renewal, he argues that evangelical theology should shift from a Christomonistic focus to a **Spirit-Christology** in which the human life of Jesus is empowered by the Spirit and the church participates in that same **anointing**. This move grounds a broader engagement with **world religions**, because the **missio Spiritus** extends beyond the church, convicting the world and sowing seeds of the Word, while still leading toward the **missio Christi** that names and completes the Spirit’s work.

This **pneumatological imagination** also reshapes ecclesiology and sacramentality. Pinnock accents the **charisms**, the Spirit’s democratizing distribution of gifts, and the **missional church** as a foretaste of the kingdom. He retrieves a moderate **sacramental realism**, seeing **baptism and Eucharist** as Spirit-mediated means of grace that form a hospitable community. The doctrine of God is likewise colored by the Spirit, for in **Most Moved Mover** he appeals to intra-Trinitarian **perichoresis** to resist solitary divine impassibility and to commend **mutuality** as a divine perfection. The Spirit becomes the theological warrant for **relational ontology**, and thus the engine that drives **openness** in providence and **synergism** in salvation.

#### 5. Scripture, authority, and apologetic stance
Pinnock’s **Scripture principle** anchors his proposals. In **The Scripture Principle** 1984 he seeks to articulate **biblical authority** as the norming norm of theology while resisting both **modernist reduction** and **docetic inerrancy**. He affirms **inspiration** and **trustworthiness**, yet urges evangelical readers to honor genre, diversity, and canonical shape, arguing for an **infallibility in accomplishing its salvific purpose** rather than a universal precision in incidental matters. He critiques **neoorthodox** subjectivism for diluting propositional truth, but also **fundamentalist harmonization** for constraining Scripture to alien standards. His later work reads the canon narratively, underwriting a **theodramatic** view of God’s engagement in which both **promises and contingencies** are real.

In epistemology, **Reason Enough** 1980 and **Tracking the Maze** 1990 mark his shift to **critical realism**, where reason serves faith by mounting a **cumulative case** from Scripture, experience, history, and the world’s intelligibility. He engages **analytic philosophy of religion** appreciatively and interacts with **Plantinga** and **Wolterstorff** in broad terms, while maintaining that **doxological rationality** and the **internal witness of the Spirit** are decisive. The apologetic core remains evangelical, yet the tone softens from combat to conversation, a posture that prepares his constructive engagements with **process thinkers** and **ecumenical theology** without ceding **Christological normativity**.

#### 6. Providence, prayer, and the problem of evil
The practical edge of Pinnock’s **open theism** is most evident in his accounts of **providence, prayer, and evil**. By rejecting hard **determinism** and **exhaustive definite foreknowledge** of future free acts, he claims to preserve the biblical logic of **petitionary prayer** as a real factor in divine decisions. God genuinely responds, changes course, and even takes counsel with creatures, an understanding he sees in the **Abrahamic intercession** and **Mosaic mediation** narratives. This responsive **providence** is neither deistic nor chaotic, since God retains unlimited **wisdom and power** and governs by **general strategies** that can be flexed in light of creaturely input. The **problem of evil** thus receives a twofold answer, a **free will defense** that secures moral responsibility, and a **risk model** that explains why God allows hazards in a creation ordered to love.

Pinnock insists that this model enhances mission and holiness, because it portrays God as the **most moved mover**, worthy of trust and open to partnership. The life of **prayer** is not an afterthought but a commanded participation in God’s governance. He argues that **prophecy** is often **conditional**, not because God is ignorant, but because God is wise, choosing to speak into a dynamic history where human responses are taken with utmost seriousness. The emphasis on **divine pathos** sharpens the reality of the **incarnation and cross**, events that display not only penal substitution and victory, but also the **costly love** of a God who bears with sinners.

#### 7. Hell, judgment, and eschatological hope
In **A Wideness in God’s Mercy** 1992 and subsequent essays, Pinnock fortifies his doctrine of **conditional immortality**, aligning with **Edward Fudge** and acknowledging convergences with **John Stott**. He argues that **immortality** is a gift in Christ rather than an innate human property, so the final fate of the impenitent is **annihilation** rather than endless torment. This claim is framed as biblical exegesis of **apollymi, gehenna, and the second death**, and as a **theological protest** on behalf of divine **justice and proportionality**. He rejects **universalism** as presumption, yet he sustains an eschatological **hopefulness** grounded in the wideness of **prevenient grace** and the universal sweep of **Christ’s atonement**, coupled with the **Spirit’s mission** in the world.

Eschatology in Pinnock remains **Christocentric** and **pneumatological**. He speaks of the **new creation** as the telos of God’s project, a reality already tasting in the church’s **charisms** and in the groaning of creation under the **Spirit’s firstfruits**. The church is a pilgrim people, called to witness and to participate in the healing of the nations, guided by the **Lamb** who reigns not by coercion but by **self-giving love**. By connecting **eschatological judgment** with **missional urgency**, he presses evangelicals to preach with **confidence and compassion**, trusting a God whose justice is restorative and whose wrath is the form taken by holy love in the face of evil.

#### 8. Controversies, institutions, and influence
Pinnock’s proposals ignited the late twentieth-century **evangelical debates**. The **Evangelical Theological Society** examined his views in 2003 after challenges regarding **inerrancy** and **openness**, alongside **John Sanders**. The Society declined to expel him, though the margin was contested, indicating a community in transition on **bibliology and divine foreknowledge**. Reformed and Baptist critics such as **Paul Helm**, **Bruce Ware**, and **John Frame** offered detailed rebuttals on **providence, immutability, and biblical exegesis**, while proponents within the **Openness** camp and **postconservative evangelicalism** such as **Stanley Grenz** and **Roger Olson** found in Pinnock a constructive model of theological development faithful to **evangelical orthodoxy** yet open to **renewal**.

Institutionally, his longest tenure at **McMaster Divinity College** formed generations of pastors and scholars in a **missional, Spirit-centered** approach. His collaborations with **Sanders, Hasker, Basinger, and Rice** lent coherence to a broader **open theism** movement, while his pneumatology influenced **Pentecostal** and **charismatic** theologians receptive to **ecumenical dialogue**. Even among critics, his work forced sharper articulations of **classical theism** and renewed exegesis of the **pathos of God**. His death in 2010 ended a restless and generous career, but the debates over **divine attributes, inclusivism, and conditional immortality** continue to cite him as a leading voice.

#### 9. Synthesis and theological profile
Theologically, Pinnock presents a unified portrait of **God’s love** refracted across **doctrine of God, salvation, Scripture, and mission**. The grammar of **openness** is not an isolated thesis but the formal cause that shapes his **pneumatology**, his **Wesleyan soteriology**, and his **ecclesial praxis**. Scripture remains the supreme authority in his **Scripture principle**, yet is read with **narratival, canonical, and experiential** sensibilities animated by the **Spirit**. His engagement with **process** and **Molinism** is instrumental, a way of clarifying evangelical commitments rather than capitulating to alien metaphysics. The distinctive notes are **relational ontology, libertarian freedom, and a universal scope of grace**, all tethered to **Trinitarian personalism** and the **mission of the Spirit** in the world. In his self-understanding, he is reclaiming a fully **biblical God** who is neither remote nor capricious, but the **most moved mover** who risks, suffers, and triumphs in love.

At the same time, the system bears internal tensions. The interplay between **biblical authority** and **philosophical reconstruction** is delicate, as is the balance between **divine sovereignty** and historical contingency. Pinnock’s project is best read as a protest against reductive **determinism** and a pastoral bid to render **prayer, mission, and holiness** meaningful for ordinary believers. The enduring contribution is a rehabilitated **doctrine of God** that foregrounds the **Spirit** and that invites evangelicals to retrieve the **affections of God** without embarrassment.

In a single integrated Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation, Pinnock’s **open theism** ruptures key coordinates of **classical Reformed orthodoxy**. By defining **omniscience** as knowledge of all that is knowable and denying **exhaustive definite foreknowledge** of future free acts, he undercuts the Reformed synthesis of **divine decree, providence, and compatibilist freedom** that grounds assurance, covenantal certainty, and the unity of the **historia salutis**. His revisions of **immutability and impassibility** collapse the careful distinction between *ad intra* and *ad extra*, jeopardizing **divine aseity** and the perfection of **simple triune life** apart from the world, whereas classical Reformed theology confesses **covenantal impassibility** alongside genuine **divine condescension** in history. His **Scripture principle**, while laudably canonical and genre-sensitive, wavers on **inerrancy**, risking a functional magisterium of **experience** that can steer exegesis toward a predetermined **relational metaphysic**. On salvation, his **prevenient grace, corporate election, inclusivism, and conditional immortality** stand at variance with **unconditional election, effectual calling, particular redemption, and the final judgment** as confessed in the **Westminster Standards**. Yet Reformed theology can appreciate his **Trinitarian focus**, his zeal for **mission and prayer**, his protest against fatalistic caricatures of **providence**, and his retrieval of biblical **divine pathos** when framed covenantally. A classical alternative would reaffirm **exhaustive divine foreknowledge** within **meticulous providence**, maintain **impassibility** as the perfection of God’s love rather than its negation, uphold **verbal plenary inspiration and inerrancy**, and ground the life of **prayer and mission** in the ordained means of grace within the **covenant of redemption** and **covenant of grace**, thus securing both **God’s freedom and faithfulness** without recourse to an open future.

- Context: **Canadian Baptist** theologian in late twentieth-century **evangelicalism**, active in **ETS** debates amid challenges from **process thought**, pluralism, and analytic philosophy.
- Focus: Recasting the **doctrine of God** around **relational love** through **open theism**, integrating **pneumatology**, **soteriology**, and **mission**.
- Works and positions: **Set Forth Your Case** 1967 and **Biblical Revelation** 1971 on **apologetics and Scripture**; **The Scripture Principle** 1984 on **authority**; **Grace Unlimited** 1975 and **The Grace of God and the Will of Man** 1989 on **Arminian grace**; **A Wideness in God’s Mercy** 1992 on **inclusivism**; **The Openness of God** 1994 and **Most Moved Mover** 2001 on **divine openness**; **Flame of Love** 1996 on **pneumatology**.
- Defining doctrines: **dynamic omniscience**, **divine self-limitation**, **libertarian freedom**, **prevenient grace**, **corporate election**, **universal atonement**, **soteriological inclusivism**, **conditional immortality**, **pneumatological imagination**.
- Interlocutors: Engaged **F. F. Bruce**, **Arminius and Wesley**, **Molina**, **Barth**, **Rahner**, **Pannenberg**, **Hartshorne** and **process**, **John Hick**, and the **Openness** cohort **Rice, Sanders, Hasker, Basinger**; opposed by **Helm, Ware, Frame**.
- Reformed critique: Challenges to **immutability, impassibility, exhaustive foreknowledge, decree, compatibilism**, and **inerrancy**; possible convergences on **Trinity, mission, and the reality of divine pathos** within **covenantal** terms.
- Legacy: Catalyst for **open theism** and **postconservative evangelicalism**, enduring influence on **pneumatology**, **mission**, and debates on **hell and inclusivism**, provoking sharper classical articulations and ongoing reassessment of **divine love**.
