### TITLE
Pastor in Context: Recasting Calvin, Bucer, and the Reformation through Spiritual-Pastoral Lenses

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth and early twenty-first century Dutch Reformed** setting; professor at **Theological University Apeldoorn**; leader of **Refo500** in the 2017 quincentenary landscape.
- **Historical-theological method** centered on primary sources, **consistory records**, letters, and biography as theological access; interpretive emphasis on **piety, practice, and pastoral care**.
- **Marriage and Divorce in the Thought of Martin Bucer** 1999; **Calvin's Theology of the Psalms** 2007; **John Calvin: A Pilgrim's Life** 2009; editor of **The Calvin Handbook** 2009; editor of **Handbook of Dutch Church History** 2015; organizer-editor for studies on the **Synod of Dort** 2018–2019.
- **Pilgrimage**, **consolation**, **consistory**, **discipline**, **providence**, **prayer**, **duplex gratia**, **peregrinatio** as central categories.
- Dialogues with **Willem van 't Spijker**, **Heiko Oberman**, **Richard Muller**, **Bruce Gordon**, **Elsie McKee**, **John Witte Jr.**, and debates on **Calvin reception**, **Bucer’s polity**, and **Dort’s pastoral intent**.
- Reformed reception: valued for **pastoral retrieval of Calvin and Bucer**, with questions about integrating **historical narrative** and **dogmatic construction** in a classical **Reformed scholastic** frame.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Herman J. Selderhuis emerges from the Dutch **Reformed** world of the late twentieth century, trained and serving at the **Theological University of Apeldoorn** within the churches of the **Christelijke Gereformeerde Kerken**. Born in 1961 and ordained to pastoral ministry before entering full-time scholarship, he became professor of **church history and church polity** in the 1990s and helped shape a generation of pastors and scholars through a curriculum grounded in **Reformed orthodoxy**, archival rigor, and attention to ecclesial practice. Under the mentoring influence of figures such as **Willem van 't Spijker**, he absorbed a method that marries **historical theology** to ecclesiastical law and piety, reading theology where it is lived: in **consistory minutes**, liturgical reforms, and pastoral letters. Selderhuis is best known publicly through his leadership of **Refo500**, an international platform for research, exhibitions, and publications on the **Reformation**, which signaled his commitment to both scholarly depth and broad dissemination. His abiding thesis is that doctrines are best understood as part of a **pastoral ecology** in which **providence**, **prayer**, **discipline**, and **consolation** shape theological expression.

This method is neither antiquarian nor ideological. Selderhuis insists on thick description of the **sixteenth-century** world while refusing anachronistic projection of modern systematic concerns. He argues that **Calvin** and **Bucer** cannot be reduced to an abstract blueprint of **predestination** or a modern narrative of autonomy, because their thought is inseparable from **peregrinatio** and care for souls under pressure. Selderhuis thus privileges categories like **consolatio** and **tentatio** to frame doctrine, drawing on the Augustinian grammar of pilgrimage and the **Lutheran** triad of *oratio, meditatio, tentatio* while letting **Reformed** specificities such as **duplex gratia** and **consistory discipline** do their own work. The resulting historical-theological stance is a hermeneutic of pastoral realism that seeks the theological center in the life of the church.

#### 2. Bucer, marriage, and pastoral polity
Selderhuis’s doctoral work on **Martin Bucer** led to his first major monograph, **Marriage and Divorce in the Thought of Martin Bucer** 1999. There he treats **marriage** not as a marginal legal interest but as a crucible of **Bucer’s ecclesiology** and **pastoral theology**. Mining Strasbourg’s municipal decrees, consistory cases, and Bucer’s counsel, he shows how the reformer grounded marital ethics in **covenant** and **pastoral cura animarum**, proposing broad remedial measures for marital breakdown that aimed at the restoration of holiness and justice. Selderhuis pushes beyond the stereotype of Bucer as mere mediator. He reconstructs a coherent program in which the **magistrate** and **consistory** cooperate to uphold marriage as a school of **sanctification**, with gradated sanctions aligned to **church discipline**. The volume engages interlocutors such as **Elsie McKee** and **John Witte Jr.**, yet Selderhuis’s distinct contribution lies in tying **marriage doctrine** to the texture of congregational life rather than to abstract legal code.

This Bucerian focus reveals the contours of Selderhuis’s wider method. The theological weight falls on the governance of concrete relations under the **Word and Spirit**, where **discipline** is a mode of **consolation** and not a mere juridical tool. By tracing how Bucer argued for expanded grounds for separation or divorce within a covenantal frame, Selderhuis exposes the reformers’ willingness to bend structures toward **pastoral mercy** while maintaining doctrinal clarity on **creation ordinance** and **sin**. Such analysis makes two further points. First, **Reformed polity** grows from a theological vision of the church as a community of mutual exhortation and care. Second, **civil authority** is not external to sanctification but is implicated in the church’s ordered peace. Thus, in Selderhuis’s Bucer, **church polity** is a doctrinal statement, a lived confession of **providence** and **grace** under discipline.

#### 3. Calvin the pilgrim and the Psalter
Selderhuis’s most influential works on **Calvin** are **Calvin’s Theology of the Psalms** 2007 and **John Calvin: A Pilgrim’s Life** 2009. The first focuses on the **Psalter** as Calvin’s school of doctrine and prayer, arguing that the Psalms furnish not only the *affectus* of faith but the structural coherence of **providence**, **Christology**, **law and gospel**, and **union with Christ**. Selderhuis synthesizes commentaries, sermons, and liturgical practice to show how **prayer** is the lived form of **Calvin’s doctrine of providence**, and how **lament** and **praise** embed **duplex gratia** in the rhythms of repentance and sanctification. This work traverses debates shaped by **T. H. L. Parker** and **Richard Muller**, correcting the false dichotomy between Calvin the exegete and Calvin the dogmatician by insisting that Calvin’s doctrinal architecture rises from his **exegesis** of the Psalms and the pastoral needs of **Geneva’s** congregations.

In **John Calvin: A Pilgrim’s Life** Selderhuis crafts a biography that rejects triumphalism and caricature. The keynote is **peregrinatio**. Calvin is a pilgrim in exile, at home only under **God’s providence** and discipline. Selderhuis draws on letters, consistory registers, and institutional records to depict Calvin’s fragile health, relentless work, and complex relations with colleagues like **Pierre Viret** and opponents like **Sebastian Castellio**. He recovers the pastoral heart of Calvin’s **consistory**, the careful deployment of **discipline** for reconciliation rather than spectacle, and the daily labor of catechesis and preaching. The book dialogues fruitfully with **Bruce Gordon’s** intellectual biography and **Bernard Cottret’s** political portrait, but Selderhuis insists that Calvin’s theological nerve lies neither simply in political consolidation nor in speculative doctrine. Rather, the center is the spiritual-pastoral praxis that anchors **autotheos**, **predestination**, and **sacramental theology** in the believer’s walk through affliction toward **gloria Dei**.

#### 4. The Calvin Handbook and method codified
As editor of **The Calvin Handbook** 2009, Selderhuis codified his program by convening an international team to present **Calvin** in his total context. The volume integrates **intellectual history**, **exegesis**, **institutions**, **liturgy**, and **reception** into a single frame, thereby resisting atomistic treatments. Selderhuis’s editorial hand prioritizes categories like **spirituality**, **pastoral care**, and **church order** alongside the expected **predestination** and **sacramental** chapters. The outcome is a textured synthesis that demonstrates how **Institutes** and commentaries breathe the same air as consistory practices, psalm-singing, and the **Genevan Academy**. In this sense, the Handbook extends a methodological manifesto: theology is to be read as a web of practices and convictions, historically located and ecclesially oriented.

The Handbook also situates Calvin within late medieval and **Renaissance** networks, following the path opened by **Heiko Oberman** and **Irena Backus**. Selderhuis highlights continuity and reform, showing that Calvin’s **spirituality of the Psalms** and **catechetical labors** are not breaks with the past but disciplined refinements. Debates over **Calvin’s sources** and **humanist method** are treated without polemic, yet the consistent thread is Selderhuis’s refusal to abstract doctrine from its life-world. He illustrates how topics like **divine providence** and **assurance** only disclose their true contours when placed against the pressures of plague, exile, and ecclesial conflict.

#### 5. Dordt, assurance, and pastoral predestination
Selderhuis’s Dutch location gives him a privileged angle on the **Synod of Dort** 1618–1619. Through edited volumes that place **Dort** in its European context and through public projects associated with the four-hundredth anniversary, he advances a reading of the **Canons of Dort** as fundamentally **pastoral**. The **decretum** is not a speculative axiom but the church’s confession designed to secure **assurance** and to protect the preaching of **free grace**. Selderhuis underlines the catechetical impulse of Dort’s canons on **perseverance**, the careful refutation of **Remonstrant** unsettling of conscience, and the intrinsic link between **election in Christ** and the ministry of the **Word and sacrament**. This angle, indebted to the best of **Reformed scholastic** clarifications yet resistant to caricature, argues that the canons must be interpreted alongside the **Church Order of Dort** and the **Heidelberg Catechism**, where consolation for the afflicted conscience provides the governing telos.

Here Selderhuis enters contemporary contestation with readers who treat **predestination** as the defining identity of Reformed theology. He insists that while the **decree** is central, its pastoral function is decisive. Theological nerves lie in the communication of **Christus pro nobis** through preaching and discipline, within a well-ordered church that defends the weak. By aligning **Dort’s** doctrinal definitions with its churchly governance, he folds even high doctrinal loci back into **consistory** and catechesis, extending the lines drawn earlier in his **Calvin** and **Bucer** studies.

#### 6. Consolation, discipline, and the texture of Reformed piety
Across Selderhuis’s corpus the paired themes of **consolation** and **discipline** dominate. He reads **consistory discipline** not as social control but as a sacramental-adjacent practice that seeks the peace and holiness of the body. From **Bucer’s** Strasbourg through **Calvin’s** Geneva to **Dort**’s codification, Selderhuis charts how **Reformed** communities crafted procedures of admonition, suspension, and reconciliation to realize **sanctification** as a corporate reality. He carefully draws lines between **providence** and the believer’s patience in affliction, between **prayer** and confidence in election, between **psalmody** and the formation of affective resilience. In this spiritual map, doctrine becomes guidance for pilgrims, and governance becomes the architecture of consolation.

This emphasis introduces a constructive proposal. Selderhuis suggests that the best entry into **Reformed doctrine** is the ordered life of the church. Reading the **Institutes** through the **Psalter**, grasping **duplex gratia** through consistory practice, or approaching **predestination** through the pastoral aim of **assurance**, the theologian proceeds not by speculative priority but by the order of edification. This is not to deny metaphysical or scholastic concerns. Rather, it is to locate them within the **ecclesia semper reformanda** where believers learn in song, in discipline, and in worship what the truth accomplishes.

#### 7. Public history and the Reformation as network
Selderhuis’s stewardship of **Refo500** exhibits his conviction that the **Reformation** is a communicative event and a living inheritance. Through exhibitions, accessible books, and curated academic collaborations, he portrays the **Reformation** as a network of cities, printers, pastors, and magistrates whose synergy was theological practice in motion. The popularity of **John Calvin: A Pilgrim’s Life** is thus programmatic. Biography functions as theology communicated, engaging readers in the **pilgrim** posture crucial to **Augustinian** and **Reformed** spirituality. Selderhuis mediates between academy and church, a translator of archival textures into narratives capable of forming contemporary **Reformed** identity.

This public orientation invites both admiration and scrutiny. To make the **Reformation** available beyond the guild, Selderhuis simplifies without distortion by selecting **pastoral** threads that ordinary readers can recognize. He highlights the warmth of **Calvin’s** pastoral letters, the tenderness of **psalmody**, and the protective intent of **discipline**. He also curates international scholarship, ensuring that **continental**, **Anglophone**, and **global south** voices find space in the commemorations and publications. The resulting portrait is a Reformation that is catholic in aspiration and locally incarnate, a **traditio** that travels because it is born of worship and care.

#### 8. Interlocutors, debates, and constructive import
Selderhuis’s work stands in debate and conversation with a range of scholars. With **Heiko Oberman** he shares the insistence on late medieval continuities, yet he resists any hard rupture that would detach **Reformed** theology from its spiritual-pastoral roots. With **Richard Muller** he shares respect for **Reformed orthodoxy** and the scholastic codification of doctrine, yet he tends to prioritize **Calvin’s** biblical-pastoral matrix over analytic reconstructions of systems. With **Bruce Gordon** and **Bernard Cottret** he navigates the complexities of **Calvin’s** political and intellectual life, while pressing the importance of **consistory** and **catechesis** as theological laboratories. His conversation with **Elsie McKee** and **John Witte Jr.** on **Bucer** refines the interplay of law, gospel, and polity. In all cases the leitmotif is clear. A **Reformed** theology worth retrieving is one learned on the way, in the company of the **Psalter**, under the rod and staff of **discipline** and **consolation**.

The constructive import for contemporary dogmatics lies in method more than in novel doctrine. Selderhuis urges theologians to locate **providence**, **election**, and **sacrament** within the matrix of **piety** and **polity**, teaching doctrine along the pathways of prayer and pastoral care. He calls for theological retrieval that begins with concrete practices and then unfolds their conceptual grammar. This pastoral-first hermeneutic is not reductionist. It is instead an attempt to perform the classical **Reformed** conviction that truth is for godliness, that **doctrina** serves **pietas**, and that saints are shaped by sound words in ordered communities.

In a concise Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation, Selderhuis offers a salutary corrective to any Reformed habit that isolates **decree** from **gospel preaching** or **discipline** from **consolation**. His historical work models how **revelation** becomes concrete in **covenant** life, how **grace** orders a people by word and table, and how **history** mediates doctrinal clarity. Yet there are tensions. By consistently privileging the pastoral matrix he sometimes risks muting the dogmatic exactitude of **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, or **autotheos**, loci that require metaphysical specification beyond biographical framing. A WTS-inflected alternative would join his pastoral retrieval to a more explicit ressourcement of **Reformed scholastic** distinctions, integrating **Calvin** and **Dort** with the analytic precision of **Turretin** and **Bavinck**. The best synthesis would keep Selderhuis’s **Psalter-shaped** emphasis on consolation and discipline while articulating nonnegotiable doctrinal grammar in terms of **Scripture’s analogia fidei**, so that the church’s **pilgrim** consolation rests on robustly confessed **triune** reality and covenantal decree.
