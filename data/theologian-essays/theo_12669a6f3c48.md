### TITLE
Retrieving Reformed Scholasticism: covenantal soteriology, classical theism, and ministerial natural theology for confessional systematics.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary Reformed context** shaped by post-Van Til debates, **Federal Vision** controversies, and **post-Reformation scholastic retrieval**.
- **Historical-theological method** with confessional norming of exegesis, **ressourcement** to **early modern Reformed orthodoxy**, and a ministerial view of **reason**.
- **Key works**: **Last Things First** (2007); **Justification** (2008); **Death in Adam, Life in Christ** (2009); **Word, Water, and Spirit** (2010); **Beyond Calvin** (2012); **The Theology of the Westminster Standards** (2014); **The Covenant of Redemption** (2016); **Reforming Apologetics** (2019); **The Covenant of Works** (2020); **The Need for Natural Theology** (2023).
- **Central terms**: **covenant of works**, **pactum salutis**, **imputation of active and passive obedience**, **divine simplicity**, **analogia fidei**, **ministerial natural theology**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Calvin**, **Turretin**, **Aquinas**, **Bavinck**, **Kuyper**, **Vos**, **Van Til**, **Frame**, **N. T. Wright**, **Federal Vision**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: strong alignment with **confessional orthodoxy** and **classical theism**, provocative critique of **Van Tilian apologetics**, careful defense of **Westminster theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and methodological stance
John V. **Fesko** emerges within the **contemporary Reformed** world as a pastor-scholar of the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church**, trained in historical and systematic theology and forged amid late twentieth-century disputes over **Van Tilian apologetics**, the **Federal Vision**, and the **New Perspective on Paul**. His academic appointments at **Westminster Seminary California** in the 2000s and later at **Reformed Theological Seminary** in Jackson frame his work within institutions committed to the **Westminster Standards**. Following the path opened by **Richard Muller** and the post-1970 revival of **post-Reformation Reformed scholasticism**, Fesko takes **ressourcement** as method: return to the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, retrieve **scholastic distinctions** and conceptual grammar, and redeploy them for present doctrinal clarity. This yields a highly self-conscious appeal to **classical theism**, a confessional reading of **Scripture**, and a ministerial use of **reason** that resists both rationalism and irrationalism. The goal is a confessional systematics that honors **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** while doing justice to covenantal history.

Fesko’s epistemic posture is anchored in the classic **principia** of Reformed orthodoxy: God as *principium essendi* of theology and **Holy Scripture** as *principium cognoscendi*. He insists that **general revelation** yields genuine though limited knowledge of God and morals, that **special revelation** in **Scripture** is sufficient and normative for saving knowledge, and that the **analogia fidei** and **analogia scripturae** are indispensable hermeneutical norms. This yields what he calls **ministerial natural theology**, a subservient deployment of **philosophical reasoning** and **theistic proofs** in the church’s apologetic and doctrinal work, always bounded by the **confessional** rule of faith. Against modern biblicism that disdains catholic metaphysics, he retrieves **Aquinas** and **Turretin** not as masters, but as servants of the text, convincing that **Reformed scholasticism** possessed a precise conceptuality that buttresses, rather than undermines, exegesis.

#### 2. Eschatology, creation, and the covenantal architecture
Early in his corpus Fesko contends that the eschatological goal is woven into creation itself, a claim developed in **Last Things First: Unlocking Genesis 1-3 with the Christ of Eschatology** 2007. Drawing from **Geerhardus Vos** and the broader **biblical-theological** tradition, he argues that **Sabbath** orientation and **imago Dei** sonship set before Adam a probationary ascent to *gloria*, codified in the **covenant of works**. He presses the point that **Genesis 1-3** is not a bare prolegomenon but a protological version of the eschatological telos unveiled in **Christ**, the last Adam. With **Westminster Confession 7** and **Turretin** in view, he frames the **covenant of works** as a graciously condescending arrangement for life upon perfect and personal obedience, with the **Sabbath** as sign of consummation. The fall then relocates Adamic eschatology into the sphere of the **pactum salutis**, wherein the **Trinity** ordains redemption and the **covenant of grace** unfolds historically through Abraham to Christ.

This covenantal architecture shapes the whole of Fesko’s system: anthropology as **image and sonship**, law-gospel as **works-grace contrast**, and Christology as **federal headship**. The eschatological grammar, indebted to **Vos** and at points to **Meredith Kline**, is however consistently clothed in **scholastic** terms to resist reduction either to literary-theological motifs or to purely redemptive-historical description. The **covenant of works**, therefore, is no optional hypothesis but a load-bearing category for his doctrine of **justification**, his defense of **imputation**, and his analysis of **sacramental** efficacy.

#### 3. Justification, imputation, and union with Christ
In **Justification: Understanding the Classic Reformed Doctrine** 2008 and **Death in Adam, Life in Christ: The Doctrine of Imputation** 2009, Fesko offers a historical and exegetical vindication of the **forensic** center of Reformed soteriology. He defends the **imputation of Christ’s active and passive obedience**, insisting that **federal headship** supplies the legal logic whereby the last Adam’s obedience counts for the many. Against **N. T. Wright** and currents within the **New Perspective on Paul**, he argues that **δικαίωσις** is a law-court declaration and that covenant membership language cannot eclipse the **imputed righteousness** of Christ. Against the **Federal Vision**, he contends that sacramental objectivity must not confuse **instrumental** means with the **ground** of justification, which remains solely the **obedience of Christ** received by **faith alone**.

The knot of **union with Christ** receives a careful historical untangling in **Beyond Calvin: Union with Christ and Justification in Early Modern Reformed Theology** 2012. There Fesko disputes the narrative that early modern scholastics disrupted **Calvin’s duplex gratia** by subordinating union to a rigid **ordo salutis**. He shows instead that figures like **Polanus**, **Ames**, and **Turretin** articulated a rich account of both **historia salutis** and **ordo salutis**, maintaining simultaneous conceptual primacy of union and logical precedence of **justification** with distinct benefits properly ordered. The upshot is a defense of doctrinal clarity against recent trends that, in the name of union, blur the **forensic-ontic** distinction and jeopardize **assurance** by subtly absorbing justification into **sanctification**.

#### 4. Sacramentology, ecclesial practice, and covenant signs
In **Word, Water, and Spirit: A Reformed Perspective on Baptism** 2010, Fesko addresses the Reformed sacramental tradition with exegetical care and historical depth. He argues for **paedobaptism** on the basis of covenant continuity between Abraham and the new covenant, a robust **ecclesiology** of household inclusion, and the **instrumentality** of the sacraments as **means of grace**. He repudiates both **ex opere operato** efficacy and purely memorialist readings, insisting that **Word and Spirit** jointly render the signs effective to the elect according to God’s promise. Baptism is thus a **seal** of **imputed righteousness** and **ingrafting into Christ**, not a bare badge of membership nor a mechanical conduit of grace. In dialog with **Calvin**, the **Heidelberg Catechism**, and **Westminster Larger Catechism**, he retrieves patristic and Reformed sources to show the catholicity of Reformed sacramentology.

He also clarifies the relation of sacramental efficacy to the doctrine of **election** and the **covenant of grace**. The sacraments signify benefits truly offered, but their saving fruition depends on **Spirit-wrought faith** in the elect. Here Fesko positions himself squarely within **confessional orthodoxy**, steering between **Baptist** construals that detach the sacrament from covenant objectivity and **Federal Vision** claims that grant a quasi-**ex opere** efficacy that endangers **justification by faith alone**. The church’s liturgical practice thus rests on the double anchor of **promise** and **Spirit**, with **baptism** and **the Lord’s Supper** operating as appointed ordinances in the economy of the **covenant**.

#### 5. The Westminster Standards and Reformed catholicity
A signature contribution is **The Theology of the Westminster Standards: Historical Context and Theological Insights** 2014. Fesko situates the **Westminster Assembly** 1643-1653 within **British Reformed scholasticism**, countering the caricature that the **Confession** is either rigidly Calvinist or idiosyncratically Puritan. Mining **assembly minutes**, **comparable confessions**, and the works of figures like **William Ames**, **John Ball**, **William Gouge**, **Ursinus**, and **Polanus**, he shows the Standards to be deeply **catholic**, embedded in the broader Protestant and patristic tradition while expressing distinctively Reformed commitments. Particularly notable is his defense of the Standards’ **covenant of works**, their affirmation of **Sabbath** as a creational-eschatological ordinance, and their account of **Scripture’s sufficiency** and **clarity**.

This work functions as a hermeneutical guide for confessional subscription and ecclesial debates. By tracing the **architectonic** coherence of the Standards across **doctrine of God**, **Christology**, **soteriology**, and **worship**, Fesko counsels against piecemeal prooftexting or importing alien metaphysics. The result is a portrait of a **Reformed catholicity** at home with **creedal orthodoxy** and with **scholastic** distinctions. For contemporary ministers and theologians, the implication is that fidelity to Westminster entails inhabiting its metaphysical and covenantal grammar, not merely echoing its language.

#### 6. The covenants ad intra and ad extra: pactum salutis and covenant of works
Fesko’s historical monographs **The Covenant of Redemption: Origins, Development, and Reception** 2016 and **The Covenant of Works: The Origins, Development, and Reception of the Doctrine** 2020 narrate the emergence and refinement of these contested doctrines. Against claims that the **pactum salutis** is speculative intrusion, he shows its deep roots in **early modern Reformed** reflection on **eternal generation**, **Trinitarian missions**, and the economy of salvation. The **Father** appoints the **Son** as mediator and the **Spirit** as anointer in an eternal counsel that grounds the historical **covenant of grace**. In similar fashion, the **covenant of works** appears not as a foil to grace but as the wise order of creation’s eschatological design, repeatedly affirmed in catechisms and systems across the seventeenth century.

Doctrinally, these studies enable Fesko to integrate **Trinitarian theology** and **federal theology**. He guards **inseparable operations** while articulating fittingness relations between **processions** and **missions**, such that the **Son’s** mediatorial obedience is coherent with **autotheos** and **divine simplicity**. Likewise, the **law-gospel** distinction is not opposed to **union with Christ**, but provides the federal logic by which the last Adam’s obedience is legally and covenantally reckoned to believers. The net effect is a defense of classic **Reformed orthodoxy** on contested terrain in a way that fosters doctrinal synthesis rather than prooftext skirmishes.

#### 7. Natural theology and apologetics: reforming the reformers
In **Reforming Apologetics: Retrieving the Classical Reformed Approach to Defending the Faith** 2019, Fesko turns to the nexus of **epistemology**, **natural theology**, and **defense of the faith**. He argues that the **Reformed orthodox** embraced a restrained but real use of **theistic proofs**, **natural law**, and **common notions** grounded in **general revelation** and the **imago Dei**. He contends that **Cornelius Van Til** and some of his heirs, such as **Greg Bahnsen** and **John Frame**, refashioned the Reformed tradition by rejecting or reshaping the **proofs** and by overhauling the **principia** doctrine. While affirming the **noetic effects of sin** and the necessity of **Scripture**, Fesko maintains that non-Christians know God truly though suppressively, that **Romans 1** and **Belgic Confession 2** authorize arguments from creation, and that **apologetics** should deploy **cosmological** and **teleological** reasoning as **ministerial** rather than **magisterial** tools.

His **The Need for Natural Theology** 2023 furthers this case by retrieving **Aquinas** with **Turretin** as a model of catholic and Reformed cooperation, qualifying the use of **analogia entis** in terms consistent with Reformed commitments to the **Creator-creature distinction**. He emphasizes the **archetypal-ectypal** distinction in theological knowledge and urges that **classical theism** supplies the metaphysical stability without which doctrines like **providence**, **simplicity**, and **immutability** lose coherence. The burden is not to enthrone reason but to recover the Reformed conviction that **grace restores nature**, and that a church allergic to **natural theology** will be vulnerable both to fideism and to cultural disintegration.

#### 8. Doctrine of God and classical theism
Across his corpus Fesko consistently affirms **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility**, grounding them exegetically and historically in **Scripture**, **Aquinas**, and the **Reformed orthodox**. He shows that early modern theologians such as **Polanus** and **Turretin** maintained robust **classical theism** while articulating rich **covenant** and **Christology**, thereby rejecting the canard that classical attributes stifle biblical dynamism. On the Trinity he emphasizes **eternal generation** and **procession**, the unity of **essence** with distinct **persons**, and the coordination of **missions** with **processions** in salvation history. He links the **pactum salutis** to the ad extra work of the Trinity without collapsing into speculative **subordinationism**, guarding **inseparable operations** and the doctrine of **autotheos** for the Son, a locus developed in early **Reformed** reflection on **Calvin’s autotheos**.

This doctrinal posture underwrites his soteriology and ecclesiology: only a **simple, immutable God** can ground the covenants that secure assurance; only a God of perfect **aseity** can condescend in free **grace**. Moreover, **classical theism** is not an optional add-on but the metaphysical chassis built into the **Westminster Standards** and the catechetical tradition. By tying attribute language to **covenant** and **federal** categories, Fesko makes classical metaphysics pastorally fruitful and biblically resonant.

#### 9. Pastoral posture, catholic instincts, and contested engagements
Though resolutely academic, Fesko’s work is never far from **pastoral** application. Works like **Songs of a Suffering King** 2014 and accessible treatments of **holiness** and **sanctification** display a concern that **doctrine** shape devotion. His writing pace and breadth reflect a conviction that the **church’s confession** requires constant historical catechesis: learn **Aquinas** with **Turretin** and **Calvin**, read the **Westminster Standards** in their own voice, and use **scholastic** distinctions to guard gospel clarity. He is a catholic Reformed theologian who can quote **Athanasius** and **Anselm**, retrieve **Ames** and **Vermigli**, and still preach **Romans** with evangelistic urgency.

Inevitably, the sharpest edges of his corpus cut against influential twentieth-century Reformed voices. His critiques of **Van Til**, **Frame**, and **Bahnsen** on **apologetics** challenge institutional habits at **Westminster**-related schools; his defense of the **covenant of works** and **imputation** confronts **Federal Vision** and **New Perspective** tendencies; his insistence on **classical theism** resists modern revisions of **impassibility**. Yet even his polemics aim at patristic and Reformed **catholicity**, summoning the church back to the grammar that generated its confessions.

#### 10. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
On balance, Fesko’s theological project offers a compelling retrieval of **Reformed scholasticism** in service to **confessional orthodoxy**. His accounts of **imputation**, **union with Christ**, **covenant of works**, and **pactum salutis** stabilize soteriology at precisely the points most buffeted by recent trends. His historical work on the **Westminster Standards** corrects anachronisms and supplies pastors with a coherent **architectonic**. His defense of **classical theism** accords with catholic consensus and undergirds doctrine across loci. The most contested front is his program for **ministerial natural theology** and reform of **apologetics**. From a Westminster Theological Seminary vantage point, one may judge that he occasionally underreads the **antithesis** and the prophetic edge of **presuppositionalism**, or deploys **Aquinas** in ways that risk flattening the redemptive-historical claims of **special revelation**. Yet the corrective he offers to Reformed amnesia about **general revelation**, **natural law**, and **theistic proofs** is salutary. A prudent synthesis would preserve Van Til’s insistence on **covenant epistemology** and **Scripture** as ultimate norm while embracing the confessional place of **general revelation**, the **proofs** as subordinate **confirmatio**, and the classical grammar of **simplicity** and **aseity**. In principle, the tensions are reconcilable within **classical Reformed orthodoxy** by distinguishing principial authority from ministerial reasoning, affirming that grace does not destroy but heals **nature**, and locating apologetic method within the broader **covenantal** economy of **revelation**.
