### TITLE
Practical divinity for a holy commonwealth: pastoral reformation, catholic concord, and an evangelical new law.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Seventeenth-century English Puritanism**, Civil War to Restoration, shaped by **Parliamentary chaplaincy**, **Savoy Conference 1661**, and **1662 ejection**; a moderate yet combative voice amid **post-Reformation scholastic orthodoxy**.
- **Method of practical divinity and casuistry**, Scripture-ruled but deploying **reason, natural law**, and **scholastic organization**; anti-sectarian **catholicity** with a **Ramist** penchant for aphorisms.
- **Key works**: **Aphorismes of Justification** 1649; **The Saints' Everlasting Rest** 1650; **The Reformed Pastor** 1656; **A Call to the Unconverted** 1658; **A Holy Commonwealth** 1659; **A Christian Directory** 1673; **Catholick Theologie** 1675; **Treatise of Justifying Righteousness** 1676; **Methodus Theologiae Christianae** 1681.
- **Central categories**: **lex nova** and **covenant of grace**, **governmental satisfaction and acceptilation**, **universal redemption**, **duplex iustificatio**, **casuistry of conscience**, **mere Christianity**.
- **Interlocutors** and influences: **William Ames**, **Perkins**, **Ussher**, **Davenant**; critical engagement with **Hugo Grotius**, **Moise Amyraut**; contested by **John Owen** and **Thomas Tully**; cooperative and adversarial ties to **Presbyterians** and **Anglicans**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: Pastoral genius and **practical divinity** warmly received; soteriological distinctives on **atonement**, **imputation**, and **law-gospel** judged problematic by **Westminster** norms.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, historical scene, and method
Richard Baxter, born in 1615 and deceased in 1691, stands within the late **Reformation and Puritan** settlement as a pastor-theologian whose life spanned the **Civil War**, **Interregnum**, **Restoration**, and the trials of **Nonconformity**. Without the laurels of Oxbridge but with the relentless autodidact’s appetite, he absorbed **William Ames’ Medulla**, **Perkins’ practical divinity**, and the English **Reformed scholastic** tradition, while retaining a marked independence. His ministry at **Kidderminster** from 1641, punctuated by service as a **Parliamentary chaplain** to the New Model Army in 1645–1647, cultivated a method that married **biblical exegesis**, **moral casuistry**, and **pastoral oversight**. He conceptualized theology as **sapientia practica**, wisdom oriented to life, and structured his arguments through aphorisms and thesis-antithesis in a **Ramist** idiom. Theologically, he privileged the clarity of **Scripture**, the universality of **natural law**, and the disciplined use of **reason** as a ministerial instrumentum, all in service to **conscience-reformation** and ecclesial peace.

Baxter’s epistemic stance blended **biblicist confidence** with scholastic precision and apologetic probability. Works like **The Reasons of the Christian Religion** 1667 and later **Methodus Theologiae Christianae** 1681 provide a map of faith’s rationality, defending Christianity through **teleological arguments**, **prudential certainty**, and a hierarchy of doctrinal essentials ordered toward **catholic concord**. He trafficked freely in the categories of **ordo salutis** and **foedus gratiae**, yet he did so to adjudicate cases of conscience rather than to construct a closed metaphysical system. His controlling passion was the **cura animarum**, and his method subordinates speculative tidiness to **pastoral efficacy**, a hallmark of **Amesian practical theology** radicalized for the parochial parish.

#### 2. Pastoral reformation and the casuistry of holiness
The youthful pastor of **Kidderminster** forged his signature program in **The Reformed Pastor** 1656, a manifesto of **oversight, catechesis, and discipline**. The work insists that the minister’s first reform is himself, pressing the practice of **house-to-house catechizing**, personal **conscience-examination**, and ordered **church discipline**. Baxter advocated for the **classical association** of ministers in county-wide collaborations, aiming at a doctrinal and practical **Agreement of Associated Pastors** that would bind parishes into reform. The parish became a school of **repentance and faith**, aided by catechetical tools, weekly exposition, and the supervision of family worship. His explicit use of **casuistry** in pastoral visitation fired the later compendium **A Christian Directory** 1673, where the granular governance of conscience is ordered under **natural law**, **Decalogue ethics**, and the **covenant of grace**.

In this pastoral optic, the key doctrine is **holiness as gospel obedience**, not a bare forensic rubric but a lived participation in the church’s regimented asceticism. The Directory’s massive treatment of **marriage, vocation, wealth, politics, and suffering** illustrates a theology where **providence**, **virtue**, and **mortification** are interwoven. Unapologetically **moral**, Baxter’s casuistry presumes that grace repairs nature and restores the reflex of **practical reason**, enabling believers to calibrate actions by the supreme end of **God’s glory** and the proximate **peace of the church**.

#### 3. Consolation and summons: eschatology turned to practice
The devotional classic **The Saints’ Everlasting Rest** 1650 shows Baxter’s eschatological pastoralism. Composed amid illness and uncertainty, it transposes the doctrine of **beatific rest** into a program of **meditation, self-examination, and perseverance**. The end of the Christian life, the **aeternum sabbatum**, feeds back into the present as motive for **evangelical obedience**. This teleological structure pervades his evangelistic tract **A Call to the Unconverted** 1658, where urgency springs from the reality of **judgment** balanced by the universal sufficiency of **Christ’s ransom**. Here Baxter the preacher and Baxter the moral theologian converge: the rhetoric of the last things functions as an instrument to awaken **conscience**, align **affections**, and secure the **ends** appropriate to the covenant.

Works of spiritual direction interact with doctrine but are not mere appendices. They manifest a conviction that **assurance** is ordinarily mediated through **obedient faith** and **holy living**, not through abstract introspective syllogisms. Yet Baxter rejects both **legalism** and **antinomianism** as eccentricities. The practical aim is a church catechized into the **evangelical yoke**, freed from the licentiousness he associated with some readings of **sola fide**, but also shielded from servile **merit** by a consistently covenantal understanding of reward.

#### 4. Justification reconfigured: lex nova, faith, and imputation
Baxter’s most controverted legacy lies in his reconfiguration of **justification** in **Aphorismes of Justification** 1649, **Of the Imputation of Christ’s Righteousness** 1675, and **Treatise of Justifying Righteousness** 1676. He affirms that the **covenant of grace** functions as a **lex nova**, a new law enacted by Christ the Mediator-king, which promises **pardon and right to life** to all who perform the covenant’s **evangelical conditions**. For Baxter, **faith** is not a passive conduit but a complex, fiducial and obedient **conditio** of the new law, essentially a **consent to the covenant** that inherently includes the will’s submission to Christ as Lord. He thus insists on **initial justification by faith** and a **continued or final justification** in which persevering, sincere **evangelical obedience** is conditionally requisite.

On **imputation**, Baxter rejects the imputation of Christ’s **active obedience** understood as a transfer of personal law-keeping to the believer’s legal account. He prefers to speak of the **imputation of Christ himself** and of the **effects of his satisfaction**, namely **pardon** and **title to life** granted by the covenant. He invokes **acceptilation**, the idea that God accepts Christ’s satisfaction not as quid pro quo equivalent, but as the rector’s wise provision that permits the **relaxation of the law** unto gracious terms. **Faith**, on this account, does not merit but is the **instrumental condition** appointed by the Legislator, such that the **iustitia evangelica** differs from both crude **merit** and a purely **forensic imputation**. Critics labeled this **neonomian**, yet Baxter understood it as guarding both **holiness** and **grace** by locating righteousness within the **mediatorial covenant** rather than a commercial exchange.

#### 5. Atonement, universal redemption, and rectoral justice
Baxter’s soteriology also pivots on **universal redemption** and a **governmental** construal of atonement. Influenced by **John Davenant’s hypothetical universalism** and engaging the juridical insights of **Hugo Grotius**, he held that Christ’s death was **sufficient for all** and **conditionally intended** for all, yet **efficient** only for believers who meet the covenant’s conditions by God’s grace. The cross satisfied God’s **rectoral justice**, upholding the moral governance of the world while authorizing the relaxed terms of the **lex nova**. Baxter opposed what he took to be the **mercantile model** of strict pecuniary substitution in some **high Calvinists**, contending instead that divine **wisdom and sovereignty** accept Christ’s oblation as a ground to forgive without compromising the credibility of **law and sanctions**.

This position drew the rebuttal of **John Owen**, who in works like **The Death of Death** 1647 and **The Doctrine of Justification by Faith** 1677 rejected both **universal redemption** and any account of atonement that compromised **penal substitution**. **Thomas Tully** in **Justificatio Paulina** 1674 likewise charged Baxter with deserting **Pauline justification**. Baxter replied that his acceptilatory and covenantal language preserved the **penal and substitutionary** dimensions while avoiding speculative transference of the **Mediator’s personal obedience**. The key for him was to secure a gospel that could be preached to all with integrity while maintaining the **integrity of law** and the call to **holiness**.

#### 6. Covenant, law and gospel, and perseverance
Within the federal schema, Baxter reinterprets the **covenant of grace** as a definitive **new law** with milder conditions, distinct from yet conceptually continuous with the **covenant of works**. He accepts **original sin** and the ruin of Adam’s race but resists some accounts of the **imputation of Adam’s sin** that, in his mind, threatened moral agency. The gospel for Baxter is not sheer declaration but a **royal charter** that summons faith as **obedient consent**. Hence his frequent appeal to **James 2** as harmonized with **Romans 3–4**, producing the language of **duplex iustificatio**: an **initial justification** through faith alone as condition, and an **adjudicative vindication** at the last day in which **evangelical works** are criteriological. He insists this second aspect neither merits nor adds to the ground of acceptance, but displays the **truth of faith** under the regime of the new covenant.

On **perseverance**, Baxter exhorts believers to fear **apostasy** and to labor for **assurance** through diligent obedience, while affirming the **faithfulness of God** to his covenant promises. The accent on **conditionality** and **sincerity** reveals his driving pastoral aim: to create no shelter for **antinomian security**, even at the cost of revisions to common **Reformed** formulations. The result is a tighter braid between **law and gospel**, in which gospel promise always bears the imperative stamp of **lex evangelica**.

#### 7. Ecclesiology, liturgy, and politics of concord
Baxter’s dream of **catholic concord** guided his ecclesial and political labors. As a leading voice at the **Savoy Conference** in 1661 he offered a **Reformed Liturgy** designed to render **Episcopal** worship acceptable to **Presbyterian conscience** by excising ceremonies and permitting lawful liberty. His **Catholick Theologie** 1675 and **The Cure of Church Divisions** 1670 argue for a platform of **mere Christianity**, distinguishing **fundamentals** from disputables to permit a broad **Protestant communion**. He was ejected under the **Act of Uniformity** 1662 and suffered harassment and an infamous trial before **Judge Jeffreys** in 1685, yet he persisted in urging **comprehension** rather than separation.

Politically, **A Holy Commonwealth** 1659 defended a **Christian commonwealth** steered by **natural law** and ordered to the **public good**, a work he later moderated amid Restoration sensitivities. His political theology is neither Erastian nor separatist but covenantal and prudential, seeking a constitutional settlement where **magistracy** secures the **external peace of religion** without usurping **pastoral jurisdiction**. He could countenance **moderate episcopacy** as a presidency of pastors, provided that the **jure divino** claims of diocesan prelacy were surrendered to the consensus of **reformed catholicity**.

#### 8. System, directory, and apologetic: the architecture of practical theology
The sprawling **A Christian Directory** 1673 is Baxter’s summa of **casuistry**, organizing Christian life under heads of **belief, worship, ethics, and polity**. It integrates **Decalogue ethics** with **gospel motives**, applying **natural law** and **Scripture** to cases ranging from **contracts** and **oaths** to **melancholy** and **spiritual desertion**. The Directory’s power lies in its fusion of **conscience-therapy** with doctrinal clarity; it models a **moral theology** recognizable across Catholic and Protestant divides while relentlessly **Reformed** in its doctrinal frame.

In **Methodus Theologiae Christianae** 1681 Baxter offers a didactic order for theology, not a speculative summa. He privileges **Scripture’s sufficiency**, employs **scholastic distinctions** to avoid confusions, and embraces **probabilistic reasoning** in matters not strictly revealed, so as to govern **prudential judgment**. His **apologetic** in **The Reasons of the Christian Religion** 1667 seeks to demonstrate Christianity’s **credibility** by convergent arguments from **prophecy, miracles, and moral transformation**, suited to persuade conscience rather than compel assent. This is a **pastoral rationalism**, disciplined by Scripture and the **ends** of edification, calm in tone yet steely in its contest with **skepticism** and **sectarianism** alike.

#### 9. Controversy, reception, and legacy
Contemporaries both admired and assailed Baxter. **John Owen** targeted his **universal redemption** and **justification**; **Thomas Tully** charged him with subverting **Pauline doctrine**; later English **Neonomian controversies** involved appeals to or repudiations of Baxter’s formulations. Yet his **pastoral genius** was acknowledged across parties, while **A Call to the Unconverted** and **The Saints’ Everlasting Rest** nourished **piety** across centuries. Posthumously, **Reliquiae Baxterianae** 1696 disclosed a life consumed by the **peace of the church** and the **purity of worship**.

Baxterian accents traveled widely. Eighteenth-century debates in English **Presbyterianism** and **Congregationalism** about **conditions of the covenant** and the role of **obedience** in salvation often invoked his authority. Evangelicals from **John Wesley** to **Andrew Fuller** read him with profit, the former savoring his **practical divinity**, the latter sifting his **soteriology** while adopting his **pastoral method**. Across the Atlantic, strands of **New England Theology** and later **moral government** theories bear distant kinship to his **rectoral justice** account, even where significant recalibrations occurred. The label **Baxterianism** thus came to signify both a **practical Anglican-Puritan spirituality** and a set of **soteriological mediations** that unsettled confessional lines.

#### 10. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a confessional **Reformed** vantage aligned with **Westminster** theology, Baxter’s legacy is a composite of profound pastoral **strengths** and real doctrinal **tensions**. Positively, his **practical divinity**, **conscience-casuistry**, and parish program in **The Reformed Pastor** exemplify the law’s third use and the catholic ambition of **edification**, modeling a ministry of **catechesis**, **discipline**, and **holiness** that Westminster pastors can own. His apologetic and ecumenical insistence on **essentials and integrity** remains salutary. Yet his soteriology strains core **Reformed** commitments. The **lex nova** construction blurs the **law-gospel distinction**, reshaping the **covenant of grace** into a milder legal regime and thereby relocating the locus of **assurance** from Christ’s finished work to the believer’s **sincere obedience**. His **governmental atonement** and **acceptilation** risk attenuating **penal substitution’s** strict satisfaction and undermine the confessional union of **particular redemption** and effectual calling. The refusal of the **imputation of Christ’s active obedience** truncates the **fullness of Christ’s righteousness** received by faith, and the language of **duplex iustificatio** clouds the final judgment’s declarative character as evidential rather than conditional causality. Reformed alternatives retain Baxter’s pastoral ends while securing them in a more robustly **monergistic** and **federal** frame: **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** distinguished yet harmonized; **justification sola fide** with the **imputation of Christ’s whole obedience**; **particular redemption** grounding effectual application; and **sanctification** as the necessary fruit, not condition, of **justifying grace**. On such a reading, Baxter’s best gifts are received, his doctrinal repairs supplied, and his aim of a **holy commonwealth** pursued under the **law of Christ** as promise-fulfillment rather than **new law**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: English **Puritan** pastor-theologian, 1615–1691, spanning **Civil War**, **Interregnum**, **Restoration**, **Savoy Conference 1661**, and **1662 ejection**.
- **Core focus**: **Practical divinity and casuistry** for parish reformation, governed by **Scripture**, **reason**, and **natural law**, aimed at **conscience** and **catholic concord**.
- **Major works**: **Aphorismes of Justification** 1649; **The Saints’ Everlasting Rest** 1650; **The Reformed Pastor** 1656; **A Call to the Unconverted** 1658; **A Holy Commonwealth** 1659; **A Christian Directory** 1673; **Catholick Theologie** 1675; **Treatise of Justifying Righteousness** 1676; **Methodus Theologiae** 1681.
- **Distinctives**: **Lex nova** account of the **covenant of grace**; **governmental satisfaction** with **acceptilation**; **universal redemption**; **duplex iustificatio**; **imputation** focused on Christ and covenant benefits rather than **active obedience**.
- **Interlocutors**: Shaped by **Ames**, **Perkins**, **Ussher**, **Davenant**; engaged **Grotius** and **Amyraut**; opposed by **John Owen** and **Thomas Tully** on **atonement** and **justification**.
- **Reformed critique**: Praises **pastoral method** and **practical divinity**; faults **new law**, **universal redemption**, denial of **imputed active obedience**, and blurred **law-gospel**; prefers **Westminster** penal substitution and **particular redemption** with **sola fide**.
- **Legacy**: Enduring influence on **Nonconformist piety**, trans-denominational **spirituality**, and later debates over **neonomianism** and **moral government**; read appreciatively but critically within **confessional Reformed** circles.
