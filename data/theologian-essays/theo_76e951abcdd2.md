### TITLE
Reframing Paul through Philippians: Participation, Citizenship, and Early High Christology in Evangelical Perspective

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Contemporary **evangelical** setting shaped by the post-1970s renewal of **Pauline studies**, the **New Perspective** and **apocalyptic** debates, and the retrieval of **Second Temple** and **Greco-Roman** contexts.
- Method marked by **historical-grammatical exegesis** integrated with **narrative-historical** and **socio-rhetorical** analysis, a high view of **Scripture’s authority**, and attention to **intertextual echoes**.
- Key works: **Reading Philippians** (2017); selected essays on **participation** and **divine-human agency** in Paul, e.g., JSNT 2011; Tyndale Bulletin 2015; JSPHL 2019.
- Central categories: **participation in Christ**, **cruciformity**, **citizenship in heaven**, **Christological monotheism**, **divine and human agency**, **Spirit-empowered sanctification**.
- Main interlocutors and influences: **Gordon Fee** on Philippians, **Richard Hays** on intertextuality, **N. T. Wright** on narrative eschatology, **Larry Hurtado** and **Richard Bauckham** on **early high Christology**, **Michael Gorman** on **cruciformity**.
- Reformed cue: natural points of contact with **union with Christ** and **redemptive-historical** reading; tensions around **monergism vs. synergism** and the balance of **forensic justification** with **participatory sanctification**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and evangelical location
Christopher Zoccali emerges within the contemporary **evangelical** renewal of **Pauline theology**, a terrain defined by the ferment of the **New Perspective on Paul**, the **apocalyptic Paul** movement, and the retrieval of **Second Temple Jewish** and **Greco-Roman** settings. His work is most closely identified with **Philippians**, where themes of **participation in Christ**, **cruciformity**, and **citizenship in heaven** converge with practical ecclesial concerns. Formationally, his intellectual debts to figures such as **Gordon Fee** on Philippians, **Richard Hays** on **intertextual echoes**, and **N. T. Wright** on **narrative eschatology** are evident, even as he appropriates **Larry Hurtado** and **Richard Bauckham** on **early high Christology** and **Michael Gorman** on **cruciform** discipleship. The result is a distinctively evangelical synthesis, anchored in **Scripture’s authority** and attentive to historical context, yet framed by ecclesial questions about **spiritual formation**, **witness**, and **unity**.

Methodologically, Zoccali positions himself within the **historical-grammatical** tradition while expanding its scope to include **socio-rhetorical** analysis, **lexical semantics**, and **discourse** study. He reads Paul’s letters as occasional communications embedded in concrete **rhetorical** and **political** settings, with **Philippi** as a Roman colony whose **honor-shame** dynamics and imperial loyalties shape how **citizenship in heaven** is heard. His engagement with **Second Temple** sources calibrates Jewish monotheism and Scripture’s intra-textual dynamics, while Greco-Roman materials illumine imperial claims challenged by the confession **Jesus is Lord**. These commitments yield a hermeneutic that is at once historically responsible and canonically alert, drawing threads across the Pauline corpus while resisting systematic flattening.

#### 2. Works and the Philippians center
Zoccali’s signature contribution is his close engagement with **Philippians**, crystallized in the accessible yet theologically rich volume **Reading Philippians** (2017), alongside essays in venues such as **Journal for the Study of the New Testament** and **Tyndale Bulletin** devoted to **participation**, **divine and human agency**, and **citizenship**. He treats Philippians as a paradigmatic instantiation of Paul’s **gospel of partnership** and **cruciform joy** rather than a marginal occasional letter. The letter’s **Carmen Christi** in 2.6–11 functions as a hermeneutical keystone that binds **Christology**, **soteriology**, and **ethics**. Chapters 1 and 3 supply the social and eschatological frame: **koinonia in the gospel**, **suffering with Christ**, pursuit of **resurrection** as knowing the **power of his resurrection and the fellowship of his sufferings**.

In this emphasis Zoccali stands in critical continuity with **Gordon Fee** on the **Spirit** and **Christology** in Philippians and with **Michael Gorman** on **cruciformity**, yet he seeks to accent the political and communal implications of **citizenship** language. The colony’s civic pride informs Paul’s imperative, Only live as citizens worthy of the **gospel of Christ**, such that ecclesial unity, humility, and sacrificial love instantiate a counter-imperial **polis** under the **kyrios**. Here Zoccali resists reduction to either private piety or overt **empire-critical** polemic, instead reading **citizenship in heaven** as the church’s public ethos grounded in **resurrection hope** and animated by the **Spirit**.

#### 3. Christological monotheism and the Carmen Christi
At the center of Zoccali’s Paul stands a robust **Christological monotheism**, aligned with the stream advanced by **Larry Hurtado** and **Richard Bauckham**. The confession **Jesus is Lord** and the reformulation of the **Shema** in 1 Cor 8.6, together with the exaltation hymn of **Phil 2.6–11**, signal an early **high Christology** where **kyrios** language and doxological practice include Jesus within the unique divine identity. Zoccali reads **kenosis** as non-competitive divine self-giving: the Son, preexistent in the form of God, empties himself not by subtraction of **divine attributes** but by the **assumption of servanthood** and obedient death, culminating in exaltation and the universal confession to the glory of **God the Father**. This reading integrates **Adam** and **Wisdom** motifs while refusing a merely comparative Adam-Christ typology.

Zoccali’s exegesis stresses the hymn’s ethical force within a **participatory** frame. The **mind of Christ** is not merely exemplary but communicative, shared through union with the **Messiah** by the **Spirit**. The hymn’s arc from preexistence to exaltation grounds the church’s **humility**, **unity**, and **service**, while the cosmic scope of the **name above every name** relativizes imperial claims. In this way, **Christology**, **ecclesiology**, and **mission** are bound together: the church confesses and embodies the Lordship of Christ through **cruciform** practices and **joyful** witness in a hostile world.

#### 4. Participation, agency, and sanctification
Zoccali’s account of **soteriology** is coordinated by the Pauline category of **participation in Christ**. For him, union with Christ is both ontological solidarity and covenantal incorporation, effected by the **Spirit** and mediated through **baptism**, **confession**, and ongoing **koinonia**. He reads Phil 3.10–11 as paradigmatic: knowing Christ, the power of his resurrection, and the fellowship of his sufferings shapes a community whose **ethical** life is cruciform and whose **hope** is resurrectional. The logic is not antithetical to judicial or forensic language but foregrounds transformative participation that issues in obedience.

Within this frame Zoccali devotes special energy to the problem of **divine and human agency** in Paul. He foregrounds the Pauline paradox in Phil 2.12–13, work out your salvation, for God is the one working in you. He construes this as a non-competitive synergy where **grace** creates and sustains human willing and acting. Divine agency is primary and pervasive, yet human agency is real and responsible, activated and upheld by the **Spirit**. This yields an account of **sanctification** that resists both quietism and voluntarism, situating the community’s practices of reconciliation, generosity, and perseverance within the empowering presence of God. The accent falls on ecclesial disciplines and missional partnership as the concrete form of participation.

#### 5. Ecclesiology, citizenship, and public witness
Zoccali’s **ecclesiology** draws deeply from the **citizenship** discourse of Philippians. The church is not merely an association of individuals but a **polis** under the **kyrios** whose **koinonia** is covenantal and missional: partnership in the **gospel** through prayer, financial support, suffering solidarity, and proclamation. The imperative πολιτεύεσθε signals a vocation of public coherence and unity in one **Spirit**, striving side by side for the **faith of the gospel**. This public dimension includes a cruciform posture toward opponents, endurance in suffering as a gift, and practices that enact the resurrection life amid imperial claims.

He locates **joy** as an eschatological disposition rather than a mere affect. Joy refracts the community’s **resurrection hope**, arising from the **Lord** who is near and the **peace of God** that guards hearts and minds. The ethical vision of Philippians 2 and 4 flows from **Christological** and **pneumatological** realities, not mere moralism. By reading Philippians as a template for public **witness**, Zoccali affords theology a pastoral telos: the doctrinal center of **Christ’s Lordship** yields communities whose practices are recognizably cruciform and whose public presence undermines idolatrous power through **self-giving love**.

#### 6. Scripture, intertextuality, and canonical coherence
Zoccali’s hermeneutic blends **historical-grammatical** rigor with **intertextual** sensitivity shaped by **Richard Hays**. He discerns echoes of **Isaiah** and **Psalms** in the hymn’s ascription of universal homage, and he positions Philippians within Paul’s wider **narrative of God’s faithfulness** to Israel now fulfilled in the **Messiah**. While resisting speculative systematization, he affirms **canonical coherence**, reading Philippians in concert with Galatians, Romans, and 1 Corinthians to illuminate how **union with Christ** integrates forensic, participatory, and transformative dimensions. The result is a layered account in which **apocalyptic invasion** and **salvation-history** are not rivals but complementary rhetorical registers describing the same drama of divine deliverance.

This integrative posture extends to **pneumatology**. Echoing **Gordon Fee**, he emphasizes the **Spirit** as the personal presence of God empowering **obedience**, catalyzing **unity**, and sealing **hope**. The Spirit mediates the **mind of Christ**, enabling communities to enact humility and mutual regard. In liturgical terms the Spirit shapes a doxological people whose prayers, hymns, and thanksgivings are material sites of participation. The canonical net thus catches doctrine, ethics, and worship together.

#### 7. Interlocutors, debates, and distinctives
Relative to the **New Perspective**, Zoccali shares the attention to **Second Temple Judaism** and corporate identity yet resists collapsing Paul’s soteriology into boundary maintenance; the accent on **participation** and **resurrection power** retains a universal soteriological horizon. With the **apocalyptic Paul**, he affirms the radicality of divine action, though he prefers to tether apocalyptic idiom to **narrative-historical** exegesis so that **new creation** is God’s faithful fulfillment rather than an arbitrary interruption. He appreciates **John Barclay’s** reframing of **grace** as incongruous yet distributes it ecclesially through **koinonia** and agency rather than restricting it to individual interiority.

Against one-sided **empire-critical** readings, Zoccali’s **citizenship** emphasis renders political significance without anachronistic overlay. He leverages **Greco-Roman** civic language to show how the church’s **politeia** subverts imperial boasts by confessing **Jesus as Lord** and enacting cruciform unity. He is cautious about totalizing sociological explanations, arguing that **Christological monotheism** and **Spirit-empowered** obedience constitute the church’s most potent political claim. This methodological discipline preserves doctrine’s priority while showing how doctrinal truths generate public practices.

#### 8. Doctrinal synthesis: Christ, the Spirit, and the church’s life
In synthesis, Zoccali’s Pauline theology is a coordinated triad: **Christology** as inclusion of Jesus within the divine identity; **pneumatology** as the personal agency of God effecting union and obedience; **ecclesiology** as a cruciform citizenry animated by **resurrection hope**. **Soteriology** is read through **participation in Christ**, cohering with yet not eclipsing forensic dimensions; **ethics** is cruciform and communal; **mission** is partnership in the **gospel**. The **Carmen Christi** grounds a theological anthropology: true human advancement is downward mobility in service patterned on the **obedient Son**, a posture only possible through the **Spirit** who works to will and to act.

Zoccali’s profile is thus resolutely evangelical: Scripture is **norming**, history is the matrix of meaning, and the church’s life is the theater of the **gospel’s** power. He writes with ecclesial ends in view, insisting that **exegesis** serve the formation of a people whose **citizenship** is elsewhere yet whose presence in the world is unmistakably public. Theological coherence arises not from abstract systems but from the **Messiah’s** story as retold and relived in the **ekklesia**.

#### 9. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Zoccali’s work aligns with several central commitments. His emphases on **union with Christ**, **Christ’s Lordship**, and **Spirit-empowered** obedience resonate with classic Reformed accounts of participation, sanctification, and the church’s pilgrim **polity** under the **Word**. His **Christological monotheism** coheres with **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** dogma, while his **kenosis** reading avoids any dilution of **divine simplicity** or ascribing mutability to the divine nature. The redemptive-historical breadth of his **narrative** approach dovetails with a **covenantal** framework that sees Scripture as one unfolding drama centered in the **Mediator**.

Tensions arise where Zoccali’s synergy of **divine and human agency** risks softening **monergism** in effectual calling and initial regeneration. While his reading of Phil 2.12–13 stresses divine primacy, a Reformed examiner will press that the grammar of grace remains unilateral in **calling** and logically prior in **sanctification**, with human agency as derivative and Spirit-enabled. Likewise, the participatory accent can obscure the forensic clarity of **justification by faith** with **imputed righteousness**, especially if ethical transformation is used as the primary index of salvation’s reality. A Reformed alternative would articulate a tighter coordination of **union with Christ** in its duplex grace, distinguishing yet inseparably joining **justification** and **sanctification**. Finally, while Zoccali’s **citizenship** model helpfully guards against privatization, care is needed to frame political claims within a **two-kingdoms** prudence or a **Christ’s kingship** over all creation that refuses ecclesial capture by ideologies. Properly integrated, his contributions can be received as salutary correctives that invite Reformed theology to press deeper into Pauline **participation** without ceding **forensic** and **covenantal** anchors.
