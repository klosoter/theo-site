### TITLE
Sin as the vandalism of shalom, a Reformed retrieval for culture, worship, and vocation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth to early twenty-first century**, **Christian Reformed Church**, **Calvin College and Calvin Theological Seminary**, North American **neo-Calvinist** public theology.
- **Augustinian retrieval** and **Kuyperian cultural theology**, using **thick description**, **biblical narrative** of **creation-fall-redemption-new creation**, and **interdisciplinary wisdom** grounded in **Scripture**.
- **Not the Way It is Supposed to Be** 1995, **Engaging God’s World** 2002, **Discerning the Spirits** 2003, **Reading for Preaching** 2013, **Morning and Evening Prayers** 2021, **Under the Wings of God** 2023.
- **Shalom**, **hamartiology**, **common grace**, **vocation**, **addiction and self-deception**, **structural evil**, **kingdom of God**, **creation’s goodness**.
- **Augustine**, **Calvin**, **Abraham Kuyper**, **Nicholas Wolterstorff**, **Alvin Plantinga**, **Reinhold Niebuhr**, with conversation partners in **literature** and **ethics**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Cornelius Plantinga Jr. emerges within the **Christian Reformed Church** and the **neo-Calvinist** institutions of **Calvin College** and **Calvin Theological Seminary**, inhabiting the late twentieth century moment when **evangelical** theology sought cultural breadth without doctrinal dilution. Serving as **Dean of the Chapel at Calvin College** from 1996 to 2001 and as **President of Calvin Theological Seminary** from 2002 to 2011, he exerted formative influence on a generation of pastors, scholars, and teachers. The governing aim of his corpus is a lucid account of **sin** and **grace** in a world God intends for **shalom**, a theme that links his early theological essays to his pastoral works on **worship**, **preaching**, and **devotion**. The intellectual horizon is Reformed and eclectic, an Augustinian **ordo amoris** joined to a Kuyperian vision of **common grace** and **cultural obedience**.

Methodologically Plantinga is an **Augustinian retriever** and a **Kuyperian cultural theologian** who prefers **thick description** and **narrative theology** to scholastic disputation. His prose unites biblical theology and literary illustration, guided by the four-chapter plot of **creation, fall, redemption, new creation**. He writes under the authority of **Scripture** while engaging **literature**, **psychology**, and **social analysis** as resources in the doctrine of **sin**. The resultat is a pastoral-systematic synthesis that interprets everyday life in the light of **biblical shalom** and that refuses to isolate **personal vice** from **structural injustice**. His method is epistemically sympathetic to **Reformed epistemology** associated with **Alvin Plantinga**, yet he deploys its confidence in properly basic Christian belief toward practical **moral and ecclesial wisdom**.

Plantinga’s conceptual nerve center is the term **shalom**, a biblically rooted portrait of flourishing, justice, and delight before the face of **God**. He follows **Nicholas Wolterstorff** in treating shalom as God’s design for the webbing together of God, humans, and all creation in justice and mutual delight. From this normative horizon he frames **hamartiology** not as abstract defect but as **culpable vandalism** of God’s shalom. The theological move is both traditional and fresh, **Augustinian** in blameworthy disorder, **Reformed** in covenantal depth, and **neo-Calvinist** in its cultural scope.

#### 2. Not the Way It is Supposed to Be, a Breviary of Sin 1995
The signature contribution of Plantinga’s career is **Not the Way It is Supposed to Be** 1995, a sustained **hamartiology** that rescues the doctrine of **sin** from banality, moralism, and reduction to mere psychological maladjustment. The book’s thesis is simple and arresting: **sin** is the **vandalism of shalom**, willful and systemic, personal and social, a negation of the goodness God has woven into creation. This reframing translates the biblical witness to **law**, **wisdom**, and **kingdom** into a thick moral vision. Sin is not only transgression but also **corruption**, **perversion**, and **pollution**, a spoiling agency that both incurs guilt and generates misery.

Plantinga catalogs the dynamics of **self-deception**, **addiction**, and **social complicity**, drawing from **Augustine** on the curved-in self, from **Reinhold Niebuhr** on pride and irony, and from a wide register of **literature** and **journalism** on the craftiness of evil. He treats the **seven deadly sins** tradition with pastoral acuity, showing how **acedia**, **envy**, **lust**, **gluttony**, **greed**, **anger**, and **pride** play as riffs on disordered love within a creation meant for **shalom**. He then widens the lens to **structural evil**, how **institutions**, **customs**, and **policies** acquire a life of sin beyond any single agent, while never dissolving **personal culpability**. The analytic gains are significant, for he preserves the **Augustinian** depth of complicity while speaking intelligibly to late modern readers alert to systemic injustice.

Exegetically **Not the Way It is Supposed to Be** works from **Genesis** to **Revelation**, showing how **creation’s goodness**, **covenantal law**, **prophetic justice**, **wisdom’s craft**, **Christ’s kingdom**, and **Spirit-wrought sanctification** cohere around God’s intent to restore **shalom**. The theological register is anchored in **Christ’s atoning work** and **resurrection**, though Plantinga refrains from adjudicating among atonement models, allowing **penal**, **sacrificial**, and **Christus Victor** motifs to converge in a narrative of restoration. The stress falls on **culpability** and **need for grace**, without sliding into mere therapeutic language. In this way the book offers a public account of moral reality that can converse with **secular ethics**, yet remains unmistakably **Reformed** in diagnosis and hope.

#### 3. Engaging God’s World 2002 and the Kuyperian horizon
With **Engaging God’s World** 2002, Plantinga articulates a **neo-Calvinist** vision of **vocation** and **learning** that complements his **hamartiology**. The grammar is again the Reformed narrative of **creation, fall, redemption, new creation**, in which **common grace** preserves and advances cultural life even as **special grace** renews persons and communities in **Christ**. He summons students and scholars to receive their callings as arenas of **kingdom service**, to love knowledge for God’s sake, and to resist the despair that follows from a reductive view of history. Here the doctrine of **shalom** widens into a charter for **education**, **arts**, **science**, and **public life** under the lordship of **Christ**.

The book’s pedagogical heart is an account of **longing** and **hope**. Drawing on **Augustine’s** **ordo amoris** and the Reformed **beatitudo** tradition, Plantinga treats human desire as teleologically oriented toward **God**, where **restless hearts** find their home. This teleology guards against both **utopianism**, which mistakes **provisional common grace** for consummation, and **quietism**, which neglects **care for the world** under the mandate of **creation stewardship**. The argument resonates with **Abraham Kuyper’s** insistence that every square inch belongs to **Christ**, yet it refuses triumphalism by embedding vocation within **cross-shaped discipleship**. The emphasis on **joy** and **delight** counters a moralistic posture, signaling that the aim of cultural obedience is communion and **praise**.

#### 4. Worship, preaching, and the moral imagination
Plantinga’s attention to **worship** and **preaching** is concentrated in **Discerning the Spirits** 2003 and in **Reading for Preaching** 2013. The former is a field guide for congregations evaluating **liturgical** and **musical** trends, testing them by **biblical** and **theological** criteria rooted in **God’s holiness** and **edification** of the body. The latter argues that preachers should cultivate a rich **moral imagination** by reading beyond theology, entering conversation with **storytellers**, **biographers**, **poets**, and **journalists**. He contends that preaching requires an eye for **grace** and **folly** in common life, which literature is uniquely suited to display, strengthening the preacher’s capacity to name **sin** and to commend **virtue** with texture and empathy.

This pastoral turn is not ancillary to his systematics, for it displays a conviction about **general revelation** and **culture**. Under **common grace** the world is strewn with lights by which Christians can recognize **truth**, **goodness**, and **beauty**. Reading intensifies perception, sharpening what **Proverbs** calls wisdom’s craft. Yet all such wisdom is normed by **Scripture**, gathered in **worship**, and oriented toward **Christ**. This interplay of sources mirrors the **Reformed** commitment to a principled **Scripture norm**, along with a generous appeal to the created order’s instructive power.

#### 5. Sin’s psychology, structurality, and the retrieval of virtue
A distinguishing feature of Plantinga’s **hamartiology** is the integration of **psychology** and **social theory** with **doctrinal** categories. On the one hand, he mines **addiction** studies to illumine the enslaving spiral of disordered desire, capturing the bond between **pleasure**, **denial**, **habit**, and **bondage**. On the other hand, he tracks **structural evil**, wherein **laws**, **markets**, and **media** conspire to habituate vice and to injure neighbors at scale. He refuses easy dichotomies between **personal sin** and **social sin**, insisting on the biblical simultaneity of **guilt** and **corruption** and on the need for both **repentance** and **reform**.

Complementing this is a retrieval of **virtue** language within a decidedly **Reformed** frame. Plantinga honors **Augustinian** and **Thomist** discourse on **virtues** as trained excellences of love, while maintaining the primacy of **grace** and the necessity of **regeneration** for any mature habitus. His treatments of **acedia** and **envy** are paradigmatic, showing how spiritual sloth resists **charity** and how envy corrodes **gratitude** for **God’s gifts**. The strategy is pedagogical, designed to cultivate **practical wisdom** in pastors and laypersons who must name and resist the subtle forms of sin in contemporary life.

#### 6. Devotion, prayer, and pastoral poise
Later works like **Beyond Doubt** 2002, **Morning and Evening Prayers** 2021, and **Under the Wings of God** 2023 extend Plantinga’s project into **devotional theology**. Here he distills **doctrinal truth** into **prayer** and **meditation**, showing how **confession**, **assurance**, and **praise** shape Christian perception of the world. The devotions are catechetical, reinforcing **creation’s goodness**, the ugliness of **sin**, the beauty of **Christ’s grace**, and the summons to **vocation**. This pastoral poise confirms that his systematics are ordered to **doxology**, that **shalom** is not a mere social program but a **God-centered** delight enacted in the church’s daily worship.

In both scholarly and devotional modes Plantinga exhibits the **Augustinian** sense that theology is learned on the way to **beatitudo**, that contemplation returns to serve the **neighbor** in ecclesial life. The style is catholic in taste, drawing from **patristic** and **Reformed** voices, and accessible without relinquishing **doctrinal clarity**. In a fragmented age, his voice models how **theological seriousness** coexists with pastoral tenderness.

#### 7. Systematic lines, without the schematic
While not a multi-volume dogmatician, Plantinga’s work sketches a coherent **systematic theology** by implication. His doctrine of **creation** asserts the intrinsic goodness of the world under **divine providence**, with **natural and cultural** mandates unfolded under **God’s blessing**. His **anthropology** is Augustinian and **Reformed**, marked by **imago Dei** dignity and by **total depravity** in the sense of pervasive corruption. His **hamartiology** is rich, as noted, attending to **guilt** and **pollution**. His **soteriology** centers on **Christ’s cross and resurrection**, with salvation construed as both **forgiveness** and **renewal**, and with sanctification aimed at **shalom** as holiness-in-communion. His **ecclesiology** prizes **worship**, **catechesis**, and **mission**, and his **eschatology** is **new creation**, the consummation of God’s shalom where **justice** and **joy** kiss.

Two accents mark this implied system. First, **common grace** is given sustained weight, safeguarding the possibility of **truth** and **goodness** among those outside explicit faith, and undergirding the call to culture making. Second, the **kingdom of God** is the frame for **sanctification** and **mission**, urging Christians to labor for **justice** and **mercy** as anticipations of the **age to come**. These accents allow Plantinga to engage **public ethics** and **education** without losing his center in **Christ** and **Scripture**.

#### 8. Interlocutors and debates
Plantinga converses fruitfully with **Augustine**, **Calvin**, and **Kuyper**, and in the modern scene with **Nicholas Wolterstorff**, **Alvin Plantinga**, and **Reinhold Niebuhr**. From **Augustine** he takes the architecture of **ordo amoris** and the grammar of **concupiscence**. From **Calvin** he draws the pastoral acuity of the **Institutes**, the centrality of **union with Christ**, and the realism about **sin** and **grace**. From **Kuyper** he receives the world-affirming energy of **sphere sovereignty** and the wideness of **Christ’s lordship**. **Wolterstorff** offers a philosophical-theological account of **justice** and **shalom**, while **Alvin Plantinga** legitimates Christian belief as **properly basic**, freeing theologians to argue from **Scripture** without epistemic embarrassment. With **Niebuhr**, Cornelius Plantinga shares a suspicion of optimistic humanism, though he supplies a richer doctrine of **creation** and a stronger hope in **new creation**.

Within evangelical debates on **sin** and **atonement**, Plantinga’s preference is to show rather than to litigate, to narrate **Christ’s saving victory** rather than to specify an exclusive atonement model. Readers from **Anabaptist**, **Wesleyan**, and **Reformed** traditions find entry points in his work, though his accent on **common grace** and **vocation** is distinctively **neo-Calvinist**. In conversations on **social justice**, he gives principled warrant for attending to **structural sin**, while refusing to collapse **personal repentance** into activism. His rhetorical strategy, mingling **biblical exegesis** with **literary testimony**, has broadened his audience without sacrificing **doctrinal substance**.

#### 9. Influence and reception
In the decades since 1995, **Not the Way It is Supposed to Be** has become a standard text in courses on **ethics**, **pastoral theology**, and **worldview** at colleges and seminaries shaped by **Reformed** and **evangelical** commitments. **Engaging God’s World** serves as an introduction to Christian learning in many **first-year** programs, offering a lean and lucid vision of the **Christian mind**. His works on **worship** and **preaching** have influenced homiletical pedagogy and congregational practice, especially in **CRC** and **RCA** contexts and beyond. The broader reception recognizes Plantinga as an interpreter of **sin** with unusual clarity, a theologian who can name **evil** without cynicism and commend **joy** without naivete.

The durability of his account of **shalom** rests in its biblical density and moral intelligibility. In a culture tempted by therapeutic reduction and punitive outrage alike, Plantinga articulates a grammar that sustains **confession**, **lament**, **reform**, and **hope**. The combination of **Augustinian realism** and **Kuyperian vocation** has made his work a bridge between **church** and **academy**, between **ethics** and **spirituality**, and between **doctrine** and **pastoral practice**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage, Plantinga’s project draws strong commendation and a few cautions. Commendation first. His **hamartiology** secures **total depravity** without Manichaean dualism, his appeal to **Scripture** as norm is steady, his esteem for **creation** under **providence** avoids Gnostic suspicion, and his emphasis on **common grace** and **vocation** extends **Calvin** and **Kuyper** faithfully. His treatment of **structural evil** aligns with **prophetic** concern for **justice**, while his insistence on **culpability** honors the **law-gospel** dialectic. His pastoral writings strengthen **worship**, **preaching**, and **piety**, anchoring cultural engagement in **doxology**.

The cautions are matters of proportion, not direction. First, the rubric of **shalom** risks softening the sharp forensic core of **justification by faith** and **federal headship** if it is not explicitly tethered to the **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace** grammar that secures the legal ground of peace. A Westminster alternative would press the **law-gospel** contrast, clarifying that the vandalism of shalom renders sinners liable under **divine law**, and that **penal substitution** is central to the restoration Plantinga celebrates. Second, the robust appeal to **common grace** requires a companion accent on **antithesis**, lest cultural analysis trade on shared moral perception without naming **idolatry’s** epistemic suppression of truth. Third, the generous use of **literature** as illustrative authority should be consistently subordinated to **Scripture** as principium, so that the canon shapes the moral imagination more decisively than the best of journalism. None of these concerns negate the achievement. They indicate how a Westminster-leaning reader might calibrate Plantinga’s **shalom** grammar by thickening **covenant**, sharpening **atonement**, and foregrounding **antithesis** alongside **common grace**. The result would retain his eloquent portrait of **sin** and **hope**, while setting it even more squarely within the bones of **Reformed orthodoxy**.
