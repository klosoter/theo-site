### TITLE
From industrial certainty to world risk: reflexive modernization and cosmopolitan responsibility

### PREVIEW NOTES

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Born in 1944 in war-torn Pomerania and formed intellectually in Munich under the shadow of divided Europe, **Ulrich Beck** emerged as a leading voice among late twentieth-century German social theorists whose primary object was the self-critique of modernity. Teaching at **University of Bamberg** and then **LMU Munich**, with appointments later at the **London School of Economics**, Beck received his public mandate at the moment when industrial progress exposed its shadow, above all in the **Chernobyl** disaster of 1986. This event gave concrete urgency to his thesis that modernity generates the **Risikogesellschaft risk society**, a condition in which manufactured hazards eclipse inherited dangers. Against both positivist certitude and postmodern resignation, Beck elaborated a **realist-constructivist method** that treats risks as socially constituted through knowledge, law, and institutions while insisting on their material reality and causal efficacy. His project is neither technocratic nor romantic; it is a historically indexed critique of modern **industrial rationality** and an invitation to a reflexive modernization that recognizes the unintended consequences of progress.

Beck frames his epistemic stance with two anchor distinctions central to the sociology of knowledge. First, following and revising **Niklas Luhmann**, he distinguishes **risk** from **danger**, locating risk on the side of decision and accountability and danger on the side of fate and contingency. Second, he theorizes **non-knowledge**, the structured ignorance that accompanies expertise and that is amplified by techno-scientific complexity. In this sense **manufactured uncertainty** becomes the signature of late modernity. His method pairs the critique of **methodological nationalism** with a proposed **methodological cosmopolitanism** that realigns social analysis with transboundary causality. The mode of agency that his method uncovers is what he calls **subpolitics**, the politics of laboratories, corporations, courts, NGOs, and consumers that operate beneath and beyond the nation-state.

#### 2. Risk Society as thesis and provocation
Published in 1986, **Risk Society** articulates Beck's canonical diagnosis. Modernization, having subdued famine and endemic disease, now generates systemic hazards such as nuclear fallout, chemical pollution, genetically modified organisms, and climate change. The motif of **boomerang effects** captures the way risks return to threaten their producers and beneficiaries. Institutional responses often create **organized irresponsibility**, a diffuse dispersal of accountability across corporations, regulatory agencies, and scientific bodies that function to produce plausible deniability. Traditional containers of identity and obligation become **zombie categories**; they are undead concepts such as class, family, and nation that no longer capture the dynamics of **individualization** and global interdependence yet continue to haunt public discourse. Beck calls this epoch the **Zweitmoderne**, the second modernity in which modernization turns upon itself. Here **reflexive modernization** does not mean enlightened self-transparency but a turbulent process in which modern institutions reconstruct themselves under the pressure of their own side effects.

The theological import of **Risk Society** appears in Beck's reconfiguration of temporality and normativity. Risk is future oriented, a projection of potential catastrophe into the present through probabilistic calculation and cultural imagination. This produces a secularized anticipation that functions analogously to **apocalyptic eschatology**, mobilizing action in view of threatened futures. Beck's concept of **manufactured uncertainty** also exposes how the modern promise of mastery is self-cancelling, directing attention to creaturely limits and to the ethical problem of distributed responsibility in complex systems. Risks are not weather, they are the byproduct of human dominion gone awry, and they demand a new **ethic of responsibility** appropriate to interdependence.

#### 3. Reflexivity, interlocutors, and the social theory of late modernity
In **Reflexive Modernization**, coauthored with **Anthony Giddens** and **Scott Lash** and published in 1994, Beck clarifies how reflexivity differentiates his perspective from both system-theoretical skepticism and communicative rationality. Against **Habermas**, who locates the emancipatory potential of modernity in **communicative action** and the public sphere, Beck argues that late modern hazards emerge precisely from rationalized systems and are amplified by the colonization of life-worlds by techno-economy. Yet he also resists the functionalist quietism of **Luhmann**, whose **systems theory** reduces risk to systemic contingencies without normative orientation. Beck's **reflexivity** is sociological and political, calling for self-critique within science, economics, and law, and for the democratization of decision structures through **subpolitics**.

Beck's dialogue with **Mary Douglas** on the cultural theory of risk is equally formative. Douglas emphasizes the symbolic and cultural frames through which communities perceive hazard, purity, and blame. Beck agrees that risks are socially framed but insists on their material novelty under late modern conditions. Against the charge of Eurocentrism raised by **Zygmunt Bauman** and **Bruno Latour**, Beck develops a more thoroughly global account in which hybrid networks defy national boundaries and where the politics of knowledge becomes inherently transnational. The through-line in these interlocutions is Beck's insistence that **zombie categories** must be retired and that the social sciences must adopt **methodological cosmopolitanism** to explain emerging forms of power and vulnerability.

#### 4. World Risk Society and the cosmopolitan turn
In **World Risk Society**, published in 1999, Beck escalates his diagnosis from national political economies to the global scale. Risks like climate change, financial contagion, pandemic disease, and terrorism ignore borders, creating a **cosmopolitan community of fate** in which societies are thrown together by shared vulnerability. This condition destabilizes sovereignty, modifies legal norms, and recalibrates political imagination. In **Power in the Global Age**, 2002, Beck further explores the diffusion and reconfiguration of power, arguing that networked actors, transnational corporations, regulatory bodies, and social movements compose a new **cosmopolitan realpolitik** no longer centered on state monopoly.

The normative upshot appears most programmatically in **Cosmopolitan Vision**, 2004, where Beck calls for the transformation of **methodological nationalism** into **methodological cosmopolitanism** and for the cultivation of a **cosmopolitan imperative** that matches the structure of risk. For Beck, cosmopolitanism is not merely a moral ideal; it is the descriptive condition of interdependence that must be reflexively internalized by law, education, media, and civil society. The theological resonance is clear: the global reach of **neighbor-love** must scale with the global diffusion of harm, and the politics of climate and finance require a moral grammar that is neither tribal nor merely statist. Beck thus links structural diagnosis with ethical exhortation, though without the metaphysical commitments that a **natural law** or **doctrine of creation** would supply.

#### 5. Individualization, family, and religion
Parallel to his macro-theory, Beck develops a micro-sociology of intimacy and identity in collaboration with **Elisabeth Beck-Gernsheim**. In **The Normal Chaos of Love**, 1990, and **Individualization**, 2002, they describe how late modern life disembeds persons from traditional scripts of family, class, and gender, producing choice biographies under conditions of risk and uncertainty. These dynamics generate both opportunities for autonomy and new forms of precarity, with **zombie categories** such as family and class persisting as rhetorical forms while losing explanatory traction. Here the trope of **subpolitics** reappears in everyday life as persons manage risk portfolios, negotiate unstable commitments, and assemble identities from heterogeneous sources.

Beck's most explicit engagement with religion comes in **A God of One's Own**, 2008. He argues that individualization processes yield an explosion of religious bricolage, generating what he calls **do-it-yourself faith**, neither reducible to secularization nor to fundamentalist retrenchment. Religions exhibit **ambivalence**, proving capable of both violence and peacemaking, and they must be assessed by their capacity to foster **cosmopolitan tolerance** in a **world risk society**. Beck refuses a flat **secularization thesis** and aligns with the insight of **multiple modernities**, yet he continues to interpret religion primarily through the lenses of risk management, identity construction, and global ethics. The result is a sociology of religion that both corrects rationalist dismissals and under-theologizes doctrines of **providence**, **sin**, and **grace** by translating them into sociological equivalents.

#### 6. Crisis politics, Europe, and metamorphosis
Beck's later interventions respond to acute crises. In **German Europe**, 2012, he contends that the Eurozone crisis revealed a de facto **hegemonic integration** under German austerity, a form of **cosmopolitan authoritarianism** that bypassed democratic legitimacy while addressing financial **systemic risk**. His response is consistent: adopt **methodological cosmopolitanism**, democratize transnational governance, and align power with the **cosmopolitan community of fate** produced by shared hazards. This argument extends his earlier claim that **organized irresponsibility** characterizes the architecture of much global governance.

Posthumously published, **The Metamorphosis of the World**, 2016, consolidates Beck's final conceptual move, arguing that late modernity is not merely a reflexive modernization but a **metamorphosis**, a qualitative shift in the social fabric where basic categories such as nature, society, and technology interpenetrate to produce novel ontologies. He introduces the provocative idea of **emancipatory catastrophism**, the claim that disasters can open windows for progressive transformation by revealing the real structure of interdependence. The tone is not chiliastic but reformist, seeking a politics that learns from catastrophe without surrendering to it. The theological echo is again audible: in place of an apocalyptic consummation, Beck proposes a civic **metanoia** wrought by the pedagogical force of risk.

#### 7. Conceptual architecture and empirical referents
Beck's conceptual toolbox is distinctive and portable. **Manufactured uncertainty** names the unintended side effects of industrial-technological systems that render probabilistic calculation volatile and call for precautionary norms. **Non-knowledge** signals the epistemic humility appropriate to complex causation and contestable expertise, encouraging inclusive deliberation and **subpolitics**. **Boomerang effects** dramatize moral causality by which wealthier actors cannot fully externalize hazard. **Organized irresponsibility** criticizes institutional designs that reward compartmentalization and hinder accountability. **Zombie categories** puncture stale social theory by exposing how inherited terms obscure emergent realities. **Methodological cosmopolitanism** reorients analysis to transboundary flows, and **cosmopolitanism** itself names both a descriptive condition and a prescriptive ethic of solidarity.

These concepts are anchored in emblematic cases that repeat across Beck's corpus. **Chernobyl**, **BSE** and food safety, **climate change**, **financial contagion**, and **global pandemics** function as laboratories for seeing reflexive modernization at work. Beck's argument is not that science or markets are inherently malign but that their institutionalization without reflexivity yields **systemic risk** and **organized irresponsibility**. In Reformed terms, his analysis can be heard as a secular discourse on **sin's social embeddedness**, the perverse effects of disordered dominion, and the necessity of structures that anticipate fallibility.

#### 8. Criticisms, limits, and replies
Critics have pressed Beck on several fronts. From **Mary Douglas** and the cultural school come charges that he overstates the novelty and materiality of modern risk and understates the symbolic matrices by which communities construe danger and blame. From **Luhmannian systems theory** comes the accusation that Beck's normative vocabulary smuggles teleology into a world governed by systemic complexity. From **postcolonial** angles and from **Zygmunt Bauman**, he is chastised for Eurocentric horizons and for neglecting the differentiated experiences of precarity outside the West. **Bruno Latour** counters that Beck remains too dualistic in his nature society framing despite gestures toward hybrid networks.

Beck's replies, explicit and implicit, are embedded in his later writings. The insistence on **metamorphosis** in 2016 pushes beyond simple novelty claims by redescribing ontological interpenetrations among nature, society, and technology. His account of **emancipatory catastrophism** acknowledges the ambivalence of disaster while asserting political possibilities for **cosmopolitan** reform. And his anti-**methodological nationalism** attempts to provincialize Europe by forcing social theory to reckon with global causality. Still, the criticisms rightly note that Beck's **cosmopolitan ethic** remains thin in metaphysical grounding and that his picture of agency invests much hope in **subpolitics** without fully specifying virtue, authority, and communal formation.

#### 9. Theological traction and constructive uses
For theology, especially Christian systematic reflection, Beck's analysis offers both diagnostic clarity and constructive traction. His **risk society** thesis reframes questions of **providence**, **theodicy**, and **eschatology** by exposing the late modern condition in which human freedom and technological power generate new forms of suffering and threat. The category of **manufactured uncertainty** underscores creaturely finitude and the need for a prudent **ethic of responsibility** that resonates with a doctrine of **secondary causes** under divine governance. The censure of **organized irresponsibility** supplies a secular idiom for **structural sin** and for institutional repentance. The emphasis on **boomerang effects** and **cosmopolitan community of fate** aligns with the command of **neighbor-love** extended across borders, compelling churches to construe **creation care** and economic justice as catholic obligations.

At the same time Beck's **individualization** analysis invites ecclesial self-examination. His portrait of **do-it-yourself faith** in **A God of One's Own** illuminates how late modern believers cobble identities from plural sources, testing the church's capacity to catechize in conditions of choice overload and **non-knowledge**. Beck's critique of **methodological nationalism** also challenges theologies overly tethered to nation-state imaginaries and opens space for ecclesial **cosmopolitanism** rooted in the **communion of saints**. Finally, the trope of **metamorphosis** cautions against nostalgic retrievals of **zombie categories** and calls for doctrinal discernment that takes seriously the real novelties of the Anthropocene while refusing despairing immanentism.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
A Reformed and WTS-oriented evaluation can affirm Beck's sober identification of **manufactured uncertainty**, **organized irresponsibility**, and **boomerang effects** as descriptions of fallen dominion within providential history while marking principled divergences. First, where Beck builds ethics on **non-knowledge** and procedural **subpolitics**, a Reformed account grounds prudence in **general revelation**, **natural law**, and **Scripture** as norma normans, maintaining that epistemic humility must be tethered to revealed **wisdom** rather than to indeterminacy as such. Second, Beck's **individualization** and **do-it-yourself faith** misdescribe the ontology of the church; against bricolage, **covenant community** constituted by Word and sacrament forms persons through liturgical and catechetical means that resist late modern volatility. Third, Beck's **cosmopolitanism** rightly names transboundary obligations yet risks collapsing subsidiarity and **sphere sovereignty**, tempting centralized technocracy and undercutting mediating institutions ordained for common good. Fourth, Beck's eschatological register remains immanent and reformist, whereas a Reformed **eschatology** insists that ultimate security rests in Christ's lordship and promised consummation; prudential activism is penultimate and covenantal, not salvific. Finally, Beck's critique of **methodological nationalism** is salutary, but a Reformed political theology prefers a differentiated pluralism under **providence**, integrating local vocations, national duties, and transnational solidarities without sacrificing jurisdictional integrity. The constructive alternative thus receives Beck's **reflexive modernization** as a call to institutional repentance and wiser stewardship while realigning agency under **providence**, recalibrating ethics by **law and gospel**, and thickening cosmopolitan concern through **covenant theology** and **creational ordinances** that honor both global interdependence and ordered diversity.
