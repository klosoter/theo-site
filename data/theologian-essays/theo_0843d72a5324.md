### TITLE
Gospel-centered contextualization for late-modern cities through church planting, apologetics, and pastoral theology.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Historical and intellectual context: **Timothy J. Keller**, 1950–2023, **PCA** pastor-theologian in late-modern **New York City**, shaped by **Westminster Theological Seminary** and urban mission debates.
- Method or epistemic stance: **soft presuppositionalism** engaging **defeater beliefs**, **plausibility structures**, and **common grace**, influenced by **Van Til**, **Harvie Conn**, and **Charles Taylor**.
- Key works with years: **Ministries of Mercy**, 1989 and revised 2015; **The Reason for God**, 2008; **The Prodigal God**, 2008; **Generous Justice**, 2010; **The Meaning of Marriage**, 2011; **Center Church**, 2012; **Every Good Endeavor**, 2012; **Prayer**, 2014; **Preaching**, 2015; **Making Sense of God**, 2016; **Proverbs commentary** via **God's Wisdom for Navigating Life**, 2017; **The Prodigal Prophet**, 2018; **Hope in Times of Fear**, 2021; **Forgive**, 2022; **Jesus the King**, 2011 and 2013.
- Central terms or categories: **gospel-centered**, **contextualization**, **theological vision**, **defeater beliefs**, **city as cultural engine**, **renewal dynamics**, **idolatry as disordered love**, **common grace and antithesis**, **creation–fall–redemption–restoration**.
- Main interlocutors or influences: **Edmund Clowney**, **Harvie Conn**, **Cornelius Van Til**, **Jonathan Edwards**, **C. S. Lewis**, **Lesslie Newbigin**, **Charles Taylor**, **Alasdair MacIntyre**, **N. T. Wright**, **Abraham Kuyper**, **H. Richard Niebuhr**.
- Reformed reception cue: Appreciated in **PCA** and **Westminster** circles for **Christ-centered preaching** and **church planting**, yet debated over **transformationalism**, **winsome apologetics**, and scope of **common grace**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
The life and work of **Timothy J. Keller** unfolded at the intersection of **Reformed orthodoxy**, late-modern urbanity, and a pastoral vocation directed toward skeptics. Born in 1950 and deceased in 2023, Keller was ordained in the **Presbyterian Church in America** and educated at **Gordon-Conwell** and **Westminster Theological Seminary**, with formative influence from **Edmund P. Clowney** and **Harvie M. Conn**. These Westminster mentors embedded in him a **Christ-centered hermeneutic** and a missionary imagination for the **city** grounded in **Reformed** doctrine. Keller absorbed from **C. S. Lewis** a literary apologetic for reason and imagination, and from **Jonathan Edwards** a robust account of the **affections** as central to sanctification. His early pastorate in Virginia, his teaching at Westminster in the mid-1980s, and the founding of **Redeemer Presbyterian Church** in **New York City** in 1989 together formed the crucible in which his project of **gospel-centered contextualization** was forged. The historical setting was one of advanced **secularization**, rising **pluralism**, and the breakdown of Protestant establishment, which required a fresh strategy for confessional **Reformed** witness in public space.

Keller’s epistemic posture can be described as **soft presuppositionalism**, an approach that acknowledges the **noetic effects of sin** and the role of **worldviews**, yet makes constant use of **cumulative-case** reasoning and **defeater-defusing** strategies. Drawing on **Cornelius Van Til**, he affirms that unbelief suppresses truth, but he also appropriates **Lesslie Newbigin** and **Charles Taylor** to diagnose Western **plausibility structures** with pastoral patience rather than combative polemic. The result is a dialogical method that seeks both *ad hominem* exposure of idols and invitation into a better account of reality. Keller speaks of **defeater beliefs**, the assumptions that make Christianity seem implausible, and proposes **apologetics as pastoral care**, somewhat akin to a *theologia viatorum*, that is a theology for pilgrims. He valorizes **common grace** not as a neutral space but as the sphere where **Romans 1** insights meet cultural goods to be appreciated and redirected toward **Christ**.

#### 2. Redeemer, City to City, and urban mission
Redeemer’s founding in Manhattan in 1989 initiated a sustained experiment in **missional church** practice within a late-modern metropolis. Keller’s ecclesial dramaturgy combined **expository preaching**, liturgical modesty with **classic hymnody**, the arts as a public face of **common grace**, and a relentless focus on **church planting**. Out of Redeemer’s growth emerged the **Redeemer Church Planting Center**, later known as **Redeemer City to City**, formally active from 2001 and expanded after 2008, catalyzing hundreds of church plants across global cities. The urban frame sharpened Keller’s theological categories. The **city as cultural engine** demanded serious engagement with the **professions**, the **arts**, and finance, pressing him to draw upon **Abraham Kuyper’s** doctrine of **sphere sovereignty** and **cultural mandate** while tempering triumphalism through an **already and not yet** eschatology. Keller’s praxis repeatedly moved along the arc from **worship** to **catechesis** to **mercy and justice**, insisting that evangelism, diaconal ministry, and faith-and-work integration must be fused in a coherent **theological vision**.

The creation of **The Gospel Coalition** with **D. A. Carson**, undergirded by a capacious but clear **confessional statement**, supplied a national platform for a **Reformed evangelical** center. Keller thus operated not only as a local pastor but as a network-builder whose theological instincts moved toward **catholicity** within **orthodoxy**, joining **Bavinckian** breadth to Westminster rigor. His urban mission remained grounded in preaching **Christ crucified** as the gospel of **grace**, with a pastoral ethic of persuasion rather than coercion, a commitment he later framed as **winsome orthodoxy** in the face of polarized culture.

#### 3. Apologetics for late moderns
Keller’s apologetic corpus inaugurated with **The Reason for God**, 2008, and continued with **Making Sense of God**, 2016, which he described as a prequel designed for the unpersuaded. The strategy targeted **defeater beliefs** such as the problem of evil, the exclusivity of **Christ**, and skepticism toward **miracles**. He balanced **negative apologetics** that remove obstacles with **positive apologetics** that commend **Christianity** as existentially satisfying and intellectually credible. Influenced by **C. S. Lewis** and **Alasdair MacIntyre**, he argued that secular narratives smuggle in **moral realism** they cannot ground, while **Christian theism** uniquely unites meaning and morality. In the realm of historical claims, Keller leaned on **N. T. Wright** for the **resurrection** as public event and on **Richard Bauckham** for the Gospels as eyewitness-laden texts, presenting a **cumulative case** that respects late-modern doubts without surrendering **biblical authority**.

Central to this apologetic is Keller’s treatment of **pluralism** and **freedom**. He contends that every community enshrines **exclusive** beliefs and that the difference lies not in the absence of exclusion but in how communities treat those with whom they disagree. Here Keller sets **liberal neutrality** against **confessional candor**, proposing a **humble confidence** grounded in **grace**. He reframes conversion less as mere assent and more as liberation from the **idolatry** of self-rule. In **The Prodigal God**, 2008, his exposition of **Luke 15** bases apologetics in the drama of **grace** that confronts both younger brother relativism and elder brother moralism, a signature **third way** motif that recurs across his work.

#### 4. Center Church and the category of theological vision
The methodological keystone of Keller’s project is **Center Church**, 2012, which introduces the mediating category of **theological vision**. This is the connective tissue between **Reformed doctrine** and **ministry practice**, a field in which exegesis of the city, congregational capacities, and cultural analysis shape how one applies biblical truth. The architecture of the book turns on three modules labeled **Gospel**, **City**, and **Movement**, yielding a practical ecclesiology for **church planting** and institutional **renewal**. The **Gospel** module clarifies doctrinal nonnegotiables centered on **substitutionary atonement** and **justification by faith**. The **City** module works out **contextualization** with appeal to **H. Richard Niebuhr’s** typology in **Christ and Culture**, yet rejects simplistic alignment with any single option by advocating a nuanced **Christ transforms culture** vision under the cross. The **Movement** module seeks to nurture ecosystems of churches exhibiting collaboration rather than competition, echoing **missio Dei** insights about the church’s sentness.

Keller’s **contextualization** principle is framed as translation without subtraction. He insists that the **gospel** must be preached in idioms that connect with local **plausibility structures**, but not by discarding offense, especially the offense of the **cross**. He defends a **centered-set** rather than **bounded-set** ecclesial vision, urging orthodoxy at the center with generous posture at the edges. In this he seeks to hold together **antithesis** and **common grace** by a hermeneutic of tension, a move that has provoked debate within **Reformed** circles about the locus of normativity in pastoral **prudence** versus **principle**.

#### 5. Doctrinal emphases: gospel, atonement, and sanctification
Keller’s dogmatic center is the **gospel of grace**. He renders the gospel as news not advice, a heralded **euangelion** rather than a moral program. On the **atonement**, Keller unapologetically foregrounds **penal substitution**, while integrating **Christus Victor** and **moral influence** motifs as necessary entailments of the cross. In **The Reason for God** and **Center Church**, he argues that only **substitutionary atonement** provides a coherent account of forgiveness, justice, and love without collapsing divine **righteousness** into sentiment. He portrays the cross as an event of **theologia crucis**, fusing divine **holiness** and **mercy** in the **incarnate Son**.

Sanctification is cast as transformation by **union with Christ**, with **adoption** and **assurance** as key features of the **ordo salutis**, and with the **affections** at the heart of ethical renewal. Drawing from **Jonathan Edwards** on **religious affections** and from **Martin Luther** on **law and gospel**, Keller consistently frames Christian growth as moving away from both **legalism** and **antinomianism**. His paradigm of **counterfeit gods** interprets sin as **idolatry**, that is, disordered love, a retrieval of **Augustine’s** *ordo amoris*. Repentance is not merely renunciation but a reordering of desire through a sight of **Christ’s** beauty, which he calls repentance by rejoicing. This reshapes spiritual disciplines around **heart renewal** rather than mere behavior modification.

#### 6. Ethics, public life, and the goods of creation
Keller’s ethical and social theology appears most clearly in **Generous Justice**, 2010, and **Every Good Endeavor**, 2012, coauthored with **Katherine Leary Alsdorf**. In **Generous Justice**, he exegetes the Old Testament pairing of **mishpat** and **tzedakah**, justice and righteous generosity, as integral to **covenant** life rather than optional charity. He argues that **justification by faith** creates a people who embody **mercy and justice**, thereby rooting social concern in **grace** rather than ideology. This leads him to commend partnership guided by **common grace**, while warning against partisan capture. In **Every Good Endeavor**, he retrieves **Kuyperian** themes to honor **vocation**, urging Christians to serve within the **creation mandate** while resisting both privatized piety and utopian politics. He develops a **theology of work** that esteems the ordinary and integrates **Sabbath** rest as a sign of eschatological hope.

In **The Meaning of Marriage**, 2011, coauthored with **Kathy Keller**, he articulates a **complementarian** vision tethered to **Ephesians 5** and the **covenant** nature of marriage. The book resists consumerist romanticism and grounds marital fidelity in the **self-giving** of **Christ**. Across these works Keller aims to demonstrate that **Reformed soteriology** generates an ethic of public presence marked by humility and service. His 2022 volume **Forgive** returns to the public square to defend **forgiveness** against both therapeutic and retributive distortions, presenting a cruciform alternative rooted in **substitutionary atonement** and the **Lord’s Prayer**.

#### 7. Spirituality, Scripture, and preaching
Keller’s program of spiritual formation unfolds in **Prayer**, 2014, and **Preaching**, 2015, as well as in sermon collections like **Jesus the King**, 2011 and 2013. In **Prayer**, he synthesizes **Augustine**, **Luther**, **Calvin**, **John Owen**, and **Edwards** into a structured method that moves from **Scripture** meditation to **adoration**, **confession**, **thanksgiving**, and **supplication**, with special emphasis on the **Psalms** as the Bible’s own prayer book. He commends prayer that shapes the **habitus** of faith and reorders loves in line with **ordo amoris**. In **Preaching**, he urges **Christ-centered** exposition following **Clowney’s** typological trajectories, to preach the **gospel** in every text without allegorical excess. The sermon must engage the **heart** and the **conscience**, speak within local **plausibility structures**, and present **Christ** as the better fulfillment of every human longing.

Keller’s homiletic ethic aims at **affectional** transformation and **repentance**, a revivalist thread mediated through **Edwards** yet ordered by sober **Reformed** ecclesiology. He often names **idolatry** from the pulpit, exposing functional saviors behind fear and anxiety, and then offers the **person of Christ** as the only stable ground for identity. His christocentric readings put him within a **redemptive-historical** stream that seeks to unify **biblical theology** and **systematics** in congregational ministry, a hallmark of the **Westminster** tradition he inherited.

#### 8. Pastoral tone, networks, and late style
Keller’s tone of **winsome orthodoxy** became both a strength and a lightning rod. He sought to speak in public without rancor, embodying **gentleness** as an apologetic argument in itself. Through **The Gospel Coalition**, public lectures, and op-eds, he functioned as a pastor to pastors, arguing for what he called a faithful presence that is neither assimilationist nor separatist. In **Hope in Times of Fear**, 2021, written amid both a pandemic and personal **pancreatic cancer**, he returned to the **resurrection** as the engine of **hope**, insisting that eschatology cannot be reduced to consolation but launches costly **mission**. His final years included a defense of **forgiveness**, 2022, in a climate he judged suspicious of grace. Keller died in May 2023, leaving an institutional legacy in **City to City**, a trove of **sermons**, and a cadre of urban pastors shaped by his **center church** framework.

The network dimension of his ministry raises a crucial interpretive theme. Keller consistently linked **doctrine**, **discipleship**, and **institutional ecosystems**, striving to seed **movements** rather than isolated successes. He envisioned churches as ecosystems of **koinonia**, where evangelism, mercy, and formation are cross-pollinating practices. This strategy relies on a theology of **providence** and **common grace** that anticipates divine activity beyond ecclesial borders, yet calls the church to offer **word and sacrament** as the locus of Christ’s kingly and priestly work in history.

#### 9. Integrations and tensions in doctrinal development
If one asks for the doctrinal center of Keller’s corpus, the answer is the **evangel**, the good news that the **Triune God** saves sinners through **Christ** and forms them into a people who serve the **city**. Along this line Keller’s theology is integrative. The **cross** grounds the church’s **justice** because forgiveness costs God and therefore propels sacrificial service. The **resurrection** animates a program of **culture care** without naivete, because the powers are defeated but not yet eradicated. **Union with Christ** yields a **third way** between moralism and relativism, because identity is a gift not a goal. His repeated use of the fourfold gospel of **creation**, **fall**, **redemption**, **restoration** supplies a catechetical frame that connects **Genesis** to **Revelation**. This synthesis is compressed into his category of **theological vision**, which functions as a prudential bridge where doctrines retain primacy but methods are contextually attuned.

Keller’s evolving apologetic shows the same integrated arc. From **The Reason for God** to **Making Sense of God**, he increasingly foregrounded the **conditions of belief** described by **Charles Taylor**, treating secular doubt as a historical mood rather than mere argument. His pastoral apologetics addresses the **imagination** as well as the **intellect**, strengthening the case for Christianity as the most coherent account of the human **self** and **society**. The integration extends to **spiritual disciplines**, where **Prayer** and **Preaching** unify head and heart, doctrine and desire, through **Scripture** read as a single **Christ-centered** canon. Across the corpus one sees an unembarrassed **Reformed** soteriology working outward into mission, ethics, and aesthetics.

#### 10. Reformed and Westminster evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage point, Keller’s achievement is considerable. He preserved **Westminster Confession** commitments on **Scripture**, **Trinity**, **incarnation**, **justification by faith**, **substitutionary atonement**, **effectual calling**, and **perseverance**, while retrieving **union with Christ** and **adoption** for pastoral use. His **Christ-centered hermeneutic** is deeply indebted to **Edmund Clowney** and coheres with **redemptive-historical** preaching. He advanced church planting as an outworking of **covenant theology** and the **Great Commission**, giving practical shape to a **confessional missiology**. Yet several tensions remain. First, the category of **theological vision** risks smuggling normative **theological judgments** into ostensibly prudential **contextualization**, blurring the line between **principle** and **method**. Classical **two-kingdoms** advocates will judge Keller’s **transformational** aspirations overly expansive, urging a sharper distinction between **church as institute** and **church as organism**, and warning against over-realized **eschatology**. Second, Keller’s **winsome apologetics** may be read as underplaying **antithesis** and the **noetic effects of sin** emphasized by **Van Til**, especially in its appeal to shared **moral intuitions** without a robust **natural law** framework to discipline the use of **common grace**. Third, the integration of **justice** into gospel outworkings, while textually anchored in **mishpat** and **tzedakah**, can drift in application if **diaconal priorities** are not tethered to **word and sacrament** ministry. A constructive alternative within classical Reformed orthodoxy would retain Keller’s **Christ-centered** heart theology and church-planting dynamism, while clarifying that **special revelation** norms all **contextualization**, distinguishing **kingdom** from **culture** without quietism, and grounding public reasoning in a more explicit **creation order** and **natural law** anchored by **covenant** and **providence**. On balance, Keller’s legacy stands as a major instance of **Reformed catholicity** in mission, a model to be emulated with the caution and clarity that Westminster theology demands.
