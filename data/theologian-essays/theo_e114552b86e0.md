### TITLE
Retrieving Augustine for a cross-shaped, virtue-formed, and culturally attuned Evangelical apologetic

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Post-9/11 secularization**, **New Atheism**, and **expressive individualism** form the late-modern context for Chatraw's **Augustinian retrieval** and public witness.
- Method marked by **Augustinian desire-teleology**, **virtue epistemology**, and a **cross-centered apologetic** that is ecclesially located and missionally oriented.
- Key works: **Truth Matters** 2014; **Truth in a Culture of Doubt** 2014; **Apologetics at the Cross** 2018; **Telling a Better Story** 2020; **The Augustine Way** 2023; **Surprised by Doubt** 2023.
- Central categories include **plausibility structures**, **inside-out persuasion**, **subversive fulfillment**, **ordered love** or *ordo amoris*, **humility as epistemic virtue**, and **public Christianity**.
- Main interlocutors and influences: **Augustine**, **Charles Taylor**, **Alasdair MacIntyre**, **Michael Polanyi**, **Lesslie Newbigin**, **C. S. Lewis**, alongside debates with **evidentialism**, **presuppositionalism**, and **reformed epistemology**.
- Reformed reception cue: appreciable alignment with **noetic effects of sin**, **common grace**, and **cross-shaped epistemology**, with questions about **principium of Scripture**, **antithesis**, and the bounds of **natural theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and methodological posture
Joshua D. Chatraw emerges within the twenty-first-century **Evangelical** world that faced the twin forces of the **New Atheism** and a diffuse **secular age** described by **Charles Taylor**. His public profile has developed through both academic and ecclesial platforms, including leadership in a **Center for Public Christianity** oriented toward the common good and the plausibility of the gospel in the late modern West. In this setting of pluralism, post-Christendom, and information overload, Chatraw appropriates **Augustine's** grammar of **ordered love** or *ordo amoris* to reconceive **apologetics** as a form of Christian **witness** shaped by the **cross**. His approach stands against Enlightenment **foundationalism** that imagines a neutral standpoint, while resisting sectarian withdrawal. He proposes an integrative method that is both **theological** and **missionally practical**, revising the apologetic posture in light of **1 Corinthians 1 to 2**, giving priority to **humility**, **virtue**, and ecclesially nourished practices that produce credibility.

A key aspect of Chatraw's formation is engagement with **virtue epistemology** and the sociology of **plausibility structures**. With **Alasdair MacIntyre** he takes rationality as tradition-embedded. With **Michael Polanyi** he underscores tacit knowledge and the personal coefficients of belief. With **Peter Berger** and **Taylor** he notes how beliefs are sustained by social imaginaries. This intellectual constellation supports his turn toward **inside-out persuasion** that begins with the audience's perceived goods and stories, before it gently but incisively unveils their **idolatrous teloi** and re-narrates them toward Christ. The program is not anti-argument but is recalibrated around **mission**, **virtue**, and **narrative plausibility**, aiming to display the **gospel** as the best account of reality rather than merely winning a debate.

#### 2. Augustinian retrieval and cross-centered epistemology
At the heart of Chatraw's method is an Augustinian construal of **desire**, **love**, and **knowledge**. By retrieving **Augustine's** conviction that we come to understand through rightly ordered love, Chatraw reframes apologetics as the *cura animarum* of a culture. The call is to address the **affections** and the **imagination** as much as the articulate intellect. The **cross-shaped epistemology** he commends moves apologetics from a triumphalist performance toward a cruciform posture that acknowledges the **noetic effects of sin**, the limits of unaided reason, and the scandal of **Christ crucified** as the power and wisdom of God. Here he explicitly contrasts **apologia** that seeks control with **witness** centered on the **kerygma** and the Spirit's work, thereby refusing to disentangle method from message.

This Augustinian reorientation does not abandon **reason**. It seeks **thick rationality** in which argument, story, and practice form a triad. Chatraw integrates classic **natural theology** arguments only within a broader field of **subversive fulfillment**, where creaturely goods point beyond themselves while being judged by the **gospel**. He keeps **common grace** in view to affirm that unbelievers can track truth, yet he maintains an evangelical seriousness about **antithesis** between rival loves. Thus his method aims to be both generous and chastened, combining **intellectual humility** with bold **public Christianity**.

#### 3. Early works on Scripture and skepticism
His initial contributions focused on the credibility of **Scripture** in the wake of popularized skepticism. In **Truth Matters** 2014 and **Truth in a Culture of Doubt** 2014, Chatraw addresses the claims of **Bart Ehrman**-style criticism regarding the **canon**, **textual transmission**, **contradictions**, and the problem of **suffering** as it bears on biblical reliability. The apologetic mode remains evidential and analytic, yet even here the seeds of his later program are present: an insistence that **biblical theology**, the **integrity of the canon**, and the **Christological center** of Scripture furnish not only defensive answers but a compelling account of reality.

In these works Chatraw frames objections within the narrative of **creation**, **fall**, **redemption**, and **consummation**, showing how **Christ** fulfills Israel's story and how the **Gospels' historical reliability** coheres with their **theological intent**. He does not isolate **textual criticism** from **theological interpretation of Scripture**. The argument is that **trustworthiness** is not merely a property of isolated propositions but a function of the canonical witness to the **triune God** who is faithful. Such positioning anticipates his later emphasis that **apologetics** is not detachable from **doctrine** or from the **church's practices** of reading and living Scripture.

#### 4. Apologetics at the Cross and the integrative taxonomy
Chatraw's programmatic statement arrives with **Apologetics at the Cross** 2018. The work both historicizes and synthesizes, offering a taxonomy of **classical**, **evidentialist**, **reformed epistemology**, and **presuppositional** approaches. He appreciates **Thomas Aquinas** and contemporary **classical theism** for metaphysical clarity, values **evidentialist** attention to public reasons, learns from **Alvin Plantinga's** properly basic beliefs, and registers **Cornelius Van Til's** covenantal critique of autonomy. Yet he seeks an integrative **cross-centered apologetic** that keeps the scandal of the **gospel** in focus while marshaling the best from these streams for missional use.

The hallmark practices of this book include what he calls **inside-out persuasion** and **subversive fulfillment**. Inside-out persuasion listens and enters the interlocutor's narrative world to identify **longings**, **loves**, and **defeaters**. Subversive fulfillment then both affirms the partial goods in those longings and challenges the **idolatries** that misdirect them, offering **Christ** as the fulfillment that both judges and completes. The method is pastoral and analytic, combining **argument**, **story**, and **practice**, and is tethered to the **church's mission** rather than to apologetics as a detached discipline. This is an apologetic shaped by the **gospel's telos** and by **virtue** dispositions like patience and courage.

#### 5. Telling a Better Story and the turn to imagination
In **Telling a Better Story** 2020, Chatraw advances his emphasis on **narrative** and **imagination**. He draws on **C. S. Lewis** for the role of story in awakening desire, on **MacIntyre** for the moral shape of traditions, and on **Taylor** for the modern social imaginary. The thesis is that late-modern people inhabit **contested plausibility structures** where standard proofs rarely penetrate. The way forward is not to abandon reasons but to embed reasons within a **richer narrative** that names the human condition, displays **beauty**, and invites participation in a **community of practice**.

Key to this work is Chatraw's handling of cultural goods through **subversive fulfillment**. He takes themes like **freedom**, **identity**, **justice**, and **hope**, showing how secular accounts overpromise and underdeliver while the **Christian story** can better satisfy the same desires. He highlights **epistemic virtues** such as **intellectual humility** and **hospitality** as evangelically mandated modes of discourse. The apologetic outcome is a posture of confidence without combativeness, a **public witness** that is honest about suffering and evil yet points to the **crucified and risen Lord** as the one who uniquely answers the human story.

#### 6. The Augustine Way and the ecclesial shape of persuasion
With **The Augustine Way** 2023, Chatraw crystallizes his **Augustinian retrieval** for contemporary apologetics and discipleship. He explicitly centers **ordered love**, articulates how **pride** corrupts the pursuit of truth, and commends **humility** as the master virtue for Christian persuasion. Apologetics becomes an expression of **sanctification**, because how one believes is inseparable from what one believes. The argument widens from individual conversations to **ecclesial formation**, contending that the **church's liturgies**, **catechesis**, and **hospitality** form the plausibility of the gospel as much as syllogisms do.

Within this frame, Chatraw engages **virtue epistemology** not as a replacement for **revelation**, but as a grammar for discipleship that enhances **credibility**. He invokes **Lesslie Newbigin's** missionary encounter, insisting that the **church** must be both a **contrast society** and a **sent people**. He presses beyond culture-war posture by modeling **convicted civility**, offering examples of how **patience**, **courage**, and **charity** open doors for the **gospel** in contested public spaces. This is a strategy forged by **theology of the cross** rather than a calculus of power.

#### 7. Surprised by Doubt and pastoral apologetics
In **Surprised by Doubt** 2023, Chatraw turns directly to the dynamics of **deconstruction** and **disillusionment** among Evangelicals. He acknowledges the emotional and social texture of doubt, taking seriously the ways religious communities can become **credibility deficits** through hypocrisy or injustice. His aim is to reframe doubt not as an enemy of faith but as an invitation to **deeper catechesis**, **confession**, and renewed **trust** in **Christ**. The volume integrates **psychology of belief**, **virtue formation**, and **doctrinal clarity** to help readers disentangle contingent cultural baggage from the **gospel's** unchanging core.

Here again the accent rests on **epistemic humility** and **ecclesial practices** that make belief plausible. He counsels cultivating habits of **truthfulness**, **repentance**, and **patient listening** as apologetically potent. The book is less about external objections and more about internal repair, yet it consolidates Chatraw's thesis that **apologetics** is best practiced as **pastoral theology**. The apologetic task in a jaded age is to make the **church** a place where **beauty**, **truth**, and **goodness** are visible, so that arguments about God do not outstrip a life lived before God.

#### 8. Doctrinal commitments and conceptual tools
Though an apologist by vocation, Chatraw's work is not methodologically agnostic about **theology**. He assumes **classical theism** in basic outline, including **divine simplicity** and **immutability**, and he affirms the **authority of Scripture** as the supreme norm. He leans on a **Christocentric hermeneutic** that sees all apologetics ordered to the proclamation of **Christ crucified**, nurturing what he calls a **cross-shaped apologetic**. Philosophically he values **reformed epistemology** for challenging evidentialist strictures, while resisting a purely **presuppositional** exclusion of public reasons. His preferred posture is a chastened **natural theology** subordinated to **special revelation**, operationalized via **subversive fulfillment** and **inside-out persuasion**.

Tooling for cultural engagement includes a focus on **plausibility structures**, an analysis of **expressive individualism**, and appropriation of **MacIntyre's** traditioned rationality. He deploys **virtue epistemology** as a theological anthropology of knowing, contending that the **Spirit's** work in conforming believers to **Christ** enhances their ability to see and speak truly. Central too is an ecclesiology of **public Christianity**, where the **church's witness** is itself apologetic. Throughout, he seeks to balance **argument** and **aesthetics**, **reason** and **imagination**, presenting the **gospel** as the truest and most beautiful story.

#### 9. Interlocutors, debates, and constructive distinctives
Chatraw's conversation partners range from classic proponents of **evidentialism** to champions of **presuppositionalism**, with modern contributions from **Plantinga** and the **analytic** tradition. He is critical of apologetics that ape **Enlightenment** confidence in neutral reason, yet he disputes the counsel of despair that would eschew **public reasons** entirely. His dialogue with **Taylor** refines his analysis of secular conditions, while **Newbigin** informs his missiology. He receives from **Lewis** a sacramental view of **imagination** as a witness to desire, and from **MacIntyre** a sense that virtue and tradition underwrite rational dispute. Across these discussions, his distinctive proposal is that a **cross-shaped**, **Augustinian**, and **virtue-formed** apologetic can both honor the offense of the **gospel** and commend it in terms that late-modern people can hear.

Practically, this yields counsel about conversational dynamics, the power of **testimony**, and the orientation of apologetics to **discipleship** rather than to a detached academic enterprise. He often counsels interrogating **elites' narratives** of freedom and authenticity, demonstrating how those scripts lack the resources for **forgiveness**, **belonging**, and **hope**. He then shows how the **Christian narrative** not only explains moral experience but also funds the pursuit of the **common good** in civic life. The apologetic is public without being triumphal, thickly **ecclesial** without being sectarian, and confident without being coercive.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** perspective, including a Westminster Theological Seminary temperament, Chatraw's project shows notable strengths and some tensions. Strongest is his retrieval of **cross-centered epistemology**, which resonates with the **theology of the cross** and with Calvin's teaching on the **noetic effects of sin** and the necessity of the **Spirit's** illumination. His use of **common grace**, his suspicion of autonomous **reason**, and his appeal to **Augustine's** *ordo amoris* align well with **Reformed anthropology** and the primacy of **special revelation**. The language of **subversive fulfillment** coheres with **covenantal** accounts of creation and redemption, where creational goods are preserved and perfected in **Christ**.

Tensions arise where **virtue epistemology** and narrative strategies risk eclipsing the principial role of **Scripture** as the **principium cognoscendi** of theology. A Westminster critique would press for clearer assertion that the **Word** normatively governs not only the content but also the method of **apologetics**, lest aesthetic or affective registers tacitly relocate authority in the hearer's desires. Likewise, while Chatraw is careful, his use of modest **natural theology** must be held under the antithetical edge of a **covenantal** critique of autonomy; otherwise the appeal to shared goods could slide into a nature-grace schema insufficiently ruled by **revelation**. Finally, the emphasis on **ecclesial practices** as plausibility generators should be paired with a strong articulation of **effectual calling** and the **sufficiency of Scripture**, ensuring that the church's beauty serves rather than substitutes for proclamation. The best Reformed alternative is not to reject Chatraw's program but to deepen it covenantally, making the **self-attesting Word**, the **Spirit's** sovereign agency, and the **church's** sacrificial witness the governing coordinates of a truly cross-shaped **public Christianity**.
