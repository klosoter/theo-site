### TITLE
Seeing Scripture and history through new eyes: a liturgical-typological architecture of reality fulfilled in Christ and enacted in the Church

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth-century American **Reformed** scene, from post-**theonomy** Tyler circles to 1990s **Biblical Horizons**; debates over **Federal Vision**, **liturgy**, and **hermeneutics** in the PCA and broader Presbyterian world.
- Method: presuppositional yet **symbolic-typological** exegesis, an **interpretive maximalism** ordered by **liturgy**, **cosmic-temple** patterns, and a maturation model of **redemptive history**.
- Key works: **The Law of the Covenant** (1984), **Judges: God’s War Against Humanism** (1985), **The Sociology of the Church** (1986), **Through New Eyes** (1988), **Primeval Saints** (1999), **From Bread to Wine** (c. 2000), plus **Biblical Horizons** and **Rite Reasons** essays in the 1990s–2000s.
- Central terms: **cosmic temple**, **objective covenant**, **paedocommunion**, **covenant maturity** (priest-king-prophet), **liturgical ontology**, **sacramental maximalism**, **postmillennial hope**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Cornelius Van Til**, **Geerhardus Vos**, and **Meredith G. Kline**; early contact with **R. J. Rushdoony** and **Greg Bahnsen**; later collaboration with **Peter J. Leithart**, **Jeffrey J. Meyers**, and **Douglas Wilson**; dialogue with liturgical voices like **Alexander Schmemann**.
- Reformed reception: enthusiasm in **CREC** and renewal-minded Presbyterians; strong critique from **Westminster**-influenced confessionalists on **sacraments**, **covenant objectivity**, and **hermeneutical controls**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and the turn to symbolic exegesis
James B. Jordan emerged in the late twentieth-century American **Reformed** ferment where **Van Tilian presuppositionalism**, **Vosian Biblical Theology**, and a revived interest in **theonomy** and **postmillennialism** converged. Born in 1949 and educated in the turbulent post-1960s era, he absorbed the confidence of **Reconstructionist** social thought while also reacting against reductionistic biblicism by insisting on the Bible’s **liturgical** and **symbolic** depth. His early theological work unfolded amid the Tyler, Texas scene of the early 1980s, where **Geneva Ministries** amplified debates on **law**, **culture**, and **church order**. As the decade advanced, Jordan’s path diverged from movement-level politics toward a sustained exploration of **cosmic-temple** patterns and a pastoral program of **covenant renewal worship**, bringing him into conversation with **Meredith G. Kline’s** covenant-architectural insights even as he offered a distinctive symbolic hermeneutic. From the late 1980s into the 1990s he developed an original synthesis under the banner of **Biblical Horizons**, insisting that the Scriptures disclose the world as a God-given **liturgy** to be enacted, not merely a code to be abstracted.

Jordan’s method is self-consciously **presuppositional** in the **Van Til** line, yet he contends that faithful presuppositionalism must be practiced within the Church’s **ritual** and **sacramental** life. He thus favors an **interpretive maximalism** that seeks canonical echoes, numerical patterns, and chiastic structures under the rule of Christ’s **anakephalaiosis** or recapitulation of all things. The Scriptures are read as a grand **temple-building** and **world-maturing** process, culminating in the **new covenant** polity of the Church. This approach requires a widened grammatical-historical lens in which grammar and history serve, rather than limit, **typology** and **symbol**. Jordan’s constant claim is that biblical **liturgy** is not an elective aesthetic but the ontology of the people of God, and that **sacramental** practices shape social forms and political imaginations as profoundly as law codes or confessions.

#### 2. Early works: law, war, and ecclesial sociology
Jordan’s early monographs map the transition from the **theonomic** debates toward his mature symbolic theology. In **The Law of the Covenant** (1984) he offers an exposition of **Exodus 21–23** that pressed beyond proof-texting to the **case-law**’s underlying symbolic and liturgical logic, thereby moving the question from bare application to discerning the moral architecture within **Torah**. In **Judges: God’s War Against Humanism** (1985) he reads Israel’s war as a liturgical-political purge of idolatry, an argument that telescopes **holy war**, **sanctuary** practice, and civic order. **The Sociology of the Church** (1986) then explores how **ecclesial** structures and rites create social patterns, anticipating his later insistence that the Church’s **worship** is the seedbed of cultural transformation. In these works one sees the incipient turn from statutory emphasis to **symbolic world-building**, a move that distinguishes Jordan from both **Rushdoony’s** legal emphases and from thin Biblicism that resists **typology**.

The publication of **Through New Eyes** (1988) consolidated this turn. Here Jordan articulates the Bible’s **symbolic cosmos**: the three-decker world of **heaven**, **firmament**, and **earth**; the **garden-sanctuary** as microcosm; the maturation from **priestly** to **kingly** to **prophetic** orders; and the liturgical weeks and feasts as temporal architecture for communal life. The thesis is that biblical symbols are not ornaments but operative **grammatica sacra**, the deep syntax of creation and covenant. Jordan thereby reframes the public task of the Church: culture is not transformed primarily by policy or power but by ritually reconstituting **time**, **space**, and **people** under Christ’s **lordship**. The **cosmic temple** paradigm anchors his exegesis of Genesis, Psalms, and Revelation, and his expositions constantly return to tabernacle patterns, priestly garments, and calendrical rites as keys to **Scripture’s** unity.

#### 3. Covenant maturity, history, and postmillennial hope
A hallmark of Jordan’s Biblical Theology is his **maturation** model of **redemptive history**. He argues that God fathers humanity through stages: a childhood of priestly tutelage under angels and tabernacle boundaries, an adolescence of regal responsibility under kings, and an adulthood of prophetic wisdom culminating in **Christ** and the **new covenant**. This triadic sequence of **priest-king-prophet** is not a mere taxonomy but an account of historical pedagogy by which God brings humanity from **infancy** to **maturity**. For Jordan, the **incarnation** and Pentecost establish the final, public phase of this maturity in which the nations are discipled and the **church** becomes a royal-priestly commonwealth. This generates an explicitly **postmillennial** eschatological confidence: the **kingdom** advances through sacrificial service, liturgical renewal, and the formation of wise households and polities.

The **objective covenant** plays a central role in this historical vision. By objective covenant Jordan means that baptismal and liturgical rites are not optional signs but real incorporations into the **church’s polity**, bearing blessings and curses according to response. He rejects both individualistic **decisionism** and a merely invisible-church ecclesiology, insisting that God deals publicly with a visible, sacramental people. The immediate consequence is a stress on **discipline**, **catechesis**, and **eucharistic** formation as the theater where Christian society is built. The later controversies over the so-called **Federal Vision** would orbit this point: Jordan’s language about covenantal objectivity and baptismal incorporation presses against certain **Reformed** habits of speech about election, perseverance, and the relation of **sign** and **thing signified**.

#### 4. Sacraments, liturgy, and the rhythms of time
Jordan’s sacramental program flows from his liturgical ontology. In **From Bread to Wine** and many **Rite Reasons** essays, he commends weekly **communion**, a robust **Sursum Corda**, covenant renewal patterns of confession and absolution, and renewed attention to the **church calendar**. He is among the most persistent **Reformed** proponents of **paedocommunion**, arguing since the early 1980s that the Lord’s Supper belongs to the baptized household as a covenant meal. He insists that Paul’s warnings in 1 Corinthians address factionalism and sacrilege rather than cognitive developmental thresholds, and he reads Passover’s inclusion of children and the tabernacle’s graded access as forward-looking types of inclusive new-covenant feasting. These positions are inseparable from his broader claim that the world’s time is claimed by Christ through ecclesial **festal** ordering, that the calendar is a catechism, and that the Eucharist is the Church’s political economy of gift.

Jordan also develops a practical **liturgical** grammar. He identifies the deep pattern of biblical rites as sacrificial ascent: **sin offering**, **ascension offering**, and **peace offering** as a threefold movement mirrored in Christian confession, Word, and Table. Worship is where **kingdom** pedagogy occurs and where **cosmic-temple** knowledge is learned in the body. He thus urges architectural, musical, and ceremonial deliberation, contending that reverent **pageantry** is not aestheticism but obedience to a biblical **cultus**. This attention to rite and time coheres with his reading of Scripture as an enacted **drama** rather than a static manual, and it places his work in proximate conversation with **Alexander Schmemann’s** liturgical theology, though Jordan’s covenantal categories remain decidedly **Reformed** and anti-sacramentalist minimalism is resolutely rejected.

#### 5. Hermeneutics: interpretive maximalism under Christ’s rule
If there is a single methodological signature, it is Jordan’s **interpretive maximalism**. He pursues designed repetition, number symbolism, chiasm, and cross-textual echoes, treating **Scripture** as a symphonic whole. While indebted to **Vos** for the unity of redemptive history and to **Kline** for covenant architecture, Jordan pushes typology farther, arguing that the authorial intention of the divine **Author** legitimates a thicker network of allusions than a narrow historicism permits. The world is a liturgy and Scripture is its canonical score, so properly trained readers must hear harmonics, not only melody. This yields creative readings of **Genesis** as temple-building, of **Judges** as liturgical warfare, and of **Revelation** as the enthronement-liturgies of the ascended **Lamb** over empire.

Jordan often relates this hermeneutic to a threefold anthropology of **priest**, **king**, and **prophet**, organizing biblical episodes by their liturgical function. The result is an exegetical habit that links narrative and rite, law and symbol, ethics and architecture. Critics worry about over-interpretation, but Jordan’s reply is that authorial superintendence by the **Spirit** makes Scripture more, not less, symbolically dense. He thus stabilizes maximalism by recurring to fixed biblical matrices: the **tabernacle** and **temple** schemata, festival calendars, covenant oaths, and sacrificial sequences. For Jordan, the control on creativity is the canon’s own **cultic** grammar and the Church’s catechetical reception, not extrinsic a priori limits derived from modern historicism.

#### 6. Ecclesiology, baptism, and the Federal Vision debates
Jordan’s ecclesial writings argue for a high view of the **visible church** and baptismal incorporation. His positive thesis is that **baptism** places one in Christ’s public order with real covenantal benefits, that apostasy is covenantal treachery from a real standing, and that assurance is nurtured in the **means of grace** within the Church’s discipline. He does not describe baptism as mechanical **ex opere operato**, but he resolutely describes it as efficacious by divine promise within the **covenant**. These emphases contributed to the early 2000s **Federal Vision** controversies, in which critics alleged confusion of **justification** and **sanctification**, flattening of the decretal and covenantal perspectives, and a threat to sola fide. Jordan and allies countered that they preserved **forensic justification** while restoring biblical attention to corporate identity, **union with Christ**, and the real stakes of covenant life.

The debate intersected with his long advocacy of **paedocommunion** and his stress on **objective covenant**, which many **Presbyterian** bodies judged out of accord with the **Westminster Standards**. Yet Jordan emphasized perseverance through **faith** and the necessity of living participation in Christ, even as he challenged American revivalist individualism. Whatever one’s judgment on formulations, it is evident that his ecclesiology cannot be detached from his **liturgy** and **typology**: the Church is a polis, its rites are its law, and its sacraments are its national seals. Jordan thus relocates soteriology within a thick account of **peoplehood**, a move that has animated pastors seeking communal renewal and alarmed theologians guarding classical distinctions regarding **decree**, **covenant**, and the duplex nature of the church.

#### 7. Later works and legacy: Biblical Horizons and beyond
From the early 1990s Jordan disseminated his program through **Biblical Horizons** and **Rite Reasons**, along with conference lectures and collaborations with **Peter J. Leithart**, **Jeffrey J. Meyers**, and **Douglas Wilson**. Volumes like **Primeval Saints** (1999) distilled his Genesis exegesis into pastoral form, while liturgical manuals elaborated the grammar of **covenant renewal worship** for local churches. His influence is pronounced in **CREC** contexts and in renewal-minded Presbyterian congregations that adopted weekly **communion**, richer liturgical orders, and a renewed **church year**. Through New Eyes became a touchstone for younger scholars and pastors drawn to **symbolic** and **typological** reading, and his insistence that Scripture’s world is architectural and festive helped reframe debates about culture and mission.

Jordan’s more technical essays expanded his schema of **covenant maturity**, applied tabernacle patterns to book structures, and offered audacious readings of **Revelation**, **Psalms**, and **Chronicles**. He also pursued musical and numerical exegesis, arguing for a biblical **aesthetics** internal to revelation. This oeuvre has been quietly generative beyond his immediate circle. Scholars sympathetic to **canonical intertextuality** and to temple-oriented readings of Scripture have found overlapping trajectories, even if they maintain different controls or ecclesiologies. Jordan’s legacy is thus both concrete, in the spread of **covenant renewal worship** and **paedocommunion** advocacy, and diffuse, in the confidence that **typology** and **liturgy** belong at the center of **Reformed** theology rather than at the periphery.

#### 8. Method in context: affinities and contrasts
Jordan belongs within the **Vosian** turn to redemptive-historical exegesis and the **Klinean** attention to covenant patterns, yet he modifies both by foregrounding **liturgy** as ontology and by pressing **typology** as the norm rather than the supplement of biblical reading. Compared to **Rushdoony** and **Bahnsen**, his ethics is sacramentalized, arguing that canonically ordered **worship** forms the habitus from which wise jurisprudence flows. Compared to **modern evangelical** biblicism, his hermeneutic is thicker, more **symbolic**, and ecclesially moored. And compared to many **Westminster**-leaning confessionalists, he accentuates the **visible church** and the sacramental means as the locus of God’s public dealings, thereby reconfiguring debates about **assurance**, **discipline**, and the economy of **grace**.

These contrasts produce both attraction and resistance. Admirers point to the recovery of **ritual** and **festival**, the healing of false dichotomies between **cult** and culture, and the opening of Scripture as a vast **temple** whose every chamber resonates with Christ. Critics warn of **hermeneutical overreach**, the need for sharper **confessional** boundaries around **justification** and **sacraments**, and the practical risks of liturgical reforms disconnected from historic **Reformed** consensus. Jordan’s work thrives as a provocation to read more canonically, to worship more deliberately, and to see the Church’s common life as the center of theological labor.

In summary, Jordan’s enduring contribution is to center **liturgy** and **symbol** within **Reformed** theology, offering a biblical cosmology in which architectural patterns, calendrical time, and sacramental rites are the very grammar of covenant life. His is a call to look through new eyes, to discern the **cosmic temple** in Eden, Sinai, Zion, and the Church, and to live accordingly under the royal-priesthood of the crucified and risen **Christ**.

Final Reformed/WTS evaluation: Jordan’s strengths lie in his **canonical typology**, his reclamation of **liturgy** as formative, and his pastoral imagination for **visible-church** renewal. Yet from a classical **Westminster** perspective, several tensions persist. First, his **interpretive maximalism** sometimes outruns grammatical-historical controls, risking readings that lack falsifiability; a more explicit submission of typological proposals to the analogy of faith and the catholic **Reformed** tradition would stabilize the program. Second, his **objective covenant** and **paedocommunion** commitments press hard against the **Westminster Standards** on the relation of **sign** and **thing signified**, on admission to the **Table**, and on distinguishing the **invisible** and **visible** church; alternatives within confessional Reformed orthodoxy emphasize the efficacy of the **means of grace** while protecting sola fide and the forensic clarity of **justification**. Third, his postmillennial **cultural** optimism can mute the pilgrim note in **Reformed** piety and underestimate the mixed character of the church *ad hoc*. The best reception of Jordan at Westminster would appropriate his liturgical seriousness and canonical typology while guarding exegetical sobriety, sacramental precision, and the confessional architecture of **covenant**, **election**, and **assurance**.
