### TITLE
A confessional defense of the self-attesting, inerrant Old Testament and the unity of prophetic Scripture through grammatical-historical exegesis

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Modern American Reformed** setting at **Westminster Theological Seminary** amid **higher criticism** and **neo-orthodoxy**
- **Grammatical-historical exegesis** joined to **presuppositional confidence** in **Scripture's autopistia** and the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum**
- **An Introduction to the Old Testament** 1949, rev. 1958; **My Servants the Prophets** 1952; **Thy Word Is Truth** 1957; **Who Wrote Isaiah?** 1958; **The Book of Isaiah** vols. 1–3, 1965–1972; **The Prophecy of Daniel** 1949
- **Verbal plenary inspiration**, **inerrancy**, **unity of Isaiah**, **predictive prophecy**, **messianic fulfillment**, **analogia fidei**

- Reformed cue: alignment with **Warfield**, **Machen**, and **Van Til** on **self-attestation** and **Christ's view of Scripture**, shaping **OPC** and **WTS** hermeneutics

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Edward Joseph Young, born in 1907 and deceased in 1968, emerged as a leading Old Testament voice within mid twentieth-century **Reformed orthodoxy** at **Westminster Theological Seminary** in Philadelphia. Trained in classical languages and shaped by the Princeton-WTS transition under **J. Gresham Machen** and **O. T. Allis**, his scholarly vocation matured as the tide of **higher criticism** and **neo-orthodoxy** pressed on American seminaries. Young occupied the chair of **Old Testament** at Westminster from 1936 until his death, forming ministers of the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church** with a curriculum that wedded **grammatical-historical exegesis** to a confessional doctrine of **verbal plenary inspiration**. His classroom emphasis echoed **B. B. Warfield's** theological realism about **inerrancy**, yet it resonated with **Cornelius Van Til's** emphasis on **autopistia** and the **self-attestation of Scripture**, insisting that exegesis is never methodologically neutral. The dialectical Christianity of **Karl Barth** and the tradition-historical program of **Gerhard von Rad** and **Martin Noth** provided a foil against which his constructive biblical theology would be forged.

Young entered his career at the very moment when **Bernhard Duhm's** thesis of **Deutero- and Trito-Isaiah** had become near axiomatic in the guild, while the **Documentary Hypothesis** of **Julius Wellhausen** still framed Pentateuchal study. He watched the discovery of the **Dead Sea Scrolls** in 1947 reshape textual discourse and he embraced the opportunity to reassert classical **Reformed** teaching on the **authority of Scripture**. His confessional location under the **Westminster Confession of Faith** provided a doctrinal horizon for his work, in which the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum**, the inner witness of the Spirit, confirmed the **divine authority** and **clarity** of the inspired text. He operated with the **analogia fidei**, read within the canon, and he insisted that the Bible's claims about history and authorship are not expendable fictions but part of the **covenant** God's Word makes with the Church.

#### 2. Programmatic method and epistemic stance
At the heart of Young's method stood a twofold commitment to **grammatical-historical analysis** and **theological presupposition**. He contended in **Thy Word Is Truth** in 1957 that Scripture is not merely a record of human religious genius but the **Word of God written**, endowed with **plenary inspiration** down to its very words. Against the **neo-orthodox** construal of the Word as event rather than text, he argued from **Christ's view of Scripture** that the Old Testament is authoritative in its history, its ascriptions, and its predictive details. He appealed to Jesus' affirmation of **Moses**, of **Davidic authorship**, and of **Isaiah's** single voice as decisive hermeneutical cues, aligning with **Machen's** strategy that one cannot accept Christ's authority and simultaneously deny the **inerrant** Scriptures Christ endorses. Young's insistence on the **autopistia** of Scripture meant that he did not pretend to a neutral starting point. He defended the legitimacy of **presuppositional exegesis**, yet insisted that with those presuppositions one must handle morphology, syntax, and lexicography with scholastic rigor and due regard for **Masoretic** tradition and comparative **Semitic philology**.

Young's theological method embraced a robust **unity of revelation**, linking **covenant history** with **prophetic promise** and **messianic fulfillment**. In **My Servants the Prophets** from 1952 he presented the prophets not as community poets or anonymous redactors but as **authorized covenant emissaries**, bearers of the *dabar* of Yahweh whose utterances are inherently **oracular** and **predictive**, though often typologically enriched. He navigated between an atomistic literalism and a purely kerygmatic reading by distinguishing the immediate **historical horizon** of an oracle and its place within the **canon**, strengthened by the **analogia fidei**. The result was a hermeneutic that treated **predictive prophecy** as neither improbable nor peripheral but organic to Israel's Scriptures.

#### 3. Introduction and authorship against critical consensus
Young's **An Introduction to the Old Testament** in 1949, revised in 1958, entered a field dominated by **R. H. Pfeiffer's** critical introduction and by the canon of **Wellhausen**. Young catalogued arguments for **Mosaic authorship** and early sources, defended the historical core of **patriarchal narratives**, and treated the **Pentateuch** as a coherent product of the Mosaic era, allowing for minor post-Mosaic annotations without abandoning **Mosaicity**. He contended that alleged doublets, stylistic variations, and theological development did not prove the **Documentary Hypothesis**, which he saw as a concatenation of unproven assumptions about literary growth, parallel sources, and ideological reconstruction. He reasserted the older **unity** thesis by pointing to covenantal structure and recurring themes such as **divine promise**, **seed**, and **land**, arguing that these were better explained by a single, historically embedded **covenant revelation** than by speculative redactional layers.

The most public and distinctive battles of Young's career were fought around the book of **Isaiah**. In **Who Wrote Isaiah?** from 1958 and in his three-volume **The Book of Isaiah** from 1965 to 1972, he mounted a sustained case for the **unity of Isaiah**. Against **Duhm's** reconstruction of a second and third Isaiah centuries after the eighth-century prophet, Young demonstrated lines of **lexical**, **stylistic**, and **theological** coherence. He argued that the shift from judgment to comfort between chapters 39 and 40 does not signal new authorship but a deliberate compositional design already anticipated in earlier chapters where **Zion's eschatology** and **Servant themes** are present. He engaged **vocabulary studies** and **form-critical** categories, but he was most persuasive in his canonical mapping of **holiness**, **remnant**, **Davidic kingship**, and the **Servant of Yahweh** as a single orchestration. He also appealed to the **Dead Sea Scrolls**, noting the **Great Isaiah Scroll** 1QIsaa as a single continuous scroll, which, while not decisive for authorship, evidenced ancient reception of **Isaiah** as one book.

#### 4. Messianic prophecy and the Immanuel sign
In **The Book of Isaiah**, Young devoted careful attention to **Isaiah 7:14** and the **Immanuel** sign. He defended the translation of **almah** as "virgin" in its contextual sense and in view of its canonical usage, pushing back against the claim that the term means only "young woman." He argued that the prophet offers a sign that is both present in **Ahaz's** crisis and expansively **messianic** in the broader horizon of Isaiah's vision. For Young, the **analogia fidei** and Matthean fulfillment in **Matthew 1:23** do not violate grammatical-historical method but disclose the text's **eschatological depth**. He similarly treated the **Servant Songs** in Isaiah 40 to 55 as pointing personally and prophetically to the **Messiah**, not merely to corporate Israel, and he defended a single referent of **vicarious suffering** and **atonement** in **Isaiah 53** that culminates in the New Testament's christological appropriation. These expositions illustrate his conviction that **predictive prophecy** is not an embarrassment but a hallmark of the **Old Testament** economy.

Young's insistence on prediction likewise governed his commentary on **Daniel**. In **The Prophecy of Daniel** from 1949, he rejected the Maccabean dating and the pseudepigraphal theory so prevalent after **Porphyry** and revived in modern criticism. He argued for a **sixth-century** provenance and for the reality of **apocalyptic symbolism** that nevertheless speaks truly about empires and the coming **kingdom of God**. He contended that the four kingdoms schema fits the Babylonian, Medo-Persian, Greek, and Roman sequences, and he showed how Daniel's visions cohere with **postexilic** prophetic eschatology. Young criticized interpretive moves that trivialized **miracle** and **prophecy** under the pressure of modern **naturalism**, arguing that such moves misunderstand the **Creator-creature** relation and the **sensus divinitatis** that grounds confidence in the God who speaks and acts.

#### 5. The doctrine of Scripture and theological polemics
The theological center of Young's corpus is **Thy Word Is Truth**, a constructive defense of **inspiration**, **inerrancy**, and **authority**. He articulated **verbal plenary inspiration** by appealing to the biblical witness about itself, with special weight on **2 Timothy 3:16**, **2 Peter 1:21**, and Jesus' testimony to Scripture's **durability** and **precision** in **John 10:35**. He appropriated **Warfield's** classic distinction that inspiration pertains to the production of the writings, not the writers in isolation, while pressing the pastoral outcome that **inerrancy** is a corollary of God’s **veracity**. Against **neo-orthodox** claims that the Bible becomes the Word only in *Verkündigung*, he argued for the identity of the inscripturated **Word** with its divine speech acts. Young coupled this with a robust account of the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum**, insisting that the Spirit not only authenticates the Word to the Church but also illuminates the mind to grasp its **literal sense**, while the canon as a whole supplies the **analogia fidei** for doctrinal coherence.

Young's polemics against **higher criticism** were neither strident nor anti-intellectual. In **An Introduction to the Old Testament**, he engaged with **R. H. Pfeiffer**, **Otto Eissfeldt**, and **Gerhard von Rad**, acknowledging gains in **form criticism** and **tradition history** at the level of genre awareness and cultic context, yet he rejected their governing hypotheses which sever the final form from the prophet's own voice. He worried that the **history-of-religions** school subordinates revelation to community experience, which dissolves the **covenant** framework and eviscerates the **prophetic office** as a bearer of divine command and promise. He preferred the **Vosian** trajectory of **redemptive-historical** biblical theology that recognizes progressive revelation without surrendering the **unity** and **truthfulness** of the text.

#### 6. Text, canon, and the Dead Sea Scrolls
Young's attention to **textual criticism** was careful and conservative. He viewed the **Masoretic Text** as a reliable base line, welcomed comparative evidence from the **Septuagint** and **Qumran**, and cautioned against speculative emendation that arises from perceived difficulty rather than manuscript support. The **Dead Sea Scrolls** for Young were a providential witness that the Old Testament text had been faithfully transmitted and that books like **Isaiah** possessed early and respected canonical form. He recognized the complexities raised by Qumran variant readings yet stressed that none imperiled any **doctrine** of the faith, a point aligned with classic **Reformed** confidence in **Providential preservation** without implying a naive textual uniformity.

On canon formation he affirmed the **threefold Hebrew canon** as part of covenantal history, grounded in the prophetic deposit and recognized rather than created by the **postexilic** community. He read **Luke 24** as Christ's own threefold acknowledgment of the **Law**, **Prophets**, and **Writings**, thereby reinforcing his hermeneutic that Christian reading of the Old Testament is authorized by the **Messiah**. This canon-consciousness fed his commentaries, where textual and thematic links are traced not as contrived harmonizations but as the result of **divine authorship** coordinating human authors across time.

#### 7. Exegesis in practice and the classroom legacy
Young's exegesis in the classroom was marked by close **Hebrew** work, crisp articulation of **syntax**, and controlled theological inference. Students testify that when he handled **Genesis** or **Isaiah** he moved from the **qal** forms on the page to the **covenant** horizon of the passage without rhetorical flourish but with pastoral directness. His written work balances the same. In **The Book of Isaiah**, technical notes are restrained yet decisive, while theological comments stress **holiness**, **righteousness**, and **the glory of the Lord** as the unifying motif of the book. He used **typology** but resisted any approach that evacuated historical particularity, so that **Davidic** and **Zion** motifs remain anchored in eighth-century Judah even as they escalate toward **eschatological** consummation in Christ.

This practice of reading shaped a generation of **Westminster** and **OPC** ministers who learned to argue with **critical scholarship** without adopting its naturalistic premises, to read the Old Testament with the **New Testament** as divinely intended sequel, and to preach **Christ** from all of Scripture. Young became a living conduit of the **Princeton-WTS** heritage, integrating **Warfieldian** precision, **Van Tilian** presuppositionalism, and **Vosion** redemptive history into a single exegetical vocation. His death in 1968 curtailed further projects, but his commentaries and treatises retained currency among conservative scholars and pastors.

#### 8. Interlocutors, debates, and doctrinal development
Young contended directly with **Bernhard Duhm** over **Isaiah's** fragmentation, with **R. H. Pfeiffer** over the competence of historical criticism to adjudicate authorship, with **Gerhard von Rad** and **Martin Noth** over the dogmatic constraints of a **tradition-historical** method, and with **neo-orthodox** proposals influenced by **Karl Barth** that separated the event of revelation from the textual deposit. His responses reveal both continuity and development. Early works like **An Introduction to the Old Testament** are primarily defensive and synthetic, while later volumes like **The Book of Isaiah** exhibit a more patient canonical exposition where Christological trajectories are drawn with quiet confidence. Over time his articulation of **self-attestation** became more explicit, leaning into **Van Til's** emphasis that the triune God's speech is the ultimate criterion for truth. Yet he never adopted an adversarial posture to philology. He argued that **faith seeking understanding** requires more, not less, attention to **grammar** and **history**, subordinated to **theology** but never eclipsed.

Young's theological axiom was that God's **veracity** entails the **inerrancy** of his Word. He took the **Creator-creature distinction** as fundamental. The created order is rational and knowable because the triune God is wise and communicative. From this flows the expectability of **miracle**, the possibility of **prophecy**, and the unity of **revelation** across epochs. He repeatedly warned that the critical presumption against the supernatural is neither proved nor impartial but is a philosophical choice incompatible with **Christian theism**. In sum, his doctrinal development is a deepening of confessional commitments exercised in increasingly detailed exegesis.

#### 9. Legacy and reception
Young's legacy within **Reformed** churches and schools has remained strong. His commentaries on **Isaiah** are still in print and still cited for their defense of **unity** and their pastoral clarity. His **Thy Word Is Truth** continues to introduce students to a classic case for **inerrancy**, while **My Servants the Prophets** trains preachers to respect the prophetic office and message. In the broader academy, his work is often ignored or dismissed as confessional, yet his influence can be traced through the **Westminster** lineage of **Old Testament** scholarship that includes those who continue to defend **predictive prophecy** and **canonical unity** with updated tools. Conservative scholars in the **evangelical** movement cite Young alongside **Gleason Archer** and **Oswald T. Allis** as exemplars of responsible, believing introduction.

The arrival of more sophisticated **rhetorical** and **canonical** methods in later decades has, in some respects, vindicated Young's insistence on final-form coherence. Readers employing **Brevard Childs' canonical approach** and **James Muilenburg's rhetorical criticism** often find the kind of unity Young defended for theological reasons displayed on literary grounds. While he would disagree with some of their assumptions, the convergence underscores his central conviction that books like **Isaiah** are crafted wholes that demand and reward canonical reading. His labors show that **confessional exegesis** can be both rigorous and constructive.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed-WTS** vantage, Young's achievement is profoundly aligned with **classical Reformed orthodoxy** on **revelation**, **covenant**, and **Scripture**, yet there are tensions worth noting. His strong appeal to **Christ's view of Scripture** as an apologetic base is potent, though some might press for greater development of a principled **autopistia** doctrine independent of historical-Jesus debates, in line with **Van Til's** transcendental critique. His defense of **Mosaic authorship** and **Isaiah's unity** sets a high bar that can tempt students to undervalue the genuine insights of **form** and **tradition** criticism where these illuminate genre and setting. A WTS evaluation would commend his robust **inerrancy** and **predictive prophecy**, and it would suggest complementary attention to covenantal structures and typology as later elaborated by **Meredith Kline** and others to avoid flattening redemptive-historical development. On the issue of **textual criticism**, Young's caution is salutary, though a fuller appropriation of Qumran evidence could refine the doctrine of **preservation** without threatening **inerrancy**. In principle, however, his synthesis of **grammatical-historical method** with **confessional presuppositions** remains a model of **Reformed** exegesis, holding together divine **self-attestation**, canonical **unity**, and pastoral **authority** in a way that continues to serve the church's preaching of the **gospel**.
