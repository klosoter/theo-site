### TITLE
Law, covenant, and Gentile inclusion: a historically grounded defense of Pauline justification against reductionist New Perspective trajectories.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth to early twenty-first century evangelical** landscape shaped by the **New Perspective on Paul**, **Qumran discoveries**, and renewed **Second Temple Judaism** studies.
- Historical-grammatical exegesis disciplined by **Second Temple sources**, with a Lutheran **law-gospel** sensibility and attention to **Greco-Roman rhetoric** and **epistolary form**.
- **Paul, the Law, and the Covenant** (2001); **Paul and the Jews** (2003); **Solving the Romans Debate** (2007); **Galatians, Concordia Commentary** (2014).
- **Works of the law**, **covenantal nomism**, **dikaiosyne theou**, **pistis Christou**, **union with Christ** and **forensic justification**, **apocalyptic deliverance** and **salvation history**.
- Engagement with **E. P. Sanders**, **James D. G. Dunn**, **N. T. Wright**, **J. Louis Martyn**, **Douglas Moo**, **Thomas R. Schreiner**, **Stephen Westerholm**, and **Paul within Judaism** voices like **Mark Nanos** and **Paula Fredriksen**.
- Reformed cue: robust defense of **forensic justification** and critique of **boundary-marker reductionism**, with Lutheran inflections on **law-gospel** that invite **Reformed covenantal** dialogue.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
A. Andrew Das emerged as a significant evangelical interpreter of **Pauline theology** in the wake of **E. P. Sanders** and the **New Perspective on Paul**, when the guild was reconfiguring central terms like **works of the law** and **justification** in light of **Second Temple Judaism**. Teaching in the greater Chicago area and publishing from the late 1990s onward, Das developed a method that braided **historical-grammatical exegesis** with careful use of **Qumran** and **Hellenistic Jewish** sources, while maintaining a Lutheran **law-gospel** hermeneutic that foregrounds **forensic righteousness** in Christ. His work is both historically modest and theologically direct: he proceeds from epistolary analysis and **Greco-Roman rhetoric**, moves through lexical and intertextual study of **nomos**, **dikaiosyne theou**, and **pistis Christou**, and then draws theological conclusions about **covenant** and **Gentile inclusion**. The setting is crucial: the 1990s to 2010s saw evangelical scholars attempting to appropriate what was correct in **covenantal nomism** without surrendering **justification by faith apart from works**. Das positioned himself as a bridge voice, resisting revisionist extremes while absorbing the gains of the **Second Temple** turn.

Das’s methodological center is the claim that **Paul’s polemic about the law** is covenantal in scope and salvation-historical in shape. He mines **4QMMT** and the **Dead Sea Scrolls** to clarify what ancient Jews meant by *maase ha-torah*, showing how communal identity and covenantal fidelity were intertwined with legal praxis. Yet in engaging **Qumran**, **Josephus**, and **Philo**, he does not reduce **works of the law** to sociological boundary markers; rather, he argues that **Torah observance under the Sinai covenant** supplied both the badge and the basis of Jewish life, making Paul’s negation soteriologically decisive. His approach is thus a conscientious correction of **James D. G. Dunn’s** boundary-marker thesis, allied with other evangelical correctives like **Stephen Westerholm** and **Douglas Moo**, while still acknowledging **Sanders’s** insight that Jewish piety was not crass legalism. Theologically, the Lutheran pressure point remains: the contrast between **law** and **gospel** must be preserved where Paul preserves it, lest **grace** become ethnic pragmatics and **justification** collapse into ecclesiology.

#### 2. Paul, the law, and the covenant
Das’s early monograph, **Paul, the Law, and the Covenant** (2001), articulates his core thesis at book-length: that **Paul’s rejection of works of the law** is a rejection of **the Sinai covenant as salvific economy**, not simply of its boundary demarcations. Reading **Galatians 3–4** and **Romans 3–4** against **Genesis 15** and **Exodus 19–24**, he argues that Paul contrasts the **Abrahamic promise** and the **Mosaic covenant** at the level of redemptive administration. For Das, the grammar of **dikaiosyne theou** in Romans 3 coheres with a **forensic acquittal** that issues from God’s covenant fidelity to promise, fulfilled in the **hilasterion** of Christ’s cross. The center of gravity is **Christ’s propitiatory death** and **resurrection**, which terminate the pedagogic and condemning function of the **nomos** as covenant headship and inaugurate **new-creation life in the Spirit**. He therefore resists a move to make Romans 3 primarily about **ecclesial inclusion**, insisting that Paul’s rhetoric targets the deeper soteriological question: on what basis is any sinner justified before the **holy God**.

Within this framework, Das deploys the **Second Temple** record judiciously. He grants **Sanders’s** demolition of the caricature that Jews were proto-Pelagians, yet he contends that **covenantal nomism** still functions as a merit-infused system insofar as covenant status and final vindication are mediated by **Torah performance**. Reading **4QMMT** alongside **Galatians**, he contends that *erga nomou* cannot be pared down to circumcision, food laws, and Sabbath alone, even if those are emblematic. The Pauline negation is totalizing at the soteriological level: whether ceremonial or moral, **law-works** belong to the epoch that culminates in condemnation, and the **pneuma** introduces a qualitatively new order of righteousness. By this move, Das preserves the **forensic center** of **justification by faith** and directs the inclusio of **Gentiles** back to the **Abrahamic promise**, thereby binding **ecclesiology** to **soteriology** rather than substituting the former for the latter.

#### 3. Romans in historical and rhetorical focus
In **Solving the Romans Debate** (2007), Das turns from programmatic theology to rhetorical and epistolary analysis of **Romans**. He disputes single-cause reconstructions, arguing that **Romans** integrates multiple aims: fundraising and mission to **Spain** in **Romans 15**, adjudication of **weak and strong** tensions in **Romans 14**, and a sustained theological exposition of **righteousness**, **law**, and **life in the Spirit** in **Romans 1–8**. The point is not fragmentation but rhetorical unity; Paul threads pastoral, missionary, and doctrinal purposes through a coherent **apostolic apologia**. Das’s careful attention to **Jewish interlocutors** in **Romans 2–4** frames the polemic as a real argument against covenantal assurance grounded in **nomos**, and his reading of **Romans 9–11** refuses to domesticate the pathos of **Israel’s unbelief**, honoring both **divine sovereignty** and **irrevocable election** of Israel as a people. The theological upshot is that the **gospel** Paul defends is not a culturally neutral ethic but a claim about **God’s saving righteousness** in Christ for Jew and Gentile alike.

Das’s Romans work also evaluates the **apocalyptic Paul** trend associated with **J. Louis Martyn** and later **Beverly Roberts Gaventa**. He appreciates the accent on **cosmic deliverance** and the enslaving powers in **Romans 5–8**, yet he insists that **apocalyptic invasion** sits within a **salvation-historical** architecture that Paul never abandons. The **Second Adam** in **Romans 5** is not an alternative to covenant; it is **federal headship** that secures **forensic acquittal** and **new-creation life**. Similarly, **pistis Christou** must not be recruited to make Christ’s subjective faithfulness displace the believer’s **faith in Christ** as the instrument of justification. Das therefore reads **pistis Christou** as an objective genitive in key texts like **Galatians 2:16** and **Romans 3:22**, while recognizing that the **faithfulness of Jesus** is presupposed in the **gospel** proclamation. Here his Lutheran insistence on the **extra nos** achievement of Christ and the **instrumental** role of faith anchors his lexical judgments.

#### 4. Galatians and the law-gospel axis
The **Galatians** commentary in the **Concordia Commentary** series (2014) is Das’s magnum opus in sustained exegesis, showcasing his balance of **Greek philology**, **ancient Jewish context**, and **theological clarity**. He reads **Galatians 1–2** as an apostolic defense against rival missionaries who threatened to reclaim **Gentiles** for the **Sinai covenant**, and he treats **Galatians 3–4** as the heart of Paul’s **Abraham–Moses contrast**, where **promise** and **law** occupy distinct economies. His exegesis of **Galatians 2:16** resolves the threefold repetition of *ouk ex ergon nomou* as a rhetorical sledgehammer against any salvific reliance on **law-works**, and his exposition of **Galatians 3:10–14** foregrounds the **curse of the law** borne by Christ, thereby consolidating **forensic justification** and **substitutionary atonement**. Throughout, Das underscores the **Spirit’s gift** as the mark of the new age, replacing **circumcision** as boundary marker, but more crucially issuing in **new obedience** that flows from **union with Christ** rather than from **nomistic compulsion**.

Das’s treatment of **Galatians 5–6** resists both antinomian reduction and nomist relapse. He traces **virtue lists** and **vice lists** to Greco-Roman moral discourse yet insists their ultimate framework is **eschatological life in the Spirit**. The **works of the flesh** and the **fruit of the Spirit** are not competing moral programs but rival dominions, and the ethical imperative is grounded in the **indicative** of **crucifixion with Christ**. Here his Lutheran instincts show in the careful guarding of **law-gospel** distinction, yet he gives respectful space to the Reformed talk of the **third use of the law**, acknowledging that Paul deploys **nomos Christou** as a norming power when refracted through **agape** and **Spirit-led** community life. The commentary thus embodies a confessional Lutheran voice conversant with **evangelical** and **Reformed** concerns while rigorously tethered to **Paul’s Greek** and **Second Temple** horizons.

#### 5. Judaism, identity, and the shape of Paul’s rupture
A recurring theme across Das’s corpus is the question of **Paul and Judaism**, developed in the accessible synthesis **Paul and the Jews** (2003). Das opposes vilifying caricatures of ancient Judaism, yet he resists the **Paul within Judaism** movement’s tendency to narrate Paul as a Jewish reformer whose critique never breaches covenantal boundaries. For Das, Paul’s Damascus encounter with the **risen Christ** reorients his reading of **Torah** so thoroughly that **Christ** becomes the end of the **law for righteousness** to all who believe. The rupture is theological and covenantal: **Sinai’s administration** cannot sustain the **eschatological righteousness** now revealed. At the same time, Das maintains Paul’s love for **Israel** and his confidence in **God’s irrevocable gifts and calling**, so that **Romans 11** continues to promise a future **ingathering**. This mediating posture grants the **Second Temple** textures their due while preserving the evangelical center of **grace alone** through **faith alone** in **Christ alone**.

Das’s middle path also recasts the **works of the law** debate. Against **boundary-marker** minimalism, he insists on the comprehensive nature of **Torah-works** in Paul’s argumentation, citing patterns in **Qumran halakhah** that connect identity practices to wider **covenant fidelity**. Yet against readings that make Judaism a religion of merit, he follows **Sanders** in locating Jewish obedience within **covenantal nomism**. The Pauline conclusion then is not that Jews sought to bribe God but that **the covenant of law** cannot reconcile sinners and must give way to **Christ’s righteousness** imputed to believers. This double move both honors the integrity of **Second Temple Judaism** and vindicates the **Reformation** stakes in **justification**.

#### 6. Key debates: pistis Christou, righteousness, and participation
On the contested genitive in **pistis Christou**, Das aligns with an **objective genitive** reading in **Galatians** and **Romans**, defending the classic evangelical and Lutheran claim that **faith in Christ** is the instrument of **justification**. He does so by surveying **Septuagintal** uses of **pistis**, Pauline syntax, and discourse flow, showing that Paul consistently contrasts **faith** with **works** as responses to God's **grace**. He concedes that **Christ’s faithfulness** is the ground of salvation, but he argues that Paul typically articulates that reality with other terms, such as **obedience** and **cross** rather than by a genitive construction. In parallel, on **dikaiosyne theou**, Das argues for a reading that encompasses both **God’s covenant faithfulness** and the **righteous status** he grants, but he prioritizes the **forensic verdict** at the center of Romans 3–4. This keeps **justification** at the point where **assurance** is secured by the **extra nos** righteousness of Christ, a hallmark of Lutheran and evangelical **soteriology**.

At the same time, Das refuses a zero-sum game between **justification** and **union with Christ**. He reads **en Christo** as the relational sphere where the **forensic** and the **transformative** coinhere, with **Romans 6–8** narrating the believer’s **Spirit-wrought participation** in Christ’s death and life. This is neither a capitulation to **apocalyptic participationism** nor a retreat to moralism; rather, it is an exegetical conclusion from **baptismal union** and **pneumatology** in Paul. The integrity of **imputation** and the reality of **renewal** stand together under the lordship of **Christ**.

#### 7. The apocalyptic horizon within salvation history
Das’s reading of the **apocalyptic** dimensions of Paul emphasizes the deliverance from enslaving powers in **Romans 8** and the new creation declared in **Galatians 6:15**, though he resists the tendency to detach these from **salvation history**. He argues that **apocalyptic invasion** is the mode by which God consummates his **Abrahamic promise**, not a rupture that negates **covenant**. Thus, **Adam–Christ** typology in **Romans 5** and **exodus** motifs in **Galatians** are not ornamental but structural: **law** magnifies sin, **Christ** absorbs the curse, and the **Spirit** inaugurates **eschatological sonship**. This integrative posture enables Das to engage **J. Louis Martyn** and others charitably while safeguarding the legal and declarative center of **justification**, preserving the **Reformation** core that **righteousness** is reckoned, even as **sanctification** unfolds within the same **union**.

Accordingly, Das situates **ecclesiology** downstream of **soteriology**. The new family of **Jew and Gentile** is the effect of **forensic grace** and **Spirit adoption**, not the hermeneutical canon for reconstructing **Paul’s gospel**. He pushes back on trajectories that compress **dikaiosyne** into communal inclusion and on readings that dissolve **law-gospel** into ecclesial pragmatics. For Das, **church** is created by **gospel**; it does not create it.

#### 8. Contribution and synthesis
Across two decades of writing, Das has offered a consistent and richly evidenced account of **Paul** that tests **New Perspective** claims against the granular texture of **Second Temple Judaism**, while conserving the **forensic** and **substitutionary** stakes of **Reformation** exegesis. The signature contributions include a total-covenantal reading of **works of the law**, a multilayered rhetorical account of **Romans** that ties **mission**, **pastoral care**, and **doctrine** together, and an exegetically disciplined defense of **objective-genitive pistis Christou**. His **Galatians** commentary in particular shows how **law-gospel** clarity can live fruitfully alongside robust attention to **Greco-Roman rhetoric**, **Jewish halakhah**, and Pauline **pneumatology**. The result is a contemporary evangelical Lutheran voice that proves both critical and constructive: critical toward **boundary-marker minimalism** and **ecclesial reductionism**, constructive in mediating **apocalyptic deliverance** with **salvation history**, and relentlessly exegetical in service of **justification by faith**.

Reformed and wider evangelical scholars have found in Das a partner for repairing the fissures introduced by the **New Perspective** debates. He shares with **Moo**, **Schreiner**, and **Westerholm** the conviction that **justification** is centrally **forensic** and **gracious**. He shares with **Wright** and **Dunn** the historical insistence that **Second Temple Judaism** must be heard on its own terms. And he shares with **Martyn** an appreciation for the **cosmic scope** of the gospel. The difference is his refusal to let any of these strands override the textual logic by which **Paul** announces **Christ crucified and risen** as the end of **law** for **righteousness** to everyone who believes.

#### 9. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Das’s work commendably safeguards **sola fide**, **imputation**, and the **federal contours** of **Romans 5** against both **boundary-marker** minimalism and **apocalyptic** overreach. His defense of **forensic justification**, his careful delimitation of **pistis Christou** as faith in Christ, and his insistence that **ecclesiology** flows from **soteriology** align with classical **Reformed orthodoxy** on revelation, grace, and history. A friendly caution arises where Lutheran **law-gospel** instincts can underplay the **third use of the law** and the covenantal architecture of **creation–fall–redemption** often expounded in Reformed **covenant of works/covenant of grace** terms. While Das does not traffic in dogmatic schemas, his exegesis could be fruitfully extended by stronger articulation of **creational law** and **covenant continuity** in ethics, themes the Reformed tie to **natural law** and **Spirit-enabled obedience**. Nevertheless, the principal lines converge: **Scripture’s sufficiency**, **redemptive-historical unity**, and **Christ’s righteousness imputed** to sinners. Das therefore stands as a reliable ally for Reformed readers who seek historically serious and confessionally robust guidance through the thickets of **Pauline law, covenant, and justification**.
