### TITLE
Reformed catholicity as biblical reasoning in service of the Triune God: retrieving classical theism for the church's reading, worship, and witness.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Context**: Contemporary **Reformed catholicity** within late modern evangelicalism, marked by retrieval of **classical theism** and postcritical **theological interpretation of Scripture**.
- **Method**: **Dogmatic exegesis** under the **rule of faith**, deploying **analogical naming**, **canon and creed**, and **biblical reasoning** disciplined by **divine simplicity** and **Trinitarian processions**.
- **Key works**: **Trinity, Revelation, and Reading** 2011; **The God of the Gospel: Robert Jenson's Trinitarian Theology** 2013; **Reformed Catholicity** 2015; **Christian Dogmatics** 2016; **Retrieving Eternal Generation** 2017; **The Trinity: An Introduction** 2020; **The Trinity and the Bible** 2021.
- **Central terms**: **divine aseity**, **divine simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, **eternal generation**, **processions and missions**, **appropriation**, **analogia fidei**, *opera ad extra indivisa sunt*.
- **Interlocutors**: **Aquinas**, **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, **Calvin**, **Bavinck**, **Owen**, **Turretin**, along with constructive engagements with **Robert Jenson**, **John Webster**, and **Fred Sanders**.
- **Reformed reception**: Strong alignment with **confessional Reformed orthodoxy** on **Trinity** and **attributes**, welcomed in **Westminster Standards** circles with careful discussion of **natural theology** and **covenantal epistemology**.

### ESSAY
Scott R. Swain has emerged as one of the clearest voices for **Reformed catholicity**, arguing that the church must retrieve **classical theism** in order to read Scripture well and worship the **Triune God** rightly. His enduring claim is that **biblical reasoning** flourishes when disciplined by the **rule of faith**, by **creedal** and **confessional** grammar, and by metaphysical clarity about **divine simplicity** and **Trinitarian processions**. Situated in the opening decades of the twenty-first century, Swain writes amid the crosswinds of evangelical biblicism, postliberal hermeneutics, and renewed attention to **Aquinas** and **Reformed scholasticism**, contending that catholic doctrine is the church's hermeneutical home. In both his scholarly monographs and pedagogical texts, he articulates how the church's confession of **Father, Son, and Holy Spirit** orders the whole of theology from *ad intra* processions to *ad extra* missions.

Formationally, Swain inhabits the institutional and ecclesial nexus of **Reformed Theological Seminary** and the confessional Presbyterian world, where he has taught and led a generation of ministers toward **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** fidelity. His intellectual debts are ecumenically Reformed and catholic. He follows **Athanasius** and **Augustine** on **eternal generation**, **Calvin** on **autotheos**, **Bavinck** on **Triune fullness**, and **Owen** and **Turretin** on **covenant** and **divine attributes**. He has also been shaped by **John Webster**'s call for renewing **dogmatics** and **biblical interpretation** as holy reason before God, and he pursues critical engagement with modern proposals such as **Robert Jenson**'s narratival ontology. Methodologically, his stance is a careful pairing of *regula fidei* with the *analogia fidei*, insisting that the canon interpreted in the church is the servant of the **Triune Lord** who names himself in revelation.

Swain's method can be summarized as **dogmatic exegesis** in the mode of **theological interpretation of Scripture**, which he names **biblical reasoning**. In **Trinity, Revelation, and Reading** 2011, he argues that Scripture is the **speech of the Triune God** whose perfection grounds its sufficiency, clarity, and authority. He maintains that the literal sense climaxes in the **Christological** and **pneumatological** fulfillment of the Old Testament, and he retrieves patristic rules of reading that respect both the unity and diversity of the canon. The technical grammar of **divine names** is vital here. Following **Aquinas** and **Bavinck**, Swain describes how we name God truly yet analogically, rejecting univocity and equivocity alike. The **analogical naming of God** safeguards **divine simplicity** and the **Creator creature distinction**, while ensuring that the names Father, Son, and Spirit refer to real **relations of origin** *ad intra* and to personal **missions** *ad extra*.

The constructive hinge of Swain's work is his dogmatic account of the **Trinity**. In **The Trinity: An Introduction** 2020, he offers a catechetical synthesis that begins from Scripture's presentation of the **one God** and proceeds to expound **consubstantial** triune relations. He foregrounds **divine aseity** and **simplicity** as axioms of unity, then explicates **eternal generation** and **spiration** as the two **processions** that ground the personal distinctions. On this basis he retrieves **inseparable operations** and **appropriation** to explain the economy, teaching that the external works of the Trinity are undivided and that certain works are fitting to be attributed to one person because of the order of origin. By tying **missions** to **processions**, Swain resists both **subordinationist** trends and flattening accounts of divine action, while securing the biblical salience of the Father sending the Son and the Spirit.

Swain has also served as a leading voice in the contemporary defense of **eternal generation**. As coeditor of **Retrieving Eternal Generation** 2017 with **Fred Sanders**, he assembled historical, exegetical, and systematic essays to rebut recent denials of the doctrine. In these debates he opposed the so called doctrine of **eternal functional subordination**, arguing that Scripture teaches the Son's eternal origin from the Father rather than an eternal hierarchy of authority. He contends that the taxis of the persons is rooted in **relations of origin** rather than in gradations of will, and that the doctrine of **inseparable operations** rules out three wills or three centers of decision in God. The practical upshot is doxological. Worship is ordered by who the persons are *ad intra* and by the shape of their saving missions *ad extra*.

The other axis of Swain's contribution is his constructive engagement with **Robert Jenson**. In **The God of the Gospel: Robert Jenson's Trinitarian Theology** 2013, he offers appreciative yet firm critique of Jenson's proposal that the identity of the **God of Israel** is eschatologically constituted by the **narrative** of Jesus. Swain praises Jenson's evangelical intent to locate God in the gospel but warns that identifying divine reality with temporal history threatens **immutability**, **impassibility**, and **aseity**. By retrieving **Athanasius** and **Aquinas** on **divine simplicity**, Swain argues that God is perfect act whose life is fully possessed without temporal becoming. The gospel narrates the missions whereby the simple God gives himself, but the divine being is not pieced together by those missions. The eternal **processions** provide the ontological ground for the temporal **missions**, and thus the story of salvation proceeds from and reveals the immanent life of God without composing it.

Swain's hermeneutical vision is programmatically cast in **Reformed Catholicity** 2015, coauthored with **Michael Allen**, and in the edited volume **Christian Dogmatics** 2016. The former argues that catholicity is a principle of reform, not its foil, advocating retrieval of **patristic** and **scholastic** resources under **Scripture** as supreme norm. The latter curates essays that model confessional **Reformed** theology for the church catholic, including his own work on the **Trinity** and **attributes**. In **The Trinity and the Bible** 2021, Swain deepens the claim that Scripture's trinitarian content is best discerned through **canonical** and **dogmatic** attentiveness. He demonstrates how **inseparable operations** function as a reading rule, for example in interpreting the **baptism of Jesus** and **Pentecost**, where the undivided action of the one God is personally instantiated according to the order of origin and mission.

A consistent theme in Swain is the evangelical fruit of **divine simplicity**. Far from a speculative add-on, simplicity underwrites the truth that God is wholly trustworthy and self-identical, so that all the **divine attributes** are identical with the one simple essence. He shows how **aseity**, **immutability**, **impassibility**, **omniscience**, and **omnipotence** stand or fall together. In preaching and catechesis, this grammar guards the gospel's comfort. The God who loves in Christ is the same in himself and toward us. Swain aligns the classical axiom with **covenantal** teaching, for the **pactum salutis** bears witness to the one will of God undividedly purposed and fittingly appropriated to the Father as origin, the Son as mediator, and the Spirit as perfecter. The scholastic tool of **appropriation** helps pastors say in the same breath that God acts undividedly and that the Father sends, the Son is sent, and the Spirit proceeds to complete.

Beyond metaphysics, Swain is a theologian of **worship** and **communion**. Borrowing from **Owen** on **communion with the Triune God**, he frames doctrine as an ordered participation in the life of God through the **means of grace**. The **divine missions** are not only events to be described but gifts to be received. Baptism is the triune name bestowed, the Lord's Supper the triune fellowship enacted, Scripture the triune speech heard. Here the grammar of **processions and missions** reaches the parish. The Father adopts through the sending of the Son and the gift of the Spirit, and the church replies with praise that is itself a participation in the Son's filial cry through the Spirit of adoption. In this key Swain's **catechesis** and **preaching** insist that doctrine is doxology and ethics, that orthodoxy orders orthopraxy.

Swain's theological habitus is integrative and pastoral. He directs retrieval toward ministerial formation, training students to handle **Scripture**, to confess the **creeds**, and to inhabit the **Westminster Standards** with integrity. His essays regularly model how to move from **text** to **dogma** to **homiletics**, linking a sound doctrine of God to the reading of Scripture's history and to the cure of souls. He promotes the classic maxim *opera ad extra indivisa sunt* as a classroom rule for interpreting biblical narratives and for avoiding both modalism and tritheism. By commending the fit of **metaphysics** to **exegesis**, he hopes to heal evangelical divides and to anchor the church's ministries in the **Triune God**.

Reformed and Westminster evaluation. Swain's project coheres strongly with **classical Reformed orthodoxy** on **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **eternal generation**, and **inseparable operations**, and it exemplifies the **Westminster** commitment to the primacy of **Scripture** read within the **catholic church**. His emphasis on **analogical naming** and on the ordered relation of **processions and missions** avoids both biblicist reduction and speculative excess. Still, two tensions invite Reformed reflection. First, while Swain distinguishes *analogia fidei* from a stand alone *analogia entis*, his robust deployment of **Thomistic** tools presses the question of how **natural theology** is normed by **covenant** and **redemptive history** in a distinctly **Van Tillian** key. Second, his hermeneutical stress on **dogmatic rules** could be more explicitly coordinated with **redemptive historical** exegesis in the line of **Vos**, ensuring that metaphysical claims are transparently tethered to the progressive unfolding of the **covenants**. Within the **Westminster** tradition, a constructive alternative is not rejection but calibration: retain Swain's **classical theism** and **Nicene** grammar while heightening the primacy of **covenantal revelation** as the principium cognoscendi, so that metaphysics consistently serves the triune God's self attestation in **Scripture** and the church's mission in history.
