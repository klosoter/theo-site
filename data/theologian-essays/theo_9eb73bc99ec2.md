### TITLE
Retrieving the consensual tradition: a Wesleyan paleo-orthodoxy ordered by the Vincentian canon and the rule of faith

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Mid-to-late twentieth-century American mainline crisis and postliberal turn, **Methodist Drew University**, from **Tillichian** liberalism to **patristic ressourcement** and ecclesial reform.
- **Consensual or Vincentian method**, Scripture read within the **regula fidei**, prioritizing **ecumenical creeds and councils** and resisting modern theological novelty.
- **Systematic Theology** 3 vols 1987, 1989, 1992; **After Modernity... What?** 1990; **Requiem** 1995; **Classical Pastoral Care** 1987–1994; **The Rebirth of Orthodoxy** 2003; **How Africa Shaped the Christian Mind** 2007; **John Wesley's Teachings** 2012–2014; **A Change of Heart** 2014.
- Central categories: **paleo-orthodoxy**, **Vincentian canon**, **regula fidei**, **classical theism**, **prevenient grace**, **entire sanctification**, **canonical exegesis**, **consensual hermeneutics**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Will Herberg**, **H. Richard Niebuhr**, early **Tillich** and **Bultmann**, later **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, **Chrysostom**, **Wesley**, **Vincent of Lérins**, and collaborators in the **Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong common cause in **Nicene and Chalcedonian dogma**, **classical attributes**, and **anti-modern liberalism**, yet tension over **synergism**, **prevenient grace**, and the authority of **ecumenical consensus** vis-a-vis **sola Scriptura**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, crisis, and conversion to tradition
Thomas C. Oden, born in 1931 and deceased in 2016, moved from the center of twentieth-century **mainline Protestant** confidence into a self-described **paleo-orthodoxy** shaped by the **Vincentian canon**. Educated at **Southern Methodist University** and receiving a PhD at **Yale** in 1960 under figures like **H. Richard Niebuhr**, Oden early absorbed the languages of **Tillichian correlation**, **Bultmannian demythologizing**, and the therapeutic **pastoral counseling** paradigms proliferating in the 1960s. His later memoir **A Change of Heart** 2014 narrates a decisive rebuke from the Jewish social philosopher **Will Herberg** at **Drew University** in the late 1960s who urged him to listen to the **ancient teachers** rather than to the latest ideologies. The result was a deliberate turn toward **patristic ressourcement**, the **regula fidei**, and the **ecumenical creeds**, culminating in an evangelical and catholic Methodist profile that took shape just as the **American mainline** entered institutional decline.

Oden named modern theological novelty a **project of self-authorization**, contrasting it with **Scripture** read in communion with the **consensual tradition**. In **After Modernity... What?** 1990 and **Requiem** 1995, he argued that mainline Protestantism had been captured by **ideological modernity**, especially historicism, romantic subjectivism, and psychologized ministry, at the cost of **classical Christian orthodoxy**. His remedy was ecclesial and hermeneutical: return to the **rule of faith** that framed **creedal formulations** at **Nicaea 325**, **Constantinople 381**, **Ephesus 431**, and **Chalcedon 451**, and receive **Scripture** through the **consensus fidelium** under the guidance of **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, and **the Cappadocians**. This epistemic stance, christened **paleo-orthodoxy**, resisted the lure of originality in favor of transmission, what he summarized as the patristic maxim, do not innovate, conserve.

#### 2. Method: Vincentian canon, regula fidei, and canonical exegesis
Oden's methodological center was the **Vincentian canon**, quod ubique, quod semper, quod ab omnibus, which he took as a disciplined way to identify **doctrinal consensus** across time, geography, and communions. He insisted that **Scripture** is materially and formally authoritative, yet Scripture is not a private text, it is the church's book interpreted through the **regula fidei** and the **baptismal creed**. His editorial leadership of the **Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture** series from 1998 forward embodied this method, setting **patristic exegesis** alongside the biblical text to model **canonical reading** within the **great tradition**. He extended the project with **Ancient Christian Doctrine** and **Ancient Christian Texts**, consolidating an infrastructure for **retrieval theology** that shaped a generation of pastors and scholars.

Crucially, Oden refused to collapse **consensus** into mere majoritarianism. Theologically, he ranked the **ecumenical councils** and the **classical dogmas** of the **Trinity** and **Christology** as nonnegotiable, while identifying a second-order of topics, for example **atonement theories**, where multiple images coexist within one apostolic gospel. He rejected **open theism** and defended **divine foreknowledge**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** in the mode of **classical theism**, arguing that centuries of prayerful contemplation had achieved a stable grammar for speaking of God. At the same time he received **John Wesley** as a practical theologian of the **consensual tradition**, reading **Wesleyan Arminianism** as a spirituality of **prevenient grace** and **holiness** that does not contradict, but rather operationalizes, **Nicene-Chalcedonian** dogma.

#### 3. Systematic Theology: The Living God, The Word of Life, Life in the Spirit
Between 1987 and 1992 Oden released his three-volume **Systematic Theology** through Harper: **The Living God** 1987, **The Word of Life** 1989, and **Life in the Spirit** 1992. Each volume displays his **consensual method** by canvassing the **fathers**, medieval doctors such as **Thomas Aquinas**, Reformation witnesses like **Calvin** and **Wesley**, and ecumenical summaries from **Lutheran** to **Orthodox** traditions. In **The Living God**, he defends **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **omniscience**, and **providence**, stressing that biblical revelation necessitates the classical attributes when properly framed by **apophatic reserve** and **cataphatic confession**. He insists that **the Trinity** is not a speculative appendix but the architectonic of Christian life, and he articulates **procession** and **mission** dynamics with the vocabulary of *homoousios* and *perichoresis* as received in **Nicene orthodoxy**.

In **The Word of Life**, Oden presents **Christology** in Chalcedonian terms, fully **homoousios** with the Father according to the Godhead and **homoousios** with us according to the humanity, one person in two natures without confusion, change, division, or separation. He insists on the historical reliability of the **resurrection**, opposes **docetism** and **Arianism**, and integrates **soteriology** through a tapestry of **patristic atonement motifs**: **recapitulation**, **ransom**, **satisfaction**, and **moral transformation**. He retrieves **Augustine's** accent on **grace** while preserving **Wesley's** insistence on **assurance** and **holiness**, affirming that **justification by faith** is the constitutive declaration while **sanctification** is the Spirit's participatory work toward **entire sanctification** understood as perfect love.

In **Life in the Spirit**, Oden treats **pneumatology**, **ecclesiology**, and **sacramental life**. He presents the **Spirit** as the bond of communion who gives **regeneration**, **illumination**, and **charisms**, while distributing ministries for the edification of the **one, holy, catholic, and apostolic church**. His sacramental account is intentionally **realist**, locating **baptism** and the **Eucharist** within the **consensus fidelium**. He affirms **baptismal incorporation** and the sanctifying presence of Christ in the Supper, blending **Augustinian** and **Wesleyan** accents, and argues for liturgical retrieval as a school of **orthodoxy and orthopraxy**. Throughout, the **rule of faith** disciplines exegesis, catechesis, and pastoral theology into a coherent whole.

#### 4. Pastoral retrieval and the care of souls
Beyond dogmatics, Oden worked to recover **classical pastoral care** as a means of ecclesial renewal. In **Pastoral Theology: Essentials of Ministry** 1983 and **Care of Souls in the Classic Tradition** 1984, together with the multi-volume **Classical Pastoral Care** series beginning in 1987, he contends that the arts of **cure of souls** were already richly developed by **Gregory the Great**, **Chrysostom**, and **Augustine**, and that modern therapy had obscured the spiritual **disciplines** of confession, forgiveness, catechesis, and spiritual direction. He substitutes **patristic casuistry** and **virtue formation** for psychologized models, insisting that the **pastor** is first a theologian of the **Word** and **sacrament**, and only derivatively a caregiver. The retrieval of **ancient catechumenate** patterns exemplifies his pastoral strategy: doctrine embeds in **liturgy**, and **liturgy** forms a people capable of **holiness**.

These pastoral commitments fed into Oden's activism within United Methodism. He helped found the **Confessing Movement** in 1994, seeking to call the denomination back to **Trinitarian confession**, **Christ's lordship**, and **scriptural authority**. His **Requiem: A Lament in Three Movements** 1995 is both diagnosis and summons, lamenting **bureaucratized denominationalism** and **ideological captivity** while urging a return to **creedal orthodoxy** and **evangelical mission**. For Oden, pastoral health and doctrinal fidelity are inseparable: **heresy** damages the flock by disfiguring **worship** and **holiness**.

#### 5. Africa and the catholicity of the consensual tradition
In the 2000s Oden turned programmatically to **African Christianity** as a decisive fountain of the **great tradition**. His **How Africa Shaped the Christian Mind** 2007 and **The African Memory of Mark** 2011 argue that **North African** and **Egyptian** teachers, **Athanasius**, **Cyril**, **Tertullian**, **Cyprian**, **Augustine**, and the **Alexandrian catechetical school**, decisively formed **Christian exegesis**, **Trinitarian doctrine**, and **monastic spirituality**. By restoring Africa's role, Oden intended to correct Eurocentric narratives and to strengthen the claim that **consensus** is truly global. The project also carried ecumenical freight: if the **Vincentian canon** is to be credible, the catholicity of its witnesses must include the African and Eastern memories that preserved **Nicene faith** through crisis.

This African turn also provided an implicit test-case for Oden's **consensual method**. He interpreted patristic Africa as demonstrating that **Scripture** received in **catechesis**, **liturgy**, and **communal discipline** yields stable dogmatic outcomes across cultures. The outcome is a chastened confidence in **catholic dogma**, without collapsing diversity in rites and spirituality, and a renewed case for **ecumenical convergence** on classical doctrine alongside legitimate **prudential pluralism** in contested second-order questions.

#### 6. Wesley as a doctor of the church
Oden's **John Wesley's Teachings** four volumes 2012–2014 presents **Wesley** as a catechist and expositor of **consensual orthodoxy** rather than a sectarian innovator. Volume one on **God and Providence**, volume two on **Christ and Salvation**, volume three on **Pastoral Care and Leadership**, and volume four on **Ethics and Society**, assemble **Wesley's sermons, journals, and treatises** into a coherent doctrinal digest. Oden identifies **prevenient grace** as the structuring insight, the Spirit's universal initiative that awakens the will and enables **repentance**, **faith**, and **holiness**. He safeguards **justification by faith** as a forensic act and frames **sanctification** as the Spirit's work issuing in **perfect love**, which he carefully distinguishes from sinless perfectionism. The **means of grace** are the God-ordained conduits for this growth, located in **Word**, **sacrament**, and **works of mercy**, in continuity with **patristic spirituality**.

By interpreting **Wesley** through the **Vincentian canon**, Oden resists romanticized individualism and aligns **Methodism** with **catholic dogma**. He presents **assurance** not as a subjective enthusiasm but as a derivative of **Christ's objective promise** mediated through **baptismal identity** and **Eucharistic fellowship**. The upshot is a Methodist confidence that sits comfortably within **Nicene faith** while maintaining **Arminian** protest against any construal of grace that would compromise **universal offer** and **responsible response**.

#### 7. Scripture, tradition, and theological authority
Oden's account of theological authority is a layered **canon within the canon** of the church's memory. **Scripture** is the norming norm, the **norma normans**, yet it is inseparable from the **regula fidei** as a hermeneutical principle embodied in **creed**, **liturgy**, and **catechesis**. He appeals to **Augustine** and **Athanasius** as exemplars of exegesis that is doctrinally responsible without forfeiting historical sense. His polemic against **historicism** rejects the reduction of the biblical text to its sources and settings in favor of **canonical reading** that confesses **theodrama**. At the same time, he honors critical scholarship, provided it is subordinated to **ecclesial reading**, a posture on display throughout the **Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture** where philology serves the **rule of faith**.

Regarding doctrinal development, Oden prefers **reception** to **innovation**. He acknowledges growth in clarity, as **Nicaea** sharpened **homoousios** and **Chalcedon** refined **communicatio idiomatum**, yet he resists proposals that present themselves as forward leaps beyond classical **dogma**. He reads the **Vincentian canon** not as a static museum but as a criterion for distinguishing **catholic continuity** from **novelty**. The church's task is not to outflank the fathers but to be taught by them in the reading of **Scripture**.

#### 8. Ethics, culture, and ecclesial reform
In ethics, Oden seeks continuity with **patristic moral theology** and **Wesleyan holiness**. He advocates **virtue ethics** ordered by **theological virtues**, retrieved through **catechesis** and **ascetic disciplines**, rather than contemporary issue-driven casuistry detached from **dogma**. He faulted the mainline for adopting **politicized theologies** at the expense of **catechetical depth**. In **The Rebirth of Orthodoxy** 2003 he predicted a spiritual realignment as evangelicals, confessional Protestants, and Catholics converge on **Nicene faith**. He viewed **liturgy**, **catechism**, and **pastoral care** as the nonnegotiable instruments of reform, and he located **mission** in the proclamation of **Christ's saving work**, not in the management of therapeutic needs.

His public theology is therefore ecclesial rather than programmatic. He contends that renewal requires repentance for **theological modernism**, the acceptance of **creedal authority**, and the rebuilding of **habitus** through **prayer**, **Scripture**, and **sacrament**. The prophetic edge of **Requiem** lies in its claim that doctrinal compromise breeds institutional sclerosis, whereas **orthodoxy** fosters **evangelical vitality**. For Oden, the church is most public when it is most **catholic** and **evangelical** in its own life.

#### 9. Legacy, influence, and limits
Oden's intellectual legacy is twofold. First, he provided an accessible, pastorally oriented **Systematic Theology** that models **consensual method** in a comprehensive way. Second, he built infrastructures of **retrieval** through the **Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture**, catalyzing a renaissance in **patristics** among evangelicals and Methodists. His role in the **Confessing Movement** and his witness in **United Methodism** made **creedal orthodoxy** publicly visible within a contested denomination. The turn to **African Christianity** expanded the scope of **catholic memory** and corrected inherited narratives. Together these form a coherent project of **paleo-orthodoxy** with practical reach.

Yet Oden's wager on consensus is not without contest. The application of the **Vincentian canon** is interpretive and therefore disputable. What counts as *quod ubique* or *quod semper* is often filtered through editorial decisions about sources and weighting of voices. Oden faced this by privileging **ecumenical councils** and giving pride of place to **Athanasius** and **Augustine**, but genuine catholicity must continue to confront the diversity of patristic and medieval witnesses, including tensions around **atonement**, **baptismal regeneration**, and **Eucharistic presence**. Even so, the project holds as a constructive protest against **modern theological individualism**, arguing that the **church's memory** is not a museum but the Spirit's providential schooling.

#### 10. Reformed or WTS evaluation: convergences and tensions
From a Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Oden is a strategic ally in the defense of **Nicene and Chalcedonian orthodoxy**, **classical theism**, and **Scripture's primacy** against **modern liberalism**. His affirmation of **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **providence**, his defense of **trinitarian grammar**, and his retrieval of **patristic exegesis** resonate with **classical Reformed scholasticism** and with a **covenantal** understanding of the unity of **Scripture** under the **regula fidei**. His critique of **therapeutic ecclesiology** aligns with Reformed calls to recover **Word and sacrament** as the church's lifeblood. Yet tensions remain at principial points. First, his **Vincentian canon** risks functioning as a parallel authority that can soften **sola Scriptura** understood as Scripture's formal sufficiency, potentially relocating adjudication from the **covenantal canon** to contested accounts of **consensus**. Second, his **Wesleyan synergism** in **prevenient grace**, his advocacy of the possibility of **apostasy**, and his aim at **entire sanctification**, stand at odds with **monergistic effectual calling**, **perseverance of the saints**, and the Reformed distinction between **definitive** and **progressive sanctification**. Third, on **sacramental theology**, Oden's realist accents will be welcomed, yet ambiguity about **baptismal efficacy** and the terms of **Eucharistic presence** can obscure Reformed commitments to **covenant signs and seals** and **spiritual presence**. The Reformed alternative is not novelty but to sharpen Oden's own instincts by re-centering the **covenant of grace** as the governing canonical architecture, insisting that **sola Scriptura** norms **regula fidei**, not the reverse, while maintaining with Oden the ecumenical core of **Trinity**, **Christology**, and **justification** as the fixed grammar of the gospel.

- American mainline setting at **Drew University**, early **Tillich/Bultmann** influence, decisive turn to **patristic ressourcement** under **Will Herberg** amid late twentieth-century ecclesial crisis.
- Core problem: how to secure **Scripture's authority** and **dogmatic stability** against **modern theological novelty**, answered by **paleo-orthodoxy** and the **Vincentian canon**.
- Major works: **Systematic Theology** 1987–1992, **After Modernity... What?** 1990, **Requiem** 1995, **Classical Pastoral Care** 1987–1994, **The Rebirth of Orthodoxy** 2003, **How Africa Shaped the Christian Mind** 2007, **John Wesley's Teachings** 2012–2014, **A Change of Heart** 2014.
- Distinctives: **consensual hermeneutics**, **classical theism**, **Nicene-Chalcedonian dogma**, **prevenient grace** and **entire sanctification**, **canonical exegesis** in the **Ancient Christian Commentary on Scripture**.
- Interlocutors and debates: against **historicism**, **open theism**, and **therapeutic ministry**; with **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, **Wesley**, **Vincent of Lérins**, and ecumenical collaboration on **patristic retrieval**.
- Reformed alignment and critique: alignment on **Trinity**, **Christology**, and **classical attributes**; critique of **Vincentian weighting** vis-a-vis **sola Scriptura**, **synergism** in grace, and sacramental ambiguities relative to **covenant theology**.
- Legacy: institutionalization of **retrieval** through ACCS, renewal of **pastoral theology**, a coherent **Methodist paleo-orthodoxy**, and an ecumenical call to **creedal fidelity** in a postmodern age.
