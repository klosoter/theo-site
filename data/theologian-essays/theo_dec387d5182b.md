### TITLE
Created co-creator: a Lutheran theological anthropology integrated with evolutionary science and technological culture

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Cold War to biotech era**, Chicago school networks, and the **religion-and-science movement**; professor at the **Lutheran School of Theology at Chicago** and editor of **Zygon** shaped a public Lutheran voice.
- Method as **critical realist theological anthropology** using **systems theory**, **cybernetics**, and **biocultural evolution**, with **myth** as normative guidance.
- Key works: **The Human Factor** 1993; **Technology and Human Becoming** 2003; leadership at **Zygon** 1988–2008; essays on **created co-creator** across the 1980s–2000s.
- Central categories: **created co-creator**, **biocultural evolution**, **myth as cybernetic guidance**, **theology of nature**, **theologia crucis** reframed in evolutionary terms.
- Interlocutors: **Ian Barbour**, **Arthur Peacocke**, **Wolfhart Pannenberg**, debates with **process theology**, dialogue with **Luther** and **Tillich** within a Lutheran horizon.
- Reformed cue: test **created co-creator** against **Creator-creature distinction**, **special revelation and Scripture**, **original sin and federal headship**, and **Christological ontology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Chicago setting, and the religion-science project
Philip Hefner emerged as a contemporary **Lutheran** theologian whose public vocation unfolded in Chicago amid the rise of the **religion-and-science movement**. As a professor at the **Lutheran School of Theology at Chicago** and longtime editor-in-chief of **Zygon: Journal of Religion and Science** from the late 1980s into the early twenty-first century, he occupied a strategic nexus joining **seminary theology**, **University of Chicago** discourse, and international **science–religion** networks. The Cold War expansion of systems theory and cybernetics, the cultural aftershocks of **Darwinian evolution**, and the technological horizon extending from computing to biotechnology form Hefner’s historical matrix. He shares with **Ian Barbour** and **Arthur Peacocke** a conviction that theology requires a public, testable discourse marked by **critical realism** rather than either instrumentalism or naive biblicism. Yet unlike some counterparts, Hefner works as a Lutheran church theologian under the rhythms of **confession**, **liturgy**, and **pastoral formation**, while also translating those rhythms into policy discussions within **bioethics** and **technology**.

#### 2. Method: critical realism, systems, and biocultural evolution
Hefner’s method is centered in **theological anthropology** under the sign of **critical realism**. Knowledge aims at reality but through fallible models, so he deploys **models-and-metaphors** discourse to relate theology to the sciences. He appropriates **systems theory**, **cybernetics**, and **information theory** to construe persons as nodes in **biocultural evolution**, where biological fitness and cultural meaning co-constitute human existence. The notion of **myth** functions decisively: a myth is a normative narrative that operates as a **cybernetic guidance system**, shaping values, identity, and communal conduct. Religion then bears a double task: it is both a truth-claim about **God and creation** and a guidance mechanism for the **human ecology** that integrates divergent drives into a coherent pattern. This warrants a theological **theology of nature** that aligns empirical accounts of origins with **doctrine of creation**, while preserving **Lutheran** insistence on the primacy of the **Word of God**. Hefner balances scientific constraint with theological teleology, arguing that empirical explanation sets boundary conditions, whereas **eschatological meaning** supplies ultimate orientation.

#### 3. The created co-creator: imago Dei, vocation, and ambiguity
Hefner’s signature thesis is the **created co-creator**: humans are created by God to participate as responsible agents in the continuing creativity of the world. The phrase reframes *imago Dei* not as a metaphysical substance but as a **vocation** defined by **responsibility**, **symbolic world-construction**, and **technological mediation**. The human is the biological creature through whom God’s **teleology of creation** becomes culturally enacted. This redefinition speaks to the Lutheran dialectic of *simul iustus et peccator*: human co-creation is both grace-enabled calling and ambiguous field of conflict. The motif borrows energy from **Luther’s** valorization of ordinary vocation and the **theologia crucis** that discerns God’s work in the hiddenness of creaturely processes. Yet Hefner’s language sharpens the ecological and cultural edges of vocation: co-creation occurs within evolutionary contingency and social systems, not only within individual piety. The created co-creator thus mediates between **divine providence** and **human cultural creativity**, anchoring moral agency while refusing Promethean autonomy.

#### 4. The Human Factor 1993: religion, evolution, and cultural systems
In **The Human Factor: Evolution, Culture, and Religion** 1993, Hefner gathers his anthropology into a comprehensive account. The book argues that humans are **biocultural** by nature: our genetic constitution sets potentials that are realized only through **symbolic culture**, ritual, and technological extension. Religion in this scheme is not a mere by-product but a necessary component of human flourishing, furnishing **mythic-linguistic maps** that guide decision, stabilize identity, and calibrate behavior to communal goods. The created co-creator provides a **normative anthropology** that can adjudicate the evolutionary ambiguities of aggression, tribalism, and desire. Against reductionism, Hefner affirms that **meaning** is irreducible within the explanatory tapestry of **evolutionary theory**. Against triumphalist theology, he insists that **natural history** must norm any viable doctrine of humanity and the **doctrine of creation**. While attentive to **Lutheran** soteriology, the volume focuses on anthropology’s public shape, seeking a shared discourse with scientists, ethicists, and policy makers through a **critical realist** model of explanation.

#### 5. Technology and Human Becoming 2003: tools, ethics, and eschatology
In **Technology and Human Becoming** 2003, Hefner intensifies the created co-creator motif in light of contemporary **technoscience**. Tools are not external add-ons; they are extensions of human **embodiment** and **symbolic capacity**. Technology mediates human co-creation by amplifying agency, reorganizing communities, and reframing moral horizons. The theological task is to discern the **eschatological horizon** within which such agency can be judged, lest enhancement projects eclipse the **humanum**. Hefner criticizes both fatalistic technophobia and naive **technophilia**. He appeals to a Lutheran **ethics of vocation** that asks how technologies serve the neighbor and the created order under **divine providence**. This is not capitulation to market forces but a call for **discernment** in public policy and communal life. The created co-creator is answerable to the **Creator**, and accountability is measured by the cross-shaped wisdom of **theologia crucis** rather than by evolutionary success or economic utility. The book positions **bioethics**, **ecology**, and **global justice** as key arenas where theology must guide human becoming.

#### 6. Sin, grace, and the cross within an evolutionary horizon
Hefner reframes **hamartiology** and **soteriology** in an evolutionary register. Sin is the distortion of **biocultural desire** and the misdirection of co-creative power. It is not reducible to genetic inheritance nor solvable by cultural engineering. In **Lutheran** idiom, the self is curved in on itself, but now described through **systems** and **information flows** that perpetuate selfish equilibria. Grace is God’s initiative to reorient the created co-creator toward the **eschatological good**, enacted in **Jesus Christ** and mediated through **Spirit** and **community**. Hefner’s account of the cross is more **theologia crucis** than **satisfaction** theory, stressing divine solidarity with evolutionary suffering and the transformation of cultural scripts by **cruciform myth**. He argues that the church’s **liturgy** and proclamation generate a guidance system that counters destructive feedback loops. The **sacraments** functions as symbolic intensifications of grace within the communicative ecology of the church, even as Hefner seeks a public language for them within **critical realism**. The **resurrection** anchors hope that co-creation participates in a future not reducible to biological or cultural progress.

#### 7. Doctrine of God and creation: providence, kenosis, and panentheistic accents
Hefner’s **doctrine of God** operates with **providential** and occasionally **panentheistic** accents, though he avoids formal process commitments. God’s creative activity is understood as **kenotic** self-limitation that makes room for genuinely contingent evolutionary processes. This aligns with a Lutheran **theology of the cross**, where divine power is revealed under **hiddenness** and weakness. God’s **providence** includes lawlike regularities, stochastic variation, and emergent complexity. The world is not a closed mechanism but a theater of **divine purpose** that unfolds through creaturely agencies. The created co-creator is the site where providence and freedom intersect: human technical and symbolic agency becomes the means by which **God’s purposes** are enacted in history. Hefner resists both deistic separation and occasionalist collapse by advocating a **double-agency** account that keeps creaturely integrity intact. He regularly cites the **doctrine of creation** to insist that theological purpose does not compete with physical causality, and he treats **eschatology** as the final determination of value that orients co-creative labor.

#### 8. Interlocutors, alignments, and debates in the science–religion field
Hefner’s work advances in dialogue with **Ian Barbour’s** fourfold models, **Arthur Peacocke’s** **theology of nature**, and **Wolfhart Pannenberg’s** historicized metaphysics. From **Barbour**, he takes **critical realism** and the use of models; from **Peacocke**, a theological appropriation of evolutionary emergence; from **Pannenberg**, the priority of **eschatology** for ontology and truth. He disputes reductive **sociobiology** when it collapses normativity into reproductive advantage, and he engages **process theology** without accepting its metaphysical inevitabilities. His **Lutheran** instincts appear in his appeal to **theologia crucis**, the emphasis on **vocation**, and the public role of **proclamation** as mythic guidance. As editor of **Zygon**, he fostered a multinational conversation that treated **ethics**, **ecology**, **genetics**, and **technology** as theological loci. He also addressed pastoral questions about **meaning**, **suffering**, and **hope** in a scientific age. The net effect is a constructive Lutheran presence in a field often dominated by **Anglican** and **process** voices.

#### 9. Ecclesial location and pastoral-theological implications
Hefner never abandoned the ecclesial task of forming communities under **Word** and **sacrament**. The created co-creator thesis aims to equip pastors and laity to navigate **biotechnology**, climate pressures, and digital culture with theological literacy. The church becomes a **counterpublic** whose **mythic-narrative** practices recalibrate desire toward the neighbor and sustain a vocation that is technically proficient yet **cruciform** in ethos. Catechesis must therefore include **scientific literacy** and **ethical discernment**, not as secular add-ons but as integral to living under **divine providence**. Hefner’s tone is sober and hopeful: the ambiguities of evolution and technology are not accidental but the very site where God calls creatures into responsible **co-creation**. His constant refrain is that theology must be public, **critical realist**, and normatively anchored in the **gospel** in order to serve the common good amidst accelerating change.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation: revelation, nature, and Christological ontology
From a **Reformed WTS** vantage, Hefner’s **created co-creator** offers a vigorous account of vocation under **providence**, yet it risks softening the **Creator-creature distinction** and relocating normativity from **special revelation** to a theologically tamed account of **natural history**. The heavy reliance on **myth as cybernetic guidance** can functionally subordinate Scripture’s **covenantal** authority to cultural systems theory. A Reformed reading would insist that the order of knowing begins with **self-authenticating Scripture**, not with **critical realism** derived from the sciences, even while welcoming scientific insights as the *ministerial* use of reason. The attenuation of **original sin** as forensic corruption under **federal headship** may underplay the depth of moral incapacity that no cultural recalibration can remedy. Likewise, a largely functional **Christology** aligned with **theologia crucis** needs to be tethered to **two-natures Christology** and **penal substitution** within the **covenant of grace**, lest soteriology devolve into symbolic reorientation. In place of panentheistic accents and kenotic metaphysics, Reformed orthodoxy commends **divine aseity**, **immutability**, and **providence** understood as meticulous yet non-competitive concursus. Still, a Reformed appropriation can adapt Hefner’s attentiveness to **vocation**, **technology**, and **public theology**, provided the grammar of **creation-fall-redemption** in Christ’s **mediatorial kingship** corrects the ambiguities of the **created co-creator** and subordinates all cultural scripts to the canonical **covenant**.
