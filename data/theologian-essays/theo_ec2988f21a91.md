### TITLE
Announcing one-way grace: a law-gospel homiletic for exhausted evangelicals

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late-modern **American Evangelicalism**, the **PCA** in the wake of **D. James Kennedy**, the **New Calvinism** and blog-era debates on **sanctification** and **law-gospel distinction**.
- Pastoral-experiential method shaped by **Luther’s theologia crucis**, reliance on **lex semper accusat** and **simul iustus et peccator**, narrative-heavy preaching aimed at conscience and culture.
- **Unfashionable** 2009; **Surprised by Grace** 2010; **Jesus + Nothing = Everything** 2011; **Glorious Ruin** 2012; **One Way Love** 2013; **It Is Finished** 2015; the **Liberate** conferences 2012–2015.
- Central categories: **one-way love**, **performancism**, **union with Christ**, **indicative-imperative**, **tertius usus legis**, **definitive sanctification**, **extra nos** righteousness.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Martin Luther**, **Gerhard Forde**, **Robert Capon**, engagement with **John Calvin** and the **Westminster Standards**, debates with **Kevin DeYoung**, **Rick Phillips**, **Mark Jones**, and dialogue near **The Gospel Coalition**.
- Reformed reception cue: tensions around **third use of the law**, **antinomianism**, and the **duplex gratia** shape of **justification and sanctification** within confessional **Reformed orthodoxy**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and setting: a Graham grandson in Reformed pulpits
Born in 1972 and raised within the orbit of **Billy and Ruth Graham**, Tullian Tchividjian inhabits the fault line where mass-movement **Evangelicalism** meets confessional **Reformed Protestantism**. His early adult years included a restless swing between youthful prodigality and renewed piety, a biographical arc he later deployed to frame a theology of **one-way grace** and **theologia crucis**. Educated at **Reformed Theological Seminary** and ordained in the **Presbyterian Church in America**, he planted **New City Presbyterian Church** in South Florida in 2003 and in 2009 was called to merge that work with **Coral Ridge Presbyterian Church**, the flagship congregation founded by **D. James Kennedy**. The election and installation at Coral Ridge marked Tchividjian’s emergence amid the late-2000s **New Calvinism**, a moment shaped by weblog polemics, conference ecosystems, and a renewed public interest in **justification by faith** and **union with Christ**. He stepped into that current as a preacher with a single leitmotif, the **law-gospel distinction**, applied to anxious evangelicals bruised by what he termed **performancism**.

Tchividjian’s pastoral environment bore the pressures of celebrity-pastor culture and institutional transition, which he narrates in **Jesus + Nothing = Everything** as a school of humiliation that recalibrated him to **simul iustus et peccator**. His pulpit instinct pulled toward Luther’s *theologia crucis*, the cruciform event as God’s own grammar of grace, in contrast to a *theologia gloriae* of triumphant metrics and spiritual heroics. This ethos located him near sectors of **The Gospel Coalition** while also setting up future collisions around **sanctification**, the **third use of the law**, and the task of Christian ethics. From 2012 to 2015 he gathered a network under the banner **Liberate**, signaling his aspiration to be an evangelist of **one-way love** for the exhausted and the estranged.

Methodologically Tchividjian never developed a technical **systematics**. He worked as a homiletic conductor, weaving autobiographical narrative, scriptural exposition, and aphoristic contrasts to enact the **law-gospel distinction**. The epistemic mood was confessional and experiential, invoking **lex semper accusat** and **extra nos** righteousness to unmask self-salvation projects. The effect was a theology-through-preaching that privileged the affective register and conscience-aimed rhetoric more than scholastic architecture. He drew vocabulary and cadence from **Gerhard Forde** and **Robert Capon**, retrieving a Lutheran-leaning critique of moralism and a relentless focus on **Christ’s finished work**. Within a **Reformed** setting this translated into emphases on **indicative-imperative** ordering, **union with Christ** as the fountain of identity, and **definitive sanctification** as God’s act that underwrites all progress.

#### 2. Early program: difference without triumphalism
His first substantial monograph, **Unfashionable: Making a Difference in the World by Being Different** in 2009, arose from the culture-war fatigue of post-seeker **Evangelicalism** and the institutional anxieties of Coral Ridge after Kennedy’s death. The thesis pressed toward an ecclesial counterculture marked by the **gospel’s indicative**, an alternative to consumerist **performancism** and political triumphalism. The rhetoric leaned on the classic **Reformed** stress upon the church’s pilgrim identity, yet the proposed posture was less **Kuyperian cultural transformationalism** and more cruciform presence resonant with **Luther’s theologia crucis**. Here Tchividjian sketched the pattern he would intensify in later works: diagnose law-shaped aspiration, apply **one-way love**, and refuse to convert the **imperative** into a motivational lever. The conceptual anchors already in place were the **law-gospel distinction**, the critique of **religious self-salvation**, and the proclamation of **union with Christ** as the stable self.

This early program signaled a homiletic epistemology. Scripture was not mined for ethical blueprints but proclaimed as **evangelium**, the announcement that God in Christ secures sinners who cannot secure themselves. The eschatological accent was pastoral assurance rather than realized victory, a stance he often rendered in the Luther-derived formula **simul iustus et peccator**. That mood of consolation framed his later account of suffering and sanctification, placing the **theology of the cross** as the regulative principle for Christian experience.

#### 3. Narrative proclamation: Jonah, Colossians, and a cruciform ministry
In **Surprised by Grace: God’s Relentless Pursuit of Rebels** in 2010, Tchividjian turned to Jonah as a narrative frame for **divine initiative**. The message was a catechesis in **unilateral grace**, showcasing a God who saves both Nineveh and the prophet from their rival idolatries. The hermeneutic was broadly **redemptive-historical** yet governed by the existential dynamics of **law** that exposes and **gospel** that delivers. He appealed to **lex semper accusat** not as a denial of the law’s didactic content but to underscore its incapacity to produce life, pushing hearers to rest in **Christ’s righteousness extra nos**.

The pattern reached rhetorical zenith in **Jesus + Nothing = Everything** in 2011, written from the crucible of leadership conflict at Coral Ridge. Expounding **Colossians**, he portrayed the **indicatives of union** as the antidote to every species of **performancism**. The formula insisted that adding anything to Christ’s sufficiency evacuates the gospel of its **sola gratia** force. For Tchividjian this was not abstract dogma but pastoral survival, a lived **theologia crucis** through institutional death and resurrection. The book fixed the impression of Tchividjian as an evangelist of **one-way love**, an identity he amplified in a conference ministry that gathered pastors and laypeople under the **Liberate** banner.

#### 4. Suffering, exhaustion, and one-way love
The middle period crystallized his pastoral agenda in **Glorious Ruin: How Suffering Sets You Free** in 2012 and **One Way Love: Inexhaustible Grace for an Exhausted World** in 2013. In the former he opposed spiritual **technologies of control** with a cruciform acceptance of suffering’s unmanageability. The claim drew from **Luther’s Heidelberg Disputation**, that God’s work is hidden under its opposite, and that a **theology of glory** resists this scandal. Grace does not explain pain so much as meet the sufferer in it, an existential echo of **justification by faith** that refuses to convert suffering into merit or ladder. In the latter he gave the most memorable name to his project, **one-way love**, an idiom that translated **sola gratia** into common speech. Here Tchividjian braided **Robert Capon’s** parabolic sensibility with **Gerhard Forde’s** axiom that **sanctification** is learning to live by **justification**. The slogan that the **law tells us what to do and the gospel does it** summarized his homiletic: imperatives announce divine demand, but only the indicative of **Christ’s accomplishment** grants both pardon and power.

These books advanced a polemic against what he labeled **performancism**, a cultural and ecclesial compulsion to secure identity through achievement, guidance systems, and progress reports. For Tchividjian **performancism** was not a secular disease but a religious one, a baptized form of self-justification. The cure was not better technique but a deeper drowning in **baptismal identity** and **union with Christ**, categories that he treated devotionally rather than sacramentally. The effect was galvanizing for many whose evangelical experience had been catechesis in anxiety, yet it carried the seeds of later disputes about **imperatives**, **moral effort**, and the **third use of the law**.

#### 5. Sanctification, law, and the contested third use
The doctrinal center of gravity in Tchividjian’s theology is the relation of **sanctification** to **justification** within the **ordo salutis** and under the **law-gospel distinction**. He pressed the **indicative-imperative** grammar with the maxim that imperatives never generate what they demand. He affirmed **definitive sanctification**, God’s once-for-all breach with sin, and treated progressive growth as the outflow of remembering the **gospel** rather than engineering virtue. In this frame the **law** was diagnostically unflinching, **lex semper accusat**, so that its role in believers’ lives was to drive them back to **Christ’s finished work** rather than to motivate obedience. He often balked at the **tertius usus legis**, the third use articulated in **Reformed orthodoxy**, fearing that it mutates into a sanctification-by-try-harder scheme that eclipses **one-way love**.

Critics charged that this schema risked an effective erasure of the third use and a collapse of **duplex gratia**, the Reformed pattern wherein **justification and sanctification** are distinct yet inseparable gifts of **union with Christ**. Tchividjian countered that he was not denying obedience but recovering its only engine, the **evangelical indicative**. He did not deny that the Spirit produces virtue, but he insisted that the preacher never blunts the accusing edge of the **law** or treats imperatives as instrumental means. The net effect was a homiletic mood in which commands were rhetorically deployed to intensify need, and Christ was proclaimed as the sole agent both of pardon and of change, a stance fed by **Luther**, mediated through **Forde**, and refracted in **Capon’s** imaginative riffs on parables.

#### 6. Interlocution and intra-Reformed dispute
Between 2012 and 2014 these emphases generated a public debate with **The Gospel Coalition** circle and allied **Reformed** pastors. **Kevin DeYoung’s** work on holiness set forth a program of Spirit-enabled effort grounded in **union with Christ** and insisted on the positive, non-accusing function of the **law** for the regenerate. **Rick Phillips** and **Mark Jones** warned of functional **antinomianism**, with Jones’s 2013 study on **Antinomianism** retrieving historic categories to frame the controversy. Tchividjian responded in posts and sermons by doubling down on the **law-gospel distinction**, contending that appeals to the **third use** often smuggled the first. He left **The Gospel Coalition** platform in 2014, citing both theological and institutional differences. Though **Michael Horton** and others sympathetic to **law-gospel** preaching pressed for nuance, the polemical atmosphere hardened the contrast between a sanctification-by-remembering-justification camp and a sanctification-in-communion-with-Christ camp.

These exchanges exposed methodological fissures. Tchividjian’s appeal to **Luther’s simul iustus et peccator** and **lex semper accusat** carried Lutheran freight that sits differently within the **Westminster Standards**, where the **moral law** functions within the **covenant of grace** as both rule of life and pointer to Christ. He affirmed **penal substitution** and stood within a **PCA** confessional location, yet his frequent citation of **Gerhard Forde** introduced anxiety among Reformed critics alert to Forde’s critique of **penal substitution** and stress on the gospel as sheer proclamation. The debate showed the difficulty of importing Lutheran idioms without recalibrating them for **Reformed** categories of **union with Christ**, **duplex gratia**, and the **third use**.

#### 7. Scandal, deposition, and the burden of biography
In June 2015 Tchividjian resigned from **Coral Ridge Presbyterian Church** after admitting to adultery. The **South Florida Presbytery** later deposed him from ministry, and further disclosures in 2016 described patterns of ministerial misconduct and abuse of power. The **Liberate** ministry shuttered. Subsequent years saw attempts at reentry to public ministry in non-denominational settings, including a congregation in Jupiter, Florida, even as multiple victims called for accountability. The biographical fall inevitably reframed reception of his theology of **one-way love**, prompting the charge that a thin doctrine of **sanctification** can serve to protect leaders from moral scrutiny. Others countered that the **gospel** of grace uniquely speaks to grievous sin and must continue to be preached without qualification.

The theological stakes of the scandal are complex. An account of **law** that only accuses raises questions about the constructive, ecclesially mediated practices of **discipline**, **repentance**, and **restoration** in the **covenant community**. The collapse of **Liberate** interrupted the publishing and conference platform that had carried his message, but fragments of his rhetoric remain in circulation, especially among pastors ministering to weary congregants. The pastoral danger and pastoral power of his message now appear in sharp relief, inviting renewed attention to the whole counsel of **Reformed** doctrine on the **Christian life**.

#### 8. Contributions and limits: a homiletic of grace
Tchividjian’s enduring contribution is a rhetorically charged, pastorally sensitive insistence that the **gospel** is not advice but news, that **Christ’s finished work** sets sinners free from the crush of **performancism**, and that the **law-gospel distinction** is a lifeline for consciences under the lash of religious anxiety. He translated **simul iustus et peccator**, **extra nos** righteousness, and **union with Christ** into idioms accessible to laypeople. He re-centered suffering under **theologia crucis**, taught congregations to hear imperatives as invitations to Christ rather than platforms for self-effort, and offered a needed rebuke to the managerial, metrics-driven patterns of late-modern **Evangelicalism**. At his best he preached **Christ alone** with disarming clarity, giving oxygen to those suffocating under spiritual performance reviews.

The limits are also clear. The doctrinal architecture around **sanctification** remained thin, with minimal engagement of **Westminster** catechetics on the **third use of the law**, sparse treatment of **sacraments** as means of grace, and little sustained account of the Spirit’s agency as distinct from the declaration of **justification**. His use of **Lutheran** axioms, shaped by **Forde** and **Capon**, sometimes eclipsed the **Reformed** grammar of **duplex gratia**, leaving the impression that growth is only the by-product of remembering acquittal. In a polemical context the rhetoric of **one-way love** tended to flatten biblical diversity, underemphasizing texts where apostolic **imperatives** function as Spirit-empowered directives within **union with Christ**. The personal scandal intensified these concerns, not as a refutation of grace but as a warning that preaching **law that only accuses** can coexist with institutional systems that fail to discipline and protect.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation: grace without collapse, law without terror
A classical **Reformed** assessment, of the kind associated with **Westminster Theological Seminary**, will affirm Tchividjian’s Christocentric preaching, his instinct to ground all obedience in **union with Christ**, and his protest against **performancism** as a species of works-righteousness. At the same time it will insist, on the basis of **Westminster Confession** chapters 13 and 19 and the **Heidelberg Catechism’s** structure of guilt, grace, and gratitude, that the **law-gospel distinction** must be integrated with the **third use of the law** as a rule of life for the regenerate. The law does not bestow power, yet within **covenant** and **Spirit** it serves as fatherly instruction, so that the **imperative** as imperative, not only as accusation, has a positive, sanctifying function. Reformed theology guards against collapsing **sanctification** into a psychology of remembering **justification** by locating both benefits within the **duplex gratia** of **union with Christ**, such that the indicative includes not only pardon but participation in the risen Lord and the imperative flows from that communion without becoming a new covenant of works. A WTS-shaped alternative would commend preaching that announces the finished work of Christ redemptive-historically, per Vosian **biblico-theological** method, while also administering the **means of grace**, fencing the Table, exercising **discipline** as pastoral care, and calling believers to Spirit-enabled, thankful obedience. In principle it is a contrast about revelation and covenant rather than rhetoric and mood: the **gospel** is unconditional gift, the **law** remains holy and good, and in the hands of the **Spirit** the church can preach both without antinomianism or legalism.
