### TITLE
Eden as proto-temple, New Jerusalem as telos: a canonical biblical theology of divine presence and priestly kingship.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Northern Irish evangelical scholarship**, IVP-UK ecosystem, and post-critical consolidations of **biblical theology** in a late twentieth-century context.
- **Canonical approach** with disciplined **intertextuality** and final-form exegesis; confidence in **Scripture’s unity** and attention to **Ancient Near Eastern** backgrounds.
- **From Paradise to the Promised Land** 1995, 3rd ed. 2012; **New Dictionary of Biblical Theology** 2000; **Dictionary of the Old Testament: Pentateuch** 2003; **The Servant King** 1998; **From Eden to the New Jerusalem** 2008; **Exodus (Apollos Old Testament Commentary)** 2017; **Discovering Jesus** 2010.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and the turn to canonical biblical theology
T. Desmond Alexander emerges as a contemporary Northern Irish voice within a broad **evangelical** landscape committed to **biblical theology** as a unifying discipline across Scripture. Situated at **Union Theological College, Belfast**, his career coincides with the consolidation of post-war British evangelical scholarship and the maturation of the IVP reference ecosystem that sought to answer **historical criticism** not by retreat but by offering positive accounts of **Scripture’s unity**. In this setting Alexander sharpened a method that reads the **Pentateuch** and the canon at large in final form, recognizing literary artistry, theological coherence, and intentional **intertextuality**. He is conversant with **Ancient Near Eastern** data but not beholden to it, deploying comparative material to clarify biblical idiom while privileging the canon’s internal logic. His work is framed by a persistent thesis: the Bible narrates the recovery and consummation of **divine presence** from **Eden** to **New Jerusalem**, forming a **kingdom of priests** whose vocation clarifies both identity and mission.

Methodologically, Alexander stands downstream from **Brevard Childs** in his **canonical approach**, while also resonating with **John Sailhamer** in foregrounding compositional strategies that guide readers theologically. He rejects the fragmenting tendencies of **Julius Wellhausen** and the classic **documentary hypothesis**, yet he appropriates from **Gerhard von Rad** a sensitivity to thematic development across time. The guiding impulse is not mere harmonization but a diachronic-canonical tracing of **motifs** that carry Israel’s story into its christological fulfillment. This synthetic posture, characteristic of late twentieth-century evangelical theology, undergirds Alexander’s editorial leadership in key dictionaries and informs his signature monographs on **Eden-temple**, **messianic kingship**, and **new creation**.

#### 2. Method and epistemic stance: canonical coherence, intertextual control
Alexander’s method presumes the theological sufficiency of the canon’s final form and proceeds by identifying textual signals that authorize **typology** and **intertextual** development. He reads **Genesis 1–3** with acute attention to lexical echoes that link Eden to later **tabernacle** and **temple** texts: the priestly pair to work and to keep in Genesis 2 evokes Israel’s cultic service, the **cherubim** that guard the way reappear on the **mercy seat**, and Eden’s precious stones and rivers foreshadow sanctuary and **New Jerusalem** imagery. Yet his **intertextuality** is not free association. He argues for authorially intended patterns, mediated by Israel’s scribal tradition and stabilized in canonical shape. The hermeneutical wager is that Scripture interprets Scripture under a unified divine authorship, so the coherence one discerns is not imposed but discovered.

Importantly, Alexander’s canonical approach does not bypass history. He attends to **Ancient Near Eastern** parallels as context for **Pentateuchal** narratives and law, but he refuses to let extra-biblical data dictate the Bible’s internal grammar. The fruit is a mode of **biblical theology** where historical referent and canonical meaning coinhere, enabling robust theological synthesis without collapsing into allegory. His epistemic confidence is evangelical rather than romantic: **Scripture’s inspiration** and **inerrancy** underwrite the presumption of unity, while a discipline of textual controls guards against fanciful correspondences. This balance allows Alexander to identify large-scale trajectories such as **seed-land-blessing**, **kingdom of priests**, and **divine presence** that hold together Law, Prophets, Writings, Gospel, and Apocalypse.

#### 3. From Paradise to the Promised Land: Pentateuchal foundations
Alexander’s **From Paradise to the Promised Land** first appeared in 1995, with significant expansions culminating in the third edition in 2012. More than a standard introduction, it is a programmatic statement on how to read the **Pentateuch** theologically amid debates over composition, sources, and historical plausibility. He engages the **documentary hypothesis** critically, showing its internal instabilities, and argues for the explanatory power of reading the Torah in its final compositional form. He tracks the development of **seed** promises from **Genesis 3:15** through the patriarchal narratives, linking genealogies to covenantal hopes and emphasizing the growing royal focus of **Genesis 49** and **Numbers 24**. The categories of **land** and **blessing** likewise structure his treatment of **Exodus** and **Deuteronomy**, where the gift of land is tethered to the reality of **divine presence** among a holy people.

A theological core of the book is the consecration of Israel as a **kingdom of priests** in **Exodus 19:4–6**, which Alexander reads as a vocation grounded in the recovery of Eden’s mandate. Torah is not merely law but the charter of a priestly polity indwelt by the Lord whose **Name** abides in the sanctuary. Alexander’s exposition foregrounds **tabernacle** theology in **Exodus 25–40**, God’s holy proximity in the **glory** that fills the tent, and the open wound of sin that complicates proximity in **Leviticus** and the **Day of Atonement**. These Pentateuchal foundations become the bedrock for his later account of **Eden-temple** and **new creation**, in which the Promised Land anticipates a renewed cosmos rather than exhausting the promise in territorial terms.

#### 4. From Eden to the New Jerusalem: temple, city, and the telos of creation
The 2008 monograph **From Eden to the New Jerusalem** distills Alexander’s canonical thesis that the Bible narrates the restoration of God’s **immediate presence** with his image-bearing people, climaxing in the **New Jerusalem** of **Revelation 21–22**. The opening move is a close reading of **Genesis 1–3** in which Eden is interpreted as a proto-**temple**, a sacred garden-sanctuary where God walks and where humanity’s priestly-royal vocation is initiated. He details Edenic markers that reappear in the sanctuary: the orientation to the east, the **cherubim**, precious materials, and the life-giving **river**. From there he traces sanctuary development from **tabernacle** to **Solomonic temple**, through the exilic crisis and prophetic hopes of a renewed, glorified dwelling, culminating in the Johannine claim that the Word became flesh and tabernacled among us, **John 1:14** using the verb *skenoō*.

Christologically, the presence theme is concentrated in **Emmanuel** and in Jesus as the locus of **glory** and the true temple, extended to the **church** as a **living temple** in **Ephesians 2** and **1 Peter 2**. Eschatologically, Alexander underscores the paradox of **Revelation 21–22**: there is no temple in the city because the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are its temple, resolving the tension of proximity and holiness through the **Lamb’s atonement** and Spirit-wrought transformation. The **New Jerusalem** thus appears as an Edenic city, integrating garden and civic imagery, radiating **priestly** and **royal** identity to the nations. The telos is not escape but **new creation**, a world saturated with God’s presence, fulfilling the creational command to fill and rule under God in a globalized Eden.

#### 5. The Servant King: messianic synthesis of rule and suffering
In **The Servant King** 1998, written with **Brian S. Rosner**, Alexander offers a lucid synthesis of **messianic** expectations that resolves the apparent dissonance between the triumphant **Davidic king** and the afflicted **Servant** of **Isaiah**. Here his canonical method locates the cruciform regal identity of Jesus in the convergence of royal psalms, prophetic promises, and Pentateuchal typologies. The figure emerging from **2 Samuel 7**, **Psalm 110**, **Isaiah 52–53**, and related passages is a monarch who conquers by vicarious suffering, enacting **atonement** and establishing an everlasting dominion. Alexander emphasizes the corporate dimension of representation: the king as Israel-in-person, recapitulating the nation’s vocation to be a **light to the nations** and to mediate **blessing** promised to **Abraham**.

This messianic profile governs his reading of the Gospels and early Christian proclamation where **Jesus** as **servant-king** embodies the **kingdom of God** and reconstitutes the people of God around himself. In **Discovering Jesus** 2010, Alexander complements this by explaining why four canonical **Gospels** are necessary to portray the complex unity of Jesus’ identity and mission. The combination of **royal** and **priestly** motifs, as well as prophetic fulfillment, aligns with the classical Reformed **munus triplex**, though Alexander frames the theme primarily through biblical-theological categories rather than systematic loci. The payoff is a christology that is narratively grounded and covenantally articulate, integrating cross and crown in a single royal-priestly vocation.

#### 6. Reference works and the shaping of evangelical biblical theology
Alexander’s editorial leadership in the **New Dictionary of Biblical Theology** 2000 and the **Dictionary of the Old Testament: Pentateuch** 2003 has had outsized influence on how **evangelicals** practice biblical theology. These volumes map themes like **kingdom**, **covenant**, **temple**, **image of God**, and **new creation**, while also providing essays on method and canon. The dictionaries embody Alexander’s conviction that **canonical coherence** and controlled **intertextuality** can anchor theological reflection and equip pastors and scholars for **redemptive-historical** preaching. His contributions model how to traverse from word-level analysis to theme-level synthesis without losing exegetical moorings.

By curating a community of scholars conversant with both **historical criticism** and ecclesial concerns, Alexander helped articulate a middle path between atomistic philology and undisciplined typology. The result is a generation of evangelical students trained to read **Torah**, **Prophets**, and **Writings** as a forward-leaning witness whose fulfillment in **Christ** and **church** is not an addendum but the canonical horizon. This editorial work institutionalizes the **Eden-temple** and **new creation** motifs as standard components of the evangelical theological toolbox, alongside classic topics such as **atonement**, **justification**, and **sanctification** as they surface within a biblical-theological frame.

#### 7. Exodus in focus: name, glory, and presence
Alexander’s **Exodus** commentary in the **Apollos Old Testament Commentary** 2017 distills years of work on **divine presence** and **priestly** vocation into sustained exegesis. He reads Exodus as a narrative of liberation unto worship, where the climactic moment is not the crossing of the sea but the indwelling **glory** of God in the **tabernacle**. The revelation of the **Name** in **Exodus 3** and its exposition in **Exodus 34:6–7** become theological centers, integrating holiness, mercy, and justice into the grammar of presence. Alexander highlights **new-creation** resonances in the plagues and in the orderly construction of the sanctuary, and he treats **Exodus 24** as a Sinai theophany that anticipates table fellowship in the kingdom.

His treatment of **Exodus 32–34** displays his canonical instincts. The golden calf crisis dramatizes the fundamental problem of proximity: how can a holy God dwell among a sinful people? The answer is mediated presence through priesthood, sacrifice, and eventually the embodiment of **Immanuel**. Alexander thus depicts Exodus as narrating the architecture of grace that sustains **covenant** communion, which carries forward into prophetic promises of a renewed heart and culminates in the **Lamb** whose blood secures a people and cleanses a space for God’s dwelling. The commentary exemplifies his balance of philological care, **Ancient Near Eastern** contextualization, and theological synthesis.

#### 8. Interlocutors, convergences, and controlled typology
Alexander’s approach converges with **G. K. Beale** on **temple** and **new creation**, even as he favors a more introductory synthesis in **From Eden to the New Jerusalem**, complementing Beale’s more exhaustive treatment. With **Brevard Childs** he shares commitment to **canonical shape**, while diverging by adopting an evangelical confidence in **inerrancy** and a more straightforward teleology toward **Christ**. He appreciates **John Sailhamer** for highlighting editorial shaping within the **Pentateuch**, yet he maintains broader thematic trajectories not limited to compositional seams. Over against **Wellhausen** and classical **source criticism**, Alexander insists that reading the final form yields richer theological outcome without denying complex prehistory.

In conversation with **Richard Bauckham** and **N. T. Wright** on **Revelation** and **eschatology**, Alexander embraces the cosmic and civic horizon of the **New Jerusalem** while centering the **presence** motif as the city’s essence. His **intertextual** method is disciplined by textual triggers, lexical repetition, and structural analogy, resisting a maximalist typology that would flatten historical particularity. This control allows motifs like **seed-land-blessing** and **kingdom of priests** to function as canonical ligatures rather than exegetical shortcuts. In sum, Alexander’s contribution is not a novel discovery of temple and city themes, but a lucid, teachable architecture for reading Scripture as a coherent **redemptive-historical** drama ordered to **divine presence**.

#### 9. Theological payoff and a Reformed/WTS evaluation
The theological payoff of Alexander’s oeuvre is a reframing of Christian identity, worship, and mission around **divine presence** and **priestly kingship**. Believers are not passive recipients of private salvation but participants in a **kingdom of priests** called to extend Eden’s life to the ends of the earth, anticipating **new creation**. The **church** as **temple** signals that holiness and mission are mutually implicating, and the **New Jerusalem** renders eschatology as consummate communion rather than mere chronology. Alexander’s christocentric synthesis in **The Servant King** further grounds this vision in the cruciform **Davidic** identity of Jesus, whose **atonement** establishes the conditions for unmediated presence and whose **resurrection** and **ascension** inaugurate cosmic dominion. The methodological virtues of **canonical approach** and carefully controlled **intertextuality** make his work accessible and durable across ecclesial contexts and scholarly guilds.

From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Alexander aligns strongly with **Vosian redemptive-historical** instincts, especially in tracing promise and fulfillment and in centering **covenant** communion as Scripture’s plot. His emphasis on **presence** harmonizes with classical Reformed **federal theology**, though he rarely states matters in terms of **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**. This creates a minor lacuna: without explicit bi-covenantal framing, the moral-legal architecture of Adam’s probation and the federal consequences of Christ’s active obedience can recede into a broader presence motif. A Westminster-friendly corrective would integrate Alexander’s **Eden-temple** theme with **Calvin’s duplex gratia** and the law-gospel distinction: Eden as prototypical **covenant of works**, Israel’s cult as typological pedagogy within the **covenant of grace**, Christ as the obedient federal head who secures both justification and sanctification, and **new creation** as the sacramental enlargement of temple communion. With that integration, Alexander’s canonical synthesis functions as a powerful engine for Reformed preaching, catechesis, and mission, anchoring ecclesial life in the joy of **God-with-us** and the vocation of a consecrated **kingdom of priests**.
