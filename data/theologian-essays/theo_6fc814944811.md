### TITLE
Paradoxical public theology: retrieving Lutheran two-kingdoms, vocation, and natural law for responsible Christian engagement in civil society.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Mid-to-late twentieth-century American **Lutheran** ethicist shaped by civil rights, Vietnam, and mainline Protestant realignment, advancing a public theology amid cultural pluralism and the **post-1960s academy**.
- Method of **Lutheran paradox** and **Augustinian realism** using **law and gospel**, *simul iustus et peccator*, and the **two-kingdoms doctrine** within a limited **natural law** and **public reason** framework.
- Key works: **The Paradoxical Vision: A Public Theology for the 21st Century** 1995; **Quality with Soul** 2001; **Ordinary Saints** 2003; **Reasonable Ethics** 2005; **Good and Bad Ways to Think About Religion and Politics** 2010.
- Central categories: **vocation**, **orders of creation**, **mediating structures**, **civil society**, **penultimate and ultimate**, **eschatological reserve**.
- Main interlocutors and influences: **Martin Luther**, **Dietrich Bonhoeffer**, **Reinhold Niebuhr**, **Peter Berger**, **Richard John Neuhaus**, with contrasts to **Karl Barth** and American **liberal Protestantism**.
- Reformed reception cue: convergences with **common grace**, **sphere sovereignty**, and **classical natural law**, but tensions with **covenant theology**, the **third use of the law**, and **Van Tillian** critiques of public reason.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and context: Lutheran realism in late modern America
Robert Benne emerges as a contemporary **Lutheran theologian** of public life whose career tracks the arc of American mainline Protestantism from mid-century confidence through post-1960s upheaval to contemporary fragmentation. His formation reflects a Midwestern **Lutheran piety** tutored by **Martin Luther** and **Augustine**, then sharpened by exposure to **Reinhold Niebuhr's Christian realism** and the methodological challenges of the **University-based divinity culture** after the Second World War. The consequential public crises of civil rights, the Cold War, and Vietnam habituated him to a posture of **moral realism** suspicious of utopianism yet impatient with quietism. Theologically he retrieves the Lutheran *Zweireichelehre* or **two-kingdoms doctrine** and the law-gospel hermeneutic as a grammar for Christian witness in pluralist democracies, convinced that the church must be simultaneously faithful to the gospel and responsibly engaged with the penultimate goods of **politics, economy, and culture**.

#### 2. Method: Lutheran paradox, law and gospel, and limited natural law
Benne's method is anchored in **Lutheran paradox**. He reads human action through *simul iustus et peccator* and distinguishes the **left-hand rule of God** in providential governance through law from the **right-hand rule of Christ** in the gospel that justifies sinners. That distinction does not divide realities but offers a **dialectical grammar** for discerning Godâ€™s work in creation and redemption. From **law and gospel** he derives both a sober political anthropology and an evangelical constraint against sacralizing any historical project. To converse in a pluralist public square, he appeals to a chastened **natural law** or **orders of creation** account, arguing that basic moral insights are widely accessible as **public reason** even as the church provides a teleological horizon and moral motivation. He relies on **mediating structures** in civil society, drawing on **Peter Berger** and **Richard John Neuhaus**, as institutional sites where religious conviction can be translated into social practice without either domination or privatization. The result is a method both descriptive and normative: descriptive of the differentiated institutions of modern society and normative in its insistence that **vocation** situates Christians within those spheres for neighbor-serving love.

#### 3. The Paradoxical Vision 1995: a Lutheran public theology
The programmatic statement of Benne's project is **The Paradoxical Vision: A Public Theology for the 21st Century** 1995. There he contends that a viable **public theology** must live within the tension of the **penultimate and the ultimate**, refusing both the politicized soteriologies of liberal Protestantism and the apolitical pietisms of sectarian withdrawal. Rooted in **Augustinian two cities** and **Luther's two kingdoms**, the vision affirms the creational goods of family, work, and political order while recognizing their radical insufficiency for salvation. The ethics that results is **Augustinian realist** in politics, pragmatic yet bounded by **moral absolutes** that respect the image of God. It privileges **vocation** as the evangelical engine of social responsibility and deploys **natural law** as a lingua franca for public persuasion. Benne engages **Karl Barth** critically here, commending Barth's Christocentric protest against natural theology while resisting Barth's suspicion of **orders of creation**, which he believes undermines the theological affirmation of **civil law**, **marriage**, and **economy** as genuine divine preserves.

#### 4. Religion and politics 2010: criteria for engagement and critique of ideologies
In **Good and Bad Ways to Think About Religion and Politics** 2010, Benne provides a compact typology that has been widely used in church study contexts and university classrooms. The bad ways include **theocracy** and **Erastianism** on the one side and **naked public square** exclusionism on the other, with **civil religion** as a constant temptation. The good ways focus on **critical engagement** and **principled participation** guided by the **two-kingdoms doctrine** and **law and gospel**. He insists that Christians should not expect comprehensive policy blueprints from Scripture, yet they must bring **basic moral principles** to bear on contested questions, such as respect for life, the dignity of the poor, responsible freedom, and institutional subsidiarity akin to **mediating structures**. The book exemplifies his reliance on **natural law** and **public reason**, but always under the **eschatological reserve** that the kingdom of God is not coextensive with the nation or any party. His interlocutors include **Niebuhr** for realism, **Neuhaus** for public visibility, and **James Davison Hunter** for analysis of culture and power, all subsumed under a decidedly **Lutheran hermeneutic** of the twofold rule of God.

#### 5. Christian life 2003: vocation and sanctification under the cross
In **Ordinary Saints: An Introduction to the Christian Life** 2003, Benne explores **sanctification** through the Lutheran lens of **vocation** under the cross. The Christian life is not a climb to spiritual heroism but the Spirit's sanctifying of ordinary roles in family, work, church, and citizenship. The abiding grammar is **justification by faith** that frees the believer for neighbor-centrism rather than self-justification through moral performance. He retrieves **Luther's doctrine of vocation** as the matrix for discipleship and links it to **orders of creation** as the structures in which the Spirit's renewal bears fruit. In this account, the **third use of the law** functions as a guide, though always subordinate to the gospel's primacy, a distinction that protects against both antinomian license and moralism. The end is a **cruciform ethics** that assumes penultimate ambiguity yet affirms creational goods as the proper arena for sacrificial love.

#### 6. Higher education 2001 and beyond: institutional identity and mediating traditions
Benne's **Quality with Soul: How Six Premier Colleges and Universities Keep Faith with Their Religious Traditions** 2001 extends his public theology to the crisis of **church-related higher education**. He analyzes how institutions preserve or lose religious identity and argues that robust **confessional markers**, intentional **ritual and moral practices**, and a living **intellectual tradition** are essential to mission integrity. The proposal draws on **mediating structures** and the importance of institutional ecology for transmitting thick convictions. He commends schools that develop coherent **core curricula**, support **faculty formation**, and maintain visible **religious leadership**, while criticizing the drift toward generic **values talk** common in mainline colleges. Later essays and reports continue that theme by urging Lutheran colleges to retrieve **Lutheran confessional identity** without sectarianism, showing how **Luther's two-kingdoms** can underwrite academic freedom and public responsibility. This is a case of the **paradoxical vision** institutionalized: unapologetic particularity combined with generous, critical engagement with the wider academy.

#### 7. Reasonable Ethics 2005: applied social ethics with Lutheran grammar
In **Reasonable Ethics: A Christian Approach to Social, Economic, and Political Concerns** 2005, Benne offers a sustained application of his **Lutheran ethical grammar** to concrete issues like economics, bioethics, and war. The book exemplifies **Augustinian realism** through proximate justice and the rejection of perfectionism in policy. On economics, he supports market mechanisms constrained by moral norms of **justice** and **solidarity**, echoing **Catholic social thought** while rooting his argument in **orders of creation**. On bioethics, he defends the sanctity of life, critiquing utilitarian reductions with a **natural-law** grounding. On war, he retrieves **just war** criteria, integrating **law and gospel** to resist crusade and pacifism alike. Throughout, he frames analysis in terms of **vocation** and the **penultimate and ultimate**, insisting that Christian hope fuels patience with incremental reform rather than revolutionary rupture. His method intentionally avoids confessional isolation by using **public reasons** that can be heard beyond the church, though always disciplined by **Lutheran doctrine**.

#### 8. Ecclesial critique and Lutheran debates: ELCA, sexuality, and orders of creation
Benne's public voice became especially pronounced in debates within the **Evangelical Lutheran Church in America** after its 1988 founding and particularly during the 2009 decisions on **sexuality** and ministry. He argued in widely read essays that the ELCA's drift reflected a loss of **Lutheran theological identity**, an eclipse of **orders of creation**, and capitulation to a **liberal Protestant** hermeneutic that confuses gospel with cultural inclusion. Drawing on **Bonhoeffer's mandates** and **Luther's two-kingdoms**, he contended that creational givens like male-female complementarity and the procreative telos of marriage are part of **lex naturae** discernible in public reason and not merely ecclesial fiat. He criticized the substitution of **therapeutic individualism** for **vocation** and warned against **civil religion** that baptizes prevailing ideologies. Even here the **paradoxical vision** restrains rhetoric: he refuses anathematizing opponents, but he calls for **confessional clarity** and institutional integrity in church bodies and their schools.

#### 9. Interlocutors and contrasts: Luther, Niebuhr, Barth, and Neuhaus
Benne's constructive dependence on **Luther** is explicit in his constant appeal to **law and gospel**, **two kingdoms**, and **vocation**, yet he reads Luther through an **Augustinian realism** akin to **Reinhold Niebuhr** rather than through existential reinterpretations. He receives **Niebuhr's** sense of irony, limiting conditions, and power analysis while refusing Niebuhr's occasional thinness on the **sacramental and ecclesial** dimensions of Christian life. Against **Karl Barth**, he insists on a modest **natural law** that allows cooperative moral knowledge, though he accepts Barth's protest against **natural theology** as a soteriological rival to revelation. With **Richard John Neuhaus**, he argues for a **naked public square** critique and the legitimacy of strong religious voices in democratic deliberation. Social theorists like **Peter Berger** supply his account of **mediating structures** and cultural plausibility, while sociologists like **James Davison Hunter** sharpen his attention to the culture-forming power of institutions. The net effect is a Lutheran **public theology** able to argue across traditions while maintaining **confessional boundaries**.

#### 10. Influence and synthesis: public theology as institutional vocation
Across his corpus, from **The Paradoxical Vision** 1995 to **Good and Bad Ways** 2010, Benne synthesizes **Lutheran ethics** of the person with an institutional theory of **civil society** and a normative account of **public reason**. He has shaped the conversation on how **church-related colleges** can retain **quality with soul**, how congregations can teach **vocation**, and how Christians can engage **politics** without idolatry or withdrawal. His characteristic conceptual tools are **penultimate and ultimate**, **vocation**, **two kingdoms**, and **natural law**, deployed against twin temptations of **politicized soteriology** and **privatized faith**. He stands within the contemporary **Lutheran retrieval** movement, arguing that classical doctrine is not an embarrassment but a resource for the common good. His voice is pastoral in tone yet **policy-aware**, skeptical of ideology yet confident that Christians, as **ordinary saints**, can serve neighbors in the complex ecologies of modern institutions.

In Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary perspective, Benne's emphasis on **orders of creation**, **common moral knowledge**, and **vocation** substantially aligns with classical **Reformed natural law** and **common grace**, and it resonates with **sphere sovereignty** in its defense of differentiated institutions and **mediating structures**. Yet tensions persist. The **two-kingdoms doctrine** in his Lutheran form can thin the integrative claims of **covenant theology** that sees all of life under the one redemptive rule of Christ administered through covenants. His reliance on **public reason** and modest **natural law** will be challenged by **Van Tillian** accounts of the noetic effects of sin, which call for explicit **revelation** normativity in public engagement. His law-gospel dialectic, while salutary, risks marginalizing the **third use of the law** and the positive role of **Torah** for sanctified cultural labor. A Reformed alternative would press a stronger **creation mandate** within a **covenantal ontology**, construe politics under **Christ's mediatorial kingship**, and deploy **Scripture** not only as church proclamation but as principled public wisdom, while still affirming Benne's **Augustinian realism**, anti-utopianism, and institutional pluralism.
