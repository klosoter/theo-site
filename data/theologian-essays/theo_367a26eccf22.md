### TITLE
Perfect charity as the intelligible form of the Trinity and the ordered ascent of contemplation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Twelfth-century **Victorine** synthesis in Paris during the **High Medieval** renaissance of schools, bridging **monastic spirituality** and emerging **scholastic theology**.
- A method of **speculative mysticism** that unites **Augustinian illumination** with disciplined **ratio** and *contemplatio*, a pedagogy of **lectio–meditatio–oratio–contemplatio**.
- Key works: **De Trinitate** c. 1170–1173, **Benjamin Minor** c. 1172, **Benjamin Major or The Mystical Ark** c. 1173, **De quattuor gradibus violentae caritatis** c. 1173.
- Central categories: **caritas** as ontological principle, **condilectio** as co-loved person, **divine simplicity** with **relational distinctions**, **allegory and anagogy**, *excessus mentis*.
- Main interlocutors and influences: **Hugh of St. Victor**, **Augustine**, **Pseudo-Dionysius**, **Anselm**, dialogue with **Peter Lombard** and contrast with **Abelard**.
- Reformed cue: value Richard’s **Trinitarian clarity** and **piety**, yet test claims of **rational necessity** for the Trinity by **sola Scriptura** and **autotheos** within **classical simplicity**.

### ESSAY
Richard of St. Victor emerges from the milieu of the **Abbey of St. Victor in Paris**, a powerhouse of the **High Medieval** renewal in the mid twelfth century. Born in Scotland and drawn to the Victorine house by the reputation of **Hugh of St. Victor**, Richard entered a community that refused to oppose **monastic contemplation** to **scholastic inquiry**. By 1162 he had become prior of the abbey, and up to his death in 1173 he supervised a school that shaped European theology through a careful balance of **lectio** and **disputatio**, **affectus** and **ratio**. The intellectual landscape included the authoritative influence of **Augustine**, the apophatic currents of **Pseudo-Dionysius**, the analytic impulse of **Anselm**, and the growing influence of **Peter Lombard** at the cathedral school of Notre Dame. Richard’s calling was to consolidate the Victorine program in which **spiritual theology** and **doctrinal precision** advance together, so that contemplative union with God and dogmatic confession of **Trinitarian** faith mutually illumine one another.

The Victorine method is best called **speculative mysticism**, a disciplined ascent that refuses anti-intellectualism while guarding the primacy of **contemplation**. Richard delineates an itinerary of the mind in which **cogitatio**, **meditatio**, and **contemplatio** designate increasing purifications and clarifications of the **intellectus** and **affectus**. The model is thoroughly **Augustinian**, for light comes from above as **gratia illuminationis**, yet the soul must be trained through ordered steps, the **ordo amoris**, to receive it. Scripture is read with **allegory** and **anagogy** not as arbitrary play but as a sacramental disclosure of God’s economy, in which the **literal sense** grounds higher senses oriented to Christ and the Church. Richard’s epistemic stance is therefore **faith seeking understanding**, reasoning not to judge revelation but to grasp its inner coherence, a method that culminates in *excessus mentis*, an overflow of intellect and affection under the pressure of **divine charity**.

Two programmatic works frame his contemplative pedagogy. In **Benjamin Minor** c. 1172, often called The Twelve Patriarchs, Richard outlines the moral and intellectual **preparations for contemplation**, insisting on **puritas cordis**, rectitude of will, and the integration of memory, understanding, and love in the soul. The goal is to stabilize the inner faculties so that **reason** can be elevated without fantasy, and **affection** can burn without disorder. This is the Victorine synthesis of **virtue** with **vision**. The states of attention are examined with clinical precision, yet every page is governed by **caritas**, the power that unifies the soul and readies it for God. Richard’s insistence that method is ministerial to grace is firm, but he also expects the human effort of **habitus** and **discipline** to serve as proximate dispositions to the gift of contemplation.

The more expansive **Benjamin Major**, also known as **The Mystical Ark**, c. 1173, deploys a symbolic exegesis of the Ark as a master image of the soul’s architecture. Here Richard correlates the **Ark’s measures and parts** with interior powers and stages of ascent, integrating **allegory** with a strikingly structured psychology. The ascent culminates in *excessus mentis*, where purified **ratio** yields to **intelligentia**, and love’s flame leads the gaze beyond similitudes into simple **divine truth**. The Ark’s geometry becomes a way to delineate the soul’s passage from knowledge of created effects *ad extra* to a stabilized beholding of God *ad intra*. This Victorine pedagogy refuses to separate **doctrine** from **devotion**. The Ark is not a mere metaphor. It is a pedagogical map by which a community is trained in **lectio**, measured examination, and obedient response to the **Word**.

Richard’s affective precision is nowhere more compact than in **De quattuor gradibus violentae caritatis** c. 1173. Here he describes four degrees by which **charity** becomes so ardent that it draws the soul into God with a holy violence, an appropriation of the **Song of Songs** as the grammar of desire. The progression moves from love that bears burdens, to love that dares, to love that wounds, to love that dies to self. The point is not ascetical heroism but the disclosure that **God’s love** precedes and empowers the soul’s motion. Richard keeps before the reader the Augustinian axiom that **grace** moves first, while urging the believer to cultivate a rightly ordered **affectus** that can receive such movement. This work reveals the Victorine conviction that moral transformation and dogmatic contemplation are inseparable under the sovereign governance of **caritas**.

The doctrinal center of Richard’s corpus is **De Trinitate**, composed during the final years of his life c. 1170–1173, a treatise that secures his enduring place in **Trinitarian theology**. The treatise aims to show the rational **convenientia** and even a kind of necessity of the **Triune** life given the perfection of **divine goodness**. Richard’s master principle is that the highest **bonitas** is diffusivum sui, self-diffusive, so that perfect goodness is necessarily **self-communicative**. From this axiom he argues that if God is perfectly **charitable**, there must be at least a lover and a beloved, whose relation is **personal** and not a relation between God and creation. So two persons, **Pater** and **Filius**, are fittingly posited *ad intra*. Yet perfect **caritas** is not exhausted by duality. Love reaches its plenitude when the lover and the beloved share a co-beloved, the **condilectus**, such that love is not self-enclosed but delights in a third. This third is **Spiritus Sanctus**, the **condilectio**, the mutual love of Father and Son as subsistent person. Richard’s argument is not a deduction from metaphysics alone but an illumination of the evangelical confession by **rational insight** into the nature of highest charity.

Richard guards **divine simplicity** and refuses both **tritheism** and **modalism** by distinguishing between the **unity of essence** and the **plurality of relations**. The divine essence remains numerically one, simple and indivisible, while the personal distinctions are constituted by **relations of origin** grounded in love. He adapts the Augustinian grammar of **paternitas**, **filiatio**, and **processio**, yet he transposes it into the key of **caritas**. The result is a conceptual bridge between **Augustine’s psychological analogy** and a more **interpersonal communion** model. For Richard, the Father is supremely **donor**, the Son supremely **recipient** and **respondent**, the Spirit the subsistent **consensus** or concord of love, which he names **condilectio** without collapsing person into relation. Throughout **De Trinitate** he employs precise definitions, aporetic questions, and syllogistic movements, yet he continually returns to **Scripture** to anchor his claims. The aim is **fides quaerens intellectum**, faith seeking a lucid articulation of the love that God is.

Richard’s interlocutors and influences illuminate his originality. He stands within the **Augustinian** current, reverencing **De Trinitate** while resisting a purely **psychological analogy** that might obscure the interpersonal fullness of divine life. He benefits from **Anselm’s** model of argument constrained by faith, yet he refuses abstraction that would detach reasoning from **devotional formation**. He enters conversation, sometimes by implicit correction, with **Peter Lombard**, who had accented **amor** as a name for the Spirit, clarifying that **condilectio** speaks not merely of a quality but of a person subsisting in relational origin. Richard rejects **Abelard’s** triad of power, wisdom, and goodness as insufficient to secure personal distinction and unity according to **simplicity**. Later, **Bonaventure** will absorb and develop Richard’s caritas-led metaphysics, and **Aquinas** will cite him with respect, while locating **processions** and **relations** more strictly in the order of **intellect** and **will**. These lines of reception show that Richard’s synthesis presses toward a grammar in which **love** is not a metaphor but an ontological predicate of the **Triune** God.

Richard’s integration of doctrine and devotion also shaped medieval **exegesis** and **spiritual direction**. The Victorine school insisted that **Scripture** is the matrix of contemplation, so Richard’s **allegorical** and **anagogical** readings curated a pedagogy for canons, clerics, and laity. His priorate fostered a culture where **communal liturgy**, rigorous **study**, and disciplined **asceticism** were mutually ordered to **contemplation**. The clarity of his **Trinitarian** teaching gave warrant to a spirituality centered on **love’s** participation in the very life of God without collapsing the **Creator–creature** distinction. Later medieval currents, especially the **Franciscan** school, prized his argument that **bonum** tends to self-diffusion, and modern ressourcement has drawn on him to recover a **relational ontology** ordered by **charity**. The Victorine way thereby serves as a hinge tradition, focused on **mystical theology** yet distilling it in disciplined **systematic** terms.

The profile of Richard’s legacy invites careful judgments. Some modern interpreters enlist him as a forerunner of **social trinitarianism**, pointing to his bold claim that personal plurality is the condition of perfect **love**. Others stress his continuity with **classical theism**, noting his insistence on **simplicity** and **relations of origin** as constitutive of the persons. The textual record supports both notes. Richard’s originality lies precisely in the way **caritas** articulates the relations without sacrificing the single **ousia** of God. His pedagogical corpus, from **Benjamin Minor** to the **Mystical Ark**, discloses how this doctrinal center rules the path of **contemplation**, so that the very ascent of the soul reenacts the **Trinitarian** logic of love. The Victorine synthesis therefore yields a theological spirituality in which **doctrine**, **exegesis**, and **practice** combine to train the Church in the knowledge and love of the **Triune** Lord.

A Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation gratefully receives Richard’s **Trinitarian** clarity and his rigorous identification of the persons with **relations of origin** under **divine simplicity**, as well as his insistence that **love** is not derivative but integral to God’s **aseity** and glory. Yet the claim of rational **necessity** for a **triune** God on the basis of perfect **charity** invites caution under **sola Scriptura**. The Reformed teacher will prefer to say that **Scripture** authoritatively reveals the Trinity, while reason offers **fittingness** accounts that never rise to proof. The language of **condilectio** is rich, but it should be normed by biblical revelation of **procession** and **mission**, guarding the identity of the **Spirit** as person rather than an impersonal bond. Richard’s **contemplative method** exhibits a noble **ordo** of *lectio–meditatio–oratio–contemplatio*, yet Reformed piety will relocate the ascent more explicitly within the ministry of the **Word and Sacraments**, union with **Christ**, and the ordinary operations of the **Spirit** in covenant history. Where Victorine pedagogy risks turning **grace** into a stepped technique of ascent, Reformed theology insists that **grace** is sovereign, monergistic, and covenantally delivered, though it gladly affirms disciplined habits that dispose to receive the means of grace. In sum, Richard’s vision of **perfect charity** as the grammar of the **Triune** life is a profound gift, provided it is received under the primacy of **revelation**, the freedom of **grace**, and the confessional guardrails of **autotheos**, **simplicity**, and **relations of origin**.

### RECAP NOTES
- Twelfth-century **Victorine** prior in Paris, bridging **monastic contemplation** and **scholastic** precision amid the **High Medieval** renaissance.
- Core problem: how **caritas** grounds both the intelligibility of **Trinitarian** doctrine and the soul’s **contemplative ascent**.
- Major works: **De Trinitate** c. 1170–1173, **Benjamin Minor** c. 1172, **Benjamin Major or The Mystical Ark** c. 1173, **De quattuor gradibus violentae caritatis** c. 1173.
- Defining tools: **divine simplicity** with **relational distinctions**, **condilectio** for the Spirit, **allegory and anagogy**, *excessus mentis* within an ordered **lectio–meditatio–oratio–contemplatio**.
- Key interlocutors: **Hugh of St. Victor**, **Augustine**, **Anselm**, engagement with **Peter Lombard**, critique of **Abelard**, influence on **Bonaventure** and notice by **Aquinas**.
- Reformed alignment: honor **Trinity** and **piety**, caution about claims of rational **necessity**, prioritize **sola Scriptura**, **autotheos**, **means of grace**, and classical **simplicity**.
- Legacy: a durable model of **speculative mysticism** where **doctrine** and **devotion** coinhere, shaping medieval **spirituality** and modern **Trinitarian** retrievals.
