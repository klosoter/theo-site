### TITLE
Baptist catholicity through Reformational retrieval for evangelical mission and confessional renewal.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Southern Baptist Resurgence context, Harvard historical training, Beeson Divinity leadership** frames a Baptist theologian shaped by **Augustine** and the **Reformers** in late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century evangelicalism.
- **Ressourcement evangelical method** that weds **sola Scriptura** to the **rule of faith** and the **Vincentian Canon**, aiming at **Baptist catholicity** rather than sectarian isolation.
- **Theology of the Reformers 1988, rev. 2013; Baptist Theology 2009; Reading Scripture with the Reformers 2011; Is the Father of Jesus the God of Muhammad 2002; Faithful Witness: The Life and Mission of William Carey 1991; Baptist Confessions, Covenants, and Catechisms 1996**.
- **Sola fide and union with Christ, regenerate church and believers baptism, sacramental realism without sacerdotalism, inerrant Scripture within a patristic-Reformational hermeneutic**.
- **Interlocutors** include **Augustine, Luther, Calvin, Anabaptists, E. Y. Mullins, Roman Catholics through ECT, Islamic thought on tawhid**, with critics like **R. C. Sproul** on justification and **fundamentalist biblicism** on tradition.
- **Reformed reception**: appreciative of **Augustinian soteriology, classical theism, and Reformational retrieval**, yet debating **imputation in ECT, covenant and baptism, and means-of-grace ecclesiology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Timothy George, born in 1950, rose within the **Southern Baptist** world even as he pursued rigorous historical study at **Harvard Divinity School** culminating in a ThD in 1979 under mentors steeped in **Reformation and Radical Reformation** scholarship. His early teaching at **The Southern Baptist Theological Seminary** coincided with the **Conservative Resurgence** that contested theological drift and reasserted **biblical inerrancy** and **confessional fidelity**. In 1988 he became the founding dean of **Beeson Divinity School** at Samford University, shaping an interdenominational faculty and liturgical life ordered by the **Apostles Creed**, **lectionary Scripture**, and a retrieval of the **great tradition**. George’s method exemplifies **ressourcement evangelicalism**. He prizes **sola Scriptura** while insisting that Scripture is read within the **regula fidei** and the **consensus of the fathers and Reformers**, an approach he names **Baptist catholicity**. The guiding conviction is that evangelical Baptists can be catholic without being Roman, Reformational without being sectarian, and missional without being reductionist.

This methodological posture yields conceptual anchors that recur throughout his corpus: the **Scripture principle** as more than a slogan, the **Vincentian Canon** as a heuristic for distinguishing heresy from orthodoxy, and a Reformation-shaped **theologia crucis** that frames the drama of redemption in **Augustinian** terms of grace and predestination. He opposes **nuda Scriptura** and embraces **traditio** as a humble, ministerial authority that serves **sola Scriptura**. The result is a hermeneutical posture that retrieves **patristic exegesis**, honors **magisterial Reformers** like **Luther** and **Calvin**, learns from **Anabaptists**, and reasserts distinctively **Baptist** commitments including **believers baptism** and **regenerate church membership**.

#### 2. Theology of the Reformers and hermeneutical retrieval
George’s breakthrough monograph, **Theology of the Reformers** in 1988 and revised in 2013, offers a compact yet comprehensive synthesis of **Luther, Calvin, Zwingli, the Anabaptists, and Cranmer**, situating each in late medieval context and tracing the doctrinal grammar of **sola Scriptura, sola fide, sola gratia, solus Christus, soli Deo gloria**. He highlights **Luther’s theologia crucis** and **bondage of the will**, **Calvin’s duplex gratia** of **justification and sanctification** in union with Christ, **Zwingli’s Scripture principle and civic Reformation**, **Anabaptist discipleship and believers church**, and **Cranmer’s liturgical reform**. The revised edition adds material on **Tyndale** and fresh debates on **justification**, underlining George’s conviction that Protestant identity is both catholic and reformational. In **Reading Scripture with the Reformers** in 2011, he advances a hermeneutic of retrieval that recovers the **literal sense** governed by the **rule of faith**, appreciates the patristic **quadriga** where appropriate, and inscribes exegesis within **catechesis and doxology**. Here the central thesis is that **sola Scriptura** never meant solo interpretation. Exegesis is ecclesial, analogical, and tethered to **creedal orthodoxy**. Through the **Reformation Commentary on Scripture** series he serves as general editor, curating a polyphonic yet orthodox conversation with **sixteenth-century expositors**, a living example of evangelical ressourcement.

This hermeneutical platform supports George’s broader retrieval of **classical theism**. He affirms **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** in the Thomist and Reformational sense while emphasizing the biblical drama of the **Trinity ad extra**. His account of **the attributes of God** appropriates **Augustine** and **Aquinas** refracted through **Calvin**, holding together the apophatic humility of *via negativa* with the cataphatic clarity of revelation in **Jesus Christ**. The overarching pattern is to receive catholic dogma through a Reformational lens, then articulate it for **Baptist** ecclesial life.

#### 3. Baptist identity, confessions, and ecclesiology
In **Baptist Theology** from 2009 George narrates the **Baptist tradition** from **John Smyth** and **Thomas Helwys** to **Particular Baptists** like **Benjamin Keach**, **John Gill**, and **Andrew Fuller**, forward to **E. Y. Mullins** and the late modern Southern Baptist experience. The central argument aims to retrieve a catholic and confessional **Baptist identity** centered on **believers baptism**, **regenerate membership**, **congregational polity**, and **religious liberty**, while critiquing reductionist appeals to **soul competency** that eclipse **ecclesial authority** and **catechesis**. George acknowledges Mullins’s gifts yet resists an overly experiential soteriology that undermines doctrinal confession. His editorial work in **Baptist Confessions, Covenants, and Catechisms** in 1996 reintroduces primary sources including the **Second London Confession 1689** and **New Hampshire Confession**, demonstrating that Baptists emerged as a confessional people within the **Reformed** family, not as doctrinal minimalists.

Ecclesiologically, George advances a **Believers Church** vision that is non-sectarian and sacramentally positive. He rejects sacerdotalism while retrieving a thick account of **Word and ordinance**, commending **preaching** and **discipline** as marks of the church in line with **Calvin’s notae ecclesiae** and **Baptist** distinctives. His biography **Faithful Witness: The Life and Mission of William Carey** in 1991 narrates the birth of the **modern missionary movement** as a downstream fruit of **Particular Baptist** theology, tying global mission to **evangelical Calvinism**. In this strand George also addresses **religious liberty and soul freedom** by returning to **Helwys** and **Roger Williams**, positioning **Baptist** public theology as both principled and neighbor-loving.

#### 4. Soteriology, justification, and evangelical catholicity
A hallmark throughout George’s work is an **Augustinian soteriology** framed by **effectual grace**, **total depravity**, **justification by faith alone**, and **union with Christ**. In popular-level treatments like **Amazing Grace: God’s Pursuit, Our Response** in 2000 he explicates the **ordo salutis** in a way that is warmly pastoral yet decisively monergistic. His Augustinianism governs his reading of **Paul** and **Luther**, and it also shapes his ecumenical engagements. As a primary drafter and signatory of **Evangelicals and Catholics Together** in 1994 and coauthor of **The Gift of Salvation** in 1997, George pressed for maximal agreement on **justification** without surrendering the **Reformation solae**. He insisted on the necessity of **fiducia** rather than mere **assensus**, affirmed **gratia praeveniens** and **justification extra nos** grounded in Christ, and used **imputation** language even as he recognized unresolved differences with Roman Catholic formulations of **infused righteousness**. The ECT process for George exemplifies **evangelical catholicity** under the authority of **Scripture**, with conscience-bound clarity about persisting disagreements and a common front in witness to **Christ’s saving lordship**.

George’s soteriology is further enriched by his emphasis on **union with Christ** as the duplex grace of **justification and sanctification**, following **Calvin**. He resists antinomian bifurcations and moralistic reductions alike, anchoring the Christian life in **pneumatological participation** while retaining the forensic priority of **justification**. He appreciates **Luther’s simul iustus et peccator** as a pastoral diagnostic and a hermeneutical safeguard for the cross-centered life.

#### 5. Scripture, tradition, and doctrinal rule
George’s doctrine of **Scripture and tradition** aligns with the *material principle* of the **Reformation** while refusing **solo Scriptura**. In **Reading Scripture with the Reformers** and across essays on the **Scripture principle**, he demonstrates how the **rule of faith**, the **creeds**, and catechesis functioned as hermeneutical scaffolding within which **sola Scriptura** operated. He advances **inerrancy** in the Chicago sense while stressing that inspiration and clarity are ordered to the church’s **catechesis**, **doxology**, and **mission**. The **Vincentian Canon** serves not as an infallible criterion but as a prudential guide to the **consensus fidelium** for boundary maintenance in the face of modern innovation. His retrieval of **patristic exegesis** refuses a nostalgic return to pre-critical methods, proposing instead a chastened **figural** reading that honors the **literal sense** and the teleological unity of Scripture in **Christus totus**.

Theologically, this places George within a stream of **paleo-orthodox** evangelicals who include **J. I. Packer** and **Thomas Oden**, yet his signature is distinctly **Baptist** and confessional. The goal is not eclecticism but **dogmatic coherence** under **canonical** governance, such that classical loci like **Trinity**, **Christology**, and **atonement** are received with confidence and preached with clarity in contemporary evangelical churches.

#### 6. Sacraments, worship, and Marian retrieval
On the **ordinances or sacraments**, George advocates a robust **sacramental realism without sacerdotalism**. He affirms **believers baptism** as the evangelical sign of **regeneration’s confession** and entrance into the visible church, and he commends **congregational discipline** as an implication of **regenerate membership**. On the **Lord’s Supper**, George urges Baptists to move beyond **bare memorialism** toward a **Reformed spiritual presence** in line with **Calvin**, in which the Holy Spirit feeds believers on **Christ’s true body and blood** by faith. In the worshiping life at **Beeson Divinity School** he modeled a blend of **Word, Table, creed, and prayer**, exhibiting the catholic impulses of his retrieval project.

George has also pressed evangelicals to recover a sober **Mariology** grounded in **Scripture and the councils**, affirming **Theotokos** and Mary’s exemplary discipleship. He rejects **immaculate conception** and **assumption** as lacking biblical warrant yet encourages honoring Mary within **evangelical catholic orthodoxy**. This same pattern appears in his retrieval of **creedal confession** in Baptist life, arguing that the **Apostles Creed** and **Nicene Creed** can and should function as doctrinal norms beneath Scripture for catechesis and unity.

#### 7. Islam, mission, and public theology
In **Is the Father of Jesus the God of Muhammad** from 2002 George offers a lucid comparison of **Christian Trinity** and **Islamic tawhid**, explaining why **incarnation**, **atonement**, and **Trinity** are non-negotiable for Christian confession. He maintains that while Christians and Muslims speak of **God** and **Abraham**, the denial of **homoousios Sonship** and the **cross** marks a decisive divide. Yet his tone is marked by **neighbor love**, urging hospitality and witness without polemical caricature. This work extends his commitment to **global mission** and **religious liberty**, themes already prominent in his profile of **William Carey** and in essays on **Baptist public theology**. The public square, for George, is a place for principled conviction and generous dialogue, a habit of mind also evident in his participation in **ECT** and in his contributions to outlets like **First Things**.

Missiologically, George sees **evangelical Calvinism** as fuel for **world evangelization**, integrating **providence**, **prayer**, and **martyr-witness** within a theology of the **cross**. He champions the church’s diaconal service while warning against **soteriological reductionism** in social activism. The missionary horizon chastens doctrinal work with urgency and hope, a pattern consistent with **Reformation** and **Baptist** exemplars.

#### 8. Editorial leadership and pedagogical legacy
Beyond monographs, George’s leadership as general editor of the **Reformation Commentary on Scripture** from 2010 onward has reintroduced a generation of pastors and students to **sixteenth-century exegesis**, including voices beyond the usual canon. His earlier editorial work such as **John Calvin and the Church: A Prism of Reform** in 1990 reflects a sustained commitment to placing **Calvin** within the broader ecclesial and liturgical renewal of the Reformation. At **Beeson Divinity School** from 1988 to 2019 he cultivated a faculty and curriculum that forms students in **biblical languages**, **historical theology**, **pastoral practice**, and **worship**, manifesting the unity of **doctrine and doxology**. George’s pedagogy mentors pastors toward **confessional fidelity**, **ecumenical charity**, and **missional urgency**.

Through decades of teaching, editing, and preaching, George has become a bridge figure between **Southern Baptists** and wider **evangelical** and **Reformed** networks. He has modeled how a **Baptist** can affirm **classical orthodoxy** with vigor, receive the **Reformation** with gratitude, and engage external traditions candidly, all while maintaining **Baptist distinctives**.

#### 9. Interlocutors, debates, and internal developments
George’s engagement with **Roman Catholicism** through **ECT** sparked debate among evangelicals, including critiques from **R. C. Sproul** and others who feared ambiguity on **imputation** and **authority**. George responded by sharpening evangelical affirmations of **forensic justification** and by naming ongoing disagreements over **merit**, **sacramental causality**, and **magisterium**. Within Baptist circles, he challenged the legacy of **E. Y. Mullins**, calling for a corrective retrieval of **confession, catechesis, and church discipline**. In historical theology, he interacts with **Anabaptist** scholarship while insisting that **Baptist** origins are best understood within a **Reformed** soteriological matrix rather than as mere heirs of **Radical Reformation** biblicism.

George’s internal development shows consistency more than rupture. Early emphasis on **Reformation theology** broadened into **evangelical catholicity**, then into global **mission** and **public theology**, while his core commitments to **Scripture, grace, and the church** remained steady. His work exemplifies a habitus of **Augustinian humility**, confident **Christocentrism**, and **Baptist** integrity.

#### 10. Reformed or WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** perspective in the Westminster stream, George’s project is warmly commended for its **Augustinian doctrine of grace**, **classical theism**, and robust **Reformational retrieval** that honors **sola Scriptura** within **creedal orthodoxy**. His call for a **spiritual presence** in the Supper, his defense of **forensic justification**, and his insistence on **church discipline** align substantially with **Westminster** convictions. Tensions emerge, however, at three points. First, his **evangelical catholicity** through **ECT** risked terminological convergence on **justification** without securing unambiguous agreement on **imputation** and the immediate, definitive character of **forensic righteousness**, where **Westminster Confession 11** is more precise. Second, his **Baptist ecclesiology** and **believers baptism** lack the covenantal architecture of the **one covenant of grace** that grounds **infant baptism** and the means-of-grace status of **Word and sacrament** as ordinary channels of **union and communion** with Christ. Third, his retrieval of **sacramentality** remains thinner than a full **means-of-grace** theology that interlaces **preaching** and **sacraments** in the church’s covenantal economy. A Westminster alternative would reinforce a tighter **covenant theology**, explicitly affirm **imputation** as the formal ground of justification, and maintain ecumenical engagement while drawing firmer lines on **magisterial authority** and the **nature-grace** relation. Even with these caveats, George’s **Baptist catholicity** stands as a salutary ally for Reformational orthodoxy and a model of learned, pastoral retrieval.
