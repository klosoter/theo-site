### TITLE
Reframing East-West division by retrieving first-millennium consensus on **filioque** and **primacy** through rigorous historical-theological analysis.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late modern Orthodoxy in a post-Vatican II, post-Communist world; ecumenical openings after 1965 and renewed focus on **East-West ecclesiology** and **trinitarian doctrine**.
- Historical-theological method grounded in **patristic sources**, **philology**, and **conciliar reception**, privileging descriptive accuracy over polemic.
- Key works: **The Filioque: History of a Doctrinal Controversy** (2010); **The Papacy and the Orthodox: Sources and History of a Debate** (2017); **Constantine: Religious Faith and Imperial Policy** (2017); **Orthodox Christianity: A Very Short Introduction** (2019).
- Central categories: **ekporeusis** and **processio/spiratio**, **monarchia Patris**, **synodality and primacy**, **symphonia**, **theosis** and **apophatic theology**.
- Interlocutors: **Augustine**, **Maximus the Confessor**, **Photios**, **Thomas Aquinas**, **Gregory Palamas**, and contemporary dialogues like **Ravenna 2007** and **Chieti 2016**.
- Reformed cue: probes classic tensions around **double procession**, **Scripture-tradition authority**, **council fallibility**, and **papal claims** vis-a-vis **presbyterial polity**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and disposition
A. Edward Siecienski emerges as a contemporary Orthodox scholar shaped by the late twentieth and early twenty-first century reopening of **East-West theological dialogue** after the 1965 lifting of mutual excommunications between **Rome** and **Constantinople**. Teaching as the Clement and Helen Pappas Professor of Byzantine Culture and Religion at **Stockton University**, his work situates the Orthodox tradition within wider currents of **Byzantine studies**, **patristic retrieval**, and comparative **ecclesiology**. The intellectual setting is defined by Vatican II s ressourcement and the Orthodox rearticulation of **conciliarity** in an age of global Orthodoxy, marked by controversies surrounding the **Holy and Great Council of Crete** in 2016 and the growing discourse on **synodality**. Within that context, Siecienski s orientation is deliberately historical-theological: a patient reconstruction of **sources**, **council acts**, and **lexicographic nuance** designed to lower the polemical temperature around **filioque** and **papal primacy**. The center of gravity is not speculative metaphysics but the concrete history of doctrine, the practices of reception, and the ecclesial weight of the first millennium.

The method that animates his corpus is a hermeneutic of historical charity ordered to clarity, seeking to distinguish genuine doctrinal incompatibilities from misprisions born of linguistic drift and divergent **canonical cultures**. In this, Siecienski aligns with trends in Orthodox and Catholic scholarship represented by figures like **John Meyendorff** and **Klaus Schatz** who reexamine **primacy** and **conciliarity** through the lens of the early church rather than through later confessional absolutizations. By attending to the semantics of **ekporeusis** in Greek and **processio/spiratio** in Latin, and to the political-theological weight of canons such as **Chalcedon 28**, he exhibits a philological scrupulosity that exemplifies the Orthodox commitment to **traditio** understood as living reception.

#### 2. The Filioque: history, terminology, and retrieval
Siecienski s **The Filioque: History of a Doctrinal Controversy** (2010) is both a chronicle and a map of the many-layered debate over **double procession**. He begins with the fourth-century consolidation of **trinitarian dogma** at **Nicaea 325** and **Constantinople 381**, tracing how the Greek fathers deployed **ekporeusis** to name the Spirit s origin from the Father as cause, while the Latin West, influenced by **Augustine s** Pneumatology in De Trinitate, elaborated the Spirit s origin as **processio** from the Father and the Son. The entry of the **filioque** into Latin creedal recitation at **Toledo 589**, its imperial defense at **Aachen 809**, and its eventual adoption at **Rome in 1014** form the historical backbone of his narrative, punctuated by the **Photian** disputes, the polarizing mutual anathemas of **1054**, and the conciliar negotiations at **Lyons 1274** and **Florence 1439**.

Central to Siecienski s analysis is the clarification of terms: the Greek **ekporeusis** denotes origin from the hypostatic cause, tied to the **monarchia Patris**, whereas Latin **processio** and **spiratio** have broader semantic ranges. Drawing on **Maximus the Confessor s** letter to Marinus, he highlights a mediating hermeneutic that reads the West s filioque as affirming the Son s role in the Spirit s eternal manifestation without compromising the Father s causality. He also canvasses the **Palamite** tradition s care to safeguard the irreducible hypostatic properties, as well as the Byzantine resolution at **Constantinople 1285** under **Gregory II of Cyprus** that spoke of the Spirit s eternal manifestation through the Son while denying a second cause. The strength of the study lies in its one-stop comprehensiveness, integrating **patristic testimony**, medieval **scholastic** development in figures like **Thomas Aquinas**, and modern ecumenical milestones such as the **North American Orthodox-Catholic Consultation** and the **Ravenna 2007** consensus that there is a level of primacy in the universal church, implying by analogy the possibility of terminological convergence on the Trinity.

Crucially, Siecienski does not collapse real difference into mere semantics. He is explicit that strong forms of the filioque which posit two coordinate causes compromise the **monarchia Patris**, and he acknowledges Eastern suspicion that the Western formula sometimes yields a de facto subordination of the Spirit s hypostasis. But his nuanced retrieval shows that much of the controversy arose from conflating distinct terms and from different strategies of safeguarding **divine simplicity** and personal properties. By joining historical detail to dogmatic mapping, he makes space for an Orthodox reception of the Latin affirmation under the maxim that the Father is the sole cause, while the Son s relation to the Spirit is an eternal order that may be named under the category of *proienai* without adopting Latin causality.

#### 3. The Papacy and the Orthodox: sources and history
If the Trinity defines metaphysical stakes, the church defines institutional stakes, and Siecienski s **The Papacy and the Orthodox: Sources and History of a Debate** (2017) narrows in on **primacy** and **jurisdiction** across the first and second millennia. The book collects and interprets key texts used by both sides, from **Irenaeus s** appeal to the church of Rome s preeminent witness, to **Cyprian s** insistence on the episcopate s unity without monarchical jurisdiction, to **Leo I** and **Gelasius I** and the maturation of Roman claims under **plenitudo potestatis**. At **Chalcedon**, with **Canon 28** elevating Constantinople by reason of the city s political status, the contest between sacramental primacy of order and juridical primacy of jurisdiction is already in play, and Siecienski tracks how **Pentarchy** functioned as a real, though not univocal, structuring principle.

The study moves through the **Photian** crisis, the **Byzantine** assertions of **oikonomia**, and the late medieval breakdown culminating in the uncompromising definitions of **Vatican I 1870** on **papal infallibility** and **universal jurisdiction**, followed by **Vatican II 1964** on episcopal **collegiality** in Lumen Gentium. Siecienski does not treat the first millennium as a golden age of administrative clarity; rather, he shows a fluctuating pattern of deference and resistance, case-law and theological argument. Contemporary dialogues, including the **Ravenna 2007** statement and **Chieti 2016**, supply a hopeful arc in which both sides can affirm a universal primacy in principle while contesting the mode of its exercise. For the Orthodox, the bishop of Rome as **primus inter pares** is intelligible within a robust **synodality** that resists claims of a jurisdictional supremacy; Siecienski provides the dossier and the conceptual apparatus to see why that position is not merely political but theological.

A strength of this work is its refusal to cherry-pick sources. Siecienski includes texts that cut both ways and admits the ambiguity of certain patristic testimonies. He also deals with the canon law axiom **prima sedes a nemine iudicatur** and offers a historically framed assessment of how such axioms functioned rhetorically and juridically. The upshot is a learned thickness: a picture of early primacy that is real, sometimes decisive, yet not the centralized, immediate jurisdiction over all churches canonized in later Catholic dogma. This nuanced account allows Orthodox and Catholic readers to see credible spaces for convergence without eliding non-negotiables.

#### 4. Constantine, empire, and the grammar of symphonia
In **Constantine: Religious Faith and Imperial Policy** (2017), Siecienski turns upstream to the Constantinian hinge that set the conditions for ecclesial life in the East. Here the keywords are **symphonia**, **imperial patronage**, **council convocation**, and the religious politics of legislation. Against older hagiography and newer cynicism, he paints **Constantine** as a ruler whose evolving Christian commitment shaped policy but did not erase pragmatism. The **Edict of Milan 313**, the imperial involvement in **Nicaea 325**, the Donatist controversy, and the construction of holy places are analyzed as moments of theology-in-power. By careful reading of **Eusebius** and other sources, Siecienski shows how the Byzantine valuation of ordered cooperation between **altar and throne** became a durable normative ideal, even as it generated tensions around coercion, episcopal autonomy, and doctrinal enforcement.

This study has dogmatic implications. The Orthodox commitment to **conciliarity** is historically entangled with imperial mechanisms that both enabled and constrained the councils. Siecienski s analysis helps explain why Orthodox ecclesiology never developed a centralized papal office: imperial unity and synodal processes served as the functional equivalent for maintaining catholicity. The later confrontation with **papal monarchy** therefore occurs against a backdrop where the church s unity is secured through **synods** and **oikonomia**, not through a singular juridical apex. That genealogy stabilizes his account of contemporary debates about **autocephaly**, **diaspora** jurisdictions, and the modern recalibration of **symphonia** in post-imperial contexts.

#### 5. Orthodoxy in primer: doctrine, worship, and ethos
Siecienski s **Orthodox Christianity: A Very Short Introduction** (2019) distills a lifetime of teaching about Orthodox faith and practice. The doctrinal anchors are **theosis** as participation in the **divine energies**, **apophatic theology** shaping liturgical and contemplative life, and the knitting of **Scripture and Tradition** under the canon of the **ecumenical councils**. He frames **icons** not as aesthetics but as christological dogma in wood and pigment, crucial to the Orthodox doctrine of **incarnation**. The sacraments, particularly **Eucharist** and **baptism**, are located within a cosmic liturgy where theology is sung before it is systematized. The book also touches the Orthodox moral and social posture, a tradition that emphasizes **asceticism** and **virtue** as the chrysalis for deification. In each domain, Siecienski consistently returns to the notion that doctrine is received liturgically, canonically, and doxologically, not merely argued in the schools.

What distinguishes this primer is the integration of his ecumenical investigations into the larger mosaic of Orthodox self-understanding. He does not bracket controversies about **filioque** or **primacy** as marginal; he shows how they are functions of deeper commitments to **monarchia Patris**, **episcopal collegiality**, and a liturgical ontology. By presenting Orthodoxy s grammar in short compass, he equips readers to see why certain doctrinal boundaries are felt to be not optional but constitutive of fidelity to the **fathers**.

#### 6. Interlocutors and ongoing dialogues
Across his works, Siecienski engages a canon of East-West dialogue partners. The Western spine runs from **Augustine** to **Aquinas**, refracted through medieval **scholasticism** and modern magisterial texts like **Pastor Aeternus** and **Lumen Gentium**. The Eastern spine runs from **Athanasius** and the **Cappadocians** through **Maximus the Confessor**, **Photios**, **Gregory Palamas**, and later Byzantine figures such as **Mark of Ephesus**. Against the common trope of two sealed canons, he highlights the use of **Maximus** as a hermeneutical bridge, evidencing a pre-schismatic capacity to read Latin and Greek claims non-competitively. He also notes how **Gregory II of Cyprus** attempted to give conceptual form to the Spirit s eternal manifestation through the Son, showing a dynamic Orthodox capacity for doctrinal creativity under the rule of the **monarchia Patris**.

In the contemporary scene, documents from the **Joint International Commission** for Theological Dialogue, especially **Ravenna 2007** and **Chieti 2016**, serve as lodestars for Siecienski s hopes for convergence. He reads these not as premature ecumenical triumphs but as careful statements that respect Orthodox commitments to conciliar process while acknowledging Roman commitments to a universal ministry. His posture is neither irenic at the expense of truth nor combative at the expense of charity; it is rather a historian s conviction that accurate memory is a precondition for doctrinal settlement.

#### 7. Doctrinal development and the limits of rapprochement
Siecienski s meta-thesis across the **filioque** and **papacy** volumes is that doctrinal development occurred in both East and West, but along different axes and under different pressures. The West s elaboration of **double procession** and **papal jurisdiction** responded to pastoral and political concerns in the post-imperial Latin world, while the East s articulation of **monarchia Patris** and **synodality** unfolded under imperial patronage and conciliar practice. He resists flattening these into merely cultural differences, insisting instead that councils and fathers set boundaries that cannot be transgressed. Thus he is open to seeing the **filioque** as reconcilable in an Orthodox framework if understood as non-causal, yet he is wary of any transposition of **Vatican I** claims into the first millennium.

His stance on doctrinal authority is similarly nuanced. Councils are determinative when received, and reception is a complex, centuries-long phenomenon. He thus grants an epistemic role to **tradition as reception** rather than purely to juridical promulgation. This has implications for how Orthodox engage Western dogmas: they may be respected as internal to Roman Catholic identity while not binding on the whole church without conciliar and pneumatic reception. Siecienski s accent on reception operates as a quiet answer to modern quests for immediate doctrinal closure.

#### 8. Methodological clarity: philology, case law, and retrieval
Methodologically, Siecienski exemplifies how **philology** and **case law** can tame grand abstractions. In **The Filioque**, he tracks the precise use of **ekporeusis**, **proienai**, **processio**, and **spiratio**, warning against anachronistic transfers. In **The Papacy and the Orthodox**, he lays out dossiers of appeals, synodal decisions, and canonical axioms, showing that practice often outruns theory and that any principled settlement must reckon with both. His **Constantine** volume functions as a preface that makes sense of why Eastern Christianity naturally thinks in terms of **symphonia** and **synodality** rather than jurisdictional centralization. This triangulation is an instance of Orthodox **ressourcement** without romanticization.

Crucially, his scholarship models an ethics of interpretation: opposing sides should state the other s case at its strongest. He therefore cites Western voices critical of overreaching papalism and Eastern voices aware of the dangers of fragmented autocephalies. The habit of naming internal critique on both sides undergirds his hope that **first-millennium consensus** can serve as a regulative ideal for future unity.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a classical Reformed and WTS vantage, there is much to commend in Siecienski s historically precise account of **filioque** and **primacy**, yet also points of principled divergence. Positively, his insistence on terminological clarity around **ekporeusis** and **processio** strengthens the Reformed dogmatic case for **double procession** by preventing category mistakes and by foregrounding **monarchia Patris** as compatible with the Son s eternal participation in the Spirit s origin under the rubric of **spiratio**. His documentation of first-millennium **primacy** without universal jurisdiction resonates with Reformed commitments to **presbyterial conciliarity** and the fallibility of all post-apostolic authorities. His retrieval of **reception** also chimes with the Reformed rule that councils may err and must be tested by **Scripture**.

Yet tension remains. The Reformed tradition affirms the **filioque** not merely as acceptable theologoumenon but as a dogmatic entailment of the biblical economy in John 15:26 and 16:7, read in light of **divine simplicity** and **Calvin s** teaching on the Spirit s being the Spirit of the Son. Siecienski s readiness to bracket the filioque as potentially reconcilable if non-causal may be judged insufficiently robust, since Reformed theology sees the Son s spiring with the Father as metaphysically grounding the economic mission, safeguarding the consubstantial communion without subordination. On **primacy**, the Reformed critique goes deeper than jurisdictional scope to the locus of authority: the norming norm remains the self-attesting **Scripture**, and no primacy, even of honor, can function as a quasi-constitutive principle for visible unity akin to the Orthodox valuation of **synodality** as a theological a priori. Finally, while appreciative of **theosis** as sanctification and communion in **Christ**, the Reformed insist that union with Christ is covenantal and forensic as well as participatory; the legal-acquittal grammar of **justification by faith** cannot be subordinated to participatory transformation. In sum, Siecienski s historical retrieval provides salutary clarity and shared ground, but Reformed orthodoxy would press for a thicker dogmatic account of **double procession**, a stricter subordination of all ecclesial structures to Scripture alone, and a unity theology grounded in **Word and sacrament** rather than in a renewed first-millennium **primacy**.
