### TITLE
Analytic clarity in service of apologetic fittingness: Christianity as the best explanation of our deepest desires.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary analytic philosophy of religion** rising after the **Plantinga–Wolterstorff** turn; engagement with **New Atheism** in elite university contexts.
- **Cumulative-case apologetics** combining **abduction** and modest **Reformed epistemology**; emphasis on **explanatory fit** and **narrative rationality**.
- **Key works**: **God and Time: Four Views** (ed., 2001); **Thinking about God** (2004); **A Reasonable God** (2009); **Our Deepest Desires** (2017).
- **Central terms**: **inference to the best explanation**, **argument from desire**, **skeptical theism**, **divine eternity** and the **A-theory/B-theory** of time, **properly basic belief**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Alvin Plantinga**, **Nicholas Wolterstorff**, **William Lane Craig**, **Paul Helm**, **Alan Padgett**; critics include **Dawkins**, **Harris**, **Dennett**, **Hitchens**; historical influences **Augustine**, **Pascal**, **C. S. Lewis**.
- **Reformed cue**: Affinity with **Reformed epistemology** and **sensus divinitatis**, yet invites **Van Tilian** questions about **covenantal epistemology** and **divine immutability** in debates on **God and time**.

### ESSAY
Gregory E. Ganssle’s career unfolds at the intersection of **philosophical theology** and **analytic philosophy of religion**, where logical clarity is pressed into the service of existentially alert **apologetics**. As a Senior Fellow with the **Rivendell Institute** at **Yale University**, and later as Professor of **Philosophy** at **Talbot School of Theology, Biola University**, he developed a public-facing but carefully argued case that the **Christian story** explains the most basic features of human life. His thesis is both modest and ambitious: theism, and more specifically Christianity, enjoys superior **explanatory power** and **explanatory scope** for the phenomena of agency, value, rationality, and longing, a claim he prosecutes through a **cumulative-case** method. Formed in the late twentieth-century renaissance inaugurated by **Alvin Plantinga** and **Nicholas Wolterstorff**, Ganssle integrates insights from **Reformed epistemology** with the techniques of **abduction** and modal analysis, seeking not proof in the Euclidean sense but rational permission and plausibility for **Christian theism**.

The historical and intellectual setting matters for Ganssle’s method. The collapse of mid-century **logical positivism**, the rise of modal logic in metaphysics, and the legitimation of **theistic arguments** in analytic circles provided space for projects like his **Thinking about God** of 2004. Located within the university’s public square, his work resists a purely confessional idiom while maintaining **orthodox evangelical** convictions about **creation**, **fall**, **redemption**, and **consummation**. The result is a two-level strategy: first, establish the rationality of **theism** by appeal to shared cognitive and moral data; second, display the distinctive **fit** of the **Christian narrative** with the **structure of human desire**. This procedure is neither evidentialist triumphalism nor fideist retreat. Instead, it is a calibrated appeal to common rational goods under the light of **general revelation**, guided by an Augustinian sense that the *ordo amoris* reveals our **teleological** orientation to the **triune God**.

His early editorial work, **God and Time: Four Views** in 2001, exemplifies his commitment to fair-minded debate over contested **divine attributes**. The volume stages the classic alternatives concerning **divine eternity**. **Paul Helm** defends **divine timelessness**, emphasizing **immutability**, **simplicity**, and **aseity**. **Nicholas Wolterstorff** articulates an **everlasting temporality**, highlighting biblical and phenomenological data of **God’s actions** in history. **William Lane Craig** argues for a mixed model in which God is timeless *sans* creation but temporal *since* creation on an **A-theory** of time, while **Alan Padgett** offers **relative timelessness**, using **relational metaphysics** to secure both sovereignty and dynamic interaction. Ganssle’s introductions clarify crucial distinctions like **A-theory vs B-theory**, **presentism vs eternalism**, and the implications of each for **omniscience**, **providence**, and **incarnation**. While the editor refrains from dictating verdicts, his framing shows how metaphysical choices in the philosophy of time reverberate through **doctrines of providence**, **petitionary prayer**, and **divine responsiveness**, pressing readers to reckon with the balance between **classical theism** and the dynamism of **biblical history**.

The didactic core of his 2004 volume, **Thinking about God: First Steps in Philosophy of Religion**, offers a map for students entering the field. Here **cosmological arguments** are re-articulated with modal precision, moving from contingent beings to a **necessary being** through **possible worlds** semantics. **Teleological reasoning** is cashed out not merely in the Paleyan key but as fine-tuning arguments, probabilistic across multiverse hypotheses, and sensitive to **Bayesian** updates. The **moral argument** is recast as metaethical pressure: robust moral realism, binding **obligation**, and irreducible **value** sit more naturally within a theistic ontology than in naturalistic projects. He handles the **ontological argument** with sympathetic caution, using it to teach modal concepts while resisting overstatement. Discussions of **miracles**, **religious experience**, and **religious language** insist on philosophical virtues of clarity and charity, while treatments of **foreknowledge and freedom** juxtapose **compatibilism**, **Molinism**, and **open theism**, guiding readers by distinguishing logical possibility from theological prudence. The text distills a pedagogy: analytic rigor, intellectual hospitality, and an eye for **explanatory elegance**.

The polemical dimension of Ganssle’s project surfaces in **A Reasonable God: Engaging the New Face of Atheism** in 2009, a book whose title signals the mood of measured response to **Richard Dawkins**, **Sam Harris**, **Daniel Dennett**, and **Christopher Hitchens**. Ganssle shows that the central claims of the **New Atheism** depend on conflations. Dawkins’s regress objection, the demand to explain the **explainer**, misunderstands **explanatory terminus** at the level of **necessary being**. Harris’s **moral landscape** does not secure the normativity of obligation without smuggling in moral facts that function like **Platonic forms** or de facto theistic axioms. Dennett’s **explanation-of-religion** is not an explanation-of-reality; causally mapping the genesis of belief does not adjudicate its **truth-conditions**. Hitchens’s moral indictments are confronted by **Augustinian** and **Kierkegaardian** accounts of sin and hypocrisy which grant the indictment while pointing to the **cross** as the decisive self-judgment of God upon human violence. Throughout, Ganssle relies on **Reformed epistemology** to defend the rational permissibility of **theistic belief** as **properly basic** in the right noetic environment, while arguing abductively that the **God hypothesis** remains the best available explanation of a morally textured and rationally ordered world.

If **A Reasonable God** plays strong defense, **Our Deepest Desires: How the Christian Story Fulfills Human Aspirations** in 2017 turns to offense by recasting apologetics as **fittingness** rather than mere **probability calculus**. Drawing on **Augustine’s** restless heart, **Pascal’s** reasons of the heart, and **C. S. Lewis’s** argument from desire, Ganssle identifies core human longings: to live a life of **significance**, to be rightly related in **love**, to become genuinely **good**, to know **truth**, and to find **home**. He then claims that the **creation–fall–redemption–consummation** schema affords a narrative in which these desires are both explained and healed. **Creation** grounds objective value and teleology; the **fall** explains the disorder of desire and the dialectic of idolatry; **redemption** in **Christ** satisfies the demands of justice and reorders love; **consummation** promises the final coherence of meaning and joy. The argument is not a strict deduction. It is an **aesthetic-rational** case in which the **beauty** and **coherence** of the Christian plot yield cumulative prima facie warrant. Ganssle’s innovation is to expand the **argument from desire** beyond a single experience of Sehnsucht to a pattern across moral, epistemic, and relational domains, thus linking existential phenomenology with **analytic** demands for explanation.

Across essays and lectures, Ganssle returns to two hard problems: **evil** and **divine hiddenness**. His deployment of **skeptical theism** is modest and tactical. He does not collapse into global skepticism but argues that human cognitive limits regarding the **goods** and **permissions** in complex moral orders undercut inferences from apparently gratuitous evils to the nonexistence of **God**. That strategy is supplemented by a **theodicy**-adjacent appeal to the goods of **soul-making**, the importance of **libertarian freedom** in the drama of love, and the role of **incarnation** and **atonement** in God’s identification with sufferers. On **hiddenness**, he engages the challenge of **J. L. Schellenberg** by distinguishing divine **noncoercive love** from mere obviousness, arguing that God’s aim to elicit freely offered **trust** is compatible with degrees of epistemic ambiguity. In both cases he returns to the central theme: which grand story places these data within a compelling pattern of **meaning**, **moral seriousness**, and **hope**.

Ganssle’s style has always been integrative across disciplines. He treats the **sciences**, not as alien empires, but as participants in a theistic frame of **intelligibility**. The intelligibility of laws, the mathematical structure of the cosmos, and the mutual calibration of **theory** and **evidence** all exhibit what he calls a theistic **fit**. He resists **God-of-the-gaps** reasoning in favor of deeper-level order arguments, akin to **fine-tuning** but widened to epistemic practices themselves. In aesthetics, he employs **beauty** not as a bare feeling but as a metaphysical clue to **form** and **finality**, resonant with the older Christian commitment to **transcendentals**. Ethically, he regards **virtue formation** as a signpost to the **beatific** horizon, using a light **Augustinian** virtue theory to show how sanctification fulfills human **teleology**. Each domain is recruited into the cumulative case, illustrating his claim that Christianity is not a local patch but a global ontology with stout **explanatory virtues**.

Although Ganssle is not a dogmatician, his philosophical work keeps company with classical doctrinal concerns. His fairness in curating positions on **divine eternity** shows both sympathy for **classical immutability** and awareness of biblical narratives that press toward **temporal relationality**. His treatment of **foreknowledge and freedom** teaches possibilities rather than legislating a single **compatibilist** or **Molinist** settlement, yet he consistently defends the breadth of **divine omniscience** against truncations. In soteriology, while he does not write technical treatises on **atonement**, he frames **substitutionary** and **victory** motifs as explanatory engines within the **Christian story** that satisfy both moral and existential debts. Even in his popular apologetics, **Christology** is not an afterthought; the figure of **Jesus** functions as the locus where the existential argument cashes out. The plausibility of a God who is both just and loving is, he argues, concretely manifested in the **incarnation** and **cross**, which anchor the apologetic of desire in a historical and doctrinal center.

Methodologically, his abiding contribution is to rehabilitate **abduction** for **Christian theism** without suggesting that probability alone settles ultimate commitment. He tracks the classical **theoretical virtues** of simplicity, coherence, explanatory depth, and fertility, then shows how these virtues take on a distinctive cast within a personalist, covenantal frame in which the **God of Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob** is the author of the world’s rational order. This protects him from a purely deistic outlook. Yet he also resists collapsing argument into testimony. For Ganssle, testimony and argument converge: the church’s **witness** offers a lived instantiation of the very goods his case predicts, and argument uncovers why such a life makes rational sense if the **gospel** is true. Thus his **philosophical theology** remains tied to ecclesial practice and moral formation, even as it navigates the canons of university reasoning.

Finally, from a Reformed and Westminster Seminary vantage, Ganssle’s project commends much and invites sharpening. His use of **Reformed epistemology** and **sensus divinitatis** coheres with a **Creator–creature distinction** that grounds moral and rational order, and his Augustinian **ordo amoris** bears covenantal fruit in showing how idolatry disorders knowledge. Yet elements of his **cumulative-case** stance can presume more **epistemic neutrality** than a **covenantal apologetic** will grant; the noetic effects of **sin** complicate appeals to common rational goods apart from **special revelation**. His editorial openness on **divine temporality** leaves classical **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** in a posture of friendly contest rather than principled confession, whereas a confessional Reformed theology would anchor **providence** and **aseity** more explicitly in a **timeless triune life**. A Van Tilian correction would press his explanatory strategy into a **transcendental** key, arguing that only the **triune covenant Lord** makes intelligible the very possibility of rational inference, moral normativity, and scientific law. Even so, Ganssle’s analytic clarity, Augustinian framing of desire, and Christocentric closure constitute a notable contemporary instance of philosophical theology that is both public and patient, offering the Reformed tradition a capable ally in showing that the **gospel** is not only true but also the most **fitting** account of reality.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Post-positivist **analytic philosophy of religion**, Ivy context with **Rivendell Institute** at **Yale**, later at **Talbot/Biola**; engagement with **New Atheism**.
- **Core problem**: How **Christian theism** secures superior **explanatory fit** for agency, value, rationality, and desire through a **cumulative-case**.
- **Works and positions**: **God and Time: Four Views** (2001) on **divine eternity**; **Thinking about God** (2004) mapping **theistic arguments**; **A Reasonable God** (2009) replying to **Dawkins** et al.; **Our Deepest Desires** (2017) on the **argument from desire**.
- **Defining tools**: **Inference to the best explanation**, modest **Reformed epistemology** and **properly basic belief**, moderated **skeptical theism**, attention to **A-theory/B-theory** stakes.
- **Interlocutors**: Influences from **Plantinga**, **Wolterstorff**, **Augustine**, **Pascal**, **C. S. Lewis**; debates with **Craig**, **Helm**, **Padgett** on **time**, and with **New Atheists** on **morality** and **science**.
- **Reformed critique**: Strong on **sensus divinitatis** and **teleology of desire**; queried by **Van Tilian** concerns about **covenantal epistemology** and safeguarding **simplicity** and **immutability** in **God-and-time** discussions.
- **Legacy**: A clear, charitable model of **philosophical theology** that weds analytic rigor to **narrative fittingness**, shaping pedagogy and apologetics in contemporary **evangelical** and **Reformed** contexts.
