### TITLE
Redemption accomplished and applied: an exegetical, covenantal soteriology ordered by union with Christ.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Scottish Presbyterianism**, **Old Princeton**, and the **fundamentalist modernist controversy** shape a transatlantic career from 1898 to 1975 culminating at **Westminster Theological Seminary** and in the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church**.
- An **exegetical systematics** grounded in Scripture’s authority, **redemptive-historical method** from **Geerhardus Vos**, and confessional **Reformed orthodoxy** with cautious use of **natural law** and rejection of autonomous **natural theology**.
- Key works: **The Free Offer of the Gospel** (1948, with N. B. Stonehouse), **Christian Baptism** (1952), **Redemption Accomplished and Applied** (1955), **Principles of Conduct** (1957), **The Epistle to the Romans** (1959 and 1965), **The Imputation of Adam’s Sin** (1959), **Collected Writings** (1976–1982).
- Central terms: **union with Christ**, **definitive sanctification**, **immediate imputation**, **particular redemption**, **federal headship**, **Adamic administration**, **covenant of grace**, **well-meant offer**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **J. Gresham Machen**, **Geerhardus Vos**, **Charles Hodge**, **B. B. Warfield**, **Herman Bavinck**, **Meredith G. Kline**, **Herman Hoeksema**, **C. H. Dodd**, broader critique of **Karl Barth**.
- Reformed reception: Strong approval for **penal substitution**, **imputation**, and exegetical rigor; debate over the **covenant of works**, **Mosaic republication**, and the logic of **natural law** and **common grace**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and the contours of method
Born in 1898 in Badbea, Scotland, **John Murray** was reared in the piety of **Scottish Presbyterianism** and marked by the sobriety of war after he lost an eye while serving in the Black Watch late in World War I. Studies at the University of Glasgow prepared him for **Old Princeton** from 1924 to 1927, where the imprint of **J. Gresham Machen**, **Geerhardus Vos**, and **Caspar Wistar Hodge** formed his lifelong instinct for a Scripture-saturated and confessional **Reformed orthodoxy**. The collapse of Old Princeton in the **fundamentalist modernist controversy** led Murray to join Machen in founding **Westminster Theological Seminary** in 1929, where he taught systematic theology from 1930 until 1966 and was ordained in the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church** in 1937. The academic and ecclesial setting pressed Murray toward an **exegetical systematics**, nourishing doctrinal construction directly from the text, with **Vosian redemptive history** governing the relation of *historia salutis* and *ordo salutis*. His historical milieu also positioned him over against **Karl Barth’s** revisionist dogmatics, against liberal moralism, and against biblicist reductionism, all in the service of a catholic yet distinctly **covenantal Calvinism**.

Murray’s method was neither speculative scholasticism nor anti-doctrinal biblicism. It was a disciplined retrieval of **federal theology** by way of rigorous exegesis, where doctrinal loci like **justification by imputed righteousness** and **union with Christ** are argued from Romans and the broader Pauline corpus. The primacy of Scripture does not exclude catholic categories; it orders them. Hence his deference to **Herman Bavinck** and **B. B. Warfield** on theological prolegomena, his adoption of **Vos’s** redemptive-historical horizon, and his careful use of **natural law** and **general revelation** without granting any autonomous **natural theology**. Murray speaks the grammar of the Reformed confessions with lexical precision, insisting that theological synthesis must be exegetically warranted and covenantally configured, never collapsing *historia salutis* into *ordo salutis*, nor dissolving the *ordo* into an amorphous experience of grace.

#### 2. Redemption accomplished and applied: soteriology under union
The signature of Murray’s dogmatics appears in **Redemption Accomplished and Applied** in 1955, a two-part architecture that distinguishes once-for-all **atonement** from the personal **application of salvation**. In the accomplished aspect, Murray contends for **penal substitution** and **propitiation**, rebutting **C. H. Dodd’s** reduction of hilas-terms to mere expiation and asserting the Godward appeasement achieved by Christ’s **active and passive obedience**. The finished work is definite and particular, yielding a robust doctrine of **particular redemption** that secures the efficacy of the cross for the elect. The applied aspect is organized by **union with Christ** as the fountainhead, from which flow **effectual calling**, **regeneration**, **faith and repentance**, **justification**, **adoption**, **sanctification**, and **glorification**. Murray carefully sequences these benefits without turning the *ordo salutis* into a mechanistic staircase, holding that the Spirit’s unitive act grounds both the forensic and the renovative gifts, while preserving the distinctness and priority of **justification** as imputed righteousness.

In shaping the *ordo*, Murray is notable for placing **regeneration** under **effectual calling**, not as a detachable prelude but as the vivifying efficacy of the call itself. Theologically, this secures **monergism** at the inception of faith while accounting for the integrity of **repentance** and **faith** as Spirit-enabled acts. The soteriology of **union with Christ** becomes the integrative motif: union is not a vague mysticism but the objective, Spirit-wrought participation in Christ’s death and resurrection, explicated by **Romans 6** and animated by the eschatological logic of the new creation. Murray’s careful terminological choices resist flattening the forensic into the renovative, even as he insists that the believer’s life cannot be hermetically sealed off from the legal verdict of **justification**.

#### 3. Definitive sanctification and moral order
Murray’s essay on **definitive sanctification**, collected posthumously but already woven through his teaching and the texture of **Redemption Accomplished and Applied**, marks a crucial conceptual advance. Defining sanctification not only as progressive growth but also as an initial, decisive breach with the dominion of sin, he grounds this **definitive sanctification** in union with Christ’s death and resurrection in **Romans 6**. The believer is transferred into a new realm where sin’s tyranny is broken, and from that definitive emancipation the progressive mortification and vivification proceed. This allows Murray to integrate **indicative and imperative** without sacrificing either, making room for the robust agency of the believer while maintaining the primacy of grace and the Spirit’s **effectual causality**.

The ethical sequel appears in **Principles of Conduct** in 1957, where Murray articulates a Reformed Christian ethics built on **creation ordinances** such as marriage, labor, and Sabbath. He unites law and gospel under the **covenant of grace**, denying antinomianism and legalism alike. The **Decalogue** is moral law, perpetually normative, and the **Sermon on the Mount** is not an impossible ideal but kingdom instruction breathed by the same Spirit who sanctifies. Murray emphasizes **Christian liberty** anchored in truth, the sanctity of **marriage** with a restrained view of **divorce**, and the consecration of work and worship, all governed by the lordship of Christ. Ethical reasoning draws on **general revelation** and **lex naturae**, yet is normed by Scripture, ensuring that any appeal to nature is christologically and covenantally framed.

#### 4. Imputation, Adam, and federal headship
With **The Imputation of Adam’s Sin** in 1959, Murray enters the contested terrain of **Romans 5:12 19**. He rejects **mediate imputation** as articulated by **Josué de la Place** and defends **immediate imputation**, arguing that the guilt of Adam’s trespass is reckoned to his posterity on the basis of **federal headship**. Yet he refuses to construe the federal in merely juridical abstraction; it is grounded in the divinely constituted solidarity of the race with Adam as representative. This yields a symmetry with the second Adam, Christ, whose obedience is likewise reckoned in a federal manner for the many. For Murray, imputation is not a naked fiction but a covenantal reality whose juridical character is anchored in God’s righteous appointment and in the redemptive-historical structure of humanity under two heads.

Murray’s analysis includes fine-grained exegesis of the prepositions and tenses in Paul, as well as theological calibration against **Charles Hodge** and **B. B. Warfield**. He preserves the classic **forensic justification** of the Reformed tradition while carefully attending to the ontological change that comes with union to Christ, thereby resisting any separation of the declarative from the transformative that would make sanctification optional. The pattern ensures that **imputed righteousness** and **definitive sanctification** coinhere without confusion, each rooted in the one Christ and activated by the one Spirit through the covenant.

#### 5. Covenant theology, Adamic administration, and Mosaic law
On covenant, Murray is both traditional and revisionary. He insists upon the unity and continuity of the **covenant of grace**, yet he questions the classical label **covenant of works** for Adam on exegetical grounds, preferring **Adamic administration** to describe a probationary order with promise of life. He does not deny a works principle in the prelapsarian order, but he is reluctant to enshrine a full-blown covenant category without textual explicitness and is wary of the language of **merit** in the pre-fall context. This posture also informs his reading of the **Mosaic economy**, which for Murray is an administration of the covenant of grace rather than a republication in substance of a **covenant of works**.

In this, Murray both aligns with and diverges from colleagues like **Meredith G. Kline**. He resists strong **republication** theses that treat Sinai as a reissue of the works principle as to its formal covenantal principle, insisting instead on the gracious redemptive context of the Exodus covenant, while acknowledging the pedagogical and typological functions of law. The moral law remains binding, yet the believer is not under law as a covenant of works. The upshot is a hermeneutic of continuity, in which **new covenant** ethics are organically related to creation and Sinai, fulfilled not abolished in Christ, and interpreted within the eschatological **covenant of grace**.

#### 6. Romans, justification, and Israel
Murray’s two-volume **The Epistle to the Romans** in 1959 and 1965 distills his exegetical systematics. On **Romans 3:21 26**, he defends **propitiation** against the background of divine wrath and justice, strengthening the case made in **Redemption Accomplished and Applied**. On **Romans 4 5**, he advances the doctrine of **imputation** and the parallelism of the two Adams. The commentary maintains an unequivocally **forensic justification** by faith apart from works, locating works not as instrumental but as evidential fruit within sanctification. On **Romans 6**, he grounds **definitive sanctification** in co-crucifixion and co-resurrection with Christ. On **Romans 7**, Murray reads the struggle as the experience of the regenerate believer, balancing realism about indwelling sin with confidence in grace. On **Romans 11**, he anticipates the **future conversion of Israel**, interpreting all Israel as the fullness of ethnic Jews brought to faith before the consummation, within an **amillennial** eschatological frame.

These exegetical stances clarify his broader polemics. He opposes any reduction of atonement to example, contends against **universalism** implicit in some **Karl Barth** trajectories, and refuses the sociological dilution of justification found in later New Perspective proposals. The coherence of Murray’s project is the Pauline center of gravity: **union with Christ** ordered to **justification** and **sanctification**, as explicated in Romans and harmonized across Scripture by a **covenantal canonics** sensitive to both continuity and eschatological fulfillment.

#### 7. Church, sacraments, and the well-meant offer
As an ecclesial theologian, Murray produced influential essays on church and sacrament. In **Christian Baptism** in 1952 he secures **paedobaptism** by the continuity of the **Abrahamic covenant** and by the inclusion of children in the visible church under the **covenant of grace**. Baptism, as sign and seal, signifies union with Christ and the washing of sins but does not confer **baptismal regeneration** ex opere operato. In pastoral ethics, his booklet **Divorce** in 1953 defends the limited grounds of adultery and desertion, aligning with a restrained reading of the Westminster standards. Throughout, the **means of grace** are central: preaching, sacraments, prayer, and the communion of saints administered under Christ’s headship.

The 1948 report **The Free Offer of the Gospel**, coauthored with **Ned B. Stonehouse**, became seminal in Reformed debates. Murray argues for the **well-meant offer**, that is, the sincere, indiscriminate proclamation of Christ to all, grounded in texts like Ezekiel 33 and Matthew 23, in which God discloses a **preceptive will** of compassionate desiderative delight in the repentance of sinners, even where his **decretive will** does not ordain their salvation. This is not a retreat from **particular redemption** but the biblical tension of divine benevolence and sovereign election. In earlier debates on **common grace**, Murray’s position stands against **Herman Hoeksema** by affirming real, non-saving divine goodness toward the reprobate, while maintaining the antithesis of faith and unbelief in soteriology and ethics.

#### 8. Prolegomena, revelation, and nature
In prolegomena Murray is a student of **Warfield** and **Bavinck**, confessing the plenary **inspiration** and **inerrancy** of Scripture and the necessity-sufficiency pairing of revelation. His actual method, however, is more akin to **Vos** in its redemptive-historical construction, avoiding both a timeless **analogia entis** and a historicist flattening. He acknowledges **general revelation** and the **sensus divinitatis**, as well as a form of **natural law** recognizable in Romans 1 and 2, yet he refuses any autonomous **natural theology** that would ground apologetic certainty apart from Scripture’s self-authenticating authority. In the Westminster orbit he neighbors **Cornelius Van Til** while not making Van Til’s **presuppositional apologetics** the architectonic of his system. Murray’s theological epistemology remains that of confessional Protestantism: Scripture as principium cognoscendi, churchly confession as rule of faith, and doctrine as disciplined exegesis.

This prolegomenal sobriety explains the tenor of his polemics. Against **C. H. Dodd** he refuses to demythologize wrath. Against modern existentializations of salvation, he insists on objective **atonement** and **imputation**. Against biblicist pragmatisms, he contends for covenantal and federal structures that pattern the whole canon. His method is neither a repristination of **scholasticism** nor a capitulation to modernity, but a restrained, text-first **Reformed dogmatics** oriented to the church’s worship and obedience.

#### 9. Legacy and a Reformed/WTS evaluation
Murray retired to Scotland in 1966, married in 1967, and died in 1975. His afterlife is carried in the **Collected Writings** and in generations of Westminster-trained pastors who imbibed his **soteriology**, **covenant theology**, and Romans exegesis. The church received from him a set of durable tools: the accomplished applied pattern of redemption, the recovery of **definitive sanctification**, a careful articulation of **immediate imputation**, and the pastoral balance of **well-meant offer** with **particular redemption**. Within the Reformed family his work functions as a stabilizing center between scholastic codification and modern volatility, an achievement inseparable from his ecclesial commitments in the **OPC** and his institutional labor at **WTS**.

An integrated **Reformed/WTS evaluation** must register both strength and tension. Strength lies in Murray’s exegetical case for **propitiation**, **imputation**, and **union with Christ**, his morally serious **creation ordinances**, and his constructive account of **definitive sanctification**. Tension arises around the **covenant of works** and the **Mosaic republication** debate. By preferring **Adamic administration** and minimizing prelapsarian **merit** language, Murray guards Scripture’s explicitness and the gratuity of life, yet risks blurring the law gospel contrast that classical Reformed orthodoxy deploys to secure the unique role of Christ’s **active obedience** under the works principle. Likewise, his continuity-leaning reading of Sinai admirably highlights grace but may understate the typological works principle later defended by **Meredith Kline** as explanatory of exile and typal kingdom. A principled alternative within the same confessional space would be to retain a carefully formulated **covenant of works** with an eschatological promise and to affirm a limited, typological **republication** at Sinai, while keeping Murray’s gains on **union with Christ** and **definitive sanctification**. On nature and culture, Murray’s cautious **natural law** appeals can coexist with a Van Tilian antithesis if the former is explicitly subordinated to special revelation. In sum, Murray’s exegetical systematics remain a gold standard at **WTS** and beyond, even as Reformed theology continues to refine covenantal grammar where law, gospel, and history meet.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Scottish Presbyterianism**, **Old Princeton**, and the founding of **Westminster Theological Seminary** locate Murray in the postwar and **fundamentalist modernist** crossroads of twentieth-century Reformed theology.
- A text-first **exegetical systematics** shaped by **Vos’s redemptive history**, confessional **Reformed orthodoxy**, and restrained appeals to **natural law** under Scripture.
- Major works: **The Free Offer of the Gospel** (1948), **Christian Baptism** (1952), **Redemption Accomplished and Applied** (1955), **Principles of Conduct** (1957), **Romans** commentary (1959, 1965), **The Imputation of Adam’s Sin** (1959).
- Distinctives: **union with Christ** as soteriological center, **definitive sanctification**, **immediate imputation** under **federal headship**, **propitiation** and **particular redemption**, cautious **Adamic administration**.
- Interlocutors: alignment with **Machen**, **Warfield**, **Bavinck**, and **Vos**; debate with **C. H. Dodd** on propitiation, **Hoeksema** on common grace, and **Kline** on **Mosaic republication**.
- Reformed appraisal: strong on **penal substitution**, **imputation**, and ethics; debated on **covenant of works** and the law gospel grammar; alternative is a typological **republication** framed by Christ’s **active obedience**.
- Legacy: lasting influence at **WTS** and in the **OPC**, a stabilizing Reformed voice for **atonement**, **justification**, **sanctification**, and covenantal exegesis.
