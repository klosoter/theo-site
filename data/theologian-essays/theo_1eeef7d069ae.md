### TITLE
A phenomenology of neoliberal positivity that dissolves otherness, ritual, and time, calling for renewed negativity, contemplation, and community

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary continental setting** shaped by **Heidegger**, **Foucault**, **Benjamin**, and Korean **Confucian and Zen** currents, within a late-capitalist Europe of platform economies and digital publics.
- **Phenomenological-cultural diagnosis** using **negativity versus positivity**, **immunological versus neuronal paradigms**, and **micro-essay aphoristics** as method.
- **Key works**: **The Scent of Time** 2009, **The Burnout Society** 2010, **Topologies of Violence** 2011, **The Agony of Eros** 2012, **The Transparency Society** 2012, **In the Swarm** 2013, **Psychopolitics** 2014, **Saving Beauty** 2015, **The Disappearance of Rituals** 2019, **Non-Things** 2021, **Infocracy** 2021, **Vita Contemplativa** 2022.
- **Central terms**: **positivity society**, **achievement subject**, **violence of positivity**, **psychopolitics**, **transparency**, **swarm**, **disenchantment of rituals**, **temporal desynchronization**.
- **Interlocutors and influences**: **Heidegger on Being and time**, **Foucault on biopolitics**, **Arendt on the public realm**, **Benjamin on aura**, **Levinas on otherness**, along with **Buddhist emptiness** and **Confucian ritual**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: read his **critique of autonomy and acceleration** through **Sabbath rest**, **covenant community**, **means of grace**, and **law and gospel**, while challenging his **immanent frame** with **triune transcendence**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Byung-Chul Han, born in 1959 in Seoul, migrated to German-speaking Europe in the 1980s and formed his voice against the backdrop of **post-Heideggerian phenomenology**, **post-Foucauldian critique**, and the globalization of **neoliberal governance**. Educated in philosophy and literature while carrying traces of **Catholic theological study**, he taught first at Karlsruhe and then at the Universität der Künste Berlin, honing a style of short, crystalline essays that function as **cultural diagnostics**. His pages are stocked with **Heideggerian questions of Being and time**, **Foucauldian power analytics**, and the tonalities of **Benjamin’s aura**, all refracted through East Asian sensibilities concerning **ritual**, **emptiness**, and **polarity**. The cultural setting is one of platform capitalism, quantified life, and digital mediation, where **subjectivity** is engineered by **protocols of optimization**. Han’s method is a **phenomenology of negativity**: he contrasts an older **immunological paradigm** that wards off the foreign with a new **neuronal paradigm** that accelerates the same, producing saturation, exhaustion, and depression under the sign of **positivity**.

Han’s epistemic stance trades in **minimal metaphysics** and **thick description of forms of life**. He is less system builder and more diagnostician, drawing on **Arendt’s distinction of vita activa and vita contemplativa**, **Levinas’s ethics of the Other**, and **Hegelian negativity**, yet translating them into a late-modern key where **self-transparency** becomes a technique of power. His prose advances by **contrastive binaries** such as **otherness and sameness**, **eros and exposure**, **ritual and performance**, **time as kairos and time as sequence**, and **community and swarm**. The wager is that renewed **negativity** liberates space for **contemplation**, **eros**, and **ritual**, against the flattening of an all-pervasive **positivity society**.

#### 2. The thesis of positivity and the achievement subject
The early manifesto-like works, especially **The Burnout Society** 2010 and **The Transparency Society** 2012, articulate a core thesis: late modernity is ruled not by prohibitions but by **exhortations to perform**, creating the *Leistungssubjekt* or **achievement subject**. Where classical **biopolitics** disciplined bodies through external limits, contemporary **psychopolitics** governs through internalized drives to optimize, share, and like. The violence is no longer immunological exclusion but **neuronal overexposure**, a **violence of positivity** in which the subject exhausts itself in permissions and imperatives to be ever available. The resulting pathologies are **depression**, **burnout**, **attention disorder**, and **anxiety**, which Han reads not primarily as medical issues but as **phenomenal symptoms** of a regime of surplus positivity that dissolves **alterity**.

In **Psychopolitics** 2014 he sharpens this into a theory of **neoliberal power**. Freedom is converted into the **coercion to choose**, transparency into **voluntary surveillance**, and authenticity into **branding of the self**. Data is not simply collected; it is confessed by subjects of desire who dream of being seen. Han reinterprets **Foucault’s governmentality** for digital capitalism, proposing that control has interiorized as **self-exploitation**. This is a crucial anthropological claim with theological resonance, since it reframes **sin as incurvatus in se** under new technological conditions, casting the subject as both master and slave within a single **positivity machine**.

#### 3. Eros, otherness, and the loss of the face
Han’s middle period explores the erosion of **otherness** and the impoverishment of **eros** under transparency. In **The Agony of Eros** 2012, he argues that desire requires **transcendence**, distance, and **opacity**, whereas the **pornographic regime of exposure** reduces the other to a mirror of the same. The disappearance of the **face**, a category indebted to **Levinas**, signals the vanishing of ethical summons and mystery. **Saving Beauty** 2015 continues the attack on a culture of **smooth surfaces**, where design and usability banish **wounds, scars, and sublimity**, thereby exiling tragedy, holiness, and the sublime pathos from the sensorium of the everyday. Beauty becomes **agreeable positivity**, stripped of **formative negativity** that once schooled desire.

Here Han combines **aesthetics, ethics, and anthropology** into a critique of **immanentism**. Desire collapses into consumption, and **eros** into **drive**. The impoverishment is not only sexual or aesthetic but spiritual, threatening the possibility of **love as relation to real otherness**. The consequence is a cultural asceticism without form, a self-optimization that destroys **intimacy and covenantal fidelity**. While Han does not build a doctrine of God, his insistence on **alterity** implicitly invokes a horizon beyond the empire of the same, akin to the theological insistence on **holiness**, **transcendence**, and **grace** as other-giving power.

#### 4. Time, ritual, and the possibility of dwelling
With **The Scent of Time** 2009 and **The Disappearance of Rituals** 2019, Han turns to **temporality** and **social form**. Modern acceleration dissolves narrative cohesion into points of **sequence** without shared **kairos**. The result is **desynchronization**, a time without rhythm, where projects pile up and attention frays. Rituals once stabilized time by installing **forms of life** that oriented desire and memory, but late modernity prizes flexibility over **binding forms**, thereby thinning solidarity. The redemption of time, in Han’s lexicon, requires **rites, repetition, and repose**, which make room for **contemplation** and the cultivation of **presence**.

This is amplified in **Vita Contemplativa** 2022, which commends **contemplation** not as withdrawal but as **political counter-practice** to incessant production. The contemplative gaze receives rather than grasps, listens rather than broadcasts, and in so doing reopens **worldhood**. Han’s retrieval of **ritual** and **contemplation** resonates with religious traditions, especially monastic **ora et labora**, yet he frames them within **cultural phenomenology** rather than dogmatics. The theological interlocutor hears the echo of **Sabbath** and **sacrament**, of **lex orandi lex credendi**, as practices that re-order **time** against the tyranny of **acceleration**.

#### 5. Transparency, swarm, and the collapse of the public
In **The Transparency Society** 2012, **In the Swarm** 2013, and **Infocracy** 2021, Han diagnoses a transformation of the **public sphere**. The ideal of **transparency** destroys **trust** by abolishing the protective veils through which promise, oath, and **covenant** are sustained. The **swarm**, a term adapted against naive celebrations of **swarm intelligence**, lacks stable form and leadership, making collective action volatile and easily manipulated. **Infocracy** argues that the regime of **information** replaces **truth** with **effects**, displacing **judgment** with immediate reaction. Digital confession produces a new **panopticon** without guard towers, where the many watch the many in the name of openness.

Han’s political anthropology retrieves **Arendt’s public realm** while marking its digital deformation. The result is a crisis of **authority** and **deliberation**, not primarily through censorship but through **noise**. Truth requires **silence, secrecy, and solemnity** in the right measure, a claim that unsettles liberal intuitions yet dovetails with the idea that **sacramental mysteries** demand **disciplined disclosure**. Theologically, one hears a critique of **iconoclasm of exposure**, a defense of **reverence**, and a plea for **liturgical time** against the permanent day of screens.

#### 6. Violence, copies, and the ontology of the present
Han extends his analysis to **violence** and **ontology** in **Topologies of Violence** 2011 and to **copying and originality** in **Shanzhai** 2011. Violence becomes **immanent and smart**, operating as **optimization pressures** rather than blows. Copying in the Chinese tradition, under the sign of **shanzhai**, reveals creativity within **variation** rather than under **authenticity** as Western metaphysics conceived it. These forays expose the metaphysical backdrop of Western modernity, which enthroned **identity, origin, and presence**, and thereby seeded the later **positivity compulsion**. The critique of **originality** is not a call to plagiarism but a metaphysical loosening toward **playful repetition**, akin to ritual’s creative conservatism.

Later works like **Non-Things** 2021 argue that the digital regime dematerializes the world into **information and options**, estranging us from **thingly resistance** and **dwelling**. Without **resistance**, there is no **formation**. Without formation, the subject remains a packet of preferences floating in a field of **stimuli**. Han’s ontology minds **thinghood**, **worldhood**, and **resistance**, which for theology signals the importance of **creation’s givenness**, **sacramental materiality**, and **ascetical limits** that shape freedom.

#### 7. Style, sources, and recurrent criticisms
Han writes in aphoristic **micro-essays**, a style that wins wide readership yet attracts charges of **homiletic overstatement**, **empirical thinness**, and sometimes **all-too-neat binaries**. His reliance on **Heidegger**, **Foucault**, and **Benjamin** sometimes appears as collage rather than construct, and his appeals to **Zen** or **Confucian ritual** can feel schematic. Critics complain that **positivity versus negativity** occludes socioeconomic structures, that **neoliberalism** becomes an omnibus culprit, and that **agency** disappears under diagnosis. Yet the very terseness of Han’s style exemplifies a **via negativa** against theoretical bloat and invites **theological appropriation** by clearing conceptual space around **eros**, **ritual**, **Sabbath-like repose**, and **otherness**.

The fruitfulness for theological work lies in his luminous **conceptual dyads**. He organizes cultural symptoms under **achievement subject**, **transparency regime**, **swarm**, **violence of positivity**, and **disappearance of rituals**. These become tools for re-reading **anthropology** and **ecclesiology** within a world of screens and scores. His prose functions like an examen for **powers and principalities**, disclosing how the **will to exposure** and the **dream of frictionless life** colonize desire. The theological reader can then re-inscribe these insights within **creation, fall, and redemption**, deploying **Sabbath**, **communion of saints**, and **means of grace** as counter-forms.

#### 8. Doctrinal trajectories and theological possibilities
Han offers no doctrine of God, but his insistence on **negativity** and **otherness** gestures toward a space in which **transcendence** can again be thinkable. The collapse of **eros** calls for an **agape** that is not reducible to likes, a love that can receive **wounds and glory**, and thus a recovery of **sacrifice** without violence. His rescue of **ritual** prepares for a reconsideration of **liturgy** as **temporal pedagogy**, with the ecclesial year schooling desire through **fast and feast**. His defense of **contemplation** repairs the **vita activa** by reintroducing **silence, patience, and attention**, virtues indispensable for **lectio divina** and the **hearing of the Word**. His care for **things** invites robust **sacramental realism**, not in order to idolize objects but to anchor the self within **created givenness** against the disincarnating pull of platforms.

In ethical and political keys, Han’s mapping of **psychopolitics** exposes the **idol of autonomy**, which theology names **pride** or **amor sui**. The subject that owns its destiny through self-exposure and optimization mirrors the **Pelagian impulse** toward self-salvation. Han’s contention that transparency undermines **trust and promise** echoes a biblical sense that **covenant** lives by **oath, secrecy rightly ordered, and patient disclosure**. His critique of **infocracy** points toward an apologetic that privileges **testimony, witness, and tradition**, rather than mere **information**. These trajectories do not build a system but open **aporetic space** in which dogmatic claims about **revelation**, **grace**, and **church** can address concrete cultural maladies.

#### 9. A single integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a Reformed and Westminster vantage, Han’s **phenomenology of positivity** is a powerful ally in unmasking modern **autonomy** and the **works-righteousness** of the *Leistungssubjekt*. His account of **self-exploitation**, **burnout**, and the **coercion to choose** accords with **total depravity** understood as pervasive corruption of desire and with **common grace** insights into cultural idols. Yet his framework remains within an **immanent frame**, rendering **negativity** a formal principle rather than the **cross of Christ** as penal and liberating **contradiction** to sin. The Reformed alternative grounds the needed **negativity** in **law and gospel**, where the **lex** exposes and the **euangelion** delivers, reconstituting persons by **union with Christ** through **Word and sacrament**. Han’s praise of **ritual** finds concrete counter-formation in the **Lord’s Day**, **ordinary means of grace**, and **church discipline**, which stabilize **time** and **identity** without romanticizing tradition. His valorization of **contemplation** is enfolded into a doctrine of **vocation** and **Sabbath**, where rest is not a technique but **covenant gift** grounded in **creation and redemption**. Against the tyranny of **transparency**, Reformed piety upholds **modesty, secrecy of the heart, and prayer in the closet**, while resisting **infocracy** by centering **Scripture’s perspicuity** rather than the algorithm’s visibility. Where Han discerns the **agony of eros**, Reformed dogmatics proclaims **agape poured out by the Spirit**, healing the **incurvatus in se** and restoring true **otherness** in the **communion of saints**. Thus appropriated and surpassed, Han’s diagnostics become handmaids to a **covenantal, Christocentric** cure that supplies what his analyses lack: **grace from above**, **truth in revelation**, and **hope in eschatological rest**.

- **Major works**: **The Burnout Society** 2010, **The Transparency Society** 2012, **The Agony of Eros** 2012, **Psychopolitics** 2014, **Saving Beauty** 2015, **The Disappearance of Rituals** 2019, **Non-Things** 2021, **Infocracy** 2021.

- **Interlocutors**: **Heidegger on time**, **Foucault on power**, **Arendt on the public**, **Levinas on the face**, with **Zen and Confucian** inflections and debates over **neoliberalism**.
- **Reformed critique**: affirm diagnosis of **autonomy and acceleration**, but supply **triune transcendence**, **law and gospel**, **union with Christ**, **Sabbath and means of grace** as the redemptive alternative.
- **Legacy**: a widely read **cultural phenomenologist** whose **diagnostic lexicon** equips theology and ethics to confront **digital capitalism**, **anxious subjectivity**, and the loss of **ritual time**.
