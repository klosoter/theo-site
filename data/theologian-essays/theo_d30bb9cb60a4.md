### TITLE
Word and world held together: expository proclamation of the crucified Christ for integral mission.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar British evangelicalism**, rebuilding after **two world wars** and against **liberal Anglicanism** and **Bultmannian demythologizing**, with global horizons through the **Lausanne Movement 1974**.
- **Double listening** method to **Scripture and culture**, an **expository preaching** epistemic stance committed to **biblical authority** and pastoral reason.
- **Basic Christianity 1958**, **Baptism and Fullness 1964**, **Christian Mission in the Modern World 1975**, **Between Two Worlds 1982**, **Issues Facing Christians Today 1984**, **The Cross of Christ 1986**, **The Contemporary Christian 1992**, **Evangelical Truth 1999**, **The Radical Disciple 2010**.
- **Penal substitution**, **integral mission**, **creation stewardship**, **discipleship and sanctification**, **ecclesial presence within Anglicanism**, and **conditional immortality** debate on **hell**.
- Interlocutors and influences include **Billy Graham**, **J. I. Packer**, **Carl F. H. Henry**, **D. M. Lloyd-Jones**, **Gustaf Aulen**, **John A. T. Robinson**, and Majority World voices through **Langham Partnership**.
- Reformed cue: strong alignment on **atonement** and **expository Scripture**, tensions over **inerrancy formulation**, **ecclesiology**, and **eternal punishment** vis-a-vis **Westminster Standards**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and pastoral setting
John R. W. Stott, born in 1921 and died in 2011, emerged as the clearest voice of postwar **evangelical Anglicanism** by joining rigorous **expository preaching** to lucid public theology. Educated at **Rugby School**, converted under **Eric Nash** in 1938, and formed at **Trinity College, Cambridge** and **Ridley Hall**, he was ordained in 1945, appointed curate and then rector of **All Souls Langham Place** in London from 1950 to 1975, and later rector emeritus. His ministry matured through the reconstruction of British evangelical identity after the devastations of war and the ascendancy of **liberal theology** marked by **Bultmannian demythologizing** and the cultural shock of **John A. T. Robinsons** Honest to God in 1963. Stotts pastoral base at All Souls became a laboratory for **biblical authority**, mission, and public engagement, and his eventual role as chief framer of the **Lausanne Covenant 1974** amplified his influence across the global evangelical network.

Stotts ecclesial location was deliberately non-separatist within the **Church of England**, which placed him at the center of a defining British evangelical debate. The 1966 confrontation with **D. M. Lloyd-Jones** at the Evangelical Alliance, where Stott urged unity in and reform of the national church rather than evangelical separatism, set the trajectory for his ecclesiology of patient presence. His leadership at the **National Evangelical Anglican Congress** in **Keele 1967** and later at **Nottingham 1977** encouraged evangelicals to embrace social engagement alongside proclamation. This Anglican context framed Stotts method as a ministry of **double listening** to the Word and to the world, a phrase he popularized to describe evangelical attentiveness to the voice of **Scripture** and the cries of contemporary culture under the supremacy of Christ.

#### 2. Method and epistemic stance: double listening and expository preaching
Stotts defining method is captured by his insistence on **double listening** and a disciplined practice of **expository preaching**. In **Between Two Worlds 1982** and its UK twin **I Believe in Preaching 1982**, he contrasted a pulpit of timeless truths with a pulpit of timely relevance and argued that true preaching bridges both. The expositor does not start with felt needs but with the text, and yet the sermon aims at the conscience and public life. The epistemic stance is confessional as to the **authority of Scripture**, rational without rationalism, and pastoral in orientation. Stott affirmed the trustworthiness and divine origin of the Bible, speaking often of **biblical infallibility** and the clarity of Scripture, while preferring the language of responsible **biblical authority** over more technical American debates about the Chicago formulation of **inerrancy**.

This method surfaces across his catechetical and apologetic works. **Basic Christianity 1958** presents a credal skeleton of evangelical faith rooted in **Christology**, **sin**, **atonement**, **resurrection**, and **discipleship**. **Your Mind Matters 1972** contends that devotion requires a renewed intellect, not anti-intellectual pietism. Stotts hermeneutic is canonical and Christocentric, disciplined by the *analogia fidei* and sensitive to literary context, yet oriented to public persuasion. His approach to **natural theology** is cautious: general revelation discloses the Creator, but the saving knowledge of God comes through the **gospel** and the **written Word**, with **expository preaching** as the ordinary means of grace for the gathered church.

#### 3. The cross at the center: atonement, justification, and holiness
If Stott had a single doctrinal center, it is the **cross**. **The Cross of Christ 1986** is his mature dogmatic statement on **atonement**, marshaling biblical exegesis, historical theology, and moral analysis to defend **penal substitution** within the wider family of atonement motifs. Against **Gustaf Aulens Christus Victor**, Stott affirmed the victory motif but insisted that the decisive victory over the powers is accomplished through substitutionary judgment borne by Christ. He integrated **objective propitiation** and **subjective transformation**, grounding **justification by faith** in the gift of imputed righteousness while urging **sanctification** as the Spirit-wrought fruit of union with the crucified and risen Lord.

Stotts moral theology flowed from the cross into discipleship. Works like **The Contemporary Christian 1992** and **The Radical Disciple 2010** sketch a cruciform ethic: humility, simplicity, balance between **creation stewardship** and **world renunciation**, and neighbor love expressed in public life. In **Baptism and Fullness 1964**, he rejected a Pentecostal second blessing and argued for a once-for-all Spirit baptism with a call to continual filling, thus aligning sanctification with the Reformation emphasis on definitive grace and progressive growth. The cross, then, governs his soteriology, his pneumatology, and his ethics, shaping a pastoral call to holiness that is evangelical rather than sacramentalist, yet deeply ecclesial and missionally engaged.

#### 4. Evangelism and social responsibility: integral mission and Lausanne
Stott is inseparable from the modern evangelical recovery of **integral mission**, the inseparability of **evangelism** and **social responsibility** under the lordship of Christ. In **Christian Mission in the Modern World 1975**, he distinguished evangelism, which centers on the verbal proclamation of the **gospel**, from social action, which is not the gospel but is a partner and consequence of the gospel. The language is careful: social responsibility is both a fruit of conversion and a pointer to the kingdom, and, in some settings, a bridge to witness. As principal drafter of the **Lausanne Covenant 1974**, Stott positioned global evangelicalism to confess both the necessity of conversion and the obligation to seek **justice**, **mercy**, and **human dignity**. Later Lausanne consultations, including **Manila 1989**, developed this trajectory, drawing Majority World leaders into a sustained conversation about **holistic mission**.

Stotts public theology found concrete expression in **Issues Facing Christians Today 1984**, a running engagement with ethics across abortion, marriage, racial injustice, economic life, and environmental care. He argued from **creation order**, the **image of God**, and the **Sermon on the Mount** toward principled involvement in public life, seeking neither Constantinian capture nor sectarian withdrawal. His founding of the **London Institute for Contemporary Christianity 1982** institutionalized this vision of equipping laity for discipleship in the marketplace. This sustained effort to shape evangelical social ethics was not an annex to evangelism but a disciplined outworking of Christological lordship and neighbor love, governed by **biblical authority** and communicated through accessible **expository application**.

#### 5. Global evangelicalism: Langham and Majority World theology
One of Stotts most prescient contributions is his turn toward the **Majority World**. With the **Langham Trust 1969** and later **Langham Partnership International**, he invested in expository resources, pastoral training, and doctoral scholarships to elevate local leadership in Africa, Asia, and Latin America. His refrain that leadership must move from the West to the rest to the world anticipated the demographic shift of **global Christianity**. He championed figures like **Christopher J. H. Wright** and encouraged worldwide expository preaching through the **Bible Speaks Today** series, to which he contributed volumes on **Ephesians 1979**, **Acts 1990**, and the **Sermon on the Mount**.

The Lausanne network provided the platform for Majority World voices to reshape evangelical agendas on **poverty**, **political oppression**, and **creation stewardship**. Stott listened and learned, deepening his commitments to **integral mission** and **environmental care**, notably in later editions of **Issues Facing Christians Today** and in **The Contemporary Christian**. This posture sustained his **double listening** method on a global scale, refusing a monocultural gospel while maintaining the supremacy of **biblical revelation**. The result is a catholic evangelicalism, centered on the cross and the Bible, yet polyphonic in its cultural expressions and mission strategies.

#### 6. Controversies and debates: Anglican comprehensiveness, inerrancy, and hell
Stotts irenic temperament did not shield him from controversy. The most visible ecclesial dispute, with **Lloyd-Jones in 1966**, concerned **ecclesiology**. Stott argued that evangelical faithfulness does not require separation from the **Church of England**, provided one resists doctrinal compromise and contends for truth from within. His public exchanges with **J. I. Packer** and American evangelicals over **Evangelicals and Catholics Together 1994** showed him cautious about cooperative statements that might blur **justification by faith alone**, though he remained open to courteous dialogue. On **biblical authority**, Stott preferred the classic British language of **infallibility** and did not champion the precise vocabulary of the **Chicago Statement on Inerrancy 1978**, while maintaining that Scripture, rightly interpreted, is wholly true in all it affirms.

The most controversial doctrinal note came late, in his acknowledgment that **conditional immortality** or **annihilationism** is an exegetically serious view of **hell**. In dialogue with **David L. Edwards** in **Essentials 1988**, and drawing on earlier British evangelicals like **John Wenham** and **Basil Atkinson**, Stott held that eternal conscious punishment is the historic majority position but argued that the language of destruction may indicate irreversible extinction. He did not make this a test of fellowship and urged charity, yet his openness provoked strong responses from defenders of **eternal conscious torment**. The posture reflects his method: return to **biblical exegesis**, weigh terms, and resist making more than Scripture warrants. It also reveals the limits of his catholicity when it comes to confessional specificity.

#### 7. Apologetics and polemics: Christ the Controversialist and public reason
Stott was a pastoral apologist rather than an academic philosopher, yet he understood modern doubt. In **Christ the Controversialist 1970**, he confronted **religious pluralism**, **moral relativism**, and **revisionist Christology** within Anglican liberalism, insisting on the exclusive claims of Jesus and the authority of the **gospels**. **Basic Christianity** had already supplied an accessible case for the incarnation, atonement, and resurrection, aiming at the university context shaped by **IFES** and **UCCF**. He critiqued **Bultmann** on demythologization and argued that the New Testament witnesses are not myth but apostolic testimony. His apologetic style models the **double listening** he commends: hear the questions of the age, but answer from **Scripture** with intellectual integrity and moral seriousness.

A related feature is his view of the **Sermon on the Mount** and the **Beatitudes** as a public counterculture. For Stott, Christian witness is credible when the church embodies the **holy love** of God in a distinctive life. This theme recurs in **The Contemporary Christian** and **The Radical Disciple**, where he calls for simplicity of lifestyle, **creation stewardship**, and a repudiation of consumerism. The apologetic is thus ecclesial and ethical: the world must see the plausibility of the gospel in a people formed by the **cross** and nourished by **expository preaching**. His tone is firm without rancor, seeking persuasion through clarity and integrity.

#### 8. Preaching and formation: a craft tradition
Stott is also an architect of a craft tradition in **expository preaching**. He trained generations of clergy at **All Souls** and abroad through the **Langham Preaching** movement, emphasizing text-driven structure, clear outlines, and application. **Between Two Worlds** codifies this craft, including his insistence that preachers sit under the text before they stand over the congregation. The goal is not novelty but **faithful proclamation** of the **whole counsel of God**. His commentaries in the **Bible Speaks Today** series model clarity without pedantry, and his homiletic counsel integrates doctrine and discipleship.

This formation was institutional as well as personal. The **London Institute for Contemporary Christianity** sought to equip lay believers for their vocations, a theme consistent with his doctrine of **creation** and the dignity of work. Stotts long pastoral tenure and global travel created a network of protégés who spread his homiletic method and theological emphases. He was, in short, a curator of evangelical habits: regular **Bible exposition**, thoughtful engagement with public life, and the **cross-centered** shape of Christian existence.

#### 9. Final synthesis and theological profile
Theologically, Stott occupies a spacious **evangelical** center with clearly defined edges. On **Scripture**, he is an infallibilist who prizes **biblical authority** and canonical exegesis; on **God and Christ**, he is creedal and Chalcedonian, emphasizing the **incarnation** and **substitutionary atonement**; on **salvation**, he is Reformation-minded, with **justification by faith alone** and **sanctification** as Spirit-driven obedience; on **church and mission**, he is Lausanne-oriented, binding **evangelism** to **social responsibility** without conflation; on **ethics**, he appeals to **creation order** and the **image of God** while urging cruciform discipleship; on **last things**, he exhibits cautious openness concerning **hell**. His style is patient, untheatrical, and eminently teachable, aiming to put the Bible back at the center of the church for the life of the world.

This synthesis is historically situated. Stott is a pastor-evangelist shaped by British **Anglicanism**, globalized by **Lausanne**, sharpened by controversy with **Lloyd-Jones**, and sustained by partnerships with **Billy Graham**, **Carl F. H. Henry**, and **J. I. Packer**. His corpus from **Basic Christianity 1958** to **The Radical Disciple 2010** shows remarkable thematic integrity: the **cross**, **expository preaching**, **double listening**, and **integral mission**. The institutions he founded, from **Langham Partnership** to **LICC**, embody his conviction that theological renewal must be yoked to preaching and public discipleship across the Majority World. His legacy is thus ecclesial and global, a gift of habits as much as of theses.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and especially **WTS** vantage, Stott offers rich convergence with classical orthodoxy on **Scripture**, **Christology**, **penal substitution**, and the necessity of **expository preaching** as an ordinary means of grace. His dogmatic heart in **The Cross of Christ** strengthens evangelical confidence in **propitiation**, **imputation**, and the God-centeredness of salvation, while his Lausanne-shaped **mission** resists both pietist privatization and revolutionary politicization. Yet several tensions remain. First, his preference for general **biblical authority** and British **infallibility** over precise **inerrancy** could be sharpened to avoid hermeneutical drift, since confessional Reformed theology grounds preaching in the plenary, verbal truthfulness of the autographa. Second, his **double listening** risks a soft norming of exegesis by cultural plausibility unless tethered explicitly to the **covenant**al drama of redemption as the controlling interpretive horizon. Third, his ecclesial strategy of Anglican comprehensiveness can blur **discipline** and confessional clarity that Reformed polity prizes. Fourth, his openness to **conditional immortality** collides with the **Westminster Confessions** teaching on **eternal conscious punishment**, and undercuts the gravity of divine wrath that his own account of the cross otherwise secures. A Reformed alternative would retain Stotts integrative mission and cross-centered preaching while anchoring them more explicitly in confessional **inerrancy**, a robust **two-age eschatology** that clarifies church and world, and a covenantal hermeneutic that guards Scripture from the tacit magisterium of cultural plausibility.
