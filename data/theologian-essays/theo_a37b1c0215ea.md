### TITLE
Hearing God’s Two Books: Evangelical Hermeneutics and Earth History in Service of Christian Discipleship

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary American evangelicalism**, post-Scopes and post-ICR, with the rise of **ASA** and **BioLogos** shaping science-faith discourse.
- Method marked by **two books theology** and **critical realism**, integrating **general revelation** and **special revelation** through **consilience** and **genre-sensitive exegesis**.
- Key works: **The Grand Canyon, Monument to an Ancient Earth** (co-editor, 2016); **Friend of Science, Friend of Faith** (2019); **The Manifold Beauty of Genesis One** with **Kenneth J. Turner** (2021).
- Central categories: **inerrancy by authorial intent**, **accommodation**, **ancient Near Eastern context**, **evolutionary creation**, **cross-checks among dating methods**, **local vs global flood**.
- Main interlocutors: **Ken Ham** and **Andrew Snelling**; **Henry Morris** and **John Whitcomb**; conversation partners like **Hugh Ross**, **John H. Walton**, **Francis Collins**, **Deborah Haarsma**, and **Joshua Swamidass**.
- Reformed reception cue: appeals to **Belgic Confession 2** on the **two books**, but pressures **historical Adam**, **original sin**, **death before the fall**, and **nature-grace** relations.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Context, and Method
Gregg Davidson emerges within late twentieth and early twenty-first century **evangelicalism**, a period marked by both the consolidation of **young-earth creationism** through **Henry Morris** and **John Whitcomb** and the maturation of integrative institutions like the **American Scientific Affiliation** and **BioLogos**. Trained as a professional **geologist and hydrologist**, Davidson has spent his career within the university, contributing to environmental and earth sciences while writing for a broad Christian public. His ecclesial location is self-consciously **evangelical**, affirming **Scripture’s authority** and the **gospel’s centrality**, yet determined to dismantle the false dilemma that pits the **book of God’s Works** against the **book of God’s Word**. This historical setting frames his project as pastoral as well as scholarly, situated amidst the exodus of young evangelicals troubled by the apparent collision between **Genesis** and the consensus of **earth history**.

Davidson’s method is summarized by a robust **two books theology** that draws explicitly on **general revelation** and **special revelation** without granting autonomy to either. He is a **critical realist**: scientific claims are fallible yet testable by multiple independent lines of evidence, while biblical claims are inerrant in what the authors intend to affirm. He proposes a layered hermeneutic grounded in **genre analysis**, **accommodation** to **ancient cosmology**, and attention to the **ancient Near Eastern context**, drawing on the emerging consensus represented by figures like **John H. Walton**. On the scientific side, he foregrounds methodological **consilience**, marshaling **radiometric dating**, **varves**, **ice cores**, **coral growth bands**, **dendrochronology**, and **paleomagnetism** as mutually reinforcing clocks that resist ad hoc dismissal. On the theological side, he presses **authorial intent** and the **analogia fidei** to constrain concordist temptation and to honor **Scripture’s sufficiency** for saving knowledge, not scientific textbook detail.

#### 2. Friend of Science, Friend of Faith: Theses and Aims
Davidson’s central programmatic volume, **Friend of Science, Friend of Faith** (2019), articulates a pragmatic yet principled framework for adjudicating science-faith controversies. The book insists that the God of **providence** does not mislead in nature and does not err in **Scripture**, so apparent conflicts reflect either scientific overreach or hermeneutical misfires. Here Davidson fleshes out his account of **inerrancy by authorial intent**, arguing that truth-telling in **Scripture** must be assessed by genre and purpose, rather than by modern expectations of technical precision. He deploys **accommodation** to explain the biblical use of **ancient cosmology** like the **firmament**, without concluding that inspired authors thereby teach cosmological error. The controlling theological conviction is that **special revelation** and **general revelation** are convergent witnesses, a Reformed-leaning appropriation of **Belgic Confession 2**.

In content, the book navigates test cases: the **age of the earth**, the reality of **biological evolution**, the nature and scope of the **Noahic flood**, and the timing of death relative to the **fall**. The strategy is consistent: a survey of independent scientific **cross-checks** coupled with a review of biblical **genre** and **ancient Near Eastern** parallels. Davidson maintains that accepting an **old earth** and even **common descent** need not compromise **Christology**, **atonement**, or **Scripture’s infallibility**. He encourages Christians to distinguish the **gospel’s nonnegotiables** from prudential interpretations of early **Genesis**, and he argues that ecclesial charity demands a posture of teachability before God’s works as well as God’s words.

#### 3. Confronting Flood Geology: The Grand Canyon Project
A signature contribution is Davidson’s co-editorship of **The Grand Canyon, Monument to an Ancient Earth** (2016), produced with **Carol Hill**, **Tim Helble**, and **Bill Hyndman**, among others. The volume is both a field guide and a catechism in **consilience**, refuting the claim that **Noah’s flood** carved the canyon in a catastrophic, geologically instantaneous event. It leverages evidence from **stratigraphy**, **paleontology**, **sedimentology**, and **geomorphology** to argue that the canyon records millions of years of deposition and erosion. Particular attention is given to volcanic ash layers datable by **radiometric methods**, to fossil assemblages consistent with gradual ecological succession rather than hydrodynamic sorting, and to incision patterns that require sustained base-level changes inconsistent with a single flood year.

Theologically, Davidson’s editorial leadership is careful and pastoral. The book avoids ridiculing **young-earth** readers, instead modeling intellectual hospitality and inviting them to see how **general revelation** coheres with a **local or regional flood** reading and broader **ANE flood traditions**. In so doing, the project exemplifies his broader claim that faithfulness to the **Noahic covenant** and **biblical authority** can coexist with scientific maturity, and that dismissing convergent evidence often proceeds from an unexamined **scientific concordism** that imports expectations alien to **Hebrew narrative**.

#### 4. Manifold Readings of Genesis: A Hermeneutical Pluralism
In **The Manifold Beauty of Genesis One** (2021), co-authored with **Kenneth J. Turner**, Davidson develops a constructive hermeneutic that identifies multiple, non-exclusive layers of meaning in **Genesis 1**. The authors affirm the canonical richness of the chapter as **polemical monotheism**, as **temple-building** liturgy, as **kingdom ordering**, as **Sabbath** structuring of time, and as a pedagogical framework for **human vocation** as **imago Dei**. They draw from **ANE cosmology** and from biblical theology of **creation and temple** to propose that the text’s primary register is theological and liturgical, not chronological reportage.

This hermeneutical pluralism is not relativism. Davidson and Turner insist on **authorial intent** at multiple levels and on the unity of **Scripture’s metanarrative**. Yet the approach clearly pushes back against a single-lens reading that would reduce **Genesis 1** to a timeline. The volume aligns with the **framework hypothesis** and **analogical day** views without simply repeating them, and it situates Davidson’s broader agenda within evangelical **hermeneutics** influenced by **John H. Walton**, **T. Desmond Alexander**, and **biblical theology** traditions that emphasize **canon**, **covenant**, and **temple**.

#### 5. Creation, Providence, and Evolutionary Means
Doctrinally, Davidson’s constructive account of **creation** emphasizes **divine providence** as the continuous agency by which God orders and sustains creaturely processes. On this account, **evolutionary mechanisms** such as mutation and selection function as secondary causes under God’s wise governance, comparable in theological status to **plate tectonics** or **stellar nucleosynthesis**. This move belongs to a broader evangelical retrieval of **secondary causality** often associated with **Aquinas** and with Reformed affirmations of **concursus**. Davidson’s argument is that locating biological novelty within providential governance preserves **creation ex nihilo** and **divine sovereignty** while removing a perceived conflict between **evolutionary creation** and **biblical theism**.

He also attempts a theodicy for **natural evil**, contending that predation and death within ecosystems can be read as ordered within a good but not yet consummated creation. This does not trivialize suffering, he argues, but resists a simplistic inference that any creaturely death renders God morally suspect. Davidson’s wider soteriological point is that **Christ’s cross** and **resurrection** address the full spectrum of cosmic disorder and human sin, irrespective of whether biological death preceded the **fall**, a position that relocates the crisis from biology to **covenantal disobedience** and **federal headship**.

#### 6. Adam, Sin, and the Flood: Models and Constraints
On **Adam and Eve**, Davidson is less programmatic than some BioLogos peers, but he canvasses models that retain **historicity** in a genealogical rather than strictly genetic sense, akin in spirit to the later proposal of **Joshua Swamidass** regarding a **genealogical Adam**. He gestures toward **representative headship** frameworks, where an historical couple is called into covenant amid a larger population, thereby preserving **Romans 5** and **1 Corinthians 15** on **original sin** and **Christ as second Adam** while acknowledging population genetics. The exegetical constraint remains **Paul’s covenantal argument**, not an extrabiblical biology driving theology.

On the **flood**, Davidson’s geological work underwrites a **regional or local flood** interpretation consistent with **ANE flood narratives** and with the literary features of **Genesis 6–9** as an act of de-creation and re-creation. He argues that **inerrancy** does not commit one to a global hydrology at odds with physics, any more than **Psalms** commits one to geocentrism. The hermeneutical engine is **accommodation** and **genre**, guided by **covenantal** and **canonical** readings that keep **Noah** central to the story of divine judgment and grace rather than to a model of canyon formation. These moves are designed to preserve **biblical authority** while reducing stumbling blocks to faith in the context of **earth sciences**.

#### 7. Pedagogy, Apologetics, and Ecclesial Pastoring
Davidson’s prose is intentionally accessible, and his public writing carries a clear pastoral thrust toward disillusioned students and educators in evangelical contexts. He often frames arguments with concrete examples that show how **cross-checked dating methods** converge, how **varves** stack with **ice cores**, or how **radiometric isochrons** resist special pleading. This pedagogy is not mere technique; it functions as an apologetic for divine faithfulness expressed through the regularity of the created order, a counterpart to **Scripture’s reliability** witnessed in the church’s life.

He also models charitable engagement with **young-earth creationists** like **Ken Ham** and **Andrew Snelling**, contrasting ad hominem or culture-war rhetoric with a discipline of patient explanation. In doing so, he embodies a missional priority aligned with **evangelical** concern for the **gospel**, urging churches to avoid tethering orthodoxy to particular scientific claims about chronology or flood geology. The hope is that by disentangling **biblical authority** from controversial scientific assertions, the church can catechize believers to rejoice in both **God’s works** and **God’s Word**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, Influences, and Distinctives
Davidson’s work stands alongside, yet distinct from, **Hugh Ross’s** **old-earth progressive creation** and from **Intelligent Design** minimalism associated with **Michael Behe** or **William Dembski**. He shares with **Ross** a rejection of **young-earth chronology** but diverges by embracing **evolutionary creation** and a more explicit **ANE hermeneutic**. He overlaps with **BioLogos** voices such as **Francis Collins** and **Deborah Haarsma** in affirming scientific consensus and Christ-centered discipleship, while his granular emphasis on **geology**, **hydrology**, and **dating consilience** marks a disciplinary distinctiveness.

Historically, Davidson resonates with **Augustine’s** counsel on difficult texts, with **Calvin’s** doctrine of **accommodation**, and with **B. B. Warfield’s** openness to **evolution** under **providence**. The recital of **Belgic Confession 2** as a theological resource aligns him with a Reformed catholic sensibility that refuses a nature-grace dualism while maintaining the **noetic effects of sin** and the need for **revelation**. These influences converge in a principled refusal of **scientific concordism** and a corollary insistence on **authorial intent** and **genre** as criteria for inerrant Scripture.

#### 9. Assessment and Trajectory
By 2021, with **The Manifold Beauty of Genesis One**, Davidson had consolidated a mature platform: an evangelical **two books** hermeneutic, a scientifically literate tour of **earth history**, and a pastorally alert apologetic aimed at reducing science-faith scandal. He is not a systematic theologian in the strict sense, but his work participates in **systematic theology** at the loci of **creation**, **providence**, **anthropology**, and **hamartiology**, and at the prolegomenal level of **Scripture and truth**. His contribution is best read as a set of methodological and pedagogical tools ordered to the service of doctrinal fidelity and public discipleship in late modernity.

Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation. Strengths first: Davidson’s appeal to **Belgic Confession 2** and **Calvin’s accommodation** is salutary, as is his insistence that **general revelation** and **special revelation** cannot finally contradict because of **divine simplicity** and truthfulness. His emphasis on **authorial intent**, **genre**, and **covenant** readings of early **Genesis** coheres with Reformed commitments to the **analogia fidei** and to the redemptive-historical unity of **Scripture**. Yet tensions remain. A Van Til-leaning **Reformed epistemology** worries that methodological naturalism, even provisionally adopted, can smuggle in an autonomy of reason that downplays the **antithesis** and the **noetic effects of sin** in science. Doctrinally, the openness to **evolutionary creation** raises pressure points around the **historical Adam**, the covenantal grounding of **original sin**, and the meaning of **death before the fall**, where classical **Reformed orthodoxy** has typically affirmed a real first couple and a prelapsarian absence of human death as integral to federal theology. Hermeneutically, a manifold reading of **Genesis 1** risks functionally marginalizing the plain-sense calendar and **Sabbath** pattern that inform **Decalogue** ethics, though **framework** or **analogical day** models within Reformed circles offer a way to preserve theological depth without collapsing history. An alternative path within classical Reformed thought would prioritize the principled primacy of **special revelation** over **general revelation** while still welcoming the latter as ministerial, deploy the **framework hypothesis** or **analogical days** to honor **Sabbath teleology**, adopt a robust **genealogical Adam** to secure covenant headship, and reassert that nature and grace are not two realms but one economy under **covenant lordship**. In short, Davidson’s integrative project can be gratefully received for its charity and clarity, while Reformed theology insists on covenantal guardrails that safeguard **revelation**, **federal headship**, and **redemptive history** as first principles.
