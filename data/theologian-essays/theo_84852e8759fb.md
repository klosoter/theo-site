### TITLE
Moral responsibility without alternative possibilities through actual-sequence control and reasons-responsiveness

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century **analytic philosophy** of action and mind, positioned amid debates over **determinism**, **sourcehood**, and **moral responsibility**.
- Method marked by **actual-sequence analysis**, **modest metaphysics**, and a naturalistic but normatively thick account of **control** and **responsibility**.
- Key works: **The Metaphysics of Free Will** 1994, **Responsibility and Control** 1998, **Oxford Handbook of Free Will** 2002 and 2011, **My Way** 2006 and 2014, **Four Views on Free Will** 2007, **Deep Control** 2012, **Near-Death Experiences** 2016, **Death, Immortality, and Meaning in Life** 2019.
- Central categories: **semi-compatibilism**, **guidance control** versus **regulative control**, **moderate reasons-responsiveness**, **mechanism ownership**, **Frankfurt-style cases**, critique of the **Principle of Alternative Possibilities**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Harry Frankfurt**, **Peter van Inwagen**, **Roderick Chisholm**, **Galen Strawson**, **Gary Watson**, **Robert Kane**, **Derk Pereboom**, **Alfred Mele**, **Manuel Vargas**, debate over **manipulation** and **flickers of freedom**.
- Reformed cue: high utility for classical **compatibilism** and **providence** discourse, yet pressure points for **divine concurrence**, **original sin**, and **grace** within a **Westminster Confession** frame.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
John Martin Fischer stands as a leading contemporary voice in the **free will** and **moral responsibility** debate, forging a distinctive **semi-compatibilist** path that preserves responsibility even where **determinism** and the absence of alternatives are granted. Emerging in the late twentieth century out of the Anglo-American **analytic** tradition, his formation is marked by close engagement with **Harry Frankfurt** on alternative possibilities, **Peter van Inwagen** on the **Consequence Argument**, and **P. F. Strawson** on the **reactive attitudes**. By the early 1990s Fischer had begun to consolidate an approach that privileges the **actual sequence** of deliberation and action, instead of counterfactual arrays of could-do-otherwise possibilities, thereby reframing the architecture of control. His method is self-consciously modest in **metaphysics** but normatively robust, aiming to secure the practices of **praise and blame** by an account of guidance that makes sense of our lived moral practices without inflating the ontology of agency.

Fischer calls this basic strategy **semi-compatibilism**, an intentionally careful label that separates the compatibility of **moral responsibility** with determinism from the compatibility of a libertarian **free will** construed as **regulative control**. On this view, the key question for responsibility is not whether an agent could have done otherwise in the strong, regulative sense, but whether the agent acted with the right kind of **control**, understood as **guidance control** exercised by a mechanism that is suitably **reasons-responsive** and that the agent aptly owns. This method relies on detailed conceptual analysis of **cases** and intricate modal characterizations of responsiveness to reasons, merging clarity of argument with a persistent concern for the **ethics of accountability**. The result is a program that sits squarely within contemporary **action theory**, yet has immediate implications for traditioned debates over **providence**, **sin**, and **culpability**.

The grounding texts that define Fischer’s method arrive in quick succession. In **The Metaphysics of Free Will** 1994 he lays out the central pivot from **regulative control** to **guidance control**, setting aside the supposed necessity of alternative possibilities as a condition for desert. With **Responsibility and Control** 1998, co-authored with **Mark Ravizza**, he develops a full-scale theory of moral responsibility that centers on **moderate reasons-responsiveness** and a historical account of **mechanism ownership**. A decade of essays collected in **My Way** 2006, the editorial stewardship of the **Oxford Handbook of Free Will** 2002 and 2011, and the synthetic agenda of **Deep Control** 2012, consolidate a body of work that revises the very grammar of responsibility within the **compatibilist** camp. These volumes are complemented by later excursions into **death**, **immortality**, and the **meaning of life**, where the metaphysics of agency meets the existential claims of human finitude.

#### 2. Semi-compatibilism, guidance control, and reasons-responsiveness
The heart of Fischer’s contribution is the redefinition of control as **guidance control**, as opposed to **regulative control**. Regulative control is the power to choose among genuine alternatives, taken by many **libertarians** as necessary for desert. Fischer argues that our responsibility practices do not actually presuppose such openness. Instead, agents are responsible if their actions issue from a mechanism that is **reasons-responsive** in the actual sequence and is genuinely theirs. This reorientation finds its vivid support in **Frankfurt-style cases**, in which an agent is intuitively blameworthy even when a counterfactual intervener would have ensured the same action, thereby undermining the **Principle of Alternative Possibilities**. The underlying thought is that what matters is the way the agent’s practical reasoning tracked and could have tracked reasons, not whether the world’s laws and past allowed another choice.

Fischer and **Ravizza** specify **moderate reasons-responsiveness** as a dispositional, modal property of the mechanism of action. The mechanism must exhibit a regular pattern of reacting appropriately to sufficient reasons across some range of possible scenarios, and it must do so for the right reasons, not merely by accident. They contrast **weak reasons-responsiveness**, which would be too cheap, with **strong reasons-responsiveness**, which risks restoring libertarian-like alternatives. The moderation is a way to secure normative competence while remaining compatible with **determinism**. The second pillar is **mechanism ownership**, for which they develop a historical condition of **taking responsibility**. Through moral education, reflection, and self-assessment, the agent comes to see herself as a fair target of **reactive attitudes**, recognizes moral demands, and integrates these into a stable self-governance. This historical dimension answers the charge that responsibility can be conferred in a mere snapshot, and it counters **manipulation** worries by requiring the right sort of diachronic formation.

This account is a deliberate actual-sequence theory that rejects the idea that responsibility is grounded in the availability of different paths at the moment of choice. By locating responsibility in the quality of **agential guidance**, the sensitivity of one’s deliberative capacities to **reasons**, and the historical shaping of one’s mechanism, Fischer secures a compatibilist-friendly picture of accountability that tracks our judgments in hard cases such as **coercion**, **compulsion**, and **addiction**. The language of **moderate reasons-responsiveness** allows graded assessments of diminished responsibility, while **ownership** furnishes a criterion for excluding responsibility when external manipulation bypasses the agent’s reflective uptake of norms.

#### 3. Frankfurt, PAP, and the flickers debate
The most visible site of Fischer’s influence is the sustained defense of **Frankfurt-style counterexamples** to the **Principle of Alternative Possibilities**. Against critics such as **David Widerker**, **Timothy O’Connor**, **Joseph Campbell**, and **Michael McKenna**, who argue that so-called **flickers of freedom** remain in these cases and thus preserve alternatives, Fischer replies that any residual flickers either are not robust enough to bear moral responsibility or can be accommodated without reintroducing regulative control. The whole dialectic, displayed across essays in **My Way** and discussions in **Deep Control**, refines the claim that the intuition of responsibility in Frankfurt cases is properly explained by **guidance control** rather than the ghost of alternatives.

Fischer strengthens this defense by distinguishing the evaluation of the **actual sequence** from the topology of surrounding possible worlds. An agent can be blameworthy even when, given the structure of the situation, alternative possibilities are blocked, so long as the agent’s mechanism is **responsive to reasons** in the right pattern. The presence of a counterfactual intervener does not erase the agent’s own guidance-level competencies in the actual sequence. In effect, Fischer trades the metaphysics of causal openness for a normative quality of agency, extrapolated from the functioning of an agent’s **practical reasoning**. This trade explains both the enduring appeal and the controversy of his view, since it implies that responsibility is a property of the agent’s actual agential profile rather than the cosmic distribution of possibility.

A corollary of this stance is Fischer’s nuanced reception of **van Inwagen’s Consequence Argument**, which, if sound, would show that if **determinism** is true then no one can do otherwise. The semi-compatibilist concedes that determinism may be incompatible with robust regulative control, yet insists that moral responsibility survives intact. The upshot is that the defeat of **PAP** through **Frankfurt-style cases** and the partial concession to the **Consequence Argument** yield a stable zone where **responsibility** remains compatible with determinism even if certain conceptions of **free will** do not. This is the technical core of **semi-compatibilism**.

#### 4. Manipulation, tracing, and the historical dimension
One of the most pressing objections to **compatibilism** comes from **manipulation** cases, presented in sophisticated form by **Derk Pereboom’s four-case argument** and further developed by **Galen Strawson** and others. These cases seek to show that if deterministic causation and bypassing interventions can produce agents whose actions are determined by factors outside their control, then ordinary agents in a deterministic world are relevantly no different. Fischer’s reply hinges on the historical conditions of **mechanism ownership** set out with **Ravizza** in **Responsibility and Control**. On their view, an agent must not only possess a **reasons-responsive** mechanism, but must have come to own it through experiences of holding and being held responsible, internalization of moral norms, and reflection that shows the agent understands that she is an apt target for **praise and blame**. Abrupt, bypassing manipulation that implants values without such uptake fails this ownership test.

A related challenge concerns **tracing**, the idea that responsibility for an outcome can trace back to earlier culpable decisions, as in the case of the intoxicated driver. Fischer employs **moderate reasons-responsiveness** to capture this intuition without appealing to robust alternatives. The drunk driver is blameworthy for the hit because he culpably disabled his **reasons-responsive** capacities earlier, and either retained some residual responsiveness or can be assessed through the responsible earlier choice to get drunk. In both manipulation and tracing, the actual-sequence machinery operates historically and normatively, preserving the link between the agent’s evaluative stance toward reasons and the assessment of her actions across time.

This historical turn is crucial for the project’s stability. It shows that **semi-compatibilism** is not a deflationary theory of responsibility that ignores the genesis of agency, but a nuanced theory that embeds present action within a narrative of formation. It also reveals a deeper connection to human practices of **moral education**, **remorse**, and **reform**, since **taking responsibility** is both a condition of accountability and an aim of pedagogy. Thus the metaphysics of control dovetails with a social-ethical account of how agents are shaped into responsible persons.

#### 5. Death, immortality, and the meaning of life
Although widely known for free will, Fischer has also made notable contributions to the **philosophy of death** and the **afterlife**, culminating in **Near-Death Experiences: Understanding Visions of the Afterlife** 2016 with **Benjamin Mitchell-Yellin**, and **Death, Immortality, and Meaning in Life** 2019. Directing the **Immortality Project** funded in the 2010s, he curated interdisciplinary research on **immortality**, **resurrection**, and **eschatological** hope, while defending a broadly **naturalist** and critical stance toward purported empirical evidence for survival. The analysis of **near-death experiences** argues that the best explanations are neurocognitive and cultural rather than evidential pointers to an afterlife, a claim developed through careful attention to phenomenology, memory, and testimony.

On the axiological side, Fischer questions the **Epicurean** claim that death is nothing to us, defending a comparative badness view on which death deprives persons of goods that would have accrued had they continued to live. He also resists the **Bernard Williams** thesis that immortality would be necessarily tedious and enervating, suggesting in a more open register that a sufficiently rich set of projects could sustain meaning in a very long, even unending, life. The concern throughout is the grammar of **value**, **meaning**, and **personal identity** under conditions of finitude or hypothetical perpetuity. While he remains skeptical about religious afterlife claims, his work exhibits respect for the existential weight of **hope** and the ethical importance of how we live under the shadow of death.

These themes connect back to responsibility, since our practices of **praise and blame**, **forgiveness**, and **punishment** are framed by temporal lives bookended by birth and death. If the shape of a life has narrative integrity, the conditions under which we count a person responsible for what she has done bear on questions of **redemption**, **reconciliation**, and the intelligibility of **final judgment**. Fischer’s conceptual apparatus offers a secular but deeply structured account of how human lives can be assessed, remembered, and evaluated within a shared moral community.

#### 6. Works, reception, and influence
The decisive volumes in Fischer’s corpus mark a progression from foundational theory to consolidation and application. **The Metaphysics of Free Will** 1994 introduces **guidance control** and the actual-sequence orientation. **Responsibility and Control** 1998 provides the full **semi-compatibilist** theory in partnership with **Ravizza**, establishing **moderate reasons-responsiveness** and historical **ownership** as the twin pillars. **My Way** 2006 collects key essays responding to critics and clarifying the role of **Frankfurt-style cases** in undermining **PAP**. As editor of the **Oxford Handbook of Free Will** 2002 and 2011, Fischer shapes the field’s agenda by giving platform to diverse positions, from **agent-causal libertarianism** to **hard incompatibilism**. **Four Views on Free Will** 2007 frames public debate by juxtaposing **Robert Kane**, **Derk Pereboom**, **Manuel Vargas**, and Fischer’s **semi-compatibilist** approach.

The maturation of the program appears in **Deep Control** 2012, where essays refine the modal profile of **reasons-responsiveness**, explore responsibility for consequences, and revisit **manipulation** and **tracing**. In **Near-Death Experiences** 2016 and **Death, Immortality, and Meaning in Life** 2019, Fischer’s interests broaden without abandoning the leitmotif of **control** and **value**, treating questions of survival and meaning with the same analytic discipline. Across these works, his influence is felt not only in sympathetic camps but also among critics, because **semi-compatibilism** has become a fixed reference point in disputes over **free will**. The debate over **flickers**, the durability of **Frankfurt-style** arguments, and the defensibility of historical **ownership** conditions continue to shape the literature, indicating both the appeal and the contestability of Fischer’s framework.

Critiques frequently target the sufficiency of **moderate reasons-responsiveness** to ground desert, the vulnerability of actual-sequence accounts to refined **manipulation** cases, and the worry that historical **ownership** is either too weak to block bypassing or too strong to avoid excluding ordinary agents. Others press for a more demanding conception of **sourcehood**, insisting that responsibility requires being the ultimate source of one’s actions, a view championed by **libertarians** such as **Kane** and by some **agent-causal** theorists. Meanwhile, **hard incompatibilists** like **Pereboom** deny that responsibility survives determinism at all, and **revisionists** like **Vargas** propose revising our responsibility practices to fit a more naturalistic psychology. Fischer’s approach holds its ground by emphasizing the lived grammar of **moral practices** and a careful mapping of control to normative competence.

#### 7. Theological pressure points and constructive use
From a theological angle, Fischer’s framework intersects with debates on **providence**, **predestination**, **sin**, and **judgment**. His **semi-compatibilism** offers tools for those who affirm strong **divine sovereignty** and yet maintain robust human **responsibility**. By decoupling responsibility from **regulative alternatives**, Fischer supplies an account of accountability under a comprehensive **decree** or **foreknowledge** without collapsing into fatalism. The notion of **moderate reasons-responsiveness** can be used to articulate the moral competence of fallen agents, while **ownership** maps onto themes of **formation**, **catechesis**, and the internalization of **law**. Moreover, his work on **death** and **meaning** engages questions of **eschatology** and **resurrection** by clarifying what would count as a life worth extending and what it means to hope for continuity of personhood.

At the same time, theological commitments press beyond Fischer’s naturalism. Where he supplies a careful **actual-sequence** psychology of control, Christian theology often invokes deeper narratives of **creation**, **fall**, **redemption**, and **consummation**. The question whether **grace** can transform the will without **bypassing** agency echoes the manipulation debate, and here Fischer’s insistence on historical **ownership** may illuminate accounts of **regeneration** and **sanctification** that respect human integrity under **divine concursus**. His refusal to trade on metaphysical ultimates also leaves space for doctrines of **primary and secondary causation**, where **God** is the first cause and creatures are true secondary causes, a picture that resonates with compatibilist-friendly strands of Christian thought.

#### 8. Reformed and WTS evaluation
Classical Reformed theology, drawing on **Westminster Confession** affirmations of **providence** in chapters 3, 5, and 9, maintains that God ordains whatsoever comes to pass, yet is not the author of sin, and that human freedom consists in acting according to one’s **nature** without external **compulsion**. On these points, Fischer’s **semi-compatibilism** and the emphasis on **guidance control** align with the Edwardsian and post-Reformed accounts of **moral inability** and voluntariness under **divine decree**. His **moderate reasons-responsiveness** provides a precise, non-libertarian account of normative competence that can explicate responsibility within a fallen nature, and his **historical ownership** can be received as an analysis of the way conscience and law are inscribed in persons through **covenant** nurture and communal discipline. Yet tensions remain. First, Fischer’s rejection of **regulative alternatives** is a virtue for Reformed affirmations of sovereignty, but his silence on **primary causality** and the metaphysics of **concursus** leaves unaddressed how divine ordination relates to creaturely acts without collapse. Second, his naturalistic orientation cannot account for **regeneration** by **grace**, which, under a theological model, is a non-manipulative divine act that renovates the heart while deepening, not bypassing, **ownership**. Third, Fischer’s secular account of **death** and skepticism toward **resurrection** and **afterlife** contrast sharply with the **Reformed** hope in bodily resurrection and the final **judgment** of Christ, where responsibility is not merely civic but covenantal and teleological. A constructive alternative within classical Reformed orthodoxy would adopt Fischer’s **actual-sequence** analysis as a phenomenology of secondary causation, embed it within a robust doctrine of **providence** and **union with Christ**, and specify that **reasons-responsiveness** and **ownership** are secured and healed by **effectual calling**, thus preserving compatibilist responsibility while grounding it in the redemptive economy rather than in a purely naturalistic framework.
