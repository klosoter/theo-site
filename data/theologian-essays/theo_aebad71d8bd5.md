### TITLE
Belonging through the Spirit: James D. G. Dunn’s New Perspective on Paul and the pluriform unity of early Christianity

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth-century British setting of **post-Bultmann historical criticism**, with Durham-based leadership and engagement with **Second Temple Judaism** and **charismatic renewal**.
- Method marked by **historical-critical rigor**, **tradition-history** and **social memory**, with a mediating, church-facing posture toward **Scripture and experience of the Spirit**.
- Key works: **Baptism in the Holy Spirit** 1970; **Jesus and the Spirit** 1975; **Unity and Diversity in the New Testament** 1977; **Christology in the Making** 1980; **Romans** WBC 1988; **The Partings of the Ways** 1991; **The Epistles to the Colossians and to Philemon** NIGTC 1996; **The Theology of Paul the Apostle** 1998; **Jesus Remembered** 2003; **Beginning from Jerusalem** 2009; **The New Perspective on Paul** 2005; **Neither Jew nor Greek** 2015; **Did the First Christians Worship Jesus?** 2010.
- Central terms: **covenantal nomism**, **works of the law as boundary markers**, **justification as inclusive verdict**, **participationist eschatology**, **Spirit-Christology**, **oral tradition and social memory**, **unity-in-diversity**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **C. F. D. Moule**, **Rudolf Bultmann**, **E. P. Sanders**, **N. T. Wright**, **J. Louis Martyn**, **Larry Hurtado**, **Richard Bauckham**, with critiques from **Douglas Moo**, **Stephen Westerholm**, **Simon Gathercole**, **Thomas Schreiner**, **John Piper**.
- Reformed reception cue: appreciation for **union with Christ** and **Jewish context**, but critique of **forensic justification’s reduction**, **high Christology’s delay**, and **canonical unity** in light of **Reformed Scripture principle**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Context, and Method
Born in 1939 and passing in 2020, **James D. G. Dunn** emerged within the British post-war renaissance of **New Testament historical criticism**, bridging church and academy as a **Methodist minister** and as **Lightfoot Professor of Divinity** at the University of Durham from 1990 to 2003. Trained under **C. F. D. Moule** at Cambridge and conversant with **Rudolf Bultmann** and post-Bultmannian currents, he combined **critical exegesis** with a pastoral sensitivity that kept the experience of the **Holy Spirit** in theological view. His intellectual milieu included the reevaluation of early Christian origins in light of **Second Temple Judaism**, fresh interrogation of **Pauline law and justification**, and renewed interest in **tradition-history** and the mechanics of **oral transmission**. Dunn’s central wager was that the earliest Christian convictions are both historically embedded and theologically weighty, and that only through rigorous **historical-critical method** can the church read Scripture with intellectual honesty and doxological integrity.

Dunn’s epistemic stance is a qualified, chastened confidence in **historical knowledge**, operating as a pragmatic critical realism that refuses positivist certainty while resisting skeptical despair. He favored **tradition-history** to map how Jesus traditions and Pauline themes moved from setting-in-life to canonical form, and he placed **social memory** at the center of **Jesus Remembered**, arguing that remembered Jesus is the historically accessible Jesus precisely because memory is the medium of transmission. The program assumes that **canonical unity-in-diversity** is real but not uniform, that **kerygma** coheres across profiles while **Christology and ecclesial practice** display pluriformity. This method integrates **socio-rhetorical exegesis**, comparative **Second Temple texts**, and sensitivity to **Jewish-Gentile boundary dynamics**, yielding a theological historiography that privileges the Spirit-marked experience of the earliest communities.

#### 2. Spirit and Ecclesial Life
Dunn’s academic beginnings tracked with the rise of **charismatic renewal**, yet he forged a critical and ecclesially responsible **Spirit-theology**. In **Baptism in the Holy Spirit** 1970 and **Jesus and the Spirit** 1975, he argued that **Spirit-baptism** belongs to Christian initiation rather than a post-conversion second blessing, integrating **Luke-Acts** and **Pauline theology** around a coherent pneumatology. Against some **Pentecostal** claims, he maintained that the decisive gift of the **Holy Spirit** accompanies faith and baptism, while leaving room for post-conversion fillings and charismata. The effect was a **charismatic ecclesiology** in which the Spirit’s presence constitutes boundaries of belonging, displacing circumcision and food laws as identity markers, and cultivating communities whose ministries are diversified by **charismata** under the lordship of the **risen Christ**.

This pneumatological center informed Dunn’s broader **ecclesiological** vision. The Spirit’s arrival among Gentiles functions for Dunn as evidential for **justification** and **inclusion**, a hermeneutical key for Acts and Paul. The Spirit also mediates **participationist eschatology**, where believers share in Christ’s death and life, and are configured by **eschatological Spirit** rather than Torah as the sphere of sanctification. In this rendering the gospel’s social form is cruciform hospitality that embraces the stranger, a thesis that will later fuel Dunn’s reading of **justification** as God’s verdict of inclusion, rather than as a narrowly introspective solution to the burden of **Augustinian guilt**.

#### 3. Unity, Diversity, and the Parting of the Ways
Dunn’s early landmark, **Unity and Diversity in the New Testament** 1977, defends a robust **pluriformity** in the canonical witness. He catalogued variant **Christologies** and soteriologies, distinguishing summary **kerygma** from diverse idioms of confession, and asserted that the New Testament’s unity is dynamic, centered in the **lordship of Jesus** and the presence of the **Spirit**, rather than in a single theological system. He engaged **Walter Bauer’s** thesis of heresy before orthodoxy, rejecting its historical skepticism while admitting real **diversity** shaped by mission, culture, and conflict. The canonical outcome exhibits what Dunn called **unity-in-diversity**, a theological equilibrium that authorizes different voices within an agreed center.

In **The Partings of the Ways** 1991, Dunn argued that the separation of **church and synagogue** was gradual, regionally varied, and essentially complete between 70 and 135, framed by the destruction of the Temple and the **Bar Kokhba revolt**. The book dismantles simplistic supersessionist narratives and insists on the enduring vitality of **Judaism** and the continued self-understanding of earliest Christianity as a Jewish sect within the plural landscape of **Second Temple Judaism**. Dunn cultivated a **non-supersessionist** posture, later amplified in **Neither Jew nor Greek** 2015, by claiming that Christian identity emerges through sustained debate over **Torah, Messiah, and nations**, not through sheer repudiation of Israel’s story.

#### 4. Paul within Judaism: The New Perspective and Beyond
Dunn’s most consequential contribution is the **New Perspective on Paul**, a term he popularized in a 1982 lecture that built on **E. P. Sanders’** description of **covenantal nomism**. He argued that Paul’s critique of **works of the law** targets not generic human legalism but specifically **ethnic boundary markers** such as circumcision, Sabbath, and food purity that impeded full Gentile inclusion. In **Romans** WBC 1988, his **Galatians** work, and the synthetic **The Theology of Paul the Apostle** 1998, Dunn coordinated **justification by faith** with the **Spirit’s** arrival among Gentiles as God’s verdict that they belong to the people of God without adopting Torah’s badge-markers. The language of **δικαιοσύνη** is thus forensic, but in first instance ecclesiological, pronouncing membership and table-fellowship, while not denying personal forgiveness.

Dunn coupled this ecclesiological reading with a **participationist eschatology**, showing that Paul’s dominant soteriological idiom is being **in Christ**, a **corporate solidarity** formed by Spirit baptism and enacted in cruciform living. He pressed the Adam-Christ typology, treating **Sin and Death** as supra-personal powers, and read **flesh** as the frailty of human sociality in Adam, not merely sarx as material. Dunn defended an objective-genitive reading of **pistis Christou** as faith in Christ, while acknowledging the theological value of speaking of the **faithfulness of Christ**. He resisted **apocalyptic readings** that decenter the continuity of Israel, engaging **J. Louis Martyn** by arguing that Paul’s radical newness does not dissolve **covenant continuity**. The result is a **Paul within Judaism** posture that upholds Torah’s goodness while limiting its role as covenant demarcation, an approach designed to heal Christian-Jewish misreadings and to rehabilitate **law** in Paul’s theology.

#### 5. Origins, Memory, and Mission
The trilogy **Christianity in the Making** advanced Dunn’s account of origins through deep historical synthesis. In **Jesus Remembered** 2003, he argued that the **oral tradition** of Jesus sayings and deeds stabilized early and reflects a **collective memory** shaped by communal worship and catechesis. Against hyper-skeptical **form criticism**, Dunn affirmed that controlled **social memory** can retain the gist and idiom of Jesus’ mission, permitting cautious reconstructions of Jesus’ proclamation of the **kingdom of God**, table practice, and prophetic enactments. He resisted the idea that eyewitness naming, defended later by **Richard Bauckham**, is the chief guarantor of historical reliability, preferring the analytics of **oral performance** and communal authority.

In **Beginning from Jerusalem** 2009 and **Neither Jew nor Greek** 2015, Dunn traced the expansion of the **law-free Gentile mission**, the Antioch dispute, and the Jerusalem agreements, portraying **Acts** and the Pauline corpus as convergent witnesses to God’s intention to form a single body from Jews and nations by the **Spirit**. He foregrounded Paul’s **collection for Jerusalem** as symbolic of unity, and he chronicled the contested identities of the late first century as communities negotiated **Torah**, **Christological confession**, and the pressures of Roman imperial order. The project’s historiography stands on **Second Temple sources**, epigraphic data, and close exegesis, presenting a coherent development from Jesus’ Galilean ministry to the diverse yet recognizably Christian movements of the second century.

#### 6. Christology in the Making
Dunn’s **Christology in the Making** 1980 staked a controversial thesis: New Testament **Christology** matures from functional attributions to ontic claims, with true **pre-existence** explicit only in later strata such as **John**. He contended that **Philippians 2** is best read in **Adamic** terms rather than as a hymn of heavenly descent, that **Wisdom** and **Word** motifs initially function as personified agency rather than as hypostatic **ontology**, and that early worship centers on the one **God** mediated through the exalted **Lord Jesus**. In **Did the First Christians Worship Jesus?** 2010, he nuanced this stance by acknowledging that early Christians directed **devotional practice** to Jesus, yet concluded that their worship was fundamentally offered to **God through Jesus**, a distinction he maintained against **Larry Hurtado’s** claim of early Jesus-devotion as explicit cultic worship and **Richard Bauckham’s** theory of **divine identity** Christology.

Dunn thus proposed a **Spirit-Christology** in which the **Spirit** mediates the presence and power of the exalted Lord, integrating resurrection-exaltation with communal experience. He was wary of reading fourth-century **homoousios** back into first-century texts, preferring a developmentally sensitive account of **Christological confession**. While critics charged him with undercutting **high Christology** and failing to do justice to texts like **Colossians 1** and **Hebrews**, Dunn’s aim was to respect the historical layers of confession and to avoid retrojection. His **NIGTC Colossians and Philemon** 1996 exhibited this method, probing the rhetorical and intertextual texture of cosmic Christology without assuming metaphysical uniformity across the New Testament.

#### 7. Exegetical Craft and Canonical Polyphony
As commentator and synthesist, Dunn prized **close exegesis** tethered to larger theological claims. In **Romans** WBC 1988 he expounded **δικαιοσύνη θεοῦ** as God’s covenant faithfulness revealed in the gospel, and set **Romans 3** within a narrative of Jew-Gentile reconciliation. His **Galatians** and **Acts** work joined literary analysis to **historical reconstruction**, while **The Theology of Paul the Apostle** 1998 offered an architectonic map of **Pauline theology** with loci on **God**, **humankind**, **Christ**, **Spirit**, **salvation**, **church**, and **ethics**, all framed by **eschatology**. Dunn persistently framed **church order**, **charisms**, **sacramental practice**, and **ethics** in the horizon of the **new age** inaugurated by Christ and the Spirit.

Thematically, Dunn’s reading of **law** as good yet relativized, of **Sin** as cosmic power, and of **faith** as relational trust coheres with his view of **justification** as ecumenically charged and socially embodied. He was openly dialogical with **N. T. Wright** on **righteousness** and **covenant**, and sparred with **J. Louis Martyn** on **apocalyptic**, often landing in a via media that preserved Israel’s story while announcing a real **new creation**. This exegetical craft served his larger claim of **unity-in-diversity**, a canonical polyphony with a recognizable center in the **crucified and risen Christ** experienced through the **Spirit**.

#### 8. Debates, Revisions, and Influence
Dunn’s proposals triggered intense debate. The **New Perspective** drew sharp critiques from **Douglas Moo**, **Stephen Westerholm**, **Simon Gathercole**, **Thomas Schreiner**, and **John Piper**, who argued that Dunn minimized **individual sin**, truncated **forensic justification**, and misread **works of law** as merely sociological. Yet Dunn’s rejoinders emphasized that he never denied personal guilt or imputation language so much as insisted that **Paul’s polemic** be heard in its **Jewish** register and ecclesial crisis. On **pistis Christou**, while Dunn preferred the objective genitive, he conceded that Paul’s soteriology is inconceivable without the **faithfulness of Christ**, thus tempering a hard dichotomy. In **Christology**, debate with **Hurtado** and **Bauckham** sharpened his attention to cultic practice and divine identity claims, leading to his 2010 volume’s careful mapping of devotion and worship.

The wider legacy is a reorientation of **Pauline studies** toward **Second Temple Judaism**, a fresh appreciation of **Torah** and **Jewish-Christian relations**, and an exegetically grounded **ecclesiology** sensitive to hospitality and inclusion. His **memory-based Jesus** stimulated methodological diversification beyond classic form criticism, while his **unity-in-diversity** thesis affected canonical theology across confessional lines. Even critics concede that after Dunn, readings of Paul must account for **ethnic boundary dynamics**, and histories of early Christianity must reckon with **Jewish roots**, **oral tradition**, and the **Spirit’s** constitutive role.

#### 9. Integrated Significance
Taken together, Dunn’s project narrates Christianity’s emergence as the **Spirit-led expansion** of Israel’s story to the nations, negotiated through disputes over **Torah**, staged in the life of communities marked by **charismata**, and confessed in **Christological** idioms that grew in metaphysical boldness over time. His center of gravity lies in **belonging**: **justification** names God’s verdict of inclusion, **participation** names the believer’s incorporation into Christ and the **eschatological Spirit**, and **unity-in-diversity** names the canonical shape of the apostolic witness. This center corrects supersessionist tendencies, honors the **Jewishness** of Jesus and Paul, and re situates the church’s social life as the arena in which **gospel truth** becomes visible at the table as much as in the pulpit.

Yet the very strengths expose tensions. By disciplining **justification** into an inclusion verdict, Dunn risks subordinating **atonement** and **imputation** to sociology and marginalizing **individual reconciliation**. By prioritizing developmental **Christology**, he risks attenuating strong New Testament testimony to **pre-existence** and **divine identity**. By celebrating **diversity**, he must work hard to ward off canonical fragmentation. Even so, Dunn’s scholarship forces a salutary rereading of Paul and the early church through the lenses of **Spirit**, **mission**, and **Israel**, and supplies a historically responsible grammar for ecumenical conversation.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS Evaluation
A Reformed and Westminster assessment of **James D. G. Dunn** begins with genuine gratitude for his insistence on the **Jewish context of Paul**, his retrieval of **union with Christ** as soteriological center, and his tireless foregrounding of the **Spirit** in ecclesial life. These emphases chime with **federal theology’s** Adam-Christ dialectic and with **redemptive-historical** readings that bind gospel to **eschatology**. Nevertheless, at crucial points Dunn’s proposals strain against classical **Reformed orthodoxy**. First, by defining **works of the law** primarily as **boundary markers**, he constricts Paul’s diagnosis of human plight to sociology and underplays the universal reach of **God’s law** and the radical depth of **original sin** and **total depravity**. The Reformed alternative insists that Paul’s polemic addresses both ethnic exclusivism and universal human **works-righteousness**, so that **justification** retains a robustly **forensic** character grounded in the **imputed righteousness of Christ** and his **active and passive obedience**. Second, Dunn’s elevation of **participation** risks displacing the classical duplex gratia in which **union with Christ** yields both **justification** and **sanctification** without collapsing the **ordo salutis** into ecclesial sociology. Third, his developmental **Christology** and hesitations about **ontic pre-existence** jeopardize the New Testament’s witness to the **eternal Son**, in tension with the churchly rule of faith that culminates in **homoousios**. Finally, the **unity-in-diversity** thesis, salutary against flattening harmonization, should be tethered by a **Reformed Scripture principle** that locates diversity within the self-authenticating, Spirit-inspired **canonical unity** of the triune God’s speech. The Reformed path would appropriate Dunn’s Jewish-sensitive exegesis and Spirit-shaped ecclesiology while reaffirming **forensic justification**, **covenant theology**, and an unambiguous **high Christology** as biblically warranted and theologically necessary.

### RECAP NOTES
- Scottish-born Methodist scholar in a **post-Bultmann** British context, leading at Durham and shaping the study of **Second Temple Judaism** and early Christianity.
- Core problem: how **belonging** to God’s people is redefined by **Jesus** and the **Spirit**, refracted through **Paul**, and negotiated across the **Jewish-Gentile** interface.
- Major works: **Baptism in the Holy Spirit** 1970; **Unity and Diversity** 1977; **Christology in the Making** 1980; **Romans** WBC 1988; **Partings of the Ways** 1991; **The Theology of Paul the Apostle** 1998; **Jesus Remembered** 2003; **Beginning from Jerusalem** 2009; **The New Perspective on Paul** 2005; **Neither Jew nor Greek** 2015; **Did the First Christians Worship Jesus?** 2010.
- Distinctive positions: **works of the law as boundary markers**; **justification as inclusive verdict**; **participationist eschatology** in the **Spirit**; **unity-in-diversity** across the canon; developmental **Christology** with functional pre-existence.
- Interlocutors: followed **E. P. Sanders** on **covenantal nomism**; dialogued with **N. T. Wright**, **J. Louis Martyn**, **Larry Hurtado**, **Richard Bauckham**; debated by **Moo**, **Westerholm**, **Gathercole**, **Schreiner**, **Piper**.
- Reformed critique: affirms **union with Christ** and Jewish context, but challenges Dunn’s construal of **forensic justification**, hesitancy on **high Christology**, and handling of **canonical unity** in light of **covenant theology** and the **Scripture principle**.
- Legacy: catalyzed the **New Perspective**, reframed **Paul within Judaism**, advanced **social memory** in Jesus research, and shaped global debates on **Spirit**, **law**, and **belonging**.
