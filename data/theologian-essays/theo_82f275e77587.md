### TITLE
Including Jesus in the unique divine identity of Israel’s God and grounding early high Christology in Jewish monotheism.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth and early twenty-first century British scholarship shaped by **post-Bultmannian historical criticism**, **Second Temple Judaism**, and **evangelical theology**.
- Method blends **historical-exegetical rigor** with a theological stance privileging **Jewish monotheism** and **testimony** as epistemic category over **ontological metaphysics**.
- Key works: **Jude and the Relatives of Jesus (1990)**; **Theology of the Book of Revelation (1993)**; **The Gospels for All Christians (ed., 1998)**; **God Crucified (1998)**; **Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006; 2nd ed. 2017)**; **Jesus and the God of Israel (2008)**; **The Testimony of the Beloved Disciple (2007)**; **Bible and Mission (2003)**; **Gospel of Glory (2015)**; **Bible and Ecology (2010)**.
- Central terms: **divine identity Christology**, **Second Temple Jewish monotheism**, **shared divine throne**, **name theology**, **worship as boundary marker**, **testimony and eyewitness**, **inclusio of eyewitness testimony**.
- Interlocutors: **Larry Hurtado** and **James D. G. Dunn** on early Christology; **Martin Hengel** on early high Christology; **R. Bultmann** and **form criticism**; **Adela Yarbro Collins** on **Revelation**; **Maurice Casey** and **Daniel Boyarin** on Jewish categories; dialogue with **N. T. Wright** and **Richard Burridge** on history and Gospel genre.
- Reformed reception: Strong alignment with **classical, biblical monotheism** and **early high Christology**; questions about **divine simplicity**, **autotheos**, and the relative eclipse of the **Spirit** within a primarily dyadic focus.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
The career of **Richard Bauckham (b. 1946)** unfolds within the ferment of late modern **New Testament studies**, where the dominance of **form criticism** and **post-Bultmannian** trajectories was being challenged by renewed attention to **Second Temple Jewish monotheism**, **ancient historiography**, and evangelical commitments to **scriptural reliability**. Educated at **Cambridge** and long a professor of **New Testament** at **St Andrews** after earlier service at **Manchester**, Bauckham exemplifies the British evangelical scholar who refuses the forced choice between **historical criticism** and **theological exegesis**. The institutional context of **St Andrews** in the 1990s and 2000s, with its blend of rigorous philology and ecclesial theology, fostered his signature synthesis. This background frames his enduring thesis: an early and distinctly Jewish **high Christology**, best described in terms of **divine identity** rather than by later **ontological metaphysics** alone, coherently accounts for the **worship of Jesus** in the earliest Christian sources.

Against the assumption of developmental trajectories from a supposedly **low Christology** to later **Nicene orthodoxy**, Bauckham advances a method that returns to **Jewish monotheism** as a historical given and to **testimony** as an epistemic category. By foregrounding **Second Temple** markers of God’s uniqueness, he contends that the earliest believers included Jesus within the **unique divine identity** without abandoning the **Shema**. While he steadfastly employs tools of **historical exegesis**, he also makes a principled case for **testimony** as a public, verifiable, and indispensable medium of knowledge, challenging the reductionism of **positivist historiography** and the skepticism of **radical form criticism**. The result is a methodological via media: robust **historical-critical** engagement that culminates in theological judgment about **Christ’s inclusion in the identity of Israel’s God**.

#### 2. Divine identity Christology: contours and claims
The phrase **divine identity Christology** becomes programmatic in **God Crucified (1998)** and is consolidated in **Jesus and the God of Israel (2008)**. Here Bauckham argues that **Jewish monotheism** in the Second Temple period defined the one God by identifying attributes and actions unique to God: the **sole Creator of all that exists** and the **sovereign Ruler of all things**. A further liturgical corollary marks this identity in practice: the **exclusive worship** due to the God of Israel. Within these historical descriptors, the New Testament includes Jesus not by dissolving monotheism into **ditheism** but by narratively and liturgically placing Jesus at the level of **Creator and Ruler**, and by directing **worship** to him as to the one God.

Key texts receive sustained attention. In **1 Corinthians 8:6**, Paul reconfigures the **Shema** by identifying the one God and the one Lord as **the Father** and **Jesus the Lord** in a way that integrates Jesus within the **divine identity** rather than alongside it. In **Philippians 2:6-11**, the bestowal of the **name above every name** and universal confession that **Jesus Christ is Lord** is read against **Isaiah’s** prerogatives of YHWH, supporting **name theology** and the conferral of YHWH’s **unique divine name** upon Jesus. In **Revelation**, the **one throne** of God and the **Lamb** is a deliberately binitarian depiction of a **shared divine sovereignty**, while worship addressed to **God and the Lamb** confirms early **devotional monotheism**. These exegetical judgments oppose the developmental thesis of **James D. G. Dunn’s Christology in the Making (1980)** and push beyond **agency** or **angelomorphic** models, while remaining complementary to **Larry Hurtado’s** analysis of a dyadic **cultic devotion** to Jesus. By framing the issue in terms of **identity** rather than **essence**, Bauckham both resists the charge of **Hellenization** and provides bridges to later **Nicene** confession of *homoousios*.

#### 3. Revelation, worship, and political theology
Bauckham’s **Theology of the Book of Revelation (1993)** and essays collected in **The Climax of Prophecy (1993)** unriddle the imagery of **Revelation** by locating it within Jewish **apocalyptic** traditions and the worship of the **one God**. The axis of the book is the **throne**: God and the **Lamb** share the single throne of universal governance, which envisions **Christ’s inclusion** in the **divine sovereignty**. Doxology becomes epistemology, since the **worship** of **God and the Lamb** generates the community’s **theodicy** and perseverance. Revelation’s critique of **imperial ideology** is theologically charged: Rome’s claims to universal dominion mimic the **Creator’s** rights, and idolatry in the form of **imperial cult** is exposed as the fundamental political error. In this analysis, liturgy is public truth, the **ekklesia** enacts counter-imperial allegiance, and the **new creation** consummates the Creator’s purposes so that **eschatology** serves **creation** rather than escapes it.

This work also features Bauckham’s careful intertextual sensitivity. The saturation of **Revelation** with **Exodus**, **Isaiah**, and **Ezekiel** motifs is not ornament but argument: the prophetic **name and glory of YHWH** are manifested in the **Lamb**. The resultant theology is deeply **monotheistic** and insistently **christocentric**, yet it invites ethical and political stakes. The **saints’ witness** is martyrial, the **beast** is unmasked political idolatry, and the **new Jerusalem** announces the **Creator’s** telos. When read alongside his **Bible in Politics (1989)**, the political bite of Bauckham’s exegesis is unmistakable: Scripture offers criteria for discerning idolatry in public life, with **Revelation** as the capstone of a **biblical theology of power**.

#### 4. Eyewitness, testimony, and the Gospels
With **Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006; 2nd ed. 2017)**, Bauckham challenges the long reach of **form criticism** from **R. Bultmann** through **Martin Dibelius**, arguing that the **Gospels** are substantially shaped by identifiable **eyewitness testimony** rather than by anonymous community traditions. He mines **ancient historiography** and **rhetoric** to show how **testimony** functioned as a recognized and prized mode of public knowledge. His argument for the **inclusio of eyewitness testimony** proposes that Gospel authors frame narratives by naming or bracketing key witnesses who guarantee the account’s reliability. He also pioneers the use of **onomastics** to demonstrate that personal names in the Gospels correlate with Palestinian **name frequencies**, thereby supporting the Palestinian provenance and **historical plausibility** of the traditions.

The second edition amplifies these claims through fresh engagement with **Papias**, reconsidering the links between **Mark**, **Peter**, and **John**, and refining how **oral tradition** and **written testimony** interact. In parallel, the edited volume **The Gospels for All Christians (1998)** disputes the popular model of Gospels written for narrow, self-enclosed **communities**, arguing instead for **inter-church circulation** and broad Christian audiences. Together, these works situate the canonical Gospels as ancient **bioi** that present Jesus through the voices of **named witnesses**, aligning with **Richard Burridge’s** genre analysis while refusing any retreat into skeptical **hermeneutics of suspicion**.

#### 5. Johannine studies and the Beloved Disciple
Bauckham’s Johannine corpus, notably **The Testimony of the Beloved Disciple (2007)** and **Gospel of Glory (2015)**, elaborates how **John** combines **eyewitness testimony** with symbolic and theological depth. He entertains the possibility that **John the Elder**, a Judean **eyewitness**, stands behind the Gospel, while insisting that the **Beloved Disciple’s testimony** is the governing authorial authority. Theologically, **John’s high Christology** is not a late Hellenizing accretion but the scriptural fulfillment of **Jewish monotheism**, integrating **glory**, **name**, and **I AM** sayings as disclosures of YHWH’s identity in Jesus.

This Johannine profile dovetails with **divine identity Christology**. The **homoousian** implications are traced through Johannine worship, the **kyrios** confession, and the shared **divine prerogatives** of Jesus with the Father, all while maintaining monotheistic integrity grounded in the **Shema**. Against notions of a sectarian **Johannine community** with peculiar theology, Bauckham portrays the Gospel as offering a universal, public **testimony** whose signs are written that readers may believe, thereby reinforcing his thesis about **eyewitness** foundations and **theological publicity**.

#### 6. Jewish monotheism, wisdom, and early high Christology
Central to Bauckham’s contribution is his detailed mapping of **Second Temple Jewish monotheism**. He argues that within Judaism the divine uniqueness is not a porous category; rather, the lines between **Creator** and **creation** were sharply drawn. He therefore critiques accounts that treat **Wisdom**, **Word**, or **principal agent** traditions as mediating a merely functional Christology. While acknowledging **Wisdom** motifs, he contends that the New Testament identifies Jesus with **YHWH’s identity**, not merely with a high-ranking agent. Engagements with **Daniel Boyarin**, **Maurice Casey**, and **Crispin Fletcher-Louis** clarify how **angelomorphic** or **agency** models fall short of the textual data in **Paul**, **John**, and **Revelation**, where Jesus exercises YHWH’s **creational** and **sovereign** prerogatives and receives **worship**.

The payoff is historical and dogmatic. Historically, the inclusion of Jesus within **the one God’s identity** is early, widespread, and liturgically anchored. Dogmatically, this yields an incipient **binitarian shape** within monotheism, which later **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** formulations explicate with **ontological** language. Bauckham is explicit that **divine identity** categories do not replace **essence** talk but show how the earliest confession already implies what later theology would articulate under the **homoousios**.

#### 7. Mission, creation, and public witness
Beyond Christology, Bauckham pursues a canonical vision of **mission** and **creation**. In **Bible and Mission (2003)** he reads the whole canon around the **Abrahamic promise** that all nations will be blessed, the **universal lordship** of the **Messiah**, and the **new creation** horizon. Mission is participation in the **missio Dei** and bears witness to the **kingdom** that is already present yet awaits consummation. In **Bible and Ecology (2010)** he retrieves a robust doctrine of **creation** as the wider community to which humanity belongs, tying **Revelation’s** new creation to the ethical summons of ecological care. These works extend the theological implications of his **Revelation** studies into lived **public theology**.

This public dimension harmonizes with **The Bible in Politics (1989)** where Bauckham argues that Scripture shapes political discernment by unveiling **idolatry**, reforming **allegiance**, and relativizing all **empire**. The thrust is not party-political but theo-political: **worship** forms a people whose loyalty to **God and the Lamb** recalibrates their engagement with the world. Such lines of thought exhibit the fruit of a **theological exegesis** that refuses to seal doctrine off from **ethics** or **mission**.

#### 8. Scripture, historiography, and theological judgment
Epistemically, Bauckham advances **testimony** as a mode of knowledge that neither collapses into **subjectivism** nor aspires to **neutralist positivism**. By stretching from **ancient historiography** to contemporary discussions of memory and evidence, he contends that believing the **Gospels** is a reasonable act of trust in the **named witnesses** commended by the communities that knew them. This dovetails with his thesis that **worship** and **doctrine** emerged together: confession that Jesus shares the **divine identity** is not an inference from abstract metaphysics but a recognition grounded in the **resurrection** and encoded in **scripture-shaped worship**.

Bauckham’s stance often functions as a corrective to both extremes: against a purely **functional christology** that evacuates ontology, he argues for **identity**; against an abstract **metaphysical dogmatics** that brackets history, he insists on **Second Temple** categories and public **testimony**. His work engages **Larry Hurtado’s** devotional pattern, **James Dunn’s** developmental model, and the legacy of **Bultmann’s** skepticism, while building bridges to **Nicene orthodoxy**. Although less programmatic on the **Holy Spirit**, he acknowledges the triadic contours of early devotion, especially in **Revelation**, where the **Spirit** speaks to the churches even as **God and the Lamb** share the throne.

#### 9. Legacy and a Reformed/WTS evaluation
By 2008, having been elected a **Fellow of the British Academy**, Bauckham’s major theses had already reshaped debates on **early high Christology**, **Gospel historiography**, and **Revelation’s** theology. His legacy is a set of durable claims: that earliest Christian communities preserved **eyewitness testimony**; that **Jewish monotheism** supplied the categories for a **high Christology**; that **worship** both reveals and regulates doctrinal development; and that Christian Scripture is inherently **public**, aimed at the whole church and the world. Subsequent scholarship continues to test, refine, or contest these claims, but their programmatic force remains. Students now rehearse **1 Corinthians 8:6**, **Philippians 2**, and **Revelation 4–5** as exemplary loci of **divine identity Christology**, and situate the **Gospels** within broader ancient **bioi** supported by **testimony** rather than anonymous community creativity.

Reformed and Westminster evaluations will praise Bauckham’s robust **biblical monotheism**, his defense of **early high Christology** consonant with **homoousian** confession, and his exegetical grounding of **lordship**, **creation**, and **worship**. They will also note tensions. First, the preference for **divine identity** over **divine essence** risks leaving **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, and **Calvin’s autotheos** underarticulated, though nothing in his account contradicts them; a classical **Reformed orthodox** retrieval could integrate his historical insights with metaphysical clarity. Second, the mainly dyadic focus on **God and the Lamb** can eclipse the **Spirit’s consubstantiality**, requiring supplementation from confessional **trinitarian** dogmatics to secure full **trinitarian taxis**. Third, his cautious stance on **Johannine authorship** and his non-programmatic doctrine of **Scripture** will invite WTS questions about **inerrancy** and **apostolicity**, even as his **eyewitness** model offers strong resources for a **covenantal apologetic** that privileges **testimony** within the economy of **revelation**. The constructive path for a Reformed reception, therefore, is not rejection but completion: receive Bauckham’s **Second Temple** and **worship-centered** arguments as exegetical scaffolding, and crown them with classical **Reformed** affirmations of **simplicity**, **autotheos**, and a fuller **pneumatology** ordered by **covenant** and **history**.

### RECAP NOTES
- British evangelical scholar in a post-Bultmannian setting, integrating **historical exegesis** with **theological judgment** within **Second Temple Jewish** horizons.
- Core problem: how earliest Christians preserved **monotheism** while confessing and **worshiping Jesus**; solution is **divine identity Christology**.
- Major works: **Theology of the Book of Revelation (1993)**; **God Crucified (1998)**; **Jesus and the Eyewitnesses (2006; 2017)**; **Jesus and the God of Israel (2008)**; **The Gospels for All Christians (1998)**; **Testimony of the Beloved Disciple (2007)**.
- Defining tools: **shared divine throne**, **name theology**, **worship as boundary marker**, **testimony and inclusio**, **Second Temple monotheism** as the frame for **high Christology**.
- Interlocutors and debates: against **Dunn’s** developmental low Christology and **Bultmann’s** form criticism; complementary to **Hurtado’s** devotional pattern; dialogue with **Boyarin**, **Casey**, **Yarbro Collins**, and **Hengel**.
- Reformed assessment: strong alignment with **homoousian** confession and **biblical monotheism**; supplement with **divine simplicity**, **autotheos**, and a fuller **pneumatology** for confessional completeness.
- Legacy: reframed debates on **early high Christology**, **Gospel historiography**, and **Revelation**; enduring influence on evangelical and critical scholarship through the lens of **divine identity** and **eyewitness testimony**.
