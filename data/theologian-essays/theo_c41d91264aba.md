### TITLE
Retrieving Holy Saturday and the Great Tradition for Evangelical Canonical Theology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary evangelical** setting shaped by postcritical **theological interpretation** and Baptist ressourcement within a fragmented North American academy.
- **Canonical biblical theology** under a **creedal rule of faith**, affirming **inerrancy** while practicing patristically informed **dogmatic exegesis**.
- **Christ and the New Creation** (2013); **The Story of Scripture** (2017); **He Descended to the Dead** (2019); co-edited **Baptists and the Christian Tradition** (2020).
- **Holy Saturday** and *descensus ad inferos*, **intermediate state**, **creedal catholicity**, **typology** and **Christocentric** reading, **regula fidei**.
- Interlocutors: **Irenaeus**, **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Luther**, **Calvin**, **Balthasar**, **Alan E. Lewis**, **Wayne Grudem**, plus influences like **Brevard Childs**, **John Webster**, **Kevin Vanhoozer**.
- Reformed reception: tension with **Heidelberg Catechism Q44** and some **Westminster** construals; opportunity for retrieval within **classical theism** and **redemptive-historical** exegesis.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, moment, and method
Matthew Y. Emerson emerges within a twenty-first century **evangelical** milieu marked by a double desire: to overcome the fragmenting effects of historical criticism and to avoid confessional amnesia by reinhabiting the **Great Tradition**. His institutional home in Baptist higher education, notably at **Oklahoma Baptist University**, situates him among scholars seeking **creedal catholicity** without surrendering **Baptist** distinctives. The intellectual weather system is the turn to **theological interpretation of Scripture** catalyzed by figures like **Brevard Childs**, **John Webster**, and **Kevin Vanhoozer**, which reasserts that Scripture is canonically ordered, ecclesially received, and doctrinally productive. Emerson positions himself squarely here, advocating a **canonical biblical theology** that reads Scripture by the **regula fidei** and in service of the church’s proclamation.

Methodologically, Emerson weds evangelical **inerrancy** to patristically attuned **dogmatic exegesis**. The controlling instincts are **Christocentrism** and **Trinitarian** clarity, guided by the **Apostles Creed**, **Nicaea**, and **Chalcedon** as hermeneutical norms rather than extrinsic constraints. He pursues ressourcement with an eye for conceptual precision: distinguishing Scripture’s authority from tradition’s ministerial service, advocating a practiced **analogia fidei** rather than a free-floating biblicism. The result is a method that foregrounds **typology**, reads the canon as a single **economy of salvation**, and seeks retrieval of neglected doctrines like **Holy Saturday** and the *descensus ad inferos* as integrative loci within **Christology** and **soteriology**.

#### 2. Early work: canonical synthesis and new creation
Emerson’s first monograph, **Christ and the New Creation** (2013), sets his trajectory. Here the synthetic aim is to render a **New Testament theology** under the pressure of the whole canon, attending to **inaugurated eschatology** and the scriptural unity of **new creation** in Christ. Following impulses from **Irenaeus’s recapitulation**, Emerson construes the Gospels and Paul as narrating the Messiah who sums up Adam and Israel and thereby inaugurates the **eschatological temple**. The dominant categories are **typology** and **covenant**, with attention to how the prophetic idiom of **new heavens and new earth** is internalized into **union with Christ** and **ecclesiology**. The book anticipates later emphases by insisting that doctrinal claims arise canonically, not atomistically, and that ecclesial reading under a **rule of faith** disciplines both method and conclusions.

A streamlined pedagogical statement arrives in **The Story of Scripture** (2017), where Emerson introduces **biblical theology** for students and pastors. The volume presses the claims of **narrative coherence**, **Christological fulfillment**, and **typological** sensibility. Its dogmatic corollaries are explicit: the **Trinity** is the architect and agent of the drama; **creation-fall-redemption-restoration** structures the whole; and **sacramental** language in Scripture is read within Baptist **ordinances** without dissolving catholic continuity. The point is not mere summary but a reform of reading habits by which **creed** and **canon** tutor evangelical interpreters into a stable **rule of faith**.

#### 3. He Descended to the Dead: thesis and exegesis
Emerson’s signature contribution, **He Descended to the Dead: An Evangelical Theology of Holy Saturday** (2019), argues that the clause **he descended to the dead** in the **Apostles Creed** is both biblical and theologically salutary. Against a spectrum of evangelical suspicion, often formed by **Wayne Grudem’s** proposal that the clause is a late intrusion meaning only burial, Emerson retrieves the catholic reading: following death, Christ’s human soul truly entered the **realm of the dead** (*Sheol/Hades*), while his body lay in the tomb, and the person of the **Son** remained united to both, consonant with **Chalcedonian** Christology and the **communicatio idiomatum**. The descent is not a postmortem opportunity for conversion nor an atoning extension of the cross, but rather the victorious passage of the **Messiah** through death’s domain, heralding judgment upon the powers and life to the saints.

The biblical case centers on texts like **1 Peter 3.18-20** and **4.6**, **Ephesians 4.8-10**, **Acts 2.27-31** reading **Psalm 16**, **Romans 10.7**, and the Lukan promises of **Paradise**. Emerson’s exegesis is cautious yet constructive: he resists exegetical maximalism while insisting that these texts, when read canonically and under the **rule of faith**, plausibly teach a real descent to **Hades**. He sets the hermeneutical table by clarifying the ancient cosmology of **Sheol** and the intermediate state, distinguishing the righteous and unrighteous dead without endorsing medieval speculations about **purgatory**. Theologically, he deploys classical tools to guard the mystery: Christ’s death entails the **separation of body and soul** in his humanity, yet the person of the Son subsists in union with each. The **extra Calvinisticum** is implicitly in view, safeguarding that the Son’s divine life and operations were never circumscribed by the tomb or **Hades**.

#### 4. Patristic, medieval, and Reformation dialogues
Historically, Emerson tracks the doctrine from **Irenaeus** and **Athanasius** through **Augustine** and **Aquinas** to **Luther** and **Calvin**, arguing that the **descensus** enjoyed broad patristic and medieval consensus. **Aquinas** in ST III, q. 52 provides a clear articulation: Christ descends to **Hell** broadly construed, to manifest power, liberate the righteous, and confound the demons, while not suffering punishment. Emerson endorses this structure while disciplining it by scriptural reserve. He reads **Augustine** as cautious yet affirming a proclamation to the dead and situates **Irenaeus** within a **recapitulation** frame in which the descent is part of Christ’s cosmic summation of Adamic history.

With the Reformation, Emerson notes divergence. **Luther** maintained a strong **victory** motif in a local descent. **Calvin**, wary of speculative cosmology and centered on the cross, famously reinterpreted the clause as Christ’s suffering of **hellish agony** under God’s wrath, a theological intensification rather than a topographical descent. Emerson argues that this Calvinian turn, codified in the **Heidelberg Catechism Q44**, breaks with the earlier tradition and truncates the creedal witness. He is critical but fair, granting Calvin’s pastoral aim to assure troubled consciences of Christ’s substitutionary depth while insisting that **classical theism** and **orthodox Christology** permit an actual descent that does not compromise penal **satisfaction** concentrated in the cross.

#### 5. Modern proposals and the limits of speculation
In modern theology, Emerson engages **Hans Urs von Balthasar** and **Alan E. Lewis** as high-water marks of Holy Saturday speculation. **Balthasar** pushes the Son’s solidarity to the brink, positing a descent of dereliction saturating the Trinitarian life with the darkness of **Holy Saturday**, while **Lewis** contemplates the eclipse of God between cross and resurrection. Emerson adjudicates these moves by appealing to **divine impassibility**, **inseparable operations**, and the completedness of penal **atonement** at the cross. He argues that a robust **Trinitarian** and **dyothelite** Christology rules out a metaphysical rupture in the Godhead. The descent reveals the crucified Lord’s victory amid the ontic reality of death’s domain; it does not widen the **Trinitarian** wound nor add to the expiation already achieved. His line is a retrieval disciplined by **classical theism** and a sober doctrine of the **intermediate state**.

This modern engagement functions as a negative theology of Holy Saturday: Emerson marks the borders where evangelical confessors can walk, and the cliffs where they cannot. The doctrine is for proclaiming **Christus Victor** within **orthodox** bounds, not for dramatizing divine suffering beyond the grammar of **Nicaea** and **Chalcedon**. As such, he folds Holy Saturday into preaching and catechesis as a comfort: the Lord has harrowed death’s house, and so the keys of **Death and Hades** belong to the **Resurrected One**.

#### 6. Doctrinal integration: soteriology, eschatology, and ecclesial catechesis
Emerson is most persuasive when he integrates the descent within a broader **soteriological** and **eschatological** web. He treats Holy Saturday as a hinge in the **state of humiliation** yielding to **exaltation**, locating it between cross and resurrection without severing it from either. The descent displays the reach of **incarnation** and the solidity of Christ’s death, thereby disarming the fear of death and the demonic realm. It also clarifies the **intermediate state** by showing that Christ has traversed it and reigns over it, offering pastoral ballast for the church’s hope in **bodily resurrection** beyond a disembodied afterlife.

In ecclesial life, Emerson presses **creedal catholicity**. The clause should be confessed, he argues, because it is part of the church’s **Apostles Creed**, because it is biblically warranted in sober measure, and because its loss impoverishes evangelical **catechesis**. This emphasis coheres with his co-edited volume, **Baptists and the Christian Tradition** (2020), which advocates a Baptist form of catholic retrieval. Emerson’s case is that evangelicalism’s future requires a stable **rule of faith**, regular use of ancient **creeds**, and a hermeneutic that hears the **communio sanctorum** as a living context for biblical reading. Holy Saturday thus becomes a case study in how recovering **traditioned** instincts can stabilize doctrine and worship.

#### 7. Hermeneutical posture: canon, creed, and church
What most characterizes Emerson’s contributions is a hermeneutical stance that refuses the dichotomy between **sola Scriptura** and **traditio**. He insists on **sola Scriptura** as material norm, while advocating **nuda Scriptura** for no one. The **rule of faith** renders the canon’s shape audible and anchors **typology** within the truth of the **Trinity** and **Christology**. This invites evangelical interpreters to read **1 Peter** or **Ephesians** about the descent not as obscure curiosities but as parts of a canonical mosaic whose center is the **crucified and risen Christ** confessed by the whole church. His repeated appeal to **classical doctrines** like **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **inseparable operations** reflects a judgment that dogmatic clarity purifies exegesis rather than contaminates it.

In the same register, Emerson’s work resists two emotivist temptations. First, he resists the anxious revisionism that would abandon **creedal** clauses under pressure of modern sensibilities. Second, he resists a triumphalist biblicism that isolates proof texts from the canon’s **economy**. The result is a moderate, churchly **ressourcement** that seeks catholic breadth and evangelical depth, exemplified by the interplay between his programmatic biblical-theological books and his dogmatic essay on **Holy Saturday**.

#### 8. Interlocutors and debates: evangelical and catholic
Emerson’s most direct evangelical interlocutor is **Wayne Grudem**, whose JETS argument that Christ did not descend has shaped late twentieth-century evangelical catechesis. Emerson answers by distinguishing **burial** from **descent**, insisting that the creed already has burial and that the descent adds a distinct truth. He also interacts with **Oliver Crisp** and other analytic theologians who have revisited the descent, often finding compatible space within **Reformed** dogmatics for a local descent. Historically, he reads **Irenaeus**, **Athanasius**, **Augustine**, and **Aquinas** as converging witnesses, while engaging **Luther** and especially **Calvin** with a critical eye. With **Balthasar** and **Alan E. Lewis**, he marks off the evangelical nonnegotiables of **atonement** and **classical theism**.

The exegetical debate centers on **1 Peter 3–4**, **Ephesians 4**, and **Acts 2**. Emerson contends that when these texts are read under **regula fidei** and with sensitivity to ancient **cosmology** and Jewish conceptions of **Sheol**, they yield a modest yet real doctrine of descent. He is careful to state limits: no **purgatorial** speculation, no postmortem evangelism doctrine, no displacement of the cross as the place of **penal substitution**. He hopes thereby to rehabilitate the clause in catechesis, preaching, and hymnody as a marker of evangelical **catholicity** rather than as a stumbling block.

#### 9. Influence and ongoing project
By retrieving **Holy Saturday** successfully within a broader program of **canonical** and **creedal** reading, Emerson has become a touchstone for evangelical pastors and teachers wary of abandoning the **Apostles Creed** yet hesitant to endorse medieval accretions. His volume **He Descended to the Dead** has been cited across the **Evangelical Theological Society**, used in seminaries, and discussed in Baptist contexts where the clause had quietly dropped from liturgy. Meanwhile, **Christ and the New Creation** and **The Story of Scripture** continue to shape curricula, linking **biblical theology** with doctrinal and pastoral outcomes. With **Baptists and the Christian Tradition**, he has helped articulate a widening Baptist **catholicity** that prizes **confessions**, **creeds**, and the wisdom of **patristic** and **medieval** doctors within a Reformational frame.

The net effect is the re-normalization of a **churchly hermeneutic** among evangelicals: Scripture read for doctrine, doctrine guided by **creed**, and creed scrutinized by Scripture. Emerson’s chosen case of **Holy Saturday** serves as both emblem and engine of this broader realignment. The shape of his ongoing project is clear: a steady, constructive **ressourcement** that guards evangelical distinctives while rejoining the broader **catholic** stream.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Emerson’s strengths are manifest: a clear **sola Scriptura** posture disciplined by **regula fidei**, affirmation of **classical theism** including **impassibility** and **inseparable operations**, and a pastoral instinct that keeps **penal substitution** at the cross while integrating **Christus Victor**. His engagement with **Calvin** and Heidelberg’s **Q44** will remain a principled tension. Many within **Westminster** lines construe the descent clause as a theological intensification of the cross, not a local descent, seeing **1 Peter 3–4** as difficult and **Ephesians 4** as an ascension text. The prudential objection is hermeneutical: do debated texts bear the dogmatic weight of an ontic descent, or should the **analogia fidei** favor catechetical consolidation around Christ’s being under the power of death until resurrection, as in **Westminster Larger Catechism** 50–52 and **Heidelberg**? Emerson’s canonical and creedal arguments answer that local descent is both permitted and edifying, yet a WTS alternative would preserve the clause’s pastoral utility by reading it as the extremity of the **state of humiliation**, concentrically focused on the cross, without asserting topography of **Hades**. A mediating Reformed path could acknowledge the legitimacy of Emerson’s retrieval as a theologically safe option within **Reformed orthodoxy**, provided that satisfaction and victory remain anchored in the crucifixion-resurrection event and that the descent is neither soteriologically augmentative nor exegetically over-realized. In sum, Emerson’s proposal is a welcome catholic correction to evangelical amnesia, even as confessional Reformed readers may prefer the Heidelberg-Calvin construal for reasons of textual economy and covenantal focus.
