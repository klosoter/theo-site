### TITLE
Judgment reimagined through Revelation 20: a canonical, apocalyptic case for a two-stage resurrection and the finality of the second death

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth and early twenty-first century evangelical** setting, with renewed focus on **Second Temple apocalyptic**, **Revelation**, and debates over **hell** and **the millennium**
- **Canonical intertextual exegesis** and **apocalyptic genre analysis**, emphasizing **lexical semantics** and the narrative coherence of **Revelation 20**
- **After the Thousand Years: Resurrection and Judgment in Revelation 20** (1992); **The End of the Unrepentant: A Study of the Biblical Themes of Judgment and Hell** (2012)
- **First resurrection**, **second death**, **millennium**, **lake of fire**, **conditional immortality**, **aionios**, **apollumi**, **inaugurated eschatology**
- Interlocutors include **Augustine** and **Irenaeus** on eschatology, **G. E. Ladd** on **historic premillennialism**, **Richard Bauckham** and **G. K. Beale** on **Revelation**, **Edward Fudge** and **John Stott** on **conditional immortality**
- Reformed cue: challenges **amillennial consensus** and **eternal conscious punishment** in **Westminster Confession** terms, while aligning with **biblical-theological intertextual method**

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, method
J. Webb Mealy emerges within the **contemporary evangelical** guild as a scholar whose work presses the canonical shape of **apocalyptic** Scripture into systematic clarity. His historical setting is that late twentieth century moment when **Second Temple Judaism**, **intertextuality**, and **genre-conscious exegesis** transformed readings of **Revelation** and biblical **eschatology**. Against the twin temptations of speculative **systematics** and thin **historicism**, Mealy works from the grain of the text: sustained engagement with the **Septuagint**, **Hebraic idiom**, and the compositional strategy of **John the Seer** in **Revelation 20**. He privileges what he calls the narrative and lexical integrity of Scripture, exploiting the semantics of **aionios** and **apollumi**, and the coherent symbolism of the **first resurrection** and the **second death**. The method is a deliberately evangelical **canonically controlled exegesis** that treats apocalyptic not as fantasy but as a theologically precise mode of **revelation**.

That method yields a hermeneutic of careful **intertextual echo**, where **Psalms**, **Isaiah**, **Daniel**, and **Ezekiel** supply the imagery that governs **John’s apocalypse**. Such a stance, indebted to the wider rediscovery of **biblical theology** and the influence of **G. E. Ladd’s inaugurated eschatology**, aims to let the **Old Testament** set the terms for Christian **eschatological** language. Mealy’s interpretive posture is resistant to importing **Platonizing anthropology** or **medieval infernalism** into the text. He tests **Augustinian** and later **scholastic** judgments by returning to the textual data of **Revelation 20**, **Romans 6**, **Matthew 25**, and **2 Thessalonians 1**, insisting that canonical discourse about **death**, **life**, **resurrection**, and **judgment** should control dogmatic construction.

#### 2. After the Thousand Years: reading Revelation 20
Mealy’s programmatic contribution in **After the Thousand Years: Resurrection and Judgment in Revelation 20** (1992) offers a sustained exegesis of the notoriously contested **millennium** and its sequential relation to the **final judgment**. He argues that **Revelation 20** presents two discrete moments: the **first resurrection** of the martyrs, who reign with Christ during the **thousand years**, and the climactic **general resurrection** and **judgment** after the millennium. The key is to read the chapter’s **narrative sequencing** not as a literary recapitulation but as a genuine forward movement where the **binding of Satan**, the **martyr-reign**, the release of **Gog and Magog**, and the **great white throne** form a coherent eschatological arc. This stance aligns broadly with **historic premillennialism** yet is tightly controlled by textual markers rather than a speculative millennial schema.

On the critical point of the **first resurrection**, Mealy resists **amillennial** spiritualization that takes the phrase as regeneration or the intermediate state of the righteous. He argues that the **Greek anastasis** and the priestly reign imagery evoke a concrete, embodied vindication of the slain witnesses who share in Christ’s **royal priesthood** as a proleptic participation in the age to come. The **thousand years** itself is read symbolically as the divinely appointed interval of victorious rule under conditions of curtailed satanic deception, a thesis indebted to **apocalyptic numerology** and **Second Temple** idiom. The decisive exegesis centers on **second death** as the terminus, defining the **lake of fire** as the annihilating judgment meted out after the millennium and thus clarifying the temporal and ontological gulf between the **two resurrections**.

Mealy’s interlocutors in this terrain include **Augustine’s amillennial recapitulation**, which he deems insufficiently attentive to the internal logic of **Revelation 20**, and contemporary apocalyptic specialists like **Richard Bauckham** and **David Aune**, from whom he borrows tools while reaching divergent interpretive conclusions. He tests Reformed expositions such as **G. K. Beale’s** amillennial commentary, pressing the lexical force of **anastasis** and the integrity of **John’s sequence**. The resulting model preserves **Ladd’s** inaugurated eschatology, yet it insists that the apocalypse’s **temporal rhetoric** carries real eschatological structure, not only theological perspective.

#### 3. The End of the Unrepentant: conditional immortality and the second death
Two decades later, **The End of the Unrepentant** (2012) extends Mealy’s canonical reading into the doctrine of **hell**, contending for **conditional immortality** and the finality of **annihilation** as the **second death**. Here he gathers **Old Testament** testimony about the fate of the wicked: the chaff and smoke imagery of **Psalms**, the consuming fire of **Isaiah**, the decay and shame of **Daniel 12**, and the totalizing language of **Deuteronomic covenant curses**. He argues that the Bible’s dominant idiom for the destiny of the impenitent is loss of life, not unending survival in misery. The move is not merely lexical but theological: only God possesses **immortality** by nature, and **eternal life** is the promised gift to the redeemed. Therefore, the axiom of **conditional immortality** stands as a soteriological corollary of biblical **creation** and **redemption**.

In New Testament terms, Mealy pays special attention to the semantics of **aionios** and **apollumi**. He contends that **aionios** modifies the quality or the age-character of a thing, so **aionios punishment** in **Matthew 25:46** denotes punishment definitive and of the age to come, not necessarily conscious duration without end. Likewise, **apollumi** and **olethros** register the notion of termination and ruin unto non-existence, not perpetual torment. He returns repeatedly to **Revelation’s second death**, the **lake of fire** wherein death and Hades are themselves destroyed, to underscore that the imagery functions as the antithesis of the **first resurrection** and **eternal life**. The hermeneutical rule is consistent: the whole canon’s death-life dialectic governs and constrains **infernal** language.

Throughout, Mealy interacts with evangelical advocates of **conditionalism** such as **Edward Fudge** and **John Stott**, while engaging critics in the **Augustinian** and **Thomist** streams who defend **eternal conscious torment**. He challenges appeals to **Luke 16** and the **rich man and Lazarus** as parabolic discourse not directly intended to define the metaphysics of final judgment, and he integrates **2 Thessalonians 1:9** and **Romans 6:23** as programmatic for the gospel’s construal of life and death. The constructive proposal is not driven by sentiment but by canonical and apocalyptic patterns: the rule of **Christ the Lamb**, the victory of **new creation**, and the purgation of evil that culminates in the **passing away** of death itself.

#### 4. Anthropology, justice, and the moral logic of judgment
Beneath these exegetical claims lies an anthropology and a doctrine of divine justice. Mealy argues that the **biblical anthropology** of dust-and-breath resists the postulated indestructibility of the soul found in **Platonist** or **Augustinian** metaphysics. The creature’s life is contingent, and **immortality** is a divine gift rather than a natural endowment. Consequently, **eternal conscious torment** presupposes precisely what the **gospel** contests: a human immortality severed from participation in God. Mealy construes **justice** as retributive in its finality yet proportionate in its outcome: the impenitent receive the **wages of sin**, which is death, while God vindicates the righteous through **resurrection life** and the **kingdom reign**. The **lake of fire** signifies not an everlasting theater of retribution but the definitive abolition of evil under the reign of **the Lamb**.

This moral scheme intertwines with his **millennial** reading. The **first resurrection** functions as a public theodicy in history, exalting the martyrs who bore witness to the truth of **Jesus’ lordship** against the **beastly powers**. The subsequent **judgment** reveals the exact justice of God, including degrees of accountability, yet issues in a final state that corresponds to the **second death** rather than to an unending infliction. Mealy contends that this better preserves the goodness of **new creation** in **Revelation 21–22**, where the absence of sorrow and death is unqualified, and the cosmos is not overshadowed by an eternal underworld of misery. The teleology of Scripture, he argues, moves through **apocalyptic crisis** to **cosmic shalom**, not to a dualism of parallel eternities.

#### 5. Interlocutors and debates across the evangelical spectrum
Mealy’s work situates itself within a constellation of dialogues. With **G. E. Ladd**, he shares **inaugurated eschatology** and a non-dispensational historicism. Against **amillennial** interpreters like **Augustine** and modern Reformed expositors such as **G. K. Beale**, he defends the concrete force of the **first resurrection** and the narrative sequence of **Revelation 20**. He draws from **Richard Bauckham’s** model of scriptural **intertextuality** and the **theology of Revelation**, though he reaches distinct conclusions regarding millennium and judgment. He disputes **eternal conscious torment** with appeal to exegesis and **biblical theology**, contesting classic **patristic** and **scholastic** formulations by reading **aionios** and **second death** through the canon’s own semantic field. The debate touches moral theology as well, as he joins writers like **Edward Fudge** and **Stephen Travis** in proposing **conditional immortality** as more consonant with **divine goodness** and **penal justice**.

Further, Mealy engages apocalyptic specialists like **David Aune**, **Adela Yarbro Collins**, and **Craig Koester** on questions of **genre**, **symbol**, and **narrative unity** in **Revelation**. He contends that a consistent intertextual method can adjudicate disputes about recapitulation versus sequence, and he insists that **John’s vision** is a theological drama ordered to the victory of **the Lamb**. The upshot is a disciplined refusal either to flatten apocalyptic into allegory or to let it be commandeered by system-driven millennial charts. The controlling doctrinal motifs remain **resurrection**, **kingdom**, **judgment**, and **new creation**, which together sustain the grammar of **first resurrection** and **second death**.

#### 6. Doctrinal integration and theological stakes
Theologically, Mealy integrates his two central theses along an axis of **life and death**, placing **resurrection** and **annihilation** as antonyms governed by the **lordship of the risen Christ**. The **millennium** becomes the historical asterisk in which **martyrdom** is vindicated and **Satan’s deception** is constrained, while the **final judgment** terminates evil by the **second death**. This structure reframes the doctrine of **hell**, not as a rival eternal order but as the last word of divine **justice** within the story of **redemption**. In his view, the Bible’s promise that death will be no more requires that death be abolished, not endlessly operative in the mode of conscious torment. The **New Jerusalem** thus stands unmarred by an eternal inferno, its holiness secured by the past-tense reality of judgment.

He also refracts soteriology through this lens. To receive **eternal life** is to be gifted **immortality** in union with Christ, the **firstborn from the dead**, while to reject the gospel is to persist toward non-being, the destiny summarized in **second death**. This closure neither diminishes the gravity of sin nor evacuates judgment of meaning. Rather, Mealy claims it magnifies the sheer gratuity of **grace** and the reality of **new creation** life. Because the **kingdom** is life in communion, the opposite must be a real and terrible loss of life. Mealy’s dense exegesis of **Revelation 20**, **Matthew 25**, and **Pauline** texts yields a system that ties **anthropology**, **eschatology**, and **ethics** into a coherent whole.

#### 7. Strengths of the approach and methodological cautions
The strength of Mealy’s work lies in its close **canonical exegesis**, its retrieval of **Hebrew idiom**, and its disciplined attention to **apocalyptic genre**. He advances the conversation by refusing to let the rhetoric of **Revelation** float free of its **Old Testament** anchors, and by taking seriously the distinctive force of **first resurrection** and **second death**. His engagement with **lexical semantics** is not superficial word-study but interwoven with narrative and theological analysis. Moreover, his critique of **infernalism** presses the church to account for how **new creation** and **divine goodness** relate to final judgment. These are not peripheral matters but the grammar of Christian hope.

Yet methodological cautions arise. The degree to which **aionios** can be cordoned from the question of duration remains contested, and the appeal to **apollumi** as annihilation rather than ruin is not self-explanatory across contexts. The sequencing of **Revelation 20** is also disputed, with strong arguments for **recapitulation** embedded in the book’s structure. Mealy’s case, while powerful, must continually be tested by the whole-canon witness, including **Johannine** dualisms and **Pauline** accounts of wrath and glory. The risk for any focused thesis is overdetermination: allowing a few controlling texts to settle what remains a web of testimonies about **judgment**, **glory**, and **the age to come**.

#### 8. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage, Mealy’s **canonical intertextuality**, his respect for **apocalyptic** idiom, and his attention to **redemptive-historical sequencing** resonate with **biblical theology** in the tradition of **Vos** and **Ridderbos**. However, two decisive tensions emerge. First, his **historic premillennial** reading of the **first resurrection** stands at odds with the **amillennial** trajectory dominant in confessional Reformed churches, where the **thousand years** is identified with the present reign of Christ and the saints, and where **recapitulation** is a central literary key to **Revelation**. Second, his **conditional immortality** and identification of **second death** with **annihilation** conflict with the **Westminster Confession’s** doctrine of **eternal conscious punishment** for the reprobate, grounded in a robust account of **divine holiness**, **justice**, and the gravity of **sin against an infinite God**. A Reformed alternative would maintain that the **first resurrection** names the believer’s **union with Christ** and the **intermediate state** triumph, and that **aionios punishment** entails an unending, conscious exclusion consonant with **covenantal sanctions** and the irreversibility of moral opposition to God. Even so, Mealy’s exegesis prods Reformed theology to sharpen its apocalyptic readings and to articulate more clearly how **new creation**, **divine justice**, and the final exclusion of evil cohere within a covenantal and doxological vision.
