### TITLE
Kenotic Christocentrism: re-centering liberal theology on the living revelation of God in Jesus Christ.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late Victorian Scotland to interwar Europe**, New College Edinburgh, engagement with **German liberalism** and early **dialectical theology**; crisis of **World War I** as backdrop.
- **Christocentric, experiential method**: knowledge of God through the **self-authenticating person of Christ**, a chastened **Ritschlian Werturteil** shaped by **critical history**.
- Key works: **The Doctrine of the Person of Jesus Christ (1912)**; **The Christian Experience of Forgiveness (1927)**; **Types of Modern Theology (1937)**; translation of **Schleiermacher, The Christian Faith (1928)**.
- Central terms: **kenosis**, **revelation-in-person**, **ethical theism**, **communicatio idiomatum**, **pre-existence**, **Chalcedon reinterpreted**, *Erlebnis* and *Offenbarung*.
- Main interlocutors: **F. Schleiermacher**, **A. Ritschl**, **W. Herrmann**, **I. A. Dorner**, **P. T. Forsyth**, **Karl Barth**; in debate with **Harnackian essence-of-Christianity** and **Anglican kenoticism**.
- Reformed reception cue: tension between **christological concentration** and revisions of **immutability**, **impassibility**, **penal substitution**, and **Sola Scriptura**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Hugh Ross **Mackintosh** 1870–1936 came of age within **Scottish Reformed** culture while absorbing the energies of **German liberal theology**. Educated and later long-serving at **New College, Edinburgh**, he traversed the bridge from late Victorian confidence to the disillusionment of **World War I**, a passage mirrored in his increasingly chastened modernity. He studied in Germany and engaged the teaching of **Wilhelm Herrmann** at Marburg, inheriting a **Ritschlian** insistence that theology concerns the **value-judgments** of faith rather than speculative metaphysics. Yet while **Albrecht Ritschl** centered Christianity on the **Kingdom of God** and the community’s moral life, Mackintosh insisted that the center is the **person of Jesus Christ**, not as an ideal but as the living **revelation** of God. This christocentric redirection is the keynote of his method: the believer’s knowledge of God arises through the **self-authenticating impact** of Jesus, corroborated by history yet not exhausted by it.

Mackintosh’s method is thus a woven fabric of **critical history**, **religious experience** *Erlebnis*, and **dogmatic construction**. He is wary of an autonomous **natural theology** and of purely **metaphysical theism**. Revelation is not a set of propositions but the **act and person of Christ**, which the church then renders in **doctrine**. He took from **Friedrich Schleiermacher** a respect for the **Gefühl** of absolute dependence, but he reversed the order: not religious consciousness yielding a christology, but **christology** informing religious consciousness. Against **Adolf von Harnack’s** reduction of the gospel to the Fatherhood of God and the brotherhood of man, Mackintosh contended that **incarnation** and **atonement** are the evangelical core. Methodologically he called this a **christocentric critical realism**: the **historical Jesus** mediates the **knowledge of God** as the **risen Lord** discloses the divine life to faith.

#### 2. Major works and constructive program
Mackintosh’s constructive statement crystallizes in **The Doctrine of the Person of Jesus Christ (1912)**, a work that stands as a classic of English-language **kenotic Christology**. There he retrieves **Chalcedon’s** intent while refusing its static metaphysics, interpreting the **hypostatic union** through the biblical motif of **kenosis**. The **pre-existent Logos** freely self-limits, renouncing the independent exercise of certain **divine prerogatives**, to live a genuinely human life. He mines **I. A. Dorner’s** dynamic Christology and the Protestant **kenoticism** of **Thomasius** and the Anglican **Charles Gore**, yet recalibrates them toward an evangelical focus: God’s **holy love** is revealed in Jesus’ obedient life and **cross**. For Mackintosh, **communicatio idiomatum** functions not as a metaphysical shuttle between natures but as a confession that in the single person of **Christ** God is present and active precisely in human lowliness.

A second pillar, **The Christian Experience of Forgiveness (1927)**, turns to **soteriology**. Here he refuses both **moral influence** reductionism and crude **penal substitution** caricatures. Forgiveness is God’s **holy-love** action that both judges and heals; it is objective before it is subjective. The cross is the place where **divine judgment** and **grace** coincide, and only such an objective event can generate the **conscience-renewal** he describes. While sympathetic to **P. T. Forsyth’s** insistence on the moral necessity of the atonement, Mackintosh resists metaphors that sever **grace** from the **Father’s character**. Forgiveness is not an administrative adjustment in God, but the **self-giving of God in Christ**, creating a new moral world in the **community of faith**.

Posthumously, **Types of Modern Theology (1937)** surveys the long nineteenth century from **Schleiermacher** to **Barth**. This typology reveals his pedagogical mastery: modern theology is read as a series of attempts to correlate **revelation** and **modern consciousness**. He appreciates **Schleiermacher’s** unitive vision yet finds it lacking in **incarnation** and **atonement**; he acknowledges **Ritschl’s** ethics yet faults its thin **Christology**; and he welcomes **Karl Barth’s** prophetic recall of **revelation** while warning against a threatened **docetism** if the historical and ethical concrete is eclipsed. The translation with **J. S. Stewart** of **Schleiermacher’s The Christian Faith (1928)** further exhibits his mediating vocation: to let **evangelical piety** converse critically with **modern thought**.

#### 3. Christology: kenosis, Chalcedon, and revelation-in-person
The heart of Mackintosh’s theology is **kenotic Christology**. He affirms **pre-existence**, **incarnation**, and the **personal unity** of the God-man, but he reinterprets them dynamically. The classic ascription of **immutability** and **impassibility** to the divine nature does not require a frozen deity, he argues; rather, it is the **holy constancy of love**. Thus the **Logos** can enter into temporal process by a **self-emptying** in which divine perfections are exercised under human conditions. The language of **anhypostasia–enhypostasia** serves to safeguard the personal center in the **Son**, who lives a truly human, Spirit-anointed life. Against **docetism**, he insists on Jesus’ real ignorance, growth, and temptation; against **adoptionism**, he insists on **pre-existent sonship** and a true **incarnation**.

This Christology underwrites his account of **revelation**. God is known not by speculative ascent but by encountering **Jesus** as the **image of the invisible God**. Scripture is the normative witness because it is the **apostolic attestation** of this **self-revelation**. The **Word of God** is, in Mackintosh’s scheme, first the **person of Christ**, then the **gospel proclamation** *Verkündigung*, then **Scripture** in its inspired capacity to mediate Christ. He employs **critical history** to test claims yet resists historicism that would reduce **Christ** to a past figure. The **resurrection**, though not dissected apologetically, functions as the eschatological ratification of Jesus’ filial relation to the **Father**, grounding the church’s **Easter faith** and sustaining the present **Lordship** that renews the believer’s ethical life.

#### 4. Atonement and the moral order of grace
In soteriology Mackintosh seeks what he calls the **moral order of grace**. He is critical of a bare **exemplarist** reading that turns the cross into mere inspiration; equally, he resists theories of **penal substitution** that imagine an internal transaction in the divine life detached from the **Son’s obedience** and the **Father’s love**. The cross is the **judgment of sin** because the **holy love** of God confronts and condemns evil; it is the **forgiveness of sinners** because, in the person of the **Son**, God bears the cost of reconciliation. In this vein, **The Christian Experience of Forgiveness (1927)** argues that forgiveness creates reality: by the Spirit, the **conscience** is reconstituted, and the **community** becomes a fellowship under the **Kingdom**.

Mackintosh’s atonement is thus **substitutionary** without being narrowly **penal**, and it is **objective** without collapsing into a forensic ledger. He aligns with **Forsyth** in emphasizing the gravity of guilt and the non-negotiable holiness of God, yet he maintains the primacy of **reconciliation** as personal communion restored. **Justification** emerges as the judicial name for the same reality that **forgiveness** names personally, while **sanctification** is the moral form of life in the **Spirit** given with the **righteousness of Christ**. Throughout, the **person of Christ** regulates soteriology; no soterian scheme may be allowed to float free of **incarnation** and **resurrection**.

#### 5. Trinity, God, and creation
Although not a systematic trinitarian of the modern speculative type, Mackintosh grounds the **Trinity** in the **christological** and **soteriological** givens. God is eternally **Father** as the source of the **Son**, and the **Spirit** is the bond of their communion and the giver of **new life** to the church. The **immanent Trinity** is confessed because the **economic Trinity** has acted. More than a cautious repetition of Nicene faith, this is a methodological commitment: the **doctrine of God** must be read from the story of **Jesus**. Hence his reformulation of **immutability** as the steadfastness of **holy love**, and his hesitation toward strong **impassibility**. The God who is revealed in **kenosis** is not a God deprived of deity, but the God whose eternal nature is **self-giving**.

On **creation** and providence, Mackintosh hews to an **ethical theism**. The world is the arena of the **Kingdom of God**, and **providence** is not a blueprint but the active lordship of the **Father** guiding history toward **eschatological** fulfillment. The **image of God** in humanity is realized in **filial trust** and **obedience**, which is to say in **Christ** as the true human. This ethical orientation fuels his engagement with social questions, though he resists treating the **Kingdom** as a mere program of **social progress**. Rather, social transformation is the indirect fruit of **reconciliation** and **discipleship** under the **crucified and risen Lord**.

#### 6. Scripture, church, and modern critical reason
Mackintosh’s doctrine of **Scripture** flows from his **revelation-in-person** framework. Scripture is the **norma normans secundaria** that witnesses authoritatively to the **norma normans** who is **Christ**. Inspiration is not mechanical but **pneumatic**, bound to the Spirit’s work of generating **apostolic testimony**. He accepts **historical criticism** within a confessional posture, convinced that the true scandal is **incarnation**, not compositional complexity. The **canon** is ecclesially received as the sphere in which the **risen Christ** still speaks, and **dogmatics** is the church’s disciplined effort to say **what must be said** if the gospel is to be itself.

The church for Mackintosh is the **community of reconciliation**, called into existence by **forgiveness** and ordered to **witness**. Sacraments are visible words of **grace**; they seal the **promise** enacted in the **Word**. He remains within a **Reformed** ambience in stressing the primacy of **preaching** and the centrality of **Scripture**, yet he refrains from theories of **inerrancy** and from liturgical sacramentalism. The **ministry** is ordered to announce **Christ** and to form consciences in the **moral order of grace**, not to interpose a priestly layer between **Christ** and believers.

#### 7. Dialogue partners and the neo-orthodox turn
Mackintosh’s career maps the tectonic shift from **liberal Protestantism** to **dialectical theology**. With **Schleiermacher**, he shares a refusal of arid scholasticism; with **Ritschl**, a focus on **ethics** and the **Kingdom**; with **Herrmann**, a Christ-centered experientialism. But he critiques all three for insufficiently robust **incarnation** and **atonement**. As **Barth** emerges after 1919, Mackintosh perceives a needed correction: **revelation** must be God’s sovereign act, not an extrapolation from experience. He welcomes **Barth’s** thunder against religious projection yet presses him to honor the **humanity of Jesus** and the moral concreteness of the **Christian life**. In **Types of Modern Theology (1937)** he positions **Barth** as a new type that recovers **revelation** while risking a neglect of **experience** and **history** if handled without evangelical care.

This dialogical posture, coupled with his **Schleiermacher** translation in 1928 with **J. S. Stewart**, makes Mackintosh a hinge figure. He stands at the end of classic **liberal theology** yet refuses its reductionisms; he anticipates **neo-orthodox** concerns without adopting its more stringent **No** to **religion**. His own synthesis is an **evangelical liberalism** or a **mediating theology**: **christocentric**, morally serious, doctrinally constructive, critical yet worshipful.

#### 8. Integrated Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **Westminster** perspective, Mackintosh’s achievement commands respect for its **christological concentration**, its defense of the **incarnation** against **rationalist** erosion, and its retrieval of the **cross** as the locus of **holy love**. Yet several tensions appear. First, the **method** subordinates **Scripture** functionally to the **person of Christ**, which risks loosening **Sola Scriptura** by appealing to a Christ beyond the **biblical canon**. A Reformed alternative insists that the **persona Christi** is inseparably bound to the **inscripturated Word**, with **Scripture** as the sufficient, perspicuous norm through which the living **Christ** speaks. Second, the **kenotic** redefinition of **immutability** and **impassibility** dilutes classical **divine simplicity** and the **aseity** of God. Reformed orthodoxy, while affirming real **incarnation**, safeguards that the **Son** assumes a human nature without surrendering divine perfections, the **communicatio idiomatum** operating at the level of the **person**, not as a limitation of the **nature**. Third, on **atonement**, Mackintosh’s moral-objective account honors **substitution**, but it hesitates at **penal** categories grounded in the **covenant of works** and the federal headship of **Christ**. Reformed theology clarifies how the **law-covenant**, **representation**, and **imputation** make **penal substitution** biblically and theologically necessary, without opposing it to **love**. Lastly, his embrace of **historical criticism** without a strong doctrine of **inerrancy** risks a norm higher than **Scripture** in adjudicating revelation. In sum, Mackintosh’s **christocentric kenosis** offers a salutary protest against reductive liberalism, but Reformed orthodoxy would press for a stricter **revelation-Word nexus**, classical **doctrine of God**, and a fully **penal-substitutionary** and **federal** soteriology as the more coherent articulation of **grace in history**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Scottish Reformed** setting engaging **German liberalism** from late 19th century into **interwar** context; New College **Edinburgh** anchor.
- Core problem: reconciling **critical modern consciousness** with a robust **christocentric** faith grounded in **incarnation** and **atonement**.
- Major works: **The Doctrine of the Person of Jesus Christ (1912)**; **The Christian Experience of Forgiveness (1927)**; **Types of Modern Theology (1937)**; translator of **Schleiermacher’s The Christian Faith (1928)**.
- Distinctives: **kenotic Christology**, **revelation-in-person**, reinterpreted **Chalcedon**, **moral order of grace**, objective yet personal **forgiveness**.
- Interlocutors: engaged **Schleiermacher**, **Ritschl**, **Herrmann**, **Dorner**, dialogued with **Forsyth** and early **Barth**, critiqued **Harnack**.
- Reformed critique: risks to **Sola Scriptura**, classical **immutability** and **impassibility**, and **penal substitution**; commendation for **christological** focus and defense of **incarnation**.
- Legacy: a mediating, **evangelical liberal** pathway from classic **liberalism** to **neo-orthodoxy**, shaping English-speaking discussions on **Christology** and **atonement**.
