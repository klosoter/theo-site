### TITLE
Experiential Calvinism retrieved: Puritan doctrine for life articulated in a full-orbed contemporary Reformed systematics.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth-century Dutch Reformed diaspora**, renewal of **confessional Reformed** identity, and global evangelical interest in **Puritan** and **Nadere Reformatie** sources.
- **Experiential, doxological Reformed scholastic method** ordered by **Sola Scriptura**, **Westminster Standards**, and the **ordinary means of grace**, using **analogia Scripturae** and pastoral casuistry.
- **The Quest for Full Assurance** 1999; **Meet the Puritans** 2006; **A Puritan Theology: Doctrine for Life** 2012; **Living for God’s Glory** 2008; **Family Worship** 2002; **Reformed Preaching** 2018; **Reformed Systematic Theology** vols. 1 2019, 2 2020, 3 2022, 4 2024 with **Paul M. Smalley**; editorial leadership on **van Mastricht, Theoretical-Practical Theology** 2018–2023.
- **Experiential Calvinism**, **assurance**, **union with Christ**, **duplex gratia**, **covenant of works and covenant of grace**, **regulative principle of worship**, **ordinary means**, **practical syllogism**, **pactum salutis**, **divine simplicity**.
- Influences and interlocutors: **Calvin**, **Perkins**, **Owen**, **Boston**, **Voetius**, **Witsius**, **van Mastricht**, **Bavinck**, **Murray**, **Muller**; engagements with **Federal Vision**, **eternal functional subordination**, **New Perspective on Paul**, and **New Calvinism**.
- Reception: strong among **confessional Presbyterian and Dutch Reformed** churches; concerns about **introspective assurance** and a dominant **Puritan lens**; substantial alignment with **WTS classical theism** and **covenant theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Joel R. Beeke emerged from the **Dutch Reformed** immigrant milieu of North America, born in 1952 and nurtured in a piety shaped by the **Nadere Reformatie** as mediated through North American congregations. Ordained to pastoral ministry and long serving in the **Heritage Reformed Congregations**, he became cofounder and president of **Puritan Reformed Theological Seminary** in Grand Rapids in 1995, coupling institutional leadership with a program of scholarly retrieval. His doctoral research at **Westminster Theological Seminary** on **assurance of faith** was later published as **The Quest for Full Assurance** in 1999, a study that positioned him at the intersection of historical theology and pastoral dogmatics. Through **Reformation Heritage Books** and the **Puritan Reformed Journal**, Beeke built a platform for recovering **Puritan** and **Further Reformation** sources as normative companions for contemporary **Reformed** confession and ministry.

Beeke’s method can be summarized as **experiential Calvinism** within an **orthodox Reformed scholastic** framework, a consciously churchly theology that aims at doxology and discipleship. He insists on **Sola Scriptura**, employing the **analogia Scripturae** and **analogia fidei** while reading Scripture with the **Westminster Standards** and the **Three Forms of Unity**. In conversation with **Herman Bavinck’s** organic approach to dogmatics and the scholastic-pastoral synthesis of **Petrus van Mastricht**, Beeke describes theology as both *theoretica* and *practica*, ending in **praxis pietatis**. His use of pastoral casuistry echoes **William Perkins** and **Gisbertus Voetius**, not as moralism but as the practical application of **union with Christ** to the conscience under the **ordinary means of grace**.

Beeke’s intellectual project unfolds through strategic works: **Meet the Puritans** 2006 with **Randall J. Pederson** surveys **Puritan** literature; **A Puritan Theology: Doctrine for Life** 2012 with **Mark Jones** distills **Puritan** dogmatics into integrative loci; **Reformed Preaching** 2018 commends **experiential, discriminating preaching** for modern pulpits; and with **Paul M. Smalley** he authored **Reformed Systematic Theology** in four volumes, 2019 through 2024, spanning **revelation and God**, **man and Christ**, **Spirit and salvation**, and **church and last things**. Alongside these, practical guides such as **Family Worship** 2002 and **Living for God’s Glory** 2008 articulate a household and vocational ethic grounded in **covenant theology**. As general editor for the English translation of **van Mastricht’s Theoretical-Practical Theology** 2018–2023, Beeke catalyzed a retrieval of **Reformed scholasticism** for contemporary pastoral theology.

#### 2. Revelation and God
In volume 1 of **Reformed Systematic Theology**, Beeke and Smalley expound **revelation** and **Scripture** with emphatic commitment to **inerrancy**, **clarity**, **necessity**, and **sufficiency**, treating the Bible as **self-authenticating** through the **Spirit’s internal testimony**. His doctrine of **natural revelation** avoids **natural theology** as autonomous while upholding **general revelation** and **common grace** in a classic **Bavinckian** register. The **analogia Scripturae** guides interpretation, while the **Westminster Confession** supplies the dogmatic canons that shape exegesis, particularly regarding Christ as the scope of Scripture and the **covenant** as its architectural principle.

On the **doctrine of God**, Beeke defends **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, **immutability**, **impassibility**, and **eternity**, explicitly opposing **theistic mutualism** and revisionist accounts of **divine passibility**. He receives **Calvin’s autotheos** of the Son while grounding triune relations in the eternal processions *ad intra*, steering clear of **eternal functional subordination** in favor of classical **inseparable operations**. His trinitarianism is catechetical and doxological, integrating **Nicene** and **Reformed scholastic** insights while prioritizing the **missions of the Spirit** as the personal agent who unites believers to Christ through the **means of grace**.

#### 3. Man, covenant, and Christ
Volume 2 advances **anthropology**, **hamartiology**, and **Christology** through a federal lens. Beeke upholds the **imago Dei** as structural and relational, sets **Adam** as **federal head** under the **covenant of works**, and interprets the fall as catastrophic corruption with **total depravity** yet without obliterating the faculties essential to moral agency. He retrieves **Witsius** to delineate **covenant of grace** as God’s single redemptive covenant administered diversely across the economies, with **Christ** as mediator according to the **pactum salutis**. In **Christology**, he is firmly **Chalcedonian**, defending the **communicatio idiomatum** and the **extra Calvinisticum**, and articulates the **active and passive obedience** of Christ as the meritorious ground of **justification** and **adoption**.

Soteriologically, Beeke’s atonement is unambiguously **penal substitutionary** and **particular**, aligning with **definite atonement** while acknowledging the catholic sufficiency-efficiency distinction. He resists both the **New Perspective on Paul** and the **Federal Vision**, defending the **imputation** of Christ’s righteousness as alien and complete. Here he mirrors the clarity of **John Murray** on **union with Christ** while retaining **Puritan** attention to the believer’s self-examination and sacramental preparation as disciplines within the **covenant of grace**.

#### 4. Spirit, ordo salutis, and assurance
Volume 3 centers on the **Holy Spirit** and the **ordo salutis**, depicting **effectual calling**, **regeneration**, **conversion**, **faith and repentance**, **justification**, **adoption**, **sanctification**, and **perseverance** within an architecture of **union with Christ** and **duplex gratia**. Beeke carefully distinguishes the **instrumentality of faith** in **justification** from the **inherence of grace** in **sanctification**, thereby preserving **sola fide**. He describes **mortification** and **vivification** in line with **Calvin**, urging believers to make diligent use of the **means of grace** under the Spirit’s agency to grow in **holiness**.

Beeke’s hallmark contribution remains his doctrine of **assurance**. In **The Quest for Full Assurance** 1999 and throughout the systematics, he presents assurance as of the **essence of saving faith** in its seed yet ordinarily grown through the **promises**, the **Spirit’s testimony**, and the **practical syllogism**. The **direct act** of looking away to Christ anchors assurance formally, while **reflexive acts** of examining **marks of grace** confirm it materially. He aligns **Richard Sibbes** and **Thomas Goodwin** on the **sealing of the Spirit**, even as he curbs excesses by returning to **Calvin’s** emphasis on Christ’s sufficiency. This synthesis displays his governing axiom: doctrine must serve life, and life must be measured by doctrine.

#### 5. Church, worship, and last things
Volume 4 turns to **ecclesiology** and **eschatology**, framing the church as **one, holy, catholic, and apostolic**, instituted under Christ’s **keys of the kingdom** and marked by **pure preaching**, **right administration of sacraments**, and **discipline**. Beeke advocates the **regulative principle of worship**, shaping the Lord’s Day by **Word**, **prayer**, **psalmody and biblically faithful hymnody**, **sacraments**, and **almsgiving**, while warmly recommending **family worship** as the domestic extension of congregational piety. He treats **baptism** and the **Lord’s Supper** as **means of grace**, urging frequent, reverent reception tethered to **self-examination** and **covenantal promises** rather than bare memorialism.

In **eschatology**, Beeke teaches a classic **amillennial** hope that is Christocentric and churchly, emphasizing **the intermediate state**, **the resurrection of the body**, **final judgment**, and **the new heavens and new earth**. He appropriates **Puritan** treatments of death and dying to cultivate a pilgrim spirituality and integrates **assurance** with **perseverance** by stressing the Spirit’s preservation of the saints within the **visible church** until glory.

#### 6. Puritan retrieval as dogmatics for ministry
Beeke’s single most distinctive motif is his retrieval of **Puritan** and **Further Reformation** sources as living partners in **systematic theology**. **A Puritan Theology** 2012 models this: loci from **God** to **glory** are expounded through **Owen**, **Perkins**, **Bunyan**, **Boston**, **Flavel**, and **Voetius**, not as antiquarian quotation but as a normative conversation. The editorial republication of **van Mastricht’s Theoretical-Practical Theology** gives modern pastors a template where scholastic precision and devotional application converge. Beeke thus bridges the research of **Richard A. Muller** on **Post-Reformation Reformed Orthodoxy** with pulpit practice, arguing that **scholastic method** and **experimental divinity** are mutually reinforcing.

This retrieval extends to **catechesis**, **family religion**, and **domestic liturgy**. Works like **Family Worship** and expositions on the **Heidelberg Catechism** and **Westminster Shorter Catechism** encourage ordinary Christians to embody **covenant piety** in the rhythms of daily Scripture reading, prayer, and singing. In Beeke’s vision, **covenant theology** grounds household discipleship, equips consciences, and protects liberty by tethering affections to **Christ’s promises** rather than to cultural techniques of spiritual formation.

#### 7. Preaching, conscience, and revival
In **Reformed Preaching** 2018, Beeke elaborates **experiential, discriminating preaching**, a homiletic that addresses the **conscience** in its varied states: the unconverted, the awakened, the assured, the tempted, the backsliding, and the dying. He champions the **uses of the law** and the **gospel’s free offer** while insisting that application be doctrinally informed and pastorally wise. His account of **revival** is resolutely **Reformed**: extraordinary enlivenings of ordinary means under sovereign **Spirit** outpourings, free from manipulation and measured by **holiness**, **doctrine**, and **love**. Here he mediates **Jonathan Edwards**, **Owen**, and **Scottish Presbyterian** models for contemporary churches.

This pastoral theology includes warnings against both **antinomianism** and **legalism**. Beeke teaches the **third use of the law** as the believer’s rule of gratitude, integrates **assurance** with **obedience** as fruit not ground of acceptance, and upholds the **conscience** as God’s herald to be schooled by the Word. His aim is a reformed catholicity of life where **union with Christ** orders the believer’s participation in worship, vocation, and family.

#### 8. Engagements and boundaries
Beeke’s polemical engagements are measured but clear. Against the **New Perspective on Paul** and **Federal Vision**, he defends **forensic justification** by the **imputed righteousness of Christ**, including the **active obedience**, and the instrumentality of **faith alone**. Against **eternal functional subordination**, he appeals to **Nicene** and **Reformed** readings of **divine simplicity** and **inseparable operations**. He critiques **therapeutic evangelicalism** by restoring the grammar of **sin**, **repentance**, and **mortification**, and he relativizes **New Calvinism** by insisting that **confession**, **liturgy**, and **discipline** are not optional add-ons but constitutive of **Reformed** identity. His constructive alternative is a churchly piety shaped by **confession**, **catechesis**, and **sacramental** practice under the **ordinary means of grace**.

His scholarly posture is ecumenically Reformed rather than broadly evangelical. He integrates **Bavinck** and **Murray** with **Owen** and **Witsius**, and he places **van Mastricht** beside **Turretin** as doctors of a stable and hospitable **classical theism**. While he appropriates insights from **biblical theology** as in **Geerhardus Vos**, he resists collapsing **systematics** into redemptive-historical narration, maintaining a dogmatic architecture where **covenant** orders the history without displacing analytic clarity.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **WTS** perspective, Beeke’s achievement is a robust retrieval of **classical theism**, **covenant theology**, and **union with Christ** in an ecclesial register that resists modern fragmentation. Strengths include his unapologetic defense of **divine simplicity** and **impassibility**, his clarity on **justification** by **imputation**, his federal framing of **Adam** and **Christ**, and his catechetical integration of **assurance** with the **means of grace**. Yet tensions remain. The **practical syllogism** and emphasis on **marks of grace** can, in some settings, tilt toward **introspective preparationism**, risking a subtle displacement of the **direct act of faith** in Christ’s sufficiency by reflexive acts that read the self as a quasi-text. A WTS alternative would press harder on the primacy of **union with Christ** and the **Spirit’s witness** to stabilize assurance, while still valuing reflexive confirmations as secondary. Moreover, the dominant **Puritan lens** can underrepresent other confessional trajectories such as **Kuyperian** accounts of **common grace** and **two-kingdoms** prudence in public theology; a fuller synthesis could place **catholic Reformed** insights on culture alongside **experimental divinity**. Within **classical Reformed orthodoxy**, these adjustments are matters of emphasis not substance, and they sharpen rather than blunt Beeke’s central thesis that **doctrine is for life** under the **ordinary means** administered in Christ’s **covenant**.
