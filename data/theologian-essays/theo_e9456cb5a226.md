### TITLE
Reclaiming classical trinitarian grammar for evangelical dogmatics through inseparable operations and triune atonement

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Post-1990s evangelical retrieval** amid debates over **social trinitarianism**, **EFS/ERAS**, and **classical theism**; situated in a wider ressourcement to **Augustine** and **Aquinas** within contemporary Anglophone theology.
- **Method of metaphysical realism** joined to canonical exegesis; retrieval of **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **inseparable operations** as regulative principles for doctrine and Scripture; economy read from and toward **processions and missions**.
- **Key works**: **Atonement, Law, and Justice** 2014; **The Same God Who Works All Things** 2021; **The Divine Missions: An Introduction** 2022.
- **Central categories**: **opera Trinitatis ad extra sunt indivisa**, **divine missions**, **eternal generation and spiration**, **appropriation**, **penal substitution**, **lex aeterna** and legal theory, *ad intra* and *ad extra*.
- **Interlocutors**: **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, **Anselm**, **Reformed scholastics**; modern debates with **Barth**, **Rahner**, **Moltmann**, **Jenson**, **Gunton**, and evangelical **EFS** proponents; conversation partners include **Fred Sanders**, **Oliver Crisp**, and classical theism retrieval.
- **Reformed reception cue**: Strong alignment with **Westminster Confession** on **simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **one will**; questions about integrating **inseparable operations** with **pactum salutis** and covenantal administrations.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and the wager of retrieval
Adonis **Vidu** is a contemporary **evangelical** theologian whose work epitomizes the post-1990s retrieval of **classical theism** within Anglophone dogmatics. Educated in both European and North American contexts and long based at **Gordon-Conwell Theological Seminary**, he writes in the wake of evangelical debates over **social trinitarianism** and the so-called **eternal functional subordination** of the Son, as well as broader reassessments of **divine simplicity** and **impassibility**. The intellectual horizon is one in which **Rahner's Rule** and its reception in **Barth**, **Moltmann**, and **Jenson** had pressed twentieth-century theology toward reading the immanent life of God directly off the economy. Vidu wagers that a careful ressourcement to **Augustine** and **Aquinas**, guided by the doctrine that the **Trinity's external works are undivided**, can heal this breach without evacuating economy of revelatory significance. His proposal is neither repristination nor mere analytic restatement; it is an evangelical dogmatic method that weds metaphysical realism to canonical exegesis, rehabilitating **divine missions** as the bridge between *ad intra* processions and *ad extra* operations.

The methodological posture is clear. Vidu retrieves **opera Trinitatis ad extra sunt indivisa** as a regulative axiom, embeds it within **divine simplicity** and **one will**, and then shows how **appropriations** identify patterned manifestations of the one undivided act according to personal **taxis** grounded in **eternal generation and spiration**. The result is a grammar that refuses rival monisms that collapse the immanent into the economic or segregate the persons into three centers of agency. He presses the difference between a **monarchy of the Father** that names order of origin and any suggestion of primary efficient causality in the economy, thereby blocking both **tritheism** and the voluntarist path to **EFS**. The central conceptual tools are classical: **mission** as a new mode of presence, **procession** as the personal relation of origin, **simplicity** as denial of composition, and **impassibility** as unshakable divine plenitude. The wager is that only such a grammar can sustain evangelical convictions about **atonement**, **Scripture**, and **salvation**.

#### 2. Atonement in legal and cultural history: Atonement, Law, and Justice 2014
In **Atonement, Law, and Justice** 2014, Vidu offers a historical-genealogical account of atonement models in tandem with developments in legal theory. The thesis is not reductionist but illuminating: construals of **divine justice** and **law** correlate to shifting horizons from Roman **retributive** forms, medieval **canon law** and **satisfaction**, early modern **natural law**, to modern **restorative** justice. Building from **Anselm's** satisfactio and **Calvin's** penal accent, through **Grotius'** governmental theory, Vidu argues that evangelical **penal substitution** need not be tethered to caricatures of retribution, nor opposed to restorative dimensions. On his reading, the cross is the act of the one **simple God**, triune in persons, satisfying divine justice not by intra-trinitarian violence but by the **Son's** hypostatic offering in the power of the **Spirit**, according to the **Father's** wise decree. The controversial trope of **divine child abuse** is thereby turned back by the classical grammar of the **one will** and the **inseparable operations**, which secure that the Father does not act upon the Son as an external agent but that the triune God reconciles the world to himself.

Vidu extends this analysis by specifying the difference between **lex aeterna** and positive legal orders. The **eternal law** names God's wise ordering of all things to their end, which grounds but does not collapse into human jurisprudence. This distinction allows him to retrieve **penal substitution** as an application of **justice** proper to God without uncritically baptizing any historical penal theory. He maps how shifts in legal metaphors often smuggle in hidden **anthropologies** and **political theologies**, and so he insists on the controlling dogmatic content: **simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** render the cross a free triune act, not a reactive passion. The upshot is a chastened but robust defense of penal substitution within a wider orchestra of **Christus Victor**, **moral transformation**, and **satisfaction** motifs, rightly harmonized by the doctrine of **triune agency**.

#### 3. Inseparable operations as trinitarian grammar: The Same God Who Works All Things 2021
Vidu's programmatic contribution appears in **The Same God Who Works All Things** 2021. Here he retrieves the patristic and scholastic axiom that the **Trinity's external works are undivided** while articulating a nuanced account of **personal appropriations** and **terminations** that secure real personal distinctions. The point is twofold. First, the one God acts as one because of **divine simplicity** and **one will**; there are not three causal sources, three deliberations, or three agencies in competition. Second, the personal **taxis** of **eternal generation** and **spiration** grounds patterned modalities of acting: the **Father** sends, the **Son** is sent to assume a created nature, the **Spirit** is sent to indwell, and yet each mission is an operation of the one God. This allows him to maintain **Rahner's Rule** in its right sense, namely that the economic reveals the immanent, while rejecting its inflationary forms that erase **immanent processions** or read off distinct wills from economic roles.

The book is not merely conceptual. Vidu works exegetically through texts such as John 5.19, 1 Corinthians 8.6, and the Johannine mission sayings, showing how the biblical idiom of sending and receiving presupposes **oneness of agency** even as it narrates missions. He engages **Athanasius** and **Augustine** on the Son's dependence according to his **human nature**, and he revisits **appropriation** as a pedagogical identification that nevertheless tracks real personal order. Special attention is given to the **incarnation**: only the **Son** assumes a human nature because his **eternal generation** images in the economy a fitting assumption, and yet the incarnation remains an operation of the one **simple God**. That line wards off innovations that posit a unique causal share to one person in contrast to others, as well as reactive strategies that dissolve the missions into manifestations of three quasi-deities. The Spirit's mission is treated in the same register: his **indwelling** is the triune God present as the Spirit's proper mode, not a slice of the divine act.

#### 4. Missions as the bridge between processions and economy: The Divine Missions 2022
With **The Divine Missions: An Introduction** 2022 Vidu provides a catechetically clear yet conceptually dense guide to **missions**, **processions**, and **appropriations**. Following **Thomas Aquinas** in Summa Theologiae I, question 43, he defines **mission** as a new mode of presence: visible when mediated by created effects such as the **incarnation** and **Pentecost**, invisible when a person is given to dwell in the soul by grace. He labors to demonstrate how **missions** reveal, without exhausting, **processions**, sustaining the claim that what is manifested in time is grounded in the eternal life of God. Pastoral dividends follow. The ordinary life of sanctification can be construed as participation in the **Spirit's invisible mission**, conforming us to the **Son**, to the glory of the **Father**, an account that clarifies prayer, sacramental participation in traditions that confess it, and evangelical practices of discipleship alike.

In this register, Vidu explores the dogmatic payoffs of missions for **soteriology** and **ecclesiology**. Because the operations are **inseparable**, the church's life is not parcelled out into three rival departments of the Trinity, but coordinated around one divine act of gathering and perfecting the saints. Yet because operations terminate personally, a real differentiation marks our participation: we are adopted by the **Father** in the **Son** through the **Spirit**. This grammar reorients spiritual theology away from **social trinitarian** psychologizing toward doctrinally governed devotion, and it reshapes proclamation of the **gospel** so that the cross and resurrection are seen as the triune God's work without internal competition. The payoff is a deeply evangelical clarity about the **name** into which baptism is administered and the **mission** into which the church is sent.

#### 5. Negotiating twentieth-century trinitarianism and contemporary evangelical debates
Vidu's interlocutors are carefully chosen. He treats **Karl Rahner** with respect, receiving **Rahner's Rule** as a check against speculative projection while insisting that the economic cannot be the exhaustive measure of the **immanent Trinity**. He opposes **JÃ¼rgen Moltmann's** path of **divine passibility** insofar as it compromises **impassibility** understood as plenitudinous life, even while acknowledging divine compassion as an **undivided operation** manifest in the incarnate passion of the **Son** according to his assumed nature. He demurs from **Robert Jenson's** identity thesis that time constitutes the being of God, arguing instead for **simplicity** and **aseity** as the ground of God's covenantal faithfulness. And against **Colin Gunton's** social construction, he argues that biblical data on the sendings are better honored by **inseparable operations** plus personal **taxis** than by multiplying divine centers of agency.

Within evangelicalism, Vidu offers a sustained critique of **EFS/ERAS** associated with figures such as **Bruce Ware**. The heart of his objection is not semantic but metaphysical: positing eternal functional subordination risks denying the **one will** of God or reconfiguring **eternal generation** into an authority relation, both of which strain **Nicene** and **pro-Nicene** commitments. By retrieving the **monarchia Patris** as origin rather than domination and clarifying that missional obedience belongs to the **Son** as incarnate, he shows how Scripture's idiom coheres with **classical trinitarianism**. This critique is constructive, for it provides evangelicals a way to retain the biblical idiom of obedience and sending without inferring rival wills in God, and to confess the **Spirit's** mission as the triune act who perfects, not a third will adding finishing touches.

#### 6. Simplicity, impassibility, and the grammar of the cross
A distinctive strength in Vidu's corpus is his integration of **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** with the **atonement**. By insisting on the **communication of idioms** and the anhypostatic-enhypostatic union of Christ's humanity, he can affirm without contradiction that the **Son of God suffered** according to the human nature he assumed while the **triune God** acted indivisibly in reconciling the world. This allows him to redescribe the **cry of dereliction** and the penal dimensions of the cross without positing rifts within the **immanent Trinity**. Penal substitution is not the Father penalizing an other, but the one God judging sin in the flesh of the **incarnate Son**, who as the **new Adam** bears the curse for us in the power of the **Spirit**. Impassibility here secures the freedom, love, and constancy of God, while impelling robust talk of God's costly mercy manifested in time.

This same grammar clarifies that the **decree** to save, the **mission** of the Son, and the **gift** of the Spirit are one simple act terminatively ordered in history. Vidu thereby avoids both dead abstraction and economism. He can say with the **Reformed scholastics** that the **decree** is eternal and simple while fully affirming historical **covenantal** administrations in which divine justice and mercy are displayed. His appropriation of legal categories in **Atonement, Law, and Justice** is chastened by this metaphysical reserve: no human court or theory can circumscribe the **lex aeterna**, which is God himself as ordering all to his goodness.

#### 7. Scripture, metaphysics, and evangelical dogmatics
In style as well as substance Vidu situates himself within a broad evangelical **ressourcement** committed to reading Scripture as the canonical theater of God's self-giving and the source for dogmatic construction under the rule of **creedal** and **confessional** norms. His exegesis is not free-floating; it is steered by **classical theism** as a guide to coherent reading, and his metaphysics is not unmoored; it is tested by Scripture's pattern of truth. This dialogical posture enables him to argue, for example, that the **mission** texts of John's Gospel do not posit competing agents but reveal **inseparable operations**, or that Pauline soteriology is triune through and through. The payoff is a methodological proposal: evangelical theology can retrieve **simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **inseparable operations** without abandoning biblical idiom or pastoral specificity.

At the same time he models how doctrinal loci interlock. The doctrine of **God** regulates **Christology** and **soteriology**, while the doctrine of **missions** informs **ecclesiology** and **spiritual theology**. The church's proclamation of the **gospel** is thus identifiably trinitarian: the **Father** elects and sends, the **Son** assumes and atones, the **Spirit** perfects and indwells, all as the one **God** working all things. It is precisely this integrative clarity that gives his project weight within contemporary evangelical dogmatics.

#### 8. Contribution and consolidation
Taken together, Vidu's corpus restores three fragile threads in contemporary evangelical theology. First, the doctrine of **inseparable operations** is reintroduced as doctrinal grammar with exegetical saturation, dislodging ad hoc appeals to **appropriation** and blocking tendencies toward **tritheism**. Second, **penal substitution** is defended within a capacious dogmatic frame that reintegrates **justice**, **mercy**, and **mission**, guarding against moralizing reductions and apologetic defensiveness. Third, **divine missions** are reclaimed as the decisive bridge between **processions** and **economy**, furnishing a map for spiritual and ecclesial life. These threads hang together on the loom of **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **one will**, with **Augustine** and **Aquinas** as master teachers and with **Reformed scholastic** instincts animating the whole, even as the tone remains broadly evangelical and ecumenically alert.

The upshot is a constructive retrieval that addresses the practical crises of evangelical theology: worries about whether **classical theism** stifles biblical drama are eased by demonstrating that the classical God is the living God of **Gospel** history; fears that **penal substitution** implies divine cruelty are quelled by the doctrine of **triune agency**; and confusion about the **Trinity** is alleviated by a scripturally argued account of **missions**. Vidu thus offers a grammar, not a new system, whose tests are clarity, catholicity, and pastoral fruitfulness.

#### 9. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Vidu's retrieval of **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, **inseparable operations**, and **one will** represents a salutary recalibration of evangelical dogmatics toward **Reformed orthodoxy**. His account aligns with the **Westminster Confession** in affirming God's **aseity**, **immutability**, and undivided **agency**, and it strengthens confessional **Christology** by tethering the **incarnation** and **atonement** to **processions** and **missions**. Two cautions arise. First, the integration of **inseparable operations** with the **pactum salutis** requires care lest the covenantal roles in redemption be flattened into a merely economic idiom; a federal account can maintain distinct covenantal relations of origin without implying **EFS**, preserving both divine **freedom** in the **decree** and the undivided execution of that decree in history. Second, the legal genealogy in **Atonement, Law, and Justice** is illuminating, yet **Reformed** theology should keep the **lex aeterna** explicitly subordinated to **revelation** within **covenant** rather than to natural jurisprudence, so that law serves grace within the **covenantal economy** rather than becoming an independent norm. These reservations are internal to the project and can be met by drawing more explicitly on **Turretin**, **Bavinck**, and the confessional treatments of **covenant of redemption**. On balance, Vidu's work provides a doctrinal grammar that powerfully equips Reformed and evangelical churches to confess the **one God and three persons** and to preach a **triune atonement** with metaphysical and biblical integrity.
