### TITLE
Witnessing Dialogue in the Spirit: A Pentecostal theology of religions grounded in Trinitarian orthodoxy and missional holiness.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth and early twenty-first century **classical Pentecostal** context in the **Wesleyan-Holiness** Church of God tradition amid global **Pentecostal/Charismatic** expansion and post-9/11 interreligious tensions.
- Method marked by **pneumatological hermeneutics**, **narrative-liturgy-experience** integration, and a dialogical yet **Christologically normative** stance shaped by **Luke-Acts** and the **Wesleyan Quadrilateral**.
- Key works and milestones: **Speaking by the Spirit: A Pentecostal Model for Interreligious Dialogue** (2011); essays in **Pneuma** and **Journal of Pentecostal Theology** 2003–2020; contributions to **Trinitarian-Oneness** and broader **ecumenical dialogues**.
- Central categories: **witnessing dialogue**, **pneumatological inclusivism**, **baptism in the Spirit**, **sanctification as theosis**, **Christus Victor**, and **Trinitarian orthodoxy** against **modalism**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **John Wesley**, **Athanasius** and **theosis**, **Amos Yong**, **Veli-Matti Kärkkäinen**, **Lesslie Newbigin**, **Gavin D’Costa**, **Eastern Orthodoxy** on **personhood** and **communion**.
- Reformed cue: alignment on **Trinitarian** fidelity and **Word-Spirit** cohesion, with tension over **pneumatological inclusivism**, **experience as norming source**, and **theosis** vis-a-vis **monergistic grace**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and the pastoral-intellectual setting
Tony Richie emerges as a contemporary **Pentecostal** theologian whose work is deliberately forged at the intersection of pastoral ministry, ecumenical encounter, and academic reflection within the **Wesleyan-Holiness** lineage of the Church of God, Cleveland, Tennessee. His intellectual formation tracks with the late twentieth-century maturation of **Pentecostal theology**, when testimonies of revival pressed toward sustained doctrinal expression, and the **Society for Pentecostal Studies** catalyzed constructive engagement with **patristic**, **evangelical**, and **ecumenical** traditions. Global **Pentecostal/Charismatic** growth reframed mission as bidirectional encounter, while the post-9/11 environment intensified the urgency for a robust **theology of religions** that would combine fidelity to **Trinitarian orthodoxy** with neighbor-love. In this setting, Richie positions his work as a constructive proposal for integrating **witness and dialogue**, a Pentecostal grammar for peaceable yet confident proclamation, underwritten by the **Spirit’s** agency.

The formative narrative is not merely sociological but liturgical and doxological: Richie’s theological instincts arise from the lived piety of **Spirit-baptism**, altar-centered **sanctification**, and healing-prayer practices, all construed as the ordinary operations of **missio Spiritus**. The result is a posture that values **Scripture** as Spirit-inspired canon, honors **ecclesial orthodoxy** codified in the creeds, and attends to **charismatic experience** as a theological datum. This Wesleyan-Pentecostal synthesis yields a theological temperament that is neither purely confessional nor merely pragmatic but a disciplined **testimony** to the works of the Triune God.

#### 2. Method: Scripture and Spirit in dialogical mission
Richie’s methodological hallmark is a **pneumatological hermeneutic** that privileges **Luke-Acts** as the canonical theater of **mission**, **witness**, and **discernment of spirits**. Within a Wesleyan frame, Scripture remains the rule of faith, while **tradition**, **reason**, and **experience** operate as ministering servants under the Spirit’s lordship. His proposal rehearses the classic Pentecostal conviction that the **Spirit** who inspired the text also animates the church’s discernment, so that theological method is both exegetical and charismatic. Yet Richie's epistemic posture insists that **Christological normativity** governs the Spirit’s economy; the Spirit’s universal operations never relativize the singularity of **Jesus Christ** as Savior, Sanctifier, Spirit-Baptizer, Healer, and Coming King, the fivefold **Full Gospel**.

This method yields the category of **witnessing dialogue**, an intentional synthesis that resists the dichotomy between apologetic exclusivism and pluralist relativism. For Richie, the church must both testify and listen, both proclaim and learn, because **missio Dei** involves the Father who sends, the Son who is the message, and the **Spirit** who precedes the church, convicts the world, and empowers discourse. The discernment touchstone is **1 John 4**: the testing of spirits by confession of **Jesus Christ**. In sum, Richie’s method is a disciplined hospitality: porous to the **Spirit’s** outreach to the nations, impermeable to **syncretism**, and anchored by **Trinitarian** doctrine.

#### 3. The central proposal: Speaking by the Spirit and the theology of religions
Richie’s book **Speaking by the Spirit: A Pentecostal Model for Interreligious Dialogue** (2011) stands as a programmatic articulation of his vision. Drawing exegetically on **Acts 10** and **Acts 17**, he argues that the **Spirit** sometimes goes ahead of the apostles to prepare the **Corneliuses** of the world, and that **Paul’s** Athenian engagement models respectful recognition of truth-fragments under the rubric of common grace, even while calling for repentance toward the **risen Lord**. Richie names his stance **pneumatological inclusivism** with **Christological exclusivity**. The Spirit’s prevenient operations can be genuinely revelatory and morally effectual among the nations, yet salvation is finally and fully in **Christ alone**, embraced through explicit faith as the Spirit draws all persons.

His ongoing essays in **Pneuma** and **Journal of Pentecostal Theology** extend the proposal: he elaborates **witnessing dialogue** as a praxis for Pentecostal mission, charts typologies among **exclusivism**, **inclusivism**, and **pluralism**, and repudiates **John Hick’s** pluralist hypothesis as incompatible with the **Incarnation** and **homoousios** Christology. Influenced by **Lesslie Newbigin’s** missional realism, Richie contends that dialogical openness is not a covert universalism but a strategy of faithful presence animated by the **Spirit’s** convicting and consoling work. His constructive goal is neither to dilute the scandal of particularity nor to absolutize Pentecostal subculture; rather, to form **Spirit-led** communities of testimony that can engage **Islam**, **Hinduism**, and **Buddhism** with humility and confidence.

#### 4. Trinitarian orthodoxy and Oneness engagements
Richie’s pastoral and scholarly commitments draw him into the contested space between **Trinitarian** Pentecostals and **Oneness** or **modalist** Pentecostal movements. Participating in and reflecting on **Trinitarian-Oneness** dialogues, Richie defends the **Nicene** confession by cultivating biblical and patristic habits of speech that distinguish divine **hypostaseis** without dividing the **ousia**. He presses **prosopological** exegesis of Scripture and articulates the Spirit’s mission as from the **Father through the Son in the Spirit**, a formulation hospitable to **East-West** rapprochement on the **filioque** by way of temporal missions rather than speculative processions. The aim is re-catechesis within Pentecostalism itself, so that the **Spirit’s** immediacy is not misread as a reduction of the divine **Triunity** to shifting masks.

At the same time Richie engages interlocutors such as **Amos Yong** and **Veli-Matti Kärkkäinen**, alongside **Eastern Orthodox** voices like **John Zizioulas**, to display the ecclesial and relational import of **Trinitarian** confession for community, charisms, and mission. His concept of an **ecumenism of the Spirit** intentionally retrieves **Athanasius** and **Cappadocian** insights for Pentecostal catechesis, while insisting that classical doctrine fuels, rather than frustrates, revivalist piety. In this way Richie serves both apologetic and doxological ends: retrieving **creedal orthodoxy** to stabilize Pentecostal worship and deploying **Trinitarian** theology to frame inter-Pentecostal dialogue.

#### 5. Soteriology and sanctification: Theosis within a Wesleyan-Pentecostal register
Within soteriology, Richie narrates conversion and sanctification through a **Wesleyan-Pentecostal** lens that sometimes borrows the grammar of **theosis**. He affirms **prevenient grace**, justification by faith, and Spirit-enabled **entire sanctification** as growth in holy love. Drawing on **Athanasius’** celebrated axiom that the Son became human so that humans might become by grace what He is by nature, Richie presents **deification** not as ontological absorption but as Spirit-wrought participation in **Christ’s** filial life. This is a doxological holiness: contemplative and activist, rooted in the **means of grace**, openly charismatic.

At the charismatic center sits **baptism in the Holy Spirit** as empowerment for mission. Richie aligns with classical Pentecostalism in affirming subsequence, frequently echoing the **initial evidence** of tongues while reframing it in pastoral terms as a sign of **missional speech** rather than a merit badge. The atonement is often cast in **Christus Victor** terms consonant with deliverance ministry and healing praxis, yet never detached from **substitutionary** and **satisfaction** motifs. The **Spirit** thus binds sanctification to vocation: to be sanctified is to be set on mission, and to be Spirit-baptized is to be sent to the nations in word and deed.

#### 6. Theology of religions: Discernment, hospitality, and proclamation
In elaborating a **Pentecostal theology of religions**, Richie traces the well-known taxonomy of **exclusivism**, **inclusivism**, and **pluralism**, then crafts what he terms **pneumatological inclusivism** tethered to **Christological finality**. The Spirit’s universal presence is not salvific in itself but preparatory, awakening conscience and truth-longing, leaving authentic traces of grace that become points of contact for **witnessing dialogue**. Scriptural exemplars such as **Cornelius** in Acts 10 model how the Spirit may precede the church without bypassing the proclamation of **Jesus**. The criterion of discernment remains the confession of **Christ** and the fruits of the **Spirit** discerned in love, joy, and peace.

Practically, Richie’s model cultivates humility, condemns religious coercion, and commends **hospitality** to the stranger while refusing **syncretism**. It leans on **Newbigin’s** insistence that the gospel is public truth, yet acknowledges **Gavin D’Costa’s** caution against theological relativism. The Spirit’s role is not to authenticate every religious aspiration but to chasten the church, open doors for wisdom from unexpected quarters, and embolden missionary proclamation. This is a theology for the street and the sanctuary, aimed at equipping Pentecostal pastors and laypersons to inhabit pluralist contexts with **boldness** and **charity**.

#### 7. Ecclesiology and ecumenism: Orthodoxy and charisms held together
Richie projects an **ecclesiology** that marries **creedal orthodoxy** with **charismatic** vitality. He often commends a retrieval of the **Fathers** and the early councils for catechesis in Pentecostal churches, forming a people sturdy in the **Trinity** and **Christology** while expectant for spiritual gifts. This supports his call for an **ecumenism of the Spirit**, where Pentecostals receive from **Roman Catholic**, **Eastern Orthodox**, and **historic Protestant** traditions without compromising revivalist identity. Liturgical learning and sacramental imagination are not set against spontaneity; rather, they provide guardrails and deep wells for Pentecostal devotion.

In ecumenical practice Richie highlights convergence on essentials such as **Trinitarian** confession, **biblical authority**, and the **mission** of the church, even as he resists any ecclesial settlement that would mute the **charismata**. His engagements with **Oneness** Pentecostals exemplify this ethos: searching for reconciliation around **Scripture** and **holiness**, while clearly delineating the non-negotiables of **Nicene** faith. The church is envisioned as a communion-in-mission, sent by the **Triune God**, sanctified for witness, and sustained by the **Spirit’s** gifts.

#### 8. Scripture, testimony, and discernment: A Pentecostal hermeneutic
At the level of hermeneutics Richie joins a long line of Pentecostal exegetes who center **Luke-Acts** as a canonical narrative of **mission** and **Spirit**. He warns against both an uncritical **canon within the canon** and a flattening biblicism; texts must be read within the church, under the **Spirit**, and for the sake of **proclamation**. Theologically, **testimony** functions as a secondary locus, akin to **tradition** and **experience** in Wesley’s scheme, yet normed by **Scripture**. The Spirit-inspired text is living and active not in an individualistic sense but within the fellowship of the saints, where gifts of **prophecy**, **discernment**, and **tongues** accompany exegesis.

This hermeneutic bears directly on the theology of religions: the church discerns **truth** and **error** in other faiths not by autonomous rational criteria but by **Christological** and **pneumatological** marks. The test remains the confession of **Jesus** as Lord and the fruits of the **Spirit**. Such discernment guards against naive accommodation and equips believers for holy reasoning in the public square. In Richie’s telling, faithful reading issues in faithful witnessing.

#### 9. Interlocutors and debates: Pentecostal voices and wider conversations
Richie’s dialogue partners reveal his synthesis. With **Amos Yong**, he shares a **pneumatological** thrust and appreciation for **religions** as potential sites of the Spirit’s preparatory work, yet he presses a firmer **Christological** boundary to avoid sliding toward pragmatic pluralism. With **Kärkkäinen**, he explores comparative theology under **Trinitarian** auspices, though he remains cautious of expansive reconstruals of salvation history that risk marginalizing Pentecostal distinctives. He appropriates **Lesslie Newbigin’s** missional epistemology as a defense of proclamation in a late-modern West skeptical of truth claims, and he engages **Gavin D’Costa** and post–**Vatican II** Catholic theologians such as **Jacques Dupuis** on the legitimacy and limits of inclusivist accounts.

In the intra-Pentecostal space Richie contends earnestly with **Oneness** theologians such as **David Bernard**, commending shared commitments to **holiness**, **evangelism**, and the **Spirit**, while arguing that **modalism** undermines doxological and soteriological coherence. He converses with **Eastern Orthodox** insights from **Athanasius** to **Zizioulas** on **theosis** and **communion**, yet translates them into a Wesleyan-Pentecostal idiom where **entire sanctification** and **Spirit baptism** name participatory realities. His work thus inhabits multiple grammars at once: **patristic orthodoxy**, **Wesleyan holiness**, and **Pentecostal** experiential theology.

#### 10. Reformed/WTS evaluation: Convergences and cautions
From a classical **Reformed** and particularly Westminsterian vantage, Richie’s theology offers real convergences alongside principled cautions. Positively, his insistence on **Trinitarian orthodoxy**, high **Christology**, and the inseparability of **Word and Spirit** resonates with confessional commitments, as does his rejection of **pluralism** and his emphasis on the church’s **mission** under the lordship of **Christ**. His retrieval of **patristic** resources and his defense of **Nicene** faith against **modalism** supply needed ballast within global Pentecostalism. Yet tensions surface around **pneumatological inclusivism**, where a Reformed reading of **special revelation** and **covenantal particularism** would resist assigning salvific or revelatory status to the **Spirit’s** work in religions apart from the **gospel** as preached Word and sacrament. Likewise, a Westminster account of grace would be wary of **theosis** language that might blur **monergism**, preferring to render participation as union with **Christ** by the **Spirit** within the covenant of grace and the ordo salutis. Methodologically, the elevation of **experience** and **testimony** as a proximate source, though chastened by Scripture, risks an unstable norm that Reformed theology would correct by a stronger doctrine of **sola Scriptura** and a consequent rule of faith. Still, Richie’s model of **witnessing dialogue** can sharpen Reformed missiology toward bold hospitality without diluting **Christ’s** exclusivity, offering a shared space for common mission even as principled disagreements remain.

- Core problem: how to hold **Christological exclusivity** with **pneumatological** openness in a theology of religions oriented to **mission**.
- Major works: **Speaking by the Spirit** (2011) and sustained essays in **Pneuma** and **Journal of Pentecostal Theology** on **witnessing dialogue** and **Trinitarian** fidelity.
- Distinctives: **pneumatological hermeneutics**, **witnessing dialogue**, **baptism in the Spirit** as missional empowerment, **sanctification as theosis**, and **Christus Victor** soteriology.

- Legacy: a credible **Pentecostal** paradigm for interreligious engagement that strengthens **Trinitarian** catechesis, shapes ecumenical practice, and informs global **mission**.
