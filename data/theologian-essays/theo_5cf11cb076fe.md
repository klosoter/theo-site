### TITLE
Heilsgeschichte and the Apostolic Word: Scripture, Kingdom, and Paul under the Axis of the Already and the Not Yet

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Dutch **neo-Calvinist** milieu in the Netherlands, 1909–2007, shaped by the Reformed Churches and postwar debates over **Formgeschichte**, **demythologizing**, and **Heilsgeschichte**.
- Diachronic **biblical-theological method** prioritizing **redemptive history** over existential anthropology while employing disciplined critical tools under **confessional authority**.
- Key works: **The Coming of the Kingdom** 1950, **Galatians** NICNT 1953, **Redemptive History and the New Testament Scriptures** 1955 Dutch 1963 Eng., **Paul: An Outline of His Theology** 1966 Dutch 1975 Eng., **The Gospel of John** 1987 Dutch 1997 Eng.
- Central categories: **kingdom of God**, **already/not yet** eschatology, **apostolicity** and canon, **kerygma** and history, **union with Christ**, **justification**, **new creation**, **church as eschatological community**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Geerhardus Vos** and **neo-Calvinism**, engagement with **Rudolf Bultmann**, **Ernst Käsemann**, **C. H. Dodd**, **Oscar Cullmann**, dialogue with **G. C. Berkouwer** and the **Religionsgeschichtliche Schule**.
- Reformed reception: welcomed in **Westminster/Vosian** circles for **redemptive-historical exegesis** and **apostolic authority**, with probing of method vis-a-vis **inerrancy**, **law-gospel**, and **sacrament**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Context, and Method
Herman Ridderbos, born in 1909 and dying in 2007, entered the Dutch **Reformed Churches** at a moment when **neo-Calvinism** was consolidating its intellectual gains and when the **Religionsgeschichtliche Schule** pressed hard on the historical reliability of the New Testament. Trained in the Netherlands and appointed in 1943 professor of **New Testament** at the Theological School in **Kampen**, he taught through the rending years of World War II and the postwar reconstruction of European theology. Against the backdrop of **Karl Barth** and **Rudolf Bultmann**, alongside the earlier work of **Geerhardus Vos**, Ridderbos forged a distinctive **Heilsgeschichte** approach: divine revelation comes as deed-embedded word where history is not raw chronology but the theater of God’s covenant works. He deliberately adopted a **biblical-theological method** that is diachronic, canon-conscious, and normed by **apostolic witness** rather than by philosophical existentialism. While he was not hostile to careful **Formgeschichte** or **Redaktionsgeschichte**, he subordinated such tools to a **confessional** claim: Scripture is the uniquely normative medium of the **kerygma** because it is the normative document of the **apostolate**.

The method crystallized around a paradox that pervades his work: past redemptive acts inaugurate an eschatological order that is already operative yet not consummated. This **already/not yet** pattern, derived from New Testament **eschatology**, functions as both hermeneutical horizon and dogmatic guide. Ridderbos thus resists both **realized eschatology** as advanced by **C. H. Dodd**, which collapses promise into present, and a purely future **apocalyptic** as in **Albert Schweitzer** and later **Ernst Käsemann**, which deprives the present of the kingdom’s power. His Kampen lectures trained pastors to preach with a **redemptive-historical** cadence, proclaiming the divine acts that establish the church’s identity in Christ and summon faith in the Spirit.

#### 2. The Coming of the Kingdom and the Shape of Revelation
The early landmark, **The Coming of the Kingdom** 1950, responds to mid-century debates about **Jesus and the kingdom of God**. Against **Dodd’s realized eschatology**, Ridderbos reads the Synoptics as testimony to an inaugurated reign: in Jesus the **basileia tou theou** arrives in act and word, yet remains oriented toward a final **parousia**. This yields a threefold grammar: the kingdom as divine sovereignty in action, as the new redemptive order intruding into the present, and as the promised consummation. In this frame, miracle, exorcism, and parable are not detachable religious experiences but components of God’s climactic rule. Ridderbos’s exposition also critiques **Bultmann’s demythologizing**, arguing that the **kerygma** presupposes and interprets events irreducible to mythic categories. Revelation is not a timeless idea; it is the historic self-communication of the King.

This book already announces his mature view of **Scripture and authority**. The Gospels are apostolic or apostolically supervised testimonies through which the exalted Christ continues to reign by his **word and Spirit**. This is not naïve historicism. Ridderbos acknowledges theological shaping within the tradition but refuses the dichotomy of **history versus proclamation**. The **kerygma** is historical proclamation because it expounds the acts of God; the **history** is kerygmatic because it is intelligible only as God’s saving history. The result is a hermeneutic where **covenant history**, **kingdom**, and **canon** interlock.

#### 3. Paul: An Outline of His Theology and the Grammar of Redemption
Ridderbos’s signature contribution, **Paul: An Outline of His Theology** 1966 Dutch, 1975 English, systematizes Pauline thought not as speculative metaphysics but as **salvation-historical proclamation**. The book arranges Pauline theology under themes governed by the new aeon: revelation in Christ, the dominating motif of **in Christ**, the **justification** of the ungodly, the **Spirit**, the **church**, and the **ethical life**. Ridderbos accents the epochal and representative character of Christ’s work: the two-Adam schema, **in Adam/in Christ**, marks the transition from the old to the new creation. This is corporate and forensic without losing personal appropriation; the apostle’s language of **justification** establishes the judicial foundation for **union with Christ**, while **union** unfolds the participatory and eschatological dimensions of justification.

Crucially, Ridderbos anticipates later debates with the **New Perspective on Paul**. While predating **E. P. Sanders** 1977 and **James D. G. Dunn**, he rejects an antithesis of covenantal membership versus individual guilt by reading **justification by faith** as God’s saving verdict that creates the eschatological people. The **law** is not mere ethnic boundary but covenant administration that is relativized in the new aeon by Christ’s cross and the **Spirit**. Election, vocation, and sanctification are summed up in the reality of the **new creation**. Ridderbos thus holds together **forensic** and **participatory** strands under a single salvation-historical headship. He engages **Käsemann** by granting apocalyptic inbreak while refusing to dissolve Paul’s **righteousness of God** into sheer lordship apart from judicial grace.

#### 4. Ecclesiology, Sacrament, and Ethics in the New Aeon
Within his Pauline synthesis, Ridderbos devotes considerable analysis to **ecclesiology**. The church is the **eschatological community** created by the Word, the Spirit, and the sacraments. The image of the **body of Christ** expresses not mysticism but corporate solidarity grounded in the representative Christ. **Baptism** signifies and seals incorporation into the new aeon, and **the Lord’s Supper** is communion with the crucified and risen Lord, anticipating the **eschatological banquet**. Here Ridderbos draws from the Reformed confessions, aligning with **Calvin** in a robust doctrine of the Spirit’s instrumental agency through the word. He resists both sacramental minimalism and sacerdotalism, insisting on the **means of grace** as covenantal instruments.

Ethics emerges as the new obedience of a people marked by **freedom in Christ** and **life in the Spirit**. Far from antinomianism, the **indicative-imperative** structure tells. The new creation produces a new moral habitus; yet the imperatives remain grace-anchored, ecclesially mediated, and eschatologically oriented. Ridderbos’s analyses of Romans 12–15 and Galatians 5–6 illustrate how the **fruit of the Spirit** and the **law of Christ** articulate a transformed life that is personal and communal, cruciform and hopeful.

#### 5. Canon, Apostolicity, and the Self-Witness of Scripture
The mid-career treatise **Redemptive History and the New Testament Scriptures** 1955 Dutch, 1963 English, offers his most influential account of **canon and authority**. Ridderbos refuses to ground the canon in a timeless inner witness detached from history, and equally he resists locating authority in postapostolic church decision. Rather, the decisive factor is **apostolicity**: the once-for-all foundation of the **apostolate** as Christ’s commissioned witnesses who, by the Spirit, mediate the normative **kerygma**. The New Testament’s authority rests in this salvation-historical uniqueness, the definitive inbreaking of the new aeon that requires a definitive documentary witness. By speaking of the church receiving the canon to which she is bound, he relates **tradition** and **Scripture** under the primacy of divine action.

In engaging **Bultmann**, **Formgeschichte**, and **Redaktionsgeschichte**, Ridderbos adopts a chastened confidence. He grants that the apostolic message came through recognizable literary forms and editorial aims, yet he rebukes any method that redefines revelation as existential self-understanding. The **self-witness of Scripture** is not circular special pleading but the necessary correlate of **revelation** as God’s speech in history. As the church recognizes the voice of her Lord in the apostolic word, she confesses the Scriptures as the norming norm. The result is a redemptive-historical **bibliology** aligned with the Reformed doctrine of **autopistia**, the self-attesting character of Scripture.

#### 6. The Fourth Gospel: Theological Exegesis and the Witness Motif
In his mature commentary, **The Gospel of John** 1987 Dutch, 1997 English, Ridderbos extends his method into Johannine terrain. He interprets the **signs** as revelatory acts that unveil the **glory** of the incarnate Word and advance the eschatological life. The prologue’s **Logos** is not mere preface but hermeneutical key; the entire narrative is a theology of **revelation and faith** ordered toward the confession that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God. Ridderbos reads **John 20.31** as programmatic: Scripture is written that you may believe and have life. He challenges readings that divorce **Johannine theology** from the concrete history of Jesus, arguing that the evangelist’s theological artistry intensifies rather than replaces history.

The commentary also develops the motif of **witness**. John the Baptist, the works of Jesus, the Father, the Spirit, the Scriptures, and the apostles together form a constellation of testimony culminating in the **Paraclete**. Ridderbos thereby secures the unity of **Christology**, **pneumatology**, and **ecclesiology**. The **Spirit of truth** does not generate new revelation but recalls and interprets Christ’s words for the church’s mission, binding the post-Easter community to the apostolic deposit. The Johannine horizon thus coheres with his broader account of **apostolicity** and **canon**.

#### 7. Dialogue and Debate: Bultmann, Dodd, Cullmann, and Vos
Ridderbos’s career unfolded as a prolonged conversation with modern New Testament theology. With **Rudolf Bultmann**, he shares the insistence on decision and kerygmatic proclamation, yet he rejects **demythologizing** because it subordinates **revelation** to existential categories and severs proclamation from God’s saving acts. With **C. H. Dodd**, he appreciates realized energies of eschatology but insists on the **not yet**. With **Oscar Cullmann**, he embraces **Heilsgeschichte** and the D-Day V-Day analogy, yet he roots the historical stages more explicitly in **covenant** and the two-Adam structure of **Paul**. With **Ernst Käsemann**, he acknowledges apocalyptic invasion but refuses to collapse **justification** into lordship devoid of forensic content. Over against the **Religionsgeschichtliche Schule**, he denies that Christian origins can be explained by religious evolution rather than **revelation** and **apostolic witness**.

A quieter, constructive dialogue links Ridderbos with **Geerhardus Vos**. Both advance a **redemptive-historical** hermeneutic that places **kingdom** and **eschatology** at the heart of biblical theology. Ridderbos differs in temper and emphasis. He writes as an exegete in constant conversation with the German academy and with contemporary hermeneutics, whereas Vos, though conversant, labored more in dogmatic-biblical categories within an American **old Princeton** to **Westminster** trajectory. Together they stabilized Reformed preaching around **history-of-redemption** rather than **moralism** or **religion-of-the-heart** biblicism.

#### 8. Thematic Synthesis: Revelation, Kingdom, Justification, and Church
The genius of Ridderbos is the integration of **revelation**, **kingdom**, **justification**, and **church** under an eschatological horizon. **Revelation** is God’s self-disclosure in and through acts culminating in the advent, cross, resurrection, and exaltation of Christ. The **kingdom of God** is the rule of the risen Lord already operative in the Spirit-filled community, pressing toward the **parousia**. **Justification** is God’s judicial verdict in the present that belongs to the new aeon and is received by faith, disentangling sinners from Adam and relocating them in Christ. The **church** is the visible, sacramented, missionized community whose existence is itself a sign of the kingdom. This synthesis accounts for the texture of New Testament diversity without sacrificing apostolic unity. It also guides hermeneutics: exegesis must locate each text within the saving economy, and dogmatics must give profile to the redemptive-historical order of truth.

Because Ridderbos thinks along the grain of **covenant history**, he resists fragmenting Scripture into diverse theologies. **Paul** and **John** articulate one **kerygma** in diverse idioms. The **Spirit** functions as the down payment of the age to come and as the agent of canonical recollection. Ethics is framed by **indicative-imperative**; sacrament by **means of grace**; mission by **apostolic** mandate. His work equips the church to read canonically and to preach Christ from all Scripture without allegorical haste, keeping both the **already** and the **not yet** in view.

#### 9. Legacy and Reformed/WTS Evaluation
Ridderbos’s influence is deep across global **Reformed** and evangelical communities, especially through translations of **The Coming of the Kingdom** and **Paul: An Outline of His Theology**. In the Netherlands he helped consolidate **redemptive-historical preaching** against pietistic biblicism and liberal historicism. In the anglophone world his Paul became a touchstone for critiques of the **New Perspective**, even if he wrote before it emerged, because his salvation-historical account secures both the **forensic** heart of **justification** and the corporate **union with Christ**. His **canon** book remains a model of how to ground authority in **apostolicity** under **autopistia**, and his **John** commentary shows theological exegesis at work under the confession of the church. He retired in 1978 with a body of work that continues to catechize pastors and scholars into a **kingdom**-centric reading of the New Testament.

A concise Reformed and Westminster evaluation registers strengths and tensions. Strengths include the **Vosian** commitment to **Heilsgeschichte**, the robust defense of **apostolic authority**, the integration of **justification** and **union with Christ**, and the consistent **already/not yet** that guards against both triumphalism and despair. Tensions include the relative thinness of explicit **inerrancy** formulations, given his emphasis on **apostolicity**, which may invite supplementation by a clearer doctrine of **verbal plenary inspiration**; the possibility that salvation-historical framing can overshadow dogmatic loci that require metaphysical clarity, such as **divine simplicity** or **immutability**; and a tendency to understate the sharpness of the **law-gospel** distinction in practical proclamation. Westminster theology would commend his **self-attesting Scripture** and **covenant** horizons while urging firmer articulation of the Scripture’s plenary truthfulness and a more programmatic deployment of **federal theology** to safeguard **imputation** and **sacramental efficacy**. Yet in principle Ridderbos stands as an ally: he shows how a confessional **Reformed** church can meet the modern academy without surrender, preaching the **kingdom** with apostolic clarity and eschatological hope.
