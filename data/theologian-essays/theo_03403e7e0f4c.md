### TITLE
Reading the Tanakh as a single narrative of kingdom: **dominion and dynasty** from creation to Chronicles

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary evangelical** setting shaped by late twentieth-century debates on **canon**, **biblical theology**, and the legacy of **historical criticism**.
- Canonical, literary-theological method privileging the **Tanakh order**, integrating diachronic history with a synchronic **canonical shape**; epistemic stance is confessional yet critically engaged with **Childs** and **Sanders**.
- Key works: **Dominion and Dynasty: A Theology of the Hebrew Bible** (2003; rev. ed. 2017), essays on **canon** and **biblical theology** in edited volumes engaging **canonical interpretation**.
- Central categories: **dominion and dynasty**, **geography and genealogy**, **kingdom of God**, **Tanakh structure** of **Torah–Prophets–Writings**, **Davidic covenant**, **imago Dei**.
- Main interlocutors and influences: **Brevard Childs**, **James A. Sanders**, **Christopher Seitz**, **D. A. Carson**, dialogue with **von Rad** and **Eichrodt** on **Old Testament theology**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong resonance with **Vosian redemptive history** and **covenant theology**, with debates over canon order, **typology**, and the relation of the **Hebrew Bible** to the **New Testament**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Stephen G. Dempster emerged in the late twentieth century within a broad **evangelical** setting deeply engaged in the renewal of **biblical theology** after the era of **historical-critical** dominance. His scholarly milieu was shaped by debates over the **canon** and the theological function of Scripture’s final form, especially between the canonical proposals of **Brevard Childs** and **James A. Sanders**. Dempster’s career, primarily as a teacher of **Hebrew Bible** and **Semitic languages** in Canada, coincided with the consolidation of **theological interpretation of Scripture** and the rise of **canonical interpretation** as a viable alternative to atomized exegesis. He locates himself self-consciously within a confessional yet dialogical stance: Scripture is God’s address that yields **theological unity** through its canonical shape, yet the insights of **philology**, **ancient Near Eastern context**, and **diachronic development** are respected as God’s providential means for that unity. His signature contribution is methodological: the claim that the **Tanakh’s order and architecture** are hermeneutically generative. By reading the Old Testament as a single narrative of **kingdom**, he advances a canonical thesis in which the categories of **dominion and dynasty** organize the diverse corpora of **Torah**, **Prophets**, and **Writings** into a coherent theological drama that runs from creation to the post-exilic retelling in **Chronicles**.

Dempster’s method is not a rejection of **historical criticism** but a reframing of evidential priorities. Following **Childs** on the primacy of the **final form**, he stresses the synchronic literary shape of the **Hebrew Bible** while still acknowledging diachronic textual growth. From **Sanders** he appropriates attentiveness to the **community’s Scripture** and the phenomenon of canonical diversity, yet he resists open-ended pluralism by arguing for a discernible plotline in the **Jewish canon**. This mediating posture yields an epistemic stance that is both theocentric and ecclesial: **Scripture** is read in the *ecclesia credens* as the authoritative witness to **God’s kingdom**, and the order of **Torah–Prophets–Writings** functions not merely as a library shelf but as a theological sequence. His approach is literary without being purely narratological, canonical without ignoring history, and confessional without foreclosing critical dialogue, a balance that commends his work to both **evangelical** and wider **ecumenical** audiences of biblical theologians.

#### 2. Dominion and Dynasty: thesis, scope, and canonical architecture
Dempster’s central thesis is distilled in his major work, **Dominion and Dynasty: A Theology of the Hebrew Bible** (2003; rev. ed. 2017). The book proposes that the Hebrew Scriptures, in their **Tanakh** arrangement, trace a single, unfolding design around two interlocking motifs: **geography** and **genealogy**, or more memorably, **dominion** and **dynasty**. Geography tracks **land**, space, and the scope of **God’s rule**; genealogy tracks **seed**, lineage, and the emergence of a **kingly line**. Beginning with **Genesis 1–2**, he foregrounds the **imago Dei** as a royal-priestly vocation to extend **God’s dominion** across creation. The fall distorts but does not cancel this mandate, and the narrative quickly tightens around **Abraham’s seed** and **land** in **Genesis 12–22**, framing Israel’s destiny as the instrument of restored **kingdom**.

The canonical trajectory then bends toward **kingship**. Dempster reads **Deuteronomy** as the constitutional charter for **covenant** life in the land, including norms for a future **king** in Deuteronomy 17. The **Former Prophets** narrate Israel’s settlement, crisis, and the emergence of **Davidic dynasty** in 2 Samuel 7, where **YHWH’s covenant with David** weds dynasty to dominion. The **Latter Prophets** prosecute covenant breach yet amplify hope for a renewed Davidic ruler whose **righteousness** and **Torah** will re-establish **shalom** in the land. For Dempster, this storyline is not an imposition but arises from the macro-structure of the **Tanakh** that moves from **Torah** foundations, through **Prophetic** commentary, to **Writings** that reflect and reframe Israel’s story after catastrophe.

A hallmark of Dempster’s contribution is his attention to the **placement** and **function** of books within the **Tanakh** corpus. He argues that reading the **Minor Prophets** as a single literary entity, the **Book of the Twelve**, clarifies a trajectory of judgment and hope focused on **Zion**, **David**, and **new creation**. Within the **Writings**, he highlights how **Psalms** opens with **Torah piety** and culminates in royal and messianic songs, how **Ruth** provides a narrative of loyal love that preserves the **Davidic line**, and how **Chronicles** re-narrates Israel to repair memory and re-center hope on **David**, **temple**, and **worship**. The canonical conclusion in **Chronicles**, rather than **Malachi**, places the reader at a liturgically reconstituted yet politically diminished community, waiting for the promised **king** to arise, an ending that both resolves and intensifies the book’s governing tension between **dominion** and **dynasty**.

#### 3. Exegetical engines: geography and genealogy as theological grammar
The engine of Dempster’s biblical-theological synthesis is the tandem of **geography** and **genealogy**. By tracking geography, he follows the way **land** mediates **God’s reign**, from Eden’s garden to the promised land, from exile out of the land to the prophetic vision of a renewed **Zion**. Geography is therefore not neutral space but covenantal arena, the field upon which **YHWH’s kingship** is displayed in history. By tracking genealogy, he attends to the dense web of **toledot** notices in **Genesis**, the complex **tribal lists**, and the high-stakes **royal genealogies** that culminate in **David**. The coupling of these axes supplies what he calls a theological grammar for the **Tanakh**: dominion represents the cosmic horizon of God’s purposes, dynasty represents the elective, particular instrumentality by which those purposes are realized in history.

This grammar reframes familiar texts. In **Genesis 49** and **Numbers 24**, oracles about **Judah** anticipate royal rule; in **1–2 Samuel**, the arc from **Saul** to **David** resolves the pentateuchal ambiguity about kingship; in **Psalms**, the enthronement psalms make explicit the link between creation’s **dominion** and Zion’s **Davidic throne**. The prophets, particularly **Isaiah**, **Jeremiah**, and **Ezekiel**, intensify the tension by insisting that only a righteous **Davidic branch** can reconcile **Torah** with the reality of exile. In the **Writings**, narrative and wisdom materials nuance the picture: **Job** and **Ecclesiastes** caution against reductive retribution theology; **Proverbs** forms the moral agents of the kingdom; **Ruth** and **Chronicles** secure and celebrate the **Davidic line**. Dempster’s reading resists fragmentation by insisting that these corpora, when read within the **Tanakh order**, interact dialogically to sustain a single, kingdom-centered teleology.

#### 4. Canon, closure, and the final shape of Scripture
Dempster’s work stands within the broader discussion of **canon** and **closure**, picking up threads from **Childs** and **Sanders** but arguing more positively for the theological significance of the **Tanakh’s final shape**. He contends that we should read the **Old Testament** first as the **Hebrew Bible**, in the **Torah–Prophets–Writings** arrangement, before correlating it to later **Christian canon** structures. This does not minimize Christian reception but honors the original **canonical environment** from which the **New Testament** authors themselves drew. By letting **Chronicles** close the canon, the reader hears a final summons to return to **Torah**, rebuild **temple**, and hope for **Davidic restoration**, rather than a mere moralistic coda.

In defending the significance of order, Dempster interacts critically with scholars who claim that **book order** is historically fluid and thus theologically inconsequential. He concedes pluralities in ancient arrangements yet argues that the tripartite **Tanakh** is sufficiently ancient and theologically thick to carry hermeneutical weight. He aligns here with **Christopher Seitz** on the integrity of the **Prophets** as a literary body and with discussions of the **Book of the Twelve** as a compositional unity. The result is a hermeneutic that recognizably privileges **canonical sequence** as part of Scripture’s meaning, not merely as editorial accident. This is not a naive harmonization but a disciplined attention to how the final form invites the reader to trace **promise**, **failure**, **judgment**, and **hope** along the spine of **dominion and dynasty**.

#### 5. Creation, kingship, and the Davidic covenant in theological development
A distinctive feature of Dempster’s biblical theology is the way **creation** and **kingship** are braided from the outset. The **imago Dei** is construed as a royal vocation to steward and extend **God’s rule**. When this vocation is localized in **Abraham** and then institutionalized in **David**, the **Davidic covenant** in 2 Samuel 7 is not an episodic spike but the structural keystone of the **Tanakh**. For Dempster, the temple, as the microcosm of creation, links **dominion** and **dynasty**: the king mediates divine rule under **Torah**, and the temple images cosmic order under the king’s patronage. The collapse of this order through idolatry and injustice explains the **exile**, which becomes not simply political displacement but the unraveling of creation’s order in Israel’s life.

The prophets then revisit creation themes to forecast restoration. **Isaiah** announces a **new heavens and new earth** under the reign of the **servant** and **Davidic shoot**; **Ezekiel** envisions a new temple and a renewed **Davidic prince**; **Jeremiah** promises a **new covenant** that secures the inner obedience that the **Torah** demanded. In the **Writings**, the re-narration in **Chronicles** compresses Israel’s narrative to foreground **David** and **temple** because they epitomize creation ordered under God’s king. The closing edict of **Cyrus** is thus not anticlimax but cliffhanger, inviting readers to await the return of **YHWH’s dominion** under a truly righteous **Davidic dynasty**. Dempster’s point is that the theology of **kingdom** is not an imposed rubric but a pattern generated by these canonical interlocks.

#### 6. Christological horizon and the Old Testament’s forward pressure
Although Dempster’s primary field is the **Hebrew Bible**, he consistently insists that the **Tanakh** exerts forward pressure toward the **New Testament**. The motifs of **geography** and **genealogy** find a natural continuation in the **Gospels**: **Matthew 1** opens with a conspicuous **genealogy** structured around **Abraham** and **David**, and **Jesus** inaugurates the **kingdom of God** in continuity with the hopes of **Isaiah** and **Psalms**. For Dempster the **Messiah** is not a foreign body in Israel’s Scriptures but the telos of **dominion and dynasty**. The **land** promise expands eschatologically in **Jesus’** Beatitudes and in the mission of **Acts**, where the scope of **dominion** extends to the ends of the earth, and the **seed** promise is fulfilled in the crucified and risen **Son of David** who gathers a renewed people.

This Christological horizon does not erase the **Tanakh**’s integrity; it honors it. Dempster argues that **Christian canon** reception should not collapse **Torah–Prophets–Writings** into a mere prologue to **Gospel** faith but should discern how **Christ** fulfills without eclipsing. That is, the **New Testament** confirms the narrative arc grounded in **creation**, **covenant**, **Davidic kingship**, and **temple**, while transposing them into a broader **new creation** horizon. The **church** thus reads the **Old Testament** not as detached oracles but as a single, kingdom-shaped witness whose narrative necessities press toward **Incarnation**, **Cross**, and **Resurrection**. Dempster’s articulation here makes common cause with **evangelical** commitments to the unity of Scripture and with the theological instincts of **redemptive-historical** traditions.

#### 7. Dialogue partners, debates, and scholarly reception
Dempster’s proposals engage a range of interlocutors in **canonical** and **biblical-theological** studies. With **Childs** he shares the conviction that canonical form is theologically determinative, though he is more explicit about the **Tanakh** sequence and about the kingdom storyline as the canon’s plot. With **James Sanders** he converses about the role of the **faith community** in shaping canon, yet he resists canon-as-process relativism by insisting on a coherent **final shape**. He converses implicitly with **Gerhard von Rad** and **Walther Eichrodt** on the search for a center of **Old Testament theology**, proposing **kingdom** as a generative center that can embrace both covenantal structures and wisdom’s plural voices. In dialogue with **Christopher Seitz**, he upholds the integrity of the **Prophets** and the cohesion of the **Book of the Twelve**, and with **D. A. Carson** as series editor, he positions his work within a broader **evangelical** renaissance in **biblical theology**.

Critical pushback has focused on the weight he places on **canon order** and the risk of minimizing alternative ancient sequences, including **Septuagintal** arrangements that influenced the **Christian Old Testament**. Others worry that his **dominion and dynasty** schema, while powerful, may occlude themes such as **priesthood**, **wisdom’s creation theology**, or the suffering of the **righteous** in **Job** and **Psalms** apart from royal frames. Dempster responds by showing how **priestly** and **sapiential** materials are not sidelined but braided into the kingdom plot, even if they retain their own tonalities. He is also alert to the **Second Temple** period’s diversity, yet he maintains that the canonical consonances he traces are robust enough to bear theological interpretation without erasing historical complexity.

#### 8. Contributions, refinements, and pedagogical impact
Beyond **Dominion and Dynasty**, Dempster has contributed numerous essays and chapters elaborating a **canonical reading** of the **Old Testament**, clarifying how **book placement**, **intertextual seams**, and **genealogical markers** function in theological interpretation. His work in collections on **canon and biblical interpretation** underscores the practical hermeneutical payoff of reading the **Tanakh** as a structured whole. He has also been a consistent advocate for retrieving the **Jewish canon** as the Old Testament’s primary literary frame even for Christian readers, followed by a disciplined exercise of **typology** and **fulfillment**. As a teacher, he has trained students to read **Hebrew** texts with literary sensitivity and to align exegesis with the **church’s confession** that Scripture possesses a divinely intended unity.

The revised edition of **Dominion and Dynasty** in 2017 refined several arguments, including expanded attention to **genealogical** trajectories into the **New Testament** and clearer articulation of how **new creation** reframes the **land** promise without spiritualizing it away. These refinements reflect Dempster’s ongoing engagement with scholarship and his commitment to a **kingdom**-centered reading that stays close to the syntax of the **Tanakh**. His influence is visible in evangelical seminaries and universities where the canonical storyline of **creation**, **covenant**, **kingdom**, and **new creation** structures curricula and preaching, aligning with the instincts of **G. K. Beale**, **Graeme Goldsworthy**, and others who champion Scripture’s unified, Christ-directed narrative.

#### 9. Theological horizon and constructive potential
Theologically, Dempster offers a hermeneutic that reorganizes doctrinal loci around **kingdom** as the Old Testament’s architectonic theme. **Theology proper** engages **kingship** and **divine rule**; **anthropology** revisits the **imago Dei** as royal vocation; **soteriology** becomes participation in the restored dominion under the **Davidic Messiah**; **ecclesiology** is the reconstituted people whose identity is shaped by **Torah** fulfilled in **Christ**; **eschatology** becomes the consummation of **new creation** and **universal dominion**. This canonical-theological frame also aids in mapping the intertestamental development: **Second Temple** hopes for **Davidic** leadership, **temple** restoration, and **Torah** fidelity are not mere political yearnings but theological impulses rooted in the **Tanakh’s** own architecture.

Constructively, Dempster’s grammar of **geography** and **genealogy** provides a bridge for conversations across disciplines. In missiology, it grounds a theology of place and peoples; in ethics, it names the creaturely goods of land, kin, and polity under **God’s rule**; in liturgy, it re-centers worship as the enactment of life under **YHWH** and his **anointed**. His approach also offers a chastened universalism in which the particular **seed** of **Abraham** is the instrument of blessing for the nations, addressing concerns about election and inclusion with a canonical narrative rather than abstract speculation. In these ways, Dempster advances a mode of **biblical theology** that is both textually demanded and ecclesially fruitful.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **Westminster Theological Seminary** perspective, Dempster’s work aligns significantly with **Geerhardus Vos’s** **redemptive-historical** method, especially in his emphasis on the unity of **revelation** and the teleological movement toward **Christ** as the fulfillment of **kingdom** promises. His palace-temple-covenant synthesis resonates with classic **covenant theology**, particularly the **covenant of grace** unfolding through **Abraham** and **David** toward the **new covenant**. He helpfully intensifies the canonical center on **kingdom** without eroding the forensic dimensions dear to **Reformed soteriology**. Yet some tensions appear. First, his strong privileging of the **Tanakh** order, while exegetically suggestive, may over-determine canonical meaning in ways that sit uneasily with the **Christian canon’s** traditional ordering and with the catholic instinct to read Scripture in the church’s received form. Second, his **dominion and dynasty** frame could be complemented by a clearer doctrinal account of the prelapsarian **covenant of works** and the legal-cultic structures that inform **atonement** and **justification**, categories that **Reformed orthodoxy** treats as architectonic. Third, in light of recent intramural debates about **Christotelic** interpretation at **WTS**, Dempster’s approach will be welcomed for its **Christological teleology**, though Reformed readers will press for explicit controls that guard against either allegorical overreach or under-realized typology. The constructive alternative is not a rejection but an integration: maintain Dempster’s **Tanakh-shaped kingdom** plot while embedding it within the classic **Reformed** covenants, ensuring that **revelation**, **covenant**, **grace**, and **history** coinhere in a canonical reading that moves confidently from **creation** through **Abraham** and **David** to **Christ** and **new creation**.
