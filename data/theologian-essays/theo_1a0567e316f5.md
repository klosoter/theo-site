### TITLE
Scholastic exegesis in service of Reformed doctrine: spiritual presence, Augustinian predestination, and Aristotelian method.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Italian humanism**, **Augustinian monasticism**, and **exilic reform** from Florence to Strasbourg, Oxford, and Zurich set Vermigli within the **magisterial Reformation**.
- A **scholastic-exegetical method** that weds **Scripture as principium** to **Aristotelian** tools and **patristic consensus**, producing early **Reformed scholasticism**.
- Key works: **Tractatio de sacramento eucharistiae** 1549, **In Epistolam ad Romanos Commentarii** 1558, **In Librum Iudicum Commentarii** 1564 posthumous, and the posthumous **Loci Communes** 1576.
- Central terms: **spiritual real presence**, **communicatio idiomatum**, **extra Calvinisticum**, **double predestination**, **primary and secondary causality**, **natural law**, and **equity of the Mosaic judicials**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Augustine**, **Thomas Aquinas**, **Aristotle**, **Zwingli**, **Bucer**, **Calvin**, and opponents from **Roman Catholic** and **Lutheran ubiquitarian** camps.
- Reformed reception: bridge figure toward **Zurich and Heidelberg** confessional piety and early **Reformed scholasticism**, often labeled a **Reformed Thomist**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, exile, and method
Born in 1499 in Florence, **Peter Martyr Vermigli** rose within the **Augustinian Canons** as prior of **San Frediano in Lucca**, trained in **Aristotelian** logic and ethics at Padua, and steeped in **Augustine** and **Thomas Aquinas**. Under the Italian **evangelical** ferment associated with **Juan de Valdes** and humanist reform circles, his preaching and lecturing oriented toward **Scripture** and **patristic** renewal drew scrutiny from the Inquisition. He fled north in 1542, moving through **Zurich** and **Strasbourg**, and in 1547 accepted **Thomas Cranmer**s invitation to Oxford as Regius Professor of Divinity. His career traces the geography of the **magisterial Reformation**, but the intellectual map is older: a union of **Augustinian soteriology**, **Thomistic metaphysics**, and **Aristotelian** causal analysis, all under the normative authority of **biblical exegesis**. Vermigli became a canonical voice in shaping a Reformed style that was scholastic in method and evangelical in content, a forerunner of later **Reformed orthodoxy**.

Vermigli’s method is best characterized as **scholastic exegesis**. The principium of theology is **Scripture**, read within the **fathers** and measured by the **ecumenical councils**, especially **Chalcedon** for Christology and **Nicaea** for trinitarian confession, while deploying **Aristotelian four causes** and **Aquinas**s doctrine of **primary and secondary causality**. He demurs from the medieval habitus framework when it encroaches on **justification sola fide**, yet he appropriates the analytic clarity of **scholastic disputation**. This method explains the shape of his corpus: exegetical commentaries that generate **loci communes**, a pattern ultimately consolidated in the posthumous **Loci Communes** 1576. The result is a coherent **Reformed Thomism** that honors **Augustinian grace** and **Calvin’s** soteriology while structuring doctrine with classical metaphysics.

#### 2. Major works and the ecosystem of loci
Vermigli’s earliest English-period writing centers on the **Eucharistic controversy**. The **Tractatio de sacramento eucharistiae** 1549 crystallizes his Oxford disputations against conservative doctors supporting **transubstantiation** and **local corporeal presence**. A decade later, the **Commentary on Romans** 1558 provides a full-scale exposition of **Augustinian predestination**, **justification by faith**, and the nature of **free will** under grace, while the **Commentary on Judges** 1564 engages **magistracy**, **tyranny**, and **natural law** in the arena of political theology. After his death in Zurich in 1562, colleagues collated doctrinal units from his commentaries, disputations, and letters into the **Loci Communes** 1576, an index of topics where **sacramental theology**, **providence**, **Christology**, **law and gospel**, and **ecclesiology** are argued with both biblical density and scholastic rigor.

This ecosystem of loci was not an afterthought. Vermigli’s lectures were deliberately architected so that exegesis yields doctrinal statements framed by **causal analysis**, **definitiones**, and **distinctiones**. On **sacraments**, for example, he recovers **Augustine’s signum et res** and **sursum corda** piety while plotting the relation of sign and thing under the **instrumental causality** typical of **Aquinas**, albeit reinterpreted to avoid the machinery of **infused habits** in justification. In the loci on **providence**, he presses the distinction between **primary cause** and **secondary causes** to reconcile exhaustive divine rule with creaturely operations. This habit of exegetical generation and scholastic consolidation is the hallmark of Vermigli’s method and the seedbed for later **Reformed scholasticism** in Zurich and Heidelberg.

#### 3. Providence, predestination, and freedom
The **Commentary on Romans** 1558 displays Vermigli’s most durable synthesis on **providence** and **predestination**. Reading **Romans 9–11**, he fortifies a robust **double predestination** in an **Augustinian** register, affirming both election to life and reprobation, though the latter is described as **preterition** and **just condemnation** rather than a symmetrical positive decree. He rejects **Pelagian** and **Semi-Pelagian** construals of grace, insists upon the **bondage of the will** after the fall, and defends a **compatibilist** account of human acting under God’s universal causality. Here Vermigli leans heavily on **Aquinas**: God is the **first cause** who moves secondary causes without violating the mode of the moved; divine providence is meticulous, yet it orders means to ends in such a way that human deliberation and civil prudence are not destroyed but established.

To dissolve the appearance of fatalism, Vermigli draws on **Boethius’s** account of divine eternity and distinguishes the **necessitas consequentiae** from the **necessitas consequentis**, preserving contingency at the level of secondary causes within the certainty of the decree. He rejects any **scientia media** construction and denies that future contingents ground divine knowledge; rather, the **divine decree** grounds the truth of all propositions about future events. In this respect, Vermigli exemplifies the classical **Reformed** commitment to God’s **aseity** and **impassibility**, while retaining a pastoral sensibility: predestination is for the consolation of the church, anchored in **Christ**, not a speculative labyrinth. His position anticipates the **Westminster Confession** on **providence** and the liberty of second causes, demonstrating how **Augustine** and **Aquinas** can be made to serve **evangelical** certainty.

#### 4. Justification and grace
On **justification**, Vermigli stands decisively with the **evangelical** consensus: sinners are declared righteous by the **imputed righteousness of Christ**, received **sola fide**, apart from works. He criticizes the **Council of Trent** 1547 on the **habitual infusion** of grace as the formal cause of justification and denies any **meritum de condigno**. Yet he does not abandon the older moral psychology; rather, he redistributes it. **Infused grace** has its place in **sanctification** and renewal by the **Spirit**, while justification is a forensic act grounded in **union with Christ**. The distinction between **imputation** and **inherent righteousness** secures assurance while sustaining the call to holiness.

Vermigli’s scholastic training enables precise articulation: **faith** is the instrumental cause of justification, **Christ’s obedience** the meritorious cause, **God’s mercy** the efficient cause, and the **glory of God** the final cause. He refines **Aquinas**s causal schema while upending the medieval ordo that tied justifying grace to a moral habit. And because his **Romans** exegesis embeds the doctrine within **predestination**, Vermigli stresses the priority of **grace** over the will without lapsing into quietism. The synergy of his **Augustinian** soteriology and **Aristotelian** analysis produces clarity of responsibility and rest in **Christ**.

#### 5. Eucharistic presence and sacramental realism
Vermigli’s mature theology flowered in the **Eucharistic** debates at Oxford and beyond. In the **Tractatio de sacramento eucharistiae** 1549, he denies **transubstantiation** and the **local corporeal presence** of Christ’s body under the accidents, arguing instead for a **spiritual real presence**. The risen Christ according to his humanity is **in heaven** at the right hand of the Father, and believers truly feed upon his body and blood by the **Spirit**, through **faith**, and in the dynamic ascent signaled by the **sursum corda**. His case draws from **John 6**, **1 Corinthians 10–11**, **Augustine’s** sacramental logic, and the **Chalcedonian** insistence on the integrity of Christ’s natures.

Against **Lutheran ubiquitarianism** associated with **Brenz** and others, Vermigli rejects the ascription of divine omnipresence to Christ’s human nature, invoking the axiom **finitum non capax infiniti** and the **extra Calvinisticum** to protect the **communicatio idiomatum** within the limits of the two-natures union. Christ is fully present to faith by the **Spirit** without collapsing the distinction between **signum** and **res**. The bread and wine are true **instruments** through which the Spirit effects communion with Christ, but they are not converted in substance. Vermigli’s sacramental theology thereby secures **real participation** without localizing Christ’s body on earth, aligning with **Calvin** while employing **Thomistic** instrumentality to clarify causation.

#### 6. Christology and the communicatio idiomatum
Behind the Eucharistic dispute stands Vermigli’s **Christology**. He fully embraces **Chalcedon’s** *inconfuse, immutabiliter, indivise, inseparabiliter*, and he explains the **communicatio idiomatum** as the predication of properties of either nature to the one **hypostasis** of the Son. He denies the **genus maiestaticum** asserted by some **Lutherans**, which would communicate divine attributes like omnipresence to the humanity as such. The **extra Calvinisticum**, that the Logos subsists beyond or extra the assumed flesh while being personally united to it, functions not as a dualistic wedge but as a guardrail against a **monophysite** drift.

Vermigli’s Christological grammar stabilizes his Eucharistic stance: Christ’s body is true, finite, and circumscribed by its human mode of presence; his divine person is not bound by those limits. Thus the church’s feeding is **mystical union** by the **Spirit**, not a topographical distribution of Christ’s flesh. By tethers to **Athanasius** and **Cyril** on the unity of the person, and to **Leo’s Tome** on the integrity of the natures, he demonstrates a catholic Reformed Christology that answers both **Roman** transubstantiation and **Lutheran** ubiquity with the same Chalcedonian discipline.

#### 7. Law, polity, and natural law
The **Commentary on Judges** 1564 advances Vermigli’s political theology. He articulates **natural law** in continuity with **Aquinas** but normed by **Scripture**, commending **magistracy** as a divine ordinance and treating **tyranny** with a doctrine of resistance through **lesser magistrates**. The **Mosaic judicial laws** are not binding per se on Christian polities, yet their **equity** remains instructive. Here again, the distinction of **primary** and **secondary causes** lets him affirm divine rule while upholding civic prudence, counsel, and deliberation as real means appointed by God.

Vermigli’s political reflections also show his method’s versatility. Drawing on **Aristotle’s Politics**, **Roman law**, and **patristic** sources, he frames Christian prudence under the **twofold government** of God by Word and providence. Ecclesiastically, he supports **presbyterial** order with magistratical support, a hallmark of **magisterial Reformed** polity. His contributions influenced **English** reform through counsel to **Cranmer** and later furnished conceptual resources for **Zurich** and **Heidelberg** on civil righteousness, public worship, and catechesis, where **natural law** and **equity** assist practical judgment under the supremacy of **Scripture**.

#### 8. Scripture, scholastic architecture, and the Loci
Vermigli’s lifelong craft was to turn **biblical exegesis** into **doctrinal clarity**. His lecture style worked from the text through **philology**, **context**, and **patristic** echoes, then out to **disputationes** on doctrinal topics. The posthumous **Loci Communes** 1576 is the natural harvest of this approach, collecting material on **God**, **creation**, **providence**, **Christ**, **sacraments**, **church**, and **last things** into a manual that reads like an early **Reformed Summa**. While less architectonic than **Aquinas’s Summa Theologiae**, its strength is its exegetical density and its habit of clarifying doctrine with the categories of **cause**, **form**, **matter**, and **end**.

This scholastic architecture gives Vermigli a durable voice in shaping **Reformed orthodoxy**. Later theologians such as **Girolamo Zanchi**, **Franciscus Junius**, and **Zacharias Ursinus** drew from his loci, especially on **sacraments**, **providence**, and **law**. His influence can be felt in the **Heidelberg Catechism** on the **Lord’s Supper** and in the Zurich school’s precise **Christology**. In England, his Oxford tenure informed **Cranmer’s** 1552 **Book of Common Prayer** and the **Forty-Two Articles**, with Vermigli’s **spiritual real presence** and rejection of **transubstantiation** leaving indelible marks.

#### 9. Interlocutors, debates, and reception
Vermigli’s dialogue partners ranged widely. He reverenced **Augustine** as the master of grace, mined **Aquinas** for metaphysical clarity, and worked alongside **Bucer** and **Calvin** in exegetical renewal. In debate he engaged **Roman Catholic** defenders of the **Mass**, anatomizing **Aristotelian substance-accident** arguments for **transubstantiation** and exposing their exegetical deficit. With **Lutherans** he disputed the **ubiquity** of Christ’s human nature and resisted reductions of **communion** to mouth and stomach. He refused **Melanchthonian** concessions on synergism after the **Interim**, holding the **Augustinian** line on **grace** and **free will** in terms that would later be ratified by **Reformed confessions**.

Reception fixed Vermigli as a **bridge-builder**: to **Anglican** formularies through BCP 1552 and the Articles, to **Zurich** and **Heidelberg** through his students and correspondents, and to emerging **Reformed scholasticism** through his method. Posthumously, his **Loci Communes** served as a schoolbook on the continent, while his **Commentary on Romans** remained a touchstone for debates on **predestination** and **assurance**. He dies in 1562 in Zurich, but his cross-confessional import ensures a voice in the seventeenth-century **Reformed orthodox** codifications and beyond.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and contemporary **WTS** vantage, Vermigli’s strengths are considerable. His affirmation of **divine simplicity**, **aseity**, and **providence** by way of **primary and secondary causality** supplies a metaphysical depth often missing in Reformation polemics. His **spiritual real presence**, disciplined by **Chalcedon** and the **extra Calvinisticum**, secures robust communion with **Christ** without violating the integrity of the **two natures**. His **justification sola fide**, with **imputation** central and **infused grace** relocated to **sanctification**, aligns with the marrow of **Reformed confessionalism**. Potential tensions cluster around the use of **Aristotelian** categories and the **nature-grace** relationship. Vermigli sometimes speaks in a register that could be read as preserving a thicker **natural law** platform alongside **special revelation**, which, if abstracted from covenantal history, risks a dualism the later **covenant theology** would correct by integrating **creation** and **redemption** under **federal** categories. A WTS reading would commend his principled subordination of **philosophy** to **Scripture**, reinforce his **Augustinian** insistence on grace’s primacy, and press further the **covenantal** unities that later culminate in the **Westminster Standards** on law, sacraments, and the decrees. In sum, Vermigli’s fusion of **scholastic clarity** and **evangelical substance** renders him a model for confessional theology that is at once deeply classical and thoroughly Reformed.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Italian Augustinian** formed by **Aristotle**, **Aquinas**, and **Augustine**, exiled to **Strasbourg**, **Oxford**, and **Zurich** within the **magisterial Reformation**.
- Core problem: integrate **biblical exegesis** with **scholastic method** to articulate **predestination**, **justification**, and **sacramental presence**.
- Major works: **Tractatio de sacramento eucharistiae** 1549, **Commentary on Romans** 1558, **Commentary on Judges** 1564, and the posthumous **Loci Communes** 1576.
- Distinctives: **spiritual real presence** governed by **Chalcedon** and the **extra Calvinisticum**, **double predestination** with **primary-secondary causality**, **sola fide** with **imputation**, and **natural law** with the **equity** of Mosaic judicials.
- Interlocutors: alignment with **Augustine**, appropriation of **Aquinas**, cooperation with **Bucer** and **Calvin**, and disputes with **Roman transubstantiation** and **Lutheran ubiquity**.
- Reformed critique/alignment: strong alignment on **providence**, **Christology**, and **justification**; caution about the **nature-grace** register and a call for fuller **federal theology** later supplied by **Westminster**.
- Legacy: catalyst for **Reformed scholasticism**, influence on **Cranmer**, **Zurich**, and **Heidelberg**, and enduring template for confessional theology marked by **scholastic exegesis**.
