### TITLE
Confidently Christian, Confidently Pluralist: Neo-Calvinist public theology for hospitality, work, and institutional life under the lordship of Christ.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Matthew Kaemingk emerges as a contemporary **Neo-Calvinist** ethicist whose central theological impulse is to render a **public theology** that is both confessionally Christian and institutionally generous. His work is situated in the civic tremors after 9-11, European and North American anxieties over **Muslim immigration**, and the workplace dislocations of late capitalism. In that environment he retrieves **Abraham Kuyper s** grammar of **common grace**, **antithesis**, and **sphere sovereignty** to articulate a way of being faithfully Reformed and structurally pluralist. Methodologically he is a synthesizer of **systematic theology**, **political theory**, and **ethnographic case work**, but with a clear center of gravity in the **Kuyper-Bavinck** dogmatic legacy. By the late 2010s he taught at **Fuller Theological Seminary** and directed the **Richard John Mouw Institute of Faith and Public Life**, linking his scholarship to pastoral formation and ecclesial practice. He self-consciously resists the fiction of **secular neutrality** and the allure of **Christian nationalism**, seeking a mode of **principled pluralism** that holds confessional particularity alongside legal and institutional protections for religious difference.

Influenced by **Richard J. Mouw s** generous orthodoxy, **James K. A. Smith s** liturgical anthropology, and **Lesslie Newbigin s** missional ecclesiology, Kaemingk positions **Neo-Calvinism** as a catholic resource for the public square. Interlocutors include **Rawls** and **Habermas** on public reason, **Charles Taylor** on secularity, **John Inazu** on **confident pluralism**, and **Oliver O Donnovan** on moral-political judgment. He retrieves **Herman Bavinck s** account of creation s goodness and sin s pervasiveness to guard both **common grace** cooperation and **antithesis** in ultimate allegiance. Theologically he moves by thick concepts rather than thin proceduralism, aiming to show that **Christ s lordship over every sphere** can ground a hospitable and just political ethic. His writing cadence is pastoral without losing conceptual rigor, repeatedly joining **doctrine**, **discipleship**, and **public institutions**.

#### 2. Christian hospitality and principled pluralism
Kaemingk s signature statement is **Christian Hospitality and Muslim Immigration in an Age of Fear** 2018. The book stages a three-way debate between **assimilationist secularism** that would privatize or suppress Islam, **multicultural relativism** that refuses principled judgments, and a **Kuyperian principled pluralism** that secures robust institutional space for confessional communities while maintaining Christian witness. Drawing from Dutch experiences with **pillarization** and the struggles over **mosque construction** and **Islamic schools**, he documents how a principled architecture of **institutional religious freedom** and **public funding parity** can serve both **public justice** and neighbor love. The argument is explicitly theological. **Common grace** explains why Christians can affirm Muslim contributions to education, welfare, and civic virtue. **Antithesis** explains why Christians must also witness to the unique lordship of **Jesus Christ** and the irreducible claims of the **gospel**.

Key to the project is the notion that **hospitality** is not sentiment but a political virtue rooted in the **Trinitarian economy**. The **church** bears a witness that is neither **Constantinian** control nor **sectarian** withdrawal. Kaemingk reframes hospitality as structurally encoded in law and institution rather than confined to private charity. He advances a legal-moral vision of **public justice** that honors Muslim communal life while preserving Christian freedom to evangelize, catechize, and critique. The result is a tone of **confident pluralism** that rejects a **theology of fear** associated with clash-of-civilizations rhetoric and that exposes the myth of **secular neutrality** as a covert establishment of unbelief. By leaning on **Kuyper s** **sphere sovereignty**, he locates religious communities, schools, and charities as norm-bearing institutions with inherent rights under God.

#### 3. Antithesis, cooperation, and the secular democratic frame
In his political-ethical development Kaemingk calibrates **common grace** and **antithesis** as twin poles for Christian engagement. **Common grace** names God s preserving and civil gifts that enable shared projects of **public justice** and social cooperation. **Antithesis** names the ultimate spiritual divide between worship of the **triune God** and idolatry. He refuses to collapse either pole, arguing that **public friendship** and **prophetic critique** must coinhere if Christian citizens are to avoid both assimilation and antagonism. Against **liberal proceduralism**, he denies that public reason can abstract from deep convictions. Against **integralist** temptations, he denies that confessional hegemony is compatible with a dispersed and creaturely authority that **sphere sovereignty** assigns to distinct institutions.

Kaemingk interacts sympathetically and critically with **John Inazu s** legal concept of **confident pluralism**, pressing beyond procedural coexistence to a theological account that can explain why Christians should defend the civil rights of religious others. He enlists **Charles Taylor** to reinterpret secularity as a contested social imaginary rather than a neutral settlement. He also engages **Rawls** and **Habermas** to argue that reciprocity and translation are real but limited tools. The ongoing claim is that **principled pluralism** does not dilute **Christ s lordship** but directs it toward the creation-order affirmation of multiple authorities under God. This intrinsically **Kuyperian** move underwrites policies of **institutional religious freedom**, equitable access to state benefits, and legal protections that keep the democratic field open to robustly religious argument and organization.

#### 4. Work, worship, and liturgical discipleship
The second axis of Kaemingk s contribution is **Work and Worship: Reconnecting Our Labor and Liturgy** 2020, coauthored with **Cory B. Willson**. The thesis is concise: the Sunday assembly must intentionally disciple God s people for Monday s labor. Drawing on **James K. A. Smith s** accounts of **cultural liturgies** and on **Alexander Schmemann s** sacramental imagination, they argue that the church s practices of **confession**, **lament**, **intercession**, and **Eucharistic sending** are the Spirit s means of reshaping workers amid the powers of economic idolatry. The authors offer concrete practices such as industry-specific intercessions, testimonies from workers, rites of commissioning and blessing, and congregational lament over workplace injustice. The goal is not romanticizing labor but forming workers to resist **consumerism**, **careerism**, and unjust **market structures** through liturgical counterformation.

Theologically, the book advances a **Reformed sacramental theology** attuned to vocation. The idea of **missio Dei** grounds a sent ecclesiology in which worship presupposes and empowers dispersed service in fields as diverse as finance, healthcare, and homemaking. The authors engage **Miroslav Volf s** theology of charisms in relation to work, and deploy **Lesslie Newbigin s** vision of mission as a public truth to challenge privatized piety. They implicitly retrieve **Herman Bavinck s** doctrine of creation s integrity and **Dooyeweerd s** distinction between **structure** and **direction**, recognizing stable creational norms that guide craft and commerce and also directing those norms toward the kingdom of God. In short, **liturgical theology**, **vocation**, and **public ethics** are bound together for the church s common life.

#### 5. Globalizing the Kuyperian imagination
With **Reformed Public Theology: A Global Vision for Life in the World** 2021, Kaemingk curates a chorus of voices that extend **Neo-Calvinist** insights across continents and issues. As editor, he convenes theologians and practitioners to test **principled pluralism** and **sphere sovereignty** in contexts such as education policy, migration, policing, and healthcare. The volume embodies a central conviction of the Kuyperian tradition, that the Reformed faith is not a tribal enclave but a catholic vision for the **life of the world** under **Christ s universal lordship**. Contributors draw from **Kuyper**, **Bavinck**, and **Dooyeweerd** while engaging contemporary debates around **human rights**, **religious freedom**, and **economic justice**.

The editorial agenda is doctrinally explicit. **Creation grace** and **redemption** are not conflated, yet they are integrated in an ethic of **public justice**. **Institutional religious freedom** is defended not as a liberal concession but as a theological recognition of differentiated authority within creation. Case studies display how **sphere sovereignty** dignifies families, schools, charities, congregations, corporations, and states as distinct bearers of responsibility before God. The book thus situates **Reformed public theology** within a global conversation, inviting comparisons with **Catholic subsidiarity**, **Anglican public theology**, and Protestant **Two-Kingdoms** proposals. The result is not uniformity but a shared lexicon of **common grace**, **antithesis**, **pluralism**, and **public justice**.

#### 6. Doctrinal synthesis: creation order, mission, and political discipleship
Across these projects Kaemingk s doctrinal synthesis is consistent. His anthropology is Augustinian and **Reformed**, locating human work within the **imago Dei**, distorted by sin yet renewed in Christ for service. His ecclesiology is missional without erasing the church s ontological distinction as the **people of God** gathered by Word and sacrament. His political theology rejects **secular neutrality** as a metaphysical fiction and refuses **theocratic** consolidation as a denial of created differentiation. The constructive centerpiece is **sphere sovereignty**, which he renders as a christologically secured order in which diverse institutions possess God-given teloi and competencies that the state must recognize and protect. This frame grounds **institutional religious freedom** and invites Christians to seek structural goods for neighbors quite apart from shared soteriological commitments.

Kaemingk deploys **common grace** to make intelligible cooperative civic projects with Muslims, secular humanists, and others. He also keeps **antithesis** alive, insisting that cooperation cannot mute proclamation of the **gospel** or confuse the claims of **Christ s kingdom** with the penultimate aims of political life. His ethics is thus a dialectic of public generosity and confessional clarity. He leans toward a **natural law**-adjacent appeal to creational norms when speaking of work and institutional design, yet he stresses that reliable access to those norms is clarified by **special revelation** and ecclesial catechesis. This balance corresponds to **Dooyeweerd s** structure-direction grammar while remaining pastorally attentive to the pressures of **late capitalism** and pluralist democracies.

#### 7. Case engagement and prudential judgment
One notable feature of Kaemingk s method is his disciplined attention to cases. In the immigration book he considers concrete controversies over **mosque construction**, **religious dress**, and **education policy**. He argues that prudence is not a secular technique but a virtue of **wisdom** rooted in the fear of the Lord. The prudential task is to translate **common grace** and **sphere sovereignty** into specific legislative and institutional designs that secure **public justice** without illegitimately privileging or penalizing confessional communities. In the worship-work project he moves from doctrine to liturgical micro-practices, showing how the **church s liturgy** can publicly name and confront **economic idols** and bless craft excellence.

Engagement with non-Christian thinkers is similarly prudential. With **Habermas**, he acknowledges the need for discursive translation in plural publics while resisting the subordination of theology to supposed neutral speech. With **Rawls**, he appreciates the intention of reciprocity while rejecting a bracketing of **ultimate commitments**. With **Taylor**, he welcomes a more realistic description of a **secular age** as cross-pressured rather than meaninglessly neutral. These encounters are neither capitulation nor polemic. They serve his constructive aim to secure space for **robustly religious institutions** within the democratic frame while equipping churches for **mission** in workplaces and neighborhoods.

#### 8. Tone, pedagogy, and ecclesial uptake
Kaemingk writes as an ethicist for pastors, educators, and lay leaders who are navigating the public square. The tone across his work is both confessional and hospitable, a blend reflective of **Richard J. Mouw s** irenic Calvinism and **James K. A. Smith s** emphasis on formative practices. He is alert to **Islamophobia**, to **nativist nationalism**, and to secular strategies that privatize faith. He is equally alert to Christian temptations toward **domination** or **withdrawal**. The pedagogical aim is to equip the **church** with conceptual tools such as **principled pluralism**, **sphere sovereignty**, and **vocational liturgy** that can be taught, preached, and embedded in congregational rhythms.

Ecclesial uptake has been significant among Evangelicals and Reformed communities who are seeking alternatives to **culture war** binaries. The immigration book has been used to reset conversations around **religious freedom** and neighbor love. The worship-work book has catalyzed experiments in **liturgical commissioning**, testimony, and prayers for specific industries. The edited volume has extended the reach of **Reformed public theology** beyond North America and the Low Countries, demonstrating its adaptability to diverse legal and cultural settings. In each case the emphasis falls on the lordship of **Christ** over every domain and the consequent obligation of the **church** to witness in generosity and truth.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster-oriented vantage, Kaemingk s project displays notable strengths and live tensions. Strengths first. His retrieval of **common grace**, **antithesis**, and **sphere sovereignty** honors **Kuyper** and **Bavinck** and resists the twin idolatries of **secular neutrality** and **Christian nationalism**. His robust defense of **institutional religious freedom** is a principled application of **creation order** that can be endorsed across confessional lines. His linkage of **liturgy** and **vocation** presses churches to disciple workers under **Christ s lordship**, answering a long-standing pastoral deficit. Tensions remain. Advocates of a **Two-Kingdoms** or **natural law**-centered account will worry that Neo-Calvinist rhetoric risks conflating the redemptive **kingdom of Christ** with the common realm and underestimating the prudential role of **lex naturae** as a public grammar. **Van Tilian** concerns arise if appeals to **common grace** do not sufficiently foreground the **antithesis** in epistemology and worship, especially in interreligious cooperation with Islam s denial of the **Trinity** and **incarnation**. Finally, some heirs of the **regulative principle of worship** will press whether liturgical expansions to serve vocation risk instrumentalizing Lord s Day worship, and whether the gathered assembly s telos can bear too much weekday freight. A constructive Reformed alternative is not a rejection but a calibration: uphold Kaemingk s **principled pluralism** while clarifying a twofold word about creation and redemption, intensify **catechesis** on Christ s unique claims in interfaith settings, and guard the **means of grace** in worship even as congregations grow intentional about forming workers for neighbor-serving excellence.
