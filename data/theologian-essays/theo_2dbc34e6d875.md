### TITLE
From Meaning to Presence: a philology of embodied intensities for late modern culture

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Postwar German humanities amid **Heidegger**, **Gadamer**, and **systems theory**; Stanford transatlantic bridge for a contemporary critique of **hermeneutics** in favor of **presence**.
- Method as **philology of presence** that privileges **materiality**, **form**, and **mood** over exclusive **semantic interpretation**; descriptive attention to **events** and **bodies**.
- Key works: **Making Sense in Life and Literature** 1992; **Materialities of Communication** 1994; **1926** 1997; **The Powers of Philology** 2003; **Production of Presence** 2004; **In Praise of Athletic Beauty** 2006; **Atmosphere, Mood, Stimmung** 2012; **After 1945** 2013; **Our Broad Present** 2014.
- Central terms: **presence vs meaning**, **epiphany of form**, **Stimmung** and atmosphere, **event and intensity**, **latency** and **broad present**, **oscillation** between interpretation and embodiment.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Heidegger**, **Gadamer**, **Luhmann**, **Blumenberg**, **Auerbach**, **Derrida**, **Kittler**, **Latour**; conversation partners on **aesthetics**, **media**, and **phenomenology**.
- Reformed cue: reception tests how **presence** relates to **Word and sacrament**, **eschatological time**, and the **Creator–creature distinction** within classical **Reformed orthodoxy**.

### ESSAY
Hans Ulrich Gumbrecht, born in 1948 in Wurzburg and deceased in 2023 in Palo Alto, fashioned a distinctive intellectual profile inside the late twentieth century German and American humanities by staging a sustained turn from exclusive **hermeneutics** to an aesthetics of **presence**. Formed amid the aftermath of 1945 and the debates around **Heidegger**, **Gadamer**, and **Luhmann**, he pursued Romance and German philology in Munich, Regensburg, Salamanca, and Pavia before appointments in Bochum and Constance, settling at Stanford in 1989 as a transatlantic broker of literary theory. The generation marked by 1968 sought critical unmasking through **structuralism** and **deconstruction**. Gumbrecht absorbed these energies yet came to suspect that a culture of relentless **meaning** occluded the felt immediacy of things. He advanced a counterproposal: a **philology of presence** attentive to bodies, surfaces, and events that manifest in time with intensity.

Gumbrecht’s method is resolutely **philological** but not in the antiquarian sense. The proposition is that texts, performances, and artifacts do not just communicate **meaning**. They also produce **presence**, a mode of experience in which material form, spatial nearness, and temporal immediacy generate palpable effects. This is less an ontology than a disciplined practice of description. Against an academic reflex of **suspicion** derived from **Marx**, **Nietzsche**, and **Freud**, and refined by **Derrida**, he recovers the craft of attending to **form**, **gesture**, and **arrangement**. The wager is that description can sponsor **events** of reception, moments when the world appears under heightened intensity. His edited volume **Materialities of Communication** in 1994 and his manifesto **The Powers of Philology** in 2003 announced this pivot, repositioning philology as a cultural technique that oscillates between **interpretation** and **embodied presence** rather than reducing the text to a mere vehicle of propositional content.

#### 1. Production of Presence and the critique of meaning culture
The programmatic statement arrives in **Production of Presence: What Meaning Cannot Convey** in 2004. Here Gumbrecht specifies a contrast between a **meaning culture** that privileges semantic decipherment, dialogical understanding, and historical narrative, and a **presence culture** that values material tangibility, **epiphany of form**, and the shock of immediacy. Presence names the effect of things that are spatially copresent, temporally synchronous, and affectively overwhelming. He insists on an **oscillation** rather than a replacement: interpretation and presence are complementary modes. Yet he wants to redistribute prestige toward practices that risk **intensity** and **attunement**. In polemical relief stand **Gadamerian hermeneutics** with its infinite dialogue and **Derridean differance** with its endless deferral. Gumbrecht wagers that humanistic knowledge must also cultivate skills that can occasion **epiphanic** experiences, those shimmering instants when form exceeds function and a work’s sheer there-ness presses upon the body.

The concept of **Stimmung** becomes the conductor for this presence-oriented philology. In **Atmosphere, Mood, Stimmung** in 2012 he retrieves the Heideggerian sense of *Stimmung* as world-disclosing attunement and translates it into a literary and cultural method. Moods are not private psychological states but shared **atmospheres** that envelop spaces, texts, and bodies. To read for **Stimmung** is to register qualities of light, rhythm, timbre, and pacing that articulate a world prior to propositional clarity. This reconception re-situates the reader as a participant within a field of forces. It is an anti-Cartesian move, de-centering the subject in favor of **situated embodiment**. While media theorists like **Kittler** emphasized infrastructures and **Latour** mapped networks of actants, Gumbrecht stays closer to **aesthetics**, describing how arrangements of form produce **presence effects** that cannot be reduced to code, signal, or discourse. The aim is not to cancel **meaning** but to liberate **description** to do justice to the felt life of art and ritual.

Gumbrecht’s temporal diagnosis complements his phenomenology of presence. In **After 1945: Latency as Origin of the Present** in 2013 and **Our Broad Present** in 2014 he argues that Western culture has shifted from modernity’s futurist dynamism to a chronotope of **broad present** characterized by **latency**, ongoing maintenance, and risk management. The expectation of decisive historical ruptures has given way to a sense of suspended time where the past lingers and the future arrives without promise of transformation. This temporal flattening yields both anxiety and the search for **events** that pierce the monotony. The cultural appetite for peaks of intensity increases as narratives of progress wane. Gumbrecht connects this to practices that contract time and augment presence, from performance and installation to ritual and sport. The historical thesis does not abandon historicity. It recalibrates the horizon within which **presence** can be experienced in a culture that feels stuck in an extended now.

Sport is the privileged laboratory in **In Praise of Athletic Beauty** in 2006. Here Gumbrecht shows how competitive athletics produce **epiphanies of form** through trained bodies moving at the edge of possibility. Spectators are not simply interpreters of rules and symbols. They are caught in waves of **Stimmung** generated by speed, precision, and risk. The book is a secular hymn to **eventfulness**. It also functions as an anthropological claim about humans as beings who crave moments of **heightened presence** that break the routine of the **broad present**. This account travels back to his literary criticism in **Making Sense in Life and Literature** in 1992 and the cultural montage **1926** in 1997, where he anatomizes a pre-crisis modernity saturated with formal invention. Across these works he develops a practice of descriptive montage, assembling artifacts to allow **presence** to arise through juxtaposition, pacing, and formal echo. The critical gesture is consistent: recover the world’s pressure on the body through the staging of **form**.

Methodologically, Gumbrecht’s appeal to **event** leans on Heidegger’s *Ereignis* understood as a happening that lets being come into unconcealedness, though he studiously avoids metaphysical commitments. His preferred vocabulary is descriptive rather than ontological. He speaks of **effects**, **intensities**, **oscillations**, and **peaks**. Yet the philosophical horizon is clear. Against a hermeneutic of endless **mediation**, he posits moments of **immediacy** that are not naive but cultivated. Against a semiotics that treats material substrate as indifferent, he insists on **materiality** as efficacious. Against historicism’s confidence in linear narratives, he offers **latency** and **broad present** as the temporal ambience of our epoch. This triangulation produces a recognizable stance in contemporary cultural theory that invites theological conversation about **presence**, **sacramentality**, and **epiphany**, while resisting confessional claims about **transcendence** or **grace**.

Interlocution runs through every phase of Gumbrecht’s work. With **Gadamer** he contests the sufficiency of dialogical **understanding**, urging the humanities to cultivate **observation** and **description** that do not bend immediately toward meaning. With **Derrida** he shares an anti-totalizing sensibility but refuses to reduce phenomena to **trace** and **deferment**, arguing that sometimes the world arrives with pressure that cannot be dissolved. With **Blumenberg** he accepts a metaphorology of human orientation but adds a stronger commitment to **form** as an agent of intensity. With **Luhmann** he borrows systems attentiveness to **operations** while retaining a humanistic regard for **aesthetic experiences**. Debates with **Kittlerian media archaeology** stress that channels shape content, yet Gumbrecht resists media determinism by defending the irreducible **event** of reception. The result is a conversation-rich method that consistently presses for recognition of **presence effects** across disciplines.

Theological readers have found in Gumbrecht’s vocabulary of **presence** and **Stimmung** an inviting yet ambivalent resource. On the one hand, his account of **epiphany of form** resonates with classical themes of **real presence**, **incarnation**, and the sacramental relation of **sign and res**. His descriptive patience models how to attend to worship as a choreography of **bodies**, **space**, **sound**, and **time** that can occasion *epiphanic* attention. The emphasis on **mood** parallels liturgical notions of *habitus* and ecclesial atmosphere that dispose a people to receive. On the other hand, Gumbrecht’s method intentionally inhabits a secular frame. The **event** he pursues need not be indexed to **divine action**. Intensity is immanent, not a theophany. He refuses an ontology of **participation** that would locate presence in God’s self-giving, preferring to keep the analysis on the level of cultural technique and affective effect. This tension renders his work valuable as a probe and limited as a doctrine.

Gumbrecht’s diagnosis of a **broad present** bears directly on eschatological questions. If late modern temporality is dominated by **latency** and maintenance, then Christian proclamation risks being heard as one more management discourse unless it bears **event-character**. His call to risk **intensity** suggests that preaching and sacramental practice should be more than cognitive transfer. It should stage an encounter where **Word and sign** impress themselves bodily and communally. Yet the difference between **aesthetic presence** and **sacred presence** must be carefully stated. For theology, the *sacramentum et res* complex Latin phrase meaning the sacramental sign and the reality signified cannot be collapsed into a pure epiphany of form. There is an unseen **res** that exceeds any measurable **effect**. Gumbrecht indirectly challenges theology to articulate how **grace** and **glory** relate to felt presence without capitulating to a cult of intensity.

#### 2. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster Seminary angle, Gumbrecht’s critique of disembodied **hermeneutics** is salutary. His care for **materiality**, **form**, and **mood** corrects a rationalist reduction of **Word** to bare propositions and resonates with **Calvin’s spiritual presence** in the Supper, where the Spirit makes Christ present to faith. His insistence on **oscillation** between **meaning** and **presence** dovetails with a doctrine of **Word and sacrament** as mutually interpreting means of grace. His historical thesis about the **broad present** incisively names pastoral pressures that blunt **eschatological** hope. Yet his program remains bound to an **immanent frame** that cannot secure the **covenantal** shape of divine self-disclosure. Presence without promise risks becoming an aesthetics of intensity untethered from **revelation**. The Reformed alternative insists that God’s **covenant Word** creates the church’s atmosphere, that **providence** and **Sabbath** order time toward **consummation**, and that true **event** is indexed by **Christ’s ascension and session**, not by peak affect. Appropriation should therefore be chastened and selective. Receive Gumbrecht’s **philology of presence** as a methodological aid for attending to the created means through which God addresses us. Resist his evacuation of **transcendence** by anchoring presence in **promise**, **Spirit**, and **covenant history**. Where he discerns **latency**, the Reformed confession proclaims **kairos**, the fullness of time, and orders cultural intensities under the **regulative principle** that the church’s practices be governed by Scripture. This preserves the Creator–creature distinction, honors the goodness of **embodiment**, and secures **eschatological** orientation beyond the **broad present**.

### RECAP NOTES
- Postwar German and American setting shaped by **Heidegger**, **Gadamer**, and **systems theory**, with Stanford as Gumbrecht’s base for a contemporary turn toward **presence**.
- Core problem: the hegemony of **meaning culture** in the humanities and the need to recover **presence**, **Stimmung**, and **event** as legitimate aims of knowledge.
- Major works: **Production of Presence** 2004 on **presence vs meaning**; **Atmosphere, Mood, Stimmung** 2012 on **mood and atmosphere**; **After 1945** 2013 and **Our Broad Present** 2014 on **latency**; **In Praise of Athletic Beauty** 2006 on **epiphany of form**.
- Defining tools: **philology of presence**, **description over suspicion**, **materiality of communication**, **oscillation** between **interpretation** and **embodied intensity**.
- Interlocutors: critical engagements with **Gadamer** and **Derrida**; affinities and contrasts with **Heidegger**, **Blumenberg**, **Luhmann**, **Kittler**, and **Latour** on **aesthetics**, **media**, and **event**.
- Reformed appraisal: affirm attention to **form**, **bodies**, and **mood** in **Word and sacrament**; resist a purely immanent **presence** by grounding intensity in **covenant**, **revelation**, and **eschatological** time.
- Legacy: enduring influence on debates about **presence**, **aesthetics**, and **humanistic method**, with ongoing uptake in theology, liturgy, and cultural theory wrestling with the **broad present**.
