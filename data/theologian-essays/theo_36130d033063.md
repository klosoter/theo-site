### TITLE
Evolutionary Creation: Scripture’s Inerrant Spiritual Message Delivered Through Ancient Scientific Incidents

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth-century evangelicalism**, the North American **creation-evolution controversy**, and the rise of **science-and-religion studies** in a university setting.
- **Interdisciplinary method** integrating the **two books of God** and a non-concordist hermeneutic grounded in **accommodation** and the **Message-Incident Principle**.
- **Evolutionary Creation: A Christian Approach to Evolution** (2008); **I Love Jesus and I Accept Evolution** (2009); **Four Views on the Historical Adam** contribution (2013); **Evolution: Scripture and Nature Say Yes!** (2016); **The Bible and Ancient Science: Principles of Interpretation** (2020).
- Core categories: **evolutionary creation**, **scientific concordism**, **ancient cosmology**, **phenomenological language**, **limited inerrancy of spiritual truths**, **divine providence without gaps**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Augustine** and **Calvin** on **accommodatio**; dialogue with **Ken Ham**, **Hugh Ross**, **Michael Behe**, **C. John Collins**, **John H. Walton**, **Francis Collins**, and **B. B. Warfield**.
- Reformed cue: tension with **Westminster inerrancy** and **federal headship of Adam**, possible alignment with **non-concordist readings** but conflict over **historical Adam**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Denis O. Lamoureux emerged within late twentieth-century North American evangelicalism at the height of the **creation-evolution debate**, moving vocationally from professional dentistry and biological research into theology and the emerging academy of **science-and-religion**. As professor at St. Joseph’s College, University of Alberta, he deliberately positioned himself against both **young-earth creationism** and **Intelligent Design**, coining and championing the distinctive label **evolutionary creation**. His biographical arc from an early conservative biblicist background to a mature integrative stance frames the logic of his project: the **God who creates** is the same Lord who speaks in Scripture, and the integrity of **general revelation** and **special revelation** calls for hermeneutical patience, not a **God-of-the-gaps**. Methodologically, he deploys the classical **two books of God** metaphor, harmonized with **Augustine’s** caution and **Calvin’s accommodatio**, but recast in contemporary terms through what he names the **Message-Incident Principle**.

Lamoureux’s epistemic posture is a disciplined anti-reductionism: he argues for a theologically thick **providence** while repudiating **scientific concordism**, the attempt to read modern empirical data into the **ancient cosmology** of Scripture. He insists that the Holy Spirit reveals life-giving theological truths through culturally embedded ancient descriptions of the world. The Bible speaks in **phenomenological language** from a three-tier universe to the **firmament**, not in modern scientific idiom; yet within those vessels, God delivers the spiritual **gospel message** without error. His voice is pastoral and apologetic at once, calling the church to dogged fidelity to **Christ’s lordship** and to responsible intellectual honesty regarding **evolution** as the best account of biological diversity.

#### 2. Message-Incident Principle and anti-concordist hermeneutics
The hermeneutical engine of Lamoureux’s corpus is the **Message-Incident Principle**. By this he means that Scripture’s **inerrancy** concerns its divinely intended theological messages, while the ancient scientific vehicles that carry those messages are **incidental** to revelation and can reflect erroneous cosmology. He deploys **accommodation** aggressively: God condescends to ancient authors and audiences, adopting their **ancient science** to convey infallible truths about **creation**, **sin**, **salvation**, and **eschatological hope**. He therefore repudiates **scientific concordism**, which he regards as hermeneutically anachronistic and theologically hazardous because it risks tethering the gospel to a superseded science. This yields a principled distinction between what the text affirms theologically and how it incidentally describes the world.

In extended treatments of **Genesis 1–11**, Lamoureux highlights the presence of **ancient Near Eastern** motifs and a premodern taxonomy of nature. The **firmament** is not a poetic metaphor to be harmonized with astronomy; it is an ancient piece of science pressed into service by the Spirit to teach that God alone is **Creator ex nihilo** and sovereign. Similarly, references to a **three-tiered universe** are literary furniture, not propositional claims for modern cosmology. His preferred predicate for Scripture is **infallibility of spiritual intention**, contrasted with what he regards as unwarranted demands of **Chicago-style inerrancy** that extend flawless truthfulness to matters of ancient science and incidental history.

#### 3. Evolutionary Creation and the rejection of gaps
In his programmatic work **Evolutionary Creation: A Christian Approach to Evolution** (2008), Lamoureux details the theology that stands behind the label. Unlike the generic **theistic evolution**, which he believes can sound deistic, **evolutionary creation** foregrounds that the triune **God creates and sustains** through evolutionary mechanisms. There are no ontological gaps in nature that require supplementary intelligent interventions; rather **providence** and **natural law** are coextensive expressions of divine action. He turns away from **Intelligent Design** arguments that hinge on irreducible complexity, arguing that they illegitimately convert the **God-of-the-gaps** into a research-stopper and tie Christian faith to vulnerable scientific inferences.

At the same time, Lamoureux affirms a robust **teleology**. He frequently distinguishes between the methodological constraints of science and the metaphysical stance of **creation**: science describes **evolutionary mechanisms**, while theology confesses that these mechanisms exist and succeed because God wills and orders them. Thus he preserves **creatio continua** without dismantling the Christological center of revelation. The result is a constructive grammar of concurrence: **God acts** through evolution, and evolution is a created means through which **God acts**.

#### 4. Pastoral apologetics and pedagogy
With **I Love Jesus and I Accept Evolution** (2009), Lamoureux shifts into a pastoral key. The book is an accessible narrative designed to lower the temperature of the culture war and to invite students into an intellectually joyful discipleship. He unpacks sample texts that have become flashpoints for evangelical anxiety, commending a hermeneutic of **phenomenological description** and **ancient cosmology** alongside rigorous Christ-centered piety. Throughout he punctures the false dichotomy of **science versus faith**, encouraging readers to confess **Jesus as Lord** without fear of the sciences.

This pedagogical instinct also entails a constructive doctrine of **Scripture** aimed at students and congregations. Lamoureux clarifies that rejecting **scientific concordism** is not rejecting biblical authority but rather honoring biblical **accommodation**. He affirms that the Bible is fully trustworthy for its **soteriological** purposes, and he insists that theological formation must include learning how ancient texts functioned in their **literary** and **cultural** worlds. The heart of faith remains the **gospel**, not the defense of a particular scientific model smuggled into Scripture.

#### 5. Adam, sin, and the apostolic witness
Lamoureux’s most controversial contributions arise in debates about **Adam** and **original sin**. In the **Four Views on the Historical Adam** volume (2013), he defends a **no-Adam** position. He contends that the **de novo creation** of Adam and Eve reflects **ancient biology** and that the Holy Spirit accommodated these ancient beliefs to deliver theological truths about universal human sinfulness and the need for **redemption**. He reads **Romans 5** and **1 Corinthians 15** as deploying Adam typologically or archetypally rather than as a commitment to a common ancestor as a historical individual. In his retelling, Paul, like the author of Genesis, participated in the **ancient science** of origins, and the Spirit used that framework to proclaim the drama of **fall and grace** without binding the church to a genetic progenitor.

This approach requires reworking elements of **hamartiology** and **anthropology**. Lamoureux rejects **imputed guilt** through a literal federal head, preferring to speak of the universality of sin as an empirical fact of human life within an evolved species endowed with **imago Dei** capacities. He often appeals to the emergence of moral awareness and the inevitability of moral failure to interpret **death** and **corruption** as part of a good yet perilous creation that the cross of **Christ** heals and transforms. The **fall** language becomes a way of narrating the tragedy of human freedom rather than a once-for-all historical collapse of an original righteousness.

#### 6. Scripture and nature in concert
In **Evolution: Scripture and Nature Say Yes!** (2016), Lamoureux frames the controversy as a conflict of categories. **Scripture** and **nature** are non-competitive voices of the one **Creator**, and their apparent discrepancies are frequently the product of forcing **scientific concordism** onto texts that are not attempting empirical description. He traverses key topics such as the age of the earth, common descent, and the presence of **death before the fall**, arguing that the biblical narrative intends to teach the gravity of sin and the trustworthiness of **God’s covenantal promise**, not the precise chronology of geology or biology. Drawing on **B. B. Warfield’s** openness to evolution and **Augustine’s** advice against bringing Christian faith into ridicule by making scientific claims on biblical grounds, he invites evangelicals to recover intellectual credibility and missionary agility.

Lamoureux also resists the anxiety that evolution implies nihilistic **naturalism**. He articulates a theologically loaded **providence** wherein God’s sovereignty embraces every layer of causation. He does not reduce the **miraculous** to metaphor; rather, he isolates the miraculous to its **salvific** and **eschatological** focal points, above all the **incarnation** and the **resurrection**, while refusing to let miracles function as patches in the fabric of natural history. In this way, he attempts to secure both the integrity of science and the non-negotiable center of **Christian dogma**.

#### 7. Ancient science and biblical interpretation
In his later primer **The Bible and Ancient Science: Principles of Interpretation** (2020), Lamoureux consolidates his exegetical case by cataloging recurrent features of **ancient cosmology** throughout Scripture. He aligns **Joshua’s long day**, **the storehouses of snow**, and the **firmament** with common ancient conceptions, arguing that these are not failed predictions but examples of Spirit-used **phenomenological language**. He develops test-cases across genres, showing how **poetry**, **narrative**, and **epistle** use the world-picture available to them to communicate God’s **covenant faithfulness**, human rebellion, and the hope borne by **Christ’s kingdom**.

Key to this proposal is his articulation of the **limited inerrancy of spiritual truths**. Lamoureux does not deny that the Bible makes claims about history. He does insist that the Bible’s historical and scientific idioms can be ancillary and enculturated. The church’s task is to discern the **theological intention**. That intention, he argues, never fails. It is this hermeneutical filtration that warrants accepting **evolution** while confessing the fullness of Christian **orthodoxy**, including **Trinity**, **creation**, **sin**, and **salvation**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, allies, and debates
Lamoureux’s public engagements traverse multiple fronts. He disputes **young-earth creationism** as represented by figures like **Ken Ham**, contending that it binds faith to discredited **chronologies** and endangers the church’s public witness. He also critiques **progressive creationism** exemplified by **Hugh Ross**, on the grounds that it often remains **concordist** and retains episodic interventions that reopen the **God-of-the-gaps**. With **Intelligent Design** proponents such as **Michael Behe** and **William Dembski**, he shares a rejection of naturalistic metaphysics but rejects their scientific program as hermeneutically confused and theologically precarious.

On the hermeneutical front, his conversation partners include **C. John Collins**, who maintains a **historical Adam** within a literary-framework reading, and **John H. Walton**, who presses for **functional ontology** in Genesis yet retains Adam in some historical sense. Lamoureux’s closest methodological cousin may be **Peter Enns** with an **incarnational model of Scripture**, though Lamoureux prefers the precise instrument of the **Message-Incident Principle**. He receives cautious welcome from **BioLogos** circles near **Francis Collins**, where **evolutionary creation** has become a recognized brand. In biblical theology discussions, he confronts the recent defense of a plausible **historical Adam** by **William Lane Craig**, arguing that such models still overread scientific paradigms into apostolic texts whose **accommodated ancient science** is incidental to their **kerygma**.

#### 9. Systematic coherence and doctrinal profile
Doctrinally, Lamoureux’s system coheres around three loci. First, in **bibliology**, he offers a high view of **inspiration** shaped by **accommodation**, in which **infallibility** attaches specifically to the saving **message** and not to the ancient scientific **incidents**. Second, in **doctrine of creation**, he advances **evolutionary creation**, which is a theologically teleological and providential reading of **evolution** devoid of **gaps** and secure against **deism**. Third, in **anthropology and hamartiology**, he denies a **historical Adam** and interprets **original sin** as the universal existential reality of human wrongdoing rather than federal guilt traced to a single ancestor. He seeks to retain the weight of **atonement** and **resurrection** as historical-salvific events, while dislocating the fall from a punctiliar prehistory to a theological description of every human story.

The net effect is a progressive yet orthodox-leaning proposal that stakes the stability of Christian confession on **Christology** and **soteriology**, while allowing maximal flexibility in **origins** and **cosmology**. Lamoureux’s wager is pedagogical as much as theoretical: that the church can catechize toward **intellectual virtue**, embrace the findings of **mainstream science**, and preach the unembarrassed **gospel** without conflating categories.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and WTS vantage, Lamoureux’s project excels in its rejection of **concordism** and its appropriation of **accommodation** to account for **ancient cosmology**. There is real consonance with strands of **Old Princeton**, where **B. B. Warfield** allowed for evolution under a high **providence**, and with Reformed literary approaches to **Genesis** that resist wooden literalism. Yet substantial tensions remain. First, his **limited inerrancy** collides with the **Westminster Confession of Faith** 1.5 and 1.8, which ground the church’s confidence not only in the saving **message** but in the truthfulness of all that Scripture affirms, including matters of **history**. Classical Reformed **accommodation** never entailed divine assertion of falsehood but concerned mode of communication in human idiom. Second, the denial of a **historical Adam** threatens the **covenantal structure** of Reformed **federal headship** in WCF 6, where the unity of the human race under Adam and under **Christ** the second Adam is constitutive of **original sin** and **justification**. Reformed alternatives exist that avoid **scientific concordism** without surrendering **historicity**: the **framework** or **analogical day** readings, **archetypal** significance in concert with a real first human pair, and an understanding of **accommodation** that speaks truly in phenomenological language while still asserting facts. Finally, Lamoureux’s symmetrical use of the **two books** risks flattening the asymmetry in Reformed epistemology, where **special revelation** normatively interprets **general revelation** within **covenant history**. A Reformed retrieval would affirm his teleological **providence**, adopt non-concordist hermeneutics, and maintain pastoral charity, while preserving a fully **inerrant Scripture** and a **historical Adam** essential to the fabric of **covenant theology**.
