### TITLE
The Sentences as the scaffolding of scholastic theology, codifying the seven sacraments and a relationally Augustinian Trinity

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **High Medieval Paris**, cathedral schools moving toward the **university curriculum**, with **Augustine**, **Boethius**, and emerging **dialectic** defining the intellectual atmosphere.
- **Scholastic method of sententiae**, assembling **auctoritates** and resolving tensions by **distinctiones** and controlled **quaestiones**, a disciplined **Augustinian** epistemic stance.
- **Libri Quattuor Sententiarum** c. 1155-1158, **Gloss on the Psalms** c. 1139-1141, **Gloss on the Pauline Epistles** c. 1140s, plus **Sermons**; elected **Bishop of Paris** 1159, died 1160.
- Key categories include **divine simplicity**, **relations of origin**, **creation ex nihilo**, **satisfaction and merit**, **caritas as the Holy Spirit**, **res et sacramentum**, and the **seven sacraments**.
- Interlocutors and sources include **Augustine**, **Ambrose**, **Gregory the Great**, **Boethius**, **Anselm**, **Hugh of St Victor**, **Peter Abelard**, **Bernard of Clairvaux**, and **Gratian**.
- Reformed cue: affirmations of **Trinity**, **Incarnation**, and **creation** are strong, yet **sacramental causality**, **merit and penance**, and the identification of **charity with the Holy Spirit** invite a **Reformed** critique centered on **Word and Spirit**, **federal theology**, and **forensic justification**.

### ESSAY
Peter Lombard, known as the **Magister Sententiarum**, stands at the hinge of the **High Medieval** transition from cathedral schools to institutionalized **university theology**. Born around 1100 in Lombardy and dying in 1160 as **Bishop of Paris**, he worked within a culture saturated with **Augustinian** authority and the logical tools of **Boethius** and the schools. The Paris of the 1140s and 1150s trained the theological habitus through careful reading of **Scripture** within the matrix of patristic **auctoritates**, disciplined by the emerging **scholastic** dialectic. Lombard’s lifetime spans the controversy surrounding **Gilbert of Poitiers** on the **Trinity**, the polemics of **Bernard of Clairvaux** against **Peter Abelard**, and the consolidation of **canon law** after **Gratian**. These currents shaped his method: to gather authoritative **sententiae**, arrange them by topic, isolate apparent contradictions, and resolve them through precise **distinctiones** in service of a catholic **concordia**. The outcome is not a speculative system like that of **Anselm**, but a pedagogical architecture that would bear the weight of **Aquinas**, **Bonaventure**, **Scotus**, and **Ockham**.

Lombard’s method, on display in the **Four Books of Sentences** c. 1155-1158, proceeds by collecting **Augustine**, **Ambrose**, **Gregory**, **Hilary**, **Boethius**, and others, then administering the logic of **quaestio** and **determinatio** so that a student learns to pass from authorities to **theological judgment**. He improves on **Abelard’s Sic et Non** by refusing the bare juxtaposition of contradictory texts and insisting upon a principled resolution by **distinction**. The Sentences’ fourfold structure is itself didactic: **Book I** on **God and Trinity**, **Book II** on **creation and sin**, **Book III** on **Incarnation and grace**, **Book IV** on **sacraments and eschatology**. Within each book the discrete **distinctiones** function as topical files that create a flexible but coherent map of doctrinal loci. The method presumes **Scriptural primacy**, yet it mediates Scripture through the tested agreement of the **Fathers** and the rational grammar of **Boethian** metaphysics, particularly in talk of **substance**, **relation**, and **person**.

The heart of **Book I** is an **Augustinian** doctrine of **divine simplicity** and **Trinity**. Lombard defends one **essence** in three **persons**, the persons distinguished by **relations of origin** as Father, Son, and Spirit. He deploys **Boethius** to say that the **persons are subsisting relations**, relatio subsistens, and that the essence is not a fourth thing. The divine names are treated through **appropriation**, so that power is fittingly attributed to the Father, wisdom to the Son, love to the Spirit, while guarding the unity of **omnipotence**, **omniscience**, and **goodness** in the simple God. Against the errors associated with **Gilbert of Poitiers**, Lombard secures the identity of **essence and person** without collapsing the relations, and he clearly affirms the **Filioque**, the **procession** of the Spirit from the Father and the Son, consonant with Latin patristic teaching. The treatment of **providence** and **predestination** is equally **Augustinian**, stressing the divine **foreknowledge** and unmerited **election**, while broaching the permissive character of **reprobation** and the inscrutability of the **divine will**.

In **Book II** Lombard turns to **creation ex nihilo**, the **angels**, humanity created in the **imago Dei**, and the reality of **original sin**. He follows the antique consensus that the world is created by the **Trinity** from nothing, that angelic natures precede human history, and that Adam’s fall inflicts a hereditary **fault** and **penal liability** on all his progeny. He draws from **Augustine** to insist that **concupiscence** remains in the baptized as an inclination, yet that its guilt is remitted by grace. He considers the freedom of the will under grace, distinguishing true freedom to the good from the slavery of sin, and thus frames later scholastic distinctions between **nature** and **grace**. Throughout, **Scripture** remains primary while **Gregory the Great** and **Ambrose** help fill out angelology and the moral psychology of temptation and habitus. The emerging vocabulary of **habitus** serves to describe the infused stability of virtues that follow regenerative grace, a theme that will become pivotal in **Book III**.

**Book III** structures **Christology** and **soteriology** around the **Incarnation**. Lombard affirms that the **Word assumes a complete human nature**, body and rational soul, into the unity of a single person, hypostasis, thus securing the **communicatio idiomatum**, that is, the predication of the properties of each nature to the one **Christ**. He treats the cross through a broadly **Anselmian** lens of **satisfaction** and **merit** and yet he famously states that **God could have forgiven sin by another means**, Deus potuit aliter, which anticipates the later distinction between **potentia absoluta** and **potentia ordinata**, absolute and ordained power. The death of Christ is therefore read not as an absolute metaphysical necessity but as the most fitting mode, convenientia, by which divine justice and mercy shine together. Lombard’s accounts of **merit**, **grace**, and **charity** lean heavily on **Augustine**, yet he presses a distinctive claim that the **charity by which we love God is the Holy Spirit given to us**. This identity thesis, caritas Spiritus Sanctus est, implies that the uncreated **Spirit** is the inner mover of all the justified and that the created habit of charity is inseparable from his indwelling work, an intensified statement of **Augustinian** indwelling that later theologians will refine.

**Book IV** codifies the medieval doctrine of **the sacraments**. Lombard offers a now classic definition, that a **sacrament is a visible sign of invisible grace that bears the image of grace and is its cause**, thus securing both signification and **causality**. He standardizes the list of **seven sacraments** of the New Law, **Baptism**, **Confirmation**, **Eucharist**, **Penance**, **Extreme Unction**, **Orders**, and **Marriage**, a taxonomy that will be affirmed by later councils. He develops the triadic analysis of signs as **sacramentum tantum**, sign alone, *res et sacramentum*, thing and sign, and **res tantum**, thing alone. In **Baptism** he speaks of the **indelible character** as the *res et sacramentum*, with **forgiveness** and **adoption** as the res tantum. In **Confirmation** and **Orders** he also recognizes a **character** which marks a conforming seal, again functioning as *res et sacramentum*. Regarding the **Eucharist** he is emphatic about the **real presence** and the **conversion** of the substance of bread and wine into the body and blood of Christ, while the accidents remain, anticipating later articulation of **transubstantiation** and employing a realist sacramental metaphysics. **Penance** appears as the second plank after shipwreck, a sacrament involving **contrition**, **confession**, **absolution**, and **satisfaction**, where satisfactions bear a medicinal and partly reparative weight. **Marriage** is treated with special clarity, echoing **Gratian**, that mutual **consent** makes the bond while consummation perfects it, thus shaping the law of marriage and its sacramental dignity.

The earlier biblical **glosses** on the **Psalms** and **Pauline Epistles** provided Lombard with an exegetical reservoir that feeds into the **Sentences**, ensuring that the loci are not detached from **Scripture**. He reads the **Psalms** through **Augustine’s** Christological key and treats **Paul** on **grace**, **law**, and **faith** with a keen eye for the dynamics of **Romans** and **Galatians**. The **Sermons** confirm the pastoral register of his theology, where sacramental practice and ethical formation are never severed from the contemplative reception of **divine revelation**. This unity of **exegesis**, **doctrine**, and **pedagogy** is what gives Lombard’s project its ecclesial density, mediating between **monastic** lectio and the schools’ **scholastic** analysis.

The legacy of the **Sentences** is nearly unrivaled. From the mid thirteenth century onward, every candidate for the **magisterium** in theology wrote **Commentaries on the Sentences**. **Aquinas** produced his in the 1250s, **Bonaventure** also in the 1250s, **Duns Scotus** in the 1290s, and **Ockham** in the early fourteenth century, each using Lombard’s **distinctiones** as a scaffold for original construction. The **quaestio disputata** format, with its objections, sed contra, and replies, emerges in symbiosis with reading Lombard as set text. The Sentences functioned as an encyclopedia of **auctoritates** ripe for creative development, so that medieval diversity unfolded within a shared **Lombardian** architecture. Even where later masters corrected him, for example on the measure of **charity** and its relation to the **Spirit**, they did so by walking the structure he built.

Doctrinally, Lombard functions as a conduit and consolidator. He hands on a maximally **Augustinian** view of **simplicity** and **Trinity**, an ordered account of **creation** and **sin**, an **incarnational** soteriology of **fitting satisfaction**, and a robust **sacramental realism**. He also bequeaths crucial conceptual instruments. The triadic semantics of **sacramentum tantum**, *res et sacramentum*, **res tantum** equips later theology to speak precisely about sign, seal, and spiritual effect. The category of **character** stabilizes the identity and repeatability of certain sacraments. The insistence on **consent** in **marriage** moves beyond merely biological considerations to a personalist account that will shape canonical norms. On the Trinity, his careful use of **relations of origin** and warnings against making the **essence** a fourth thing locate him firmly within **Latin orthodoxy**, while his loyal **Filioque** is both patristically grounded and ecclesially decisive.

At several points Lombard registers disputed or audacious notes that catalyzed debate. The thesis that **charity is the Holy Spirit** in us drew fire and refinement, generating later distinctions between **uncreated grace**, the Spirit himself, and **created grace**, the infused habitus of **caritas**. His claim that **God could have forgiven without the cross** spurred famous analyses of the **necessity of satisfaction**, prompting later theologians to differentiate necessity of the end from necessity of the means and to clarify the relation of **divine justice** and **mercy** within the order of **ordination**. His account of **penance** and **satisfaction** shaped medieval penitential practice and, by reaction, the **Reformation** polemics, since his causalist definition of sacraments implies a strong account of sacramental **efficacy** that later councils would formulate under the phrase **ex opere operato**.

From a **Reformed and WTS** vantage, Lombard’s achievement is monumental in **Trinity**, **Incarnation**, and **creation**, yet it displays clear points of tension regarding **revelation**, **grace**, and **sacramentality**. Positively, his **Augustinian** **divine simplicity** and **relations of origin** are classical touchstones, his **Christology** aligns with **Chalcedon**, and his moral psychology of **concupiscence** and **grace** is rich. Yet the identification of **charity** with the **Holy Spirit** tends to blur the line between **uncreated** and **created** grace, a line that **Reformed** orthodoxy preserves by locating the Spirit’s indwelling as personal presence while defining **justification** in strictly **forensic** terms by **imputation** of Christ’s righteousness and receiving it by **faith as instrument**. Lombard’s suggestion that **God could forgive without satisfaction** threatens the necessary connection, consequent upon the divine decree, between **covenantal justice** and **atonement**, whereas **Reformed federal theology** asserts that the **covenant of redemption** and the **covenant of works** together render Christ’s **penal satisfaction** the ordained and fitting means by which God is both just and justifier. In **sacramentology**, Lombard’s causalist definition and the sevenfold enumeration depart from a **Word and Spirit** paradigm in which **Baptism** and **the Lord’s Supper** are **signs and seals**, efficacious by the **Spirit** through the **promise**, not as intrinsic causes. The **res et sacramentum** distinction remains heuristically valuable, but **Reformed** teaching resists **ex opere operato** implications and a penitential economy of **satisfaction**, preferring **repentance and faith** tied to the preached **Gospel**. The enduring alternative, then, is to receive Lombard’s **scholastic architecture** for its analytic clarity and its **Augustinian** core, while reforming its soteriology, sacramentology, and doctrine of grace along **Scripture-principled**, **covenantal**, and **Christocentric** lines.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: **High Medieval** Paris, cathedral schools becoming a **university** culture, dominated by **Augustine**, **Boethius**, and the rise of **dialectic**.
- **Core problem**: How to achieve **concordia** among patristic **auctoritates** using **scholastic** method while teaching a complete **sacra doctrina**.
- **Major works**: **Libri Quattuor Sententiarum** c. 1155-1158, **Gloss on the Psalms** c. 1139-1141, **Gloss on the Pauline Epistles** c. 1140s, plus **Sermons**; elected **Bishop of Paris** 1159.
- **Distinctives**: **Divine simplicity** and **relations of origin**, **creation ex nihilo**, **satisfaction and merit** with fittingness, **caritas as the Holy Spirit**, **res et sacramentum**, and the codified **seven sacraments**.
- **Interlocutors**: Heavy reliance on **Augustine**, plus **Ambrose**, **Gregory**, **Boethius**, **Anselm**, **Hugh of St Victor**, with debates around **Abelard**, **Gilbert of Poitiers**, and **Gratian**.
- **Reformed critique**: Affirm **Trinity** and **Incarnation**, revise **sacramental causality** and **penance**, reject identifying **charity** with the **Spirit**, insist on **forensic justification** and two sacraments as **signs and seals** empowered by **Word and Spirit**.
- **Legacy**: The **Sentences** became the medieval textbook, spawning commentaries by **Aquinas**, **Bonaventure**, **Scotus**, and **Ockham**, and shaping the grammar of **scholastic theology** into the late Middle Ages.
