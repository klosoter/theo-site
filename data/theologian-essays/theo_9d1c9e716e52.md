### TITLE
Practical divinity disciplined by Ramist method: William Perkins as architect of Puritan casuistry, preaching, and the ordered way of salvation.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
William Perkins, born in 1558 and dying in 1602, stands at the hinge of the English **Reformation** and the flowering of **Puritan practical divinity**. Educated at Christ's College, Cambridge, and ordained within the Church of England, he ministered as university preacher at Great St Andrews in Cambridge, shaping a generation of clergy. Perkins inhabited the post-Tridentine world of **confessionalization**, where **Roman Catholic** reform under **Robert Bellarmine** pressed hard claims and English ecclesiastical settlement remained negotiated. His formative intellectual tool was **Ramist method**, received from **Petrus Ramus** and adapted for theology. Ramism demanded analytic clarity by dichotomia and methodus, producing clean taxonomies that could structure catechesis, sermons, and casuistry. Hermeneutically, Perkins placed **Scripture as principium** for dogma and ethics, valorized preaching as the chief **means of grace**, and subordinated scholastic speculation to **use**. The result was a methodological fusion of **Reformed scholastic clarity** with homiletical immediacy, where **ordo salutis** diagrams, doctrine-laden sermons, and counsel to troubled consciences formed one integrated project.

Perkins navigated the contested path of a conforming **Puritan**. He wore the surplice and remained within the established church, yet he pressed for a **further reformation** of preaching, discipline, and moral life. This mediating posture shaped his method: he prized catholic continuity while distinguishing essentials from non-essentials, a move formalized in his later **A Reformed Catholic**. In his academic context at **Cambridge**, he drew on **Calvin** and **Beza** for doctrine, on **Ramus** for pedagogical form, and on the English homiletical tradition for the **plain style** of preaching. His self-understanding was that of a physician of souls, crafting a theology of **conscience** that could diagnose and heal, a theology of **calling** that organized common life under God, and a robust **covenant** frame that aligned individual salvation with the church's public ministry.

#### 2. Catechesis and preaching as principled practice
Perkins's earliest popular influence emerged through **The Foundation of Christian Religion**, his six principles catechism published in 1590. There he distilled **creation**, **fall**, **redemption in Christ**, **repentance and faith**, **sacraments**, and **obedience** into a compact pedagogy for household instruction. The catechism exemplified **Ramist method** in its binary divisions and succinct definitions, reinforcing his commitment to **perspicuity** and **order**. For Perkins, catechesis habituated the mind to a coherent system whose center was Christ's person and work and whose circumference set the practical duties of the regenerate life.

That catechetical clarity was operationalized in **The Art of Prophesying** 1592, arguably the classic Puritan manual on preaching. Perkins articulated a theology of **Scripture** as sufficient and authoritative, of **illumination** by the Spirit, and of **preaching** as the central ordinance for gathering and perfecting the elect. He taught the preacher to analyze a text into doctrine and uses, to distinguish **law and gospel**, and to apply truth to diverse spiritual estates. The sermon moved from lectio to sense to doctrine to **uses**, exemplifying the Ramist drive from theoretical to practical. The aim was not rhetorical flourish but **edification**, so that hearers might be converted, assured, and conformed to Christ. In this homiletic, the **ordo salutis** becomes pastoral map, the **covenant of grace** the controlling context, and the **conscience** the theater of divine witness.

#### 3. Predestination, order of salvation, and the covenant frame
The most iconic emblem of Perkins's dogmatics is the famous diagram in **A Golden Chain** 1591, titled Armilla Aurea, which visually renders the **duplex praedestinatio** of God and its temporal execution in the **ordo salutis**. Perkins affirmed God's eternal **decretum absolutum** that elects some to life and passes by others unto just condemnation, aligning with the emerging Reformed consensus of **double predestination**. Though not primarily occupied with lapsarian debates, his causal ordering seems **infralapsarian** at key points, since God considers mankind as fallen in Adam when ordering mercy and justice. The Golden Chain advances the temporal sequence of **effectual calling**, **faith**, **justification**, **adoption**, **sanctification**, and **glorification**, while tracing the tragic mirror-sequence of **hardening**, **infidelity**, **impenitence**, and **damnation** for the reprobate. The diagram is more than scholastic map. It is a pastoral instrument, orienting the pilgrim in the economy of grace.

Perkins framed this soteriology within **covenant theology**, identifying a **foedus operum** with Adam that promised life upon perfect obedience and a **foedus gratiae** in Christ that freely gives righteousness to believers. While less architectonic than later federal scholastics, Perkins used the **duplex foedus** to maintain both the absolute gratuity of salvation and the abiding normativity of the moral law. The **law** convicts and directs through its three uses, the **gospel** offers Christ and seals him by the Spirit. This law and gospel distinction grounds his preaching method and his counsel to consciences, preserving the evangelical logic that Christ's **imputed righteousness** precedes and produces sanctification.

#### 4. Conscience, casuistry, and moral theology
Perkins's signature contribution lies in his consolidation of **casuistry** under the banner of **conscience**, most systematically expressed in **A Discourse of Conscience** 1596 and the posthumous **Cases of Conscience** 1606. Conscience is the proximate tribunal where divine law, illumined by the Spirit, judges human acts. It is informed by **Scripture**, trained by doctrine, and applied by prudence to the complexity of cases. Perkins advances a disciplined taxonomy of **casus conscientiae** that ranges from public duties to private scruples, from oath-taking and Sabbath-keeping to marriage, commerce, and governance. He insists that casuistry is not moralism but **practical divinity**, ministering the **gospel** to the afflicted and the **law** to the secure.

The deliberative instrument within this moral theology is the **practical syllogism**, a structured reasoning that infers particular comfort from general promises. The major premise declares a Scriptural promise such as all who believe are justified in Christ. The minor premise identifies the Spirit's work in the believer such as by **fiducia** and new obedience. The conclusion yields **assurance** or direction. This reasoning is not autonomous ratiocination but **reason sanctified** and governed by the Spirit's **testimonium**. Perkins balances the **reflexive acts** of faith, what he calls **fides reflexa**, with the direct gaze upon Christ, **fides directa**, and he labors to prevent an anxious inwardness by anchoring all assurance in Christ's sufficiency.

#### 5. Justification, sanctification, and assurance
Within the **ordo salutis**, Perkins gives a classic **Reformed** articulation of **justification by faith alone** as a forensic act in which the righteousness of Christ is imputed to the ungodly. He is alert to the **Roman Catholic** conflation of justification with sanctification, and he engages **Bellarmine** to defend the imputed righteousness and the certainty of faith. His **A Reformed Catholic** 1597 is pivotal here. It aims to reclaim catholicity by showing areas of true agreement with Rome while identifying mortal disagreements. The formal and material principles remain decisive. **Scripture alone** norms doctrine, and **Christ alone** saves by grace through faith. Perkins's rhetorical move is charitable yet firm, distinguishing errors in the **foundation** from errors that can be tolerated without loss of churchly communion. In doing so, he offers a strategy for apologetics and ecclesial identity that shaped later **Reformed orthodoxy**.

Assurance stands at the heart of Perkins's pastoral project. He insists that faith by its nature has **certitudo** grounded in the promise and the **Spirit's witness**. Nevertheless, the weak and tempted require confirmatory signs. Hence the prominence of the **practical syllogism** and the cultivation of marks of grace, such as hunger for Christ, hatred of sin, and love for the brethren. Perkins wrestles with the dialectic of **simul iustus et peccator**, urging believers to look first to Christ and only then reflect on fruits. His sermons on **Galatians**, published in 1604, repeatedly return to the freedom of justification and the life of the Spirit, stabilizing assurance by the indicative of the gospel and then directing obedience by the imperative of the law.

#### 6. Vocation, common life, and the sanctification of culture
Perkins's **Treatise of Vocations** 1603 elaborates a theology of **calling** that sanctifies ordinary work within providence and the **covenant of grace**. Every lawful calling becomes an arena for love of neighbor and obedience to God. The doctrine is deliberately anti-monastic. Holiness is not withdrawal but fidelity in station. Magistrates, merchants, mothers, and ministers all serve God when they act in faith according to his word. This ethic draws on the **creation** order, the **Decalogue**, and the **gospel**, integrating a robust **natural law** register with evangelical motives. In many respects, the Treatise of Vocations functioned as a charter for **Puritan** social discipline, supplying principles for labor, wealth, and governance under Christ's kingship.

The breadth of Perkins's moral attention surfaces also in occasional works such as his posthumously published treatise on **witchcraft**, where he dissects superstition by **Scripture** and reason, and in his counsels on oath-taking, Sabbath observance, and family governance. These reflect a posterity often called **practical divinity**, the attempt to fold theological precision into the warp and woof of communal life. The organizing center remains constant. Conscience must be taught by **Scripture**, governed by **law and gospel**, and kept tender under the **Spirit**.

#### 7. Polemics and catholic strategy
Perkins engaged **Roman Catholic** polemicists, most notably **Bellarmine**, by deploying a twofold strategy in **A Reformed Catholic**. First, he argued for genuine commonality with the ancient church on **Trinity**, **Christology**, and the canon, thereby refusing the Roman monopoly on catholicity. Second, he identified irreconcilable divergences on **Scripture alone**, **justification**, **merit**, and **sacraments**. His method is notable. Rather than railing, he organizes points of agreement and disagreement systematically, weighted by their proximity to the foundation, Christ's mediatorial sufficiency. Perkins also contended against English sectarians and antinomian tendencies by reasserting the **covenant** structure and the abiding **moral law**. He foresaw the incipient **Arminian** threat in a synergistic construal of grace and defended **effectual calling** and the perseverance of the saints as necessary implications of divine **election**.

In sacramentology he maintained the **Reformed** spiritual presence of Christ in the Supper and affirmed infant baptism as sign and seal of the **covenant of grace**. His sacramental teaching aligned with **Calvin** and **Beza**, stressing the Spirit's instrumentality and the necessity of **faith**. Against **transubstantiation** and **ubiquity**, he asserted the integrity of Christ's ascended body and the sufficiency of the Spirit to unite believers to Christ. The sacraments function pastorally in Perkins's framework, strengthening weak faith and securing assurance within the visible **church**.

#### 8. Influence, transmission, and consolidation
Perkins died in 1602, yet his influence expanded through prolific posthumous publishing and translation. His thought crossed the Channel and the Atlantic, shaping the **Nadere Reformatie** in the Netherlands and **New England** Puritanism. His most immediate heir was **William Ames**, whose **Medulla Theologiae** distilled Perkinsian emphases in a succinct scholastic form. English divines such as **Richard Sibbes** and **John Preston** also absorbed his homiletic and casuistic program. The **Golden Chain** became a visual shorthand for **Reformed** soteriology, while **The Art of Prophesying** standardized preaching praxis for generations. Even critics borrowed his tools. Early **Arminians** reworked the practical syllogism toward conditional assurance, and **Laudian** preachers adopted aspects of the plain style.

Perkins thus appears as a pivotal school-founder. He crystallized a **Puritan** synthesis of **doctrine**, **piety**, and **pastoral craft**. He bequeathed a template for how to think theologically in public and in private, from Parliament to parlor. His casuistry systematized moral reasoning without ceding evangelical joy. His catechesis stabilized households in a mobile age. His covenant frame tethered exegesis to the canon's unity. And his methodological reliance on **Ramist method** passed into the arteries of **Reformed orthodoxy**, even as later scholastics modified it with Aristotelian precision.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster perspective, the strengths of Perkins are manifest. His **Scripture principle**, his law and gospel contrast held within a **covenant** frame, and his robust **Christocentrism** in justification align deeply with the **Westminster Standards**. His emphasis on preaching as **means of grace** and his catechetical labors anticipate Reformed confessionalism at its best. Yet several tensions invite refinement. First, the heavy reliance on **Ramist method** can flatten redemptive-historical contours, risking a deductive moralism where the storyline of **covenant history** should carry more weight. A Westminster alternative holds the priority of the historia salutis alongside the **ordo salutis**, integrating the unfolding **covenants** to prevent atomistic casuistry. Second, the **practical syllogism** as ordinary instrument of assurance can drift into introspective probation if **fides reflexa** eclipses **fides directa**. A balanced Reformed account grounds assurance principally in the promises of God and the **Spirit's inward witness**, with the **fruits of obedience** as secondary confirming evidences. Third, Perkins's presentation of **double predestination** occasionally reads as speculative decretalism when detached from the **covenant of grace** and the public **means of grace**. Classical Reformed clarity is maintained when election is preached through Christ and his benefits rather than as a naked decree. Finally, while Perkins affirms the spiritual presence in the **Supper**, his pastoral theology could strengthen sacramental objectivity as a Christ-appointed anchor for weak consciences. With these calibrations, Perkins remains a vital teacher whose **practical divinity** continues to tutor a church committed to **Scripture**, **covenant**, and **Christ**.
