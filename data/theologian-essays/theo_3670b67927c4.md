### TITLE
Ethics under commanding grace: a Barthian account of being-in-action ordered by union with Christ.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Scottish Reformed context**, post-Barthian retrieval, University of Aberdeen leadership since 2019, shaped by late modern debates over **ethics, sanctification, and dogmatics**.
- **Constructive dogmatics** grounded in **Christological actualism** and **ecclesial theology**, a method that correlates **doctrine of God** with **moral ontology** and **divine command ethics**.
- **Being in Action: The Theological Shape of the Ethical Vision of Karl Barth** 2007; **The Cambridge Companion to Reformed Theology** 2016, co-editor; **Karl Barth: A Guide for the Perplexed** 2017; **The Oxford Handbook of Karl Barth** 2019, co-editor.
- Core categories: **being-in-action**, **grace and command**, **union with Christ**, **analogia relationis**, **actualistic ontology**, **justification and sanctification**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Karl Barth**, **John Calvin**, **Eberhard Jüngel**, **John Webster**, and debates with **virtue ethics** and **natural law** traditions.
- Reformed reception cue: warmly received in **Barth studies** and constructive **Reformed ethics**, queried by confessional readers on **covenant theology**, **Scripture**, and **natural theology**.

### ESSAY
Paul T. Nimmo emerges from the contemporary **Reformed tradition** as a dogmatician who unites **ethics** and **soteriology** within a strongly **Christological** frame. His career is anchored in the Scottish academy and the broader anglophone retrieval of **Karl Barth**, culminating in his appointment to the **King’s Chair of Systematic Theology at the University of Aberdeen** in 2019. The post-2000 renaissance in **Barth studies** formed the historical matrix for Nimmo’s project, with new editions and handbooks providing a scholarly platform for rethinking **moral theology** through the lens of **grace**. Contextually, he works downstream of **Eberhard Jüngel’s** reading of **Barth’s actualism**, the conviction that God’s **being-in-act** underwrites the history of divine **grace and command**, and he often aligns this ontological thesis with concerns native to **Calvin’s** emphasis on **union with Christ** and the **duplex gratia** of justification and sanctification. This is a distinctively ecclesial endeavor: doctrine ordered to discipleship, a **dogmatics for ethics**.

The methodological engine that drives Nimmo’s corpus is an integrated account of **dogmatic ontology** and **moral agency**. He operates with a **Christological actualism** derived from **Barth**: Gottes Sein ist in seinem Tun, God’s being is in God’s act, and analogously human **being-in-action** is determined by the **gracious command** of the triune God. The methodological poles are thus the **doctrine of God** and the **doctrine of sanctification**, mediated by the **Holy Spirit** who unites believers to Christ, *unio cum Christo*, and summons them to responsible correspondence. Against autonomous moral theory, Nimmo advances an account of **divine command ethics** as gospel-shaped, refusing any tension between **grace** and **obedience**. He prefers **analogia relationis** over **analogia entis**, contending that creaturely action is ordered by participation in reconciled relation rather than by a metaphysical chain of being. His is an epistemic stance that privileges **revelation** without collapsing ethics into mere voluntarism, since the **command** is always the **form of grace** and the **grace** always the **content of command**.

The decisive programmatic statement is his monograph **Being in Action: The Theological Shape of the Ethical Vision of Karl Barth** 2007. There Nimmo reads **Church Dogmatics II.2** and **III.4** as the blueprint of a theological ethics in which the agent’s identity is constituted by **divine address**. Human personhood is teleologically described as **being-in-action** precisely because God’s **gracious election** and **reconciling command** determine the form and end of Christian life. Nimmo reconstrues **Barth’s divine command ethics** not as sheer heteronomy but as the moral ontology of **freedom-in-grace**. The ethical subject is summoned and enabled; responsibility is response, *responsio*, to the living Lord. That response is concrete and communal, unfolding in the **church’s practices** under **Word and sacrament**, where the Spirit’s sanctifying act grants what is commanded. By insisting on this ontological register, Nimmo addresses standard worries that **Barth** ignores **virtue** or **character**; he argues that the **habitus** of the Christian is graced existence-in-correspondence, a theological account that both critiques and reconfigures classical **virtue ethics**.

Expanding this vision, Nimmo has devoted considerable editorial and interpretive labor to mapping the terrain of **Barth’s theology** and the **Reformed tradition**. As co-editor of **The Cambridge Companion to Reformed Theology** 2016 with **David Fergusson**, he gathers essays that delineate **Reformed catholicity** around **Scripture**, **covenant**, **Word and sacrament**, and **sanctification**. This volume showcases Nimmo’s constructive instincts: to display the inner coherence of **Reformed dogmatics** as a living tradition oriented to **piety** and **mission**. His **Karl Barth: A Guide for the Perplexed** 2017 distills the architecture of the **Church Dogmatics**, clarifying the relation of **election**, **reconciliation**, and **redemption** to the shape of Christian life. His co-editing of **The Oxford Handbook of Karl Barth** 2019 with **Paul Dafydd Jones** further evidences his leadership in Barth studies, situating debates about **actualism**, **Scripture**, **politics**, and **ethics** within a global scholarly conversation. Across these platforms, Nimmo presses one theme: the **gracious lordship of Christ** determines the content and form of Christian existence.

Within soteriology, Nimmo’s work on **justification and sanctification** is central. He retrieves the **Reformed duplex gratia** through a **Barthian** lens, uniting **forensic** and **transformative** aspects within **union with Christ**. Against sequential construals of the **ordo salutis**, he argues for simultaneity under the primacy of **grace**: justification and sanctification are twin gifts, distinguished but never separated in the *unio*. In various essays, Nimmo shows how **Barth** refuses both moralism and quietism by rooting ethical transformation in the **indicative** of reconciliation, thereby preserving the **Reformed dictum** simul iustus et peccator. Sanctification is not human self-cultivation but the Spirit’s enactment of holy correspondence; the believer really is set apart, yet always in dependence on Christ’s alien righteousness. Nimmo thus aligns **Calvin’s autotheos** and **Barth’s Christological actualism** to insist that the ground and measure of holy life is God’s own holiness communicated in Christ by the Spirit.

Nimmo’s ethical construction also engages contemporary **virtue ethics** and **natural law** debates. Reading **Barth** against **eudaimonist** models, he resists any account in which the **summum bonum** is secured by creaturely teleology independent of **revelation**. Yet he does not reject **virtues** as such. Instead he recasts **virtus** as the Spirit’s graced **habitus** of correspondence to the divine **command**, aligning virtue language with **sanctification** rather than with autonomous moral theory. This creates space for critical dialogue with **Alasdair MacIntyre** and **Stanley Hauerwas**, while maintaining **divine command ethics** as theological grammar. For Nimmo, the normativity of the moral life is evangelical: the **lex** is fulfilled in **Christ**, the **law** serves the **gospel** as the Spirit conforms believers to Christ’s image, and the **third use of the law** is understood as the gracious discipline of communion. The criterion remains the particularity of **Jesus Christ** witnessed in **Scripture** and confessed by the **church**.

Ecclesiology and sacramentality consequently play significant roles in Nimmo’s account of **sanctification**. He locates moral formation under the **means of grace**, where **Word and sacrament** are the Spirit’s instruments to enact the **union with Christ** that sanctifies. In dialog with **Calvin’s duplex gratia**, Nimmo underscores that the **church’s practices** are not merely pedagogical but participatory signs that mediate Christ’s presence and command. This yields an ecclesial ethic in which the **Eucharist** and **baptism** shape Christian **being-in-action** by situating believers within Christ’s reconciling history, *historia salutis*. Importantly, Nimmo resists reducing ethics to politicized activism or private piety; the moral life is a public witness of the body of Christ, yet always a response to the **evangel**, not an autonomous program. The community exists as a **holy people** because the **Holy One** sanctifies, and its ethical posture flows from **thanksgiving** and **petition** rather than from self-assertion.

A distinctive feature of Nimmo’s dogmatics is the way **election** structures ethics. Following **Barth’s** reconfiguration in **Church Dogmatics II.2**, he sees the **election of Jesus Christ** as the fountainhead of the moral ontology of grace. The divine decision to be God for us in Christ grounds the assurance and summons of Christian life. Nimmo emphasizes that the **command** issues from **grace**, so the ethical task is not to secure divine favor but to participate in its mission. While cognizant of anxieties about **universalism**, he reads **Barth** as preserving the seriousness of **sin** and the reality of **judgment** within the history of **reconciliation**. This lets him articulate a sober account of **simul iustus et peccator** that maintains both evangelical confidence and penitential humility in the moral life. The point is not speculative soteriology but pastoral clarity: the **gospel** creates a people who act because they have been acted upon.

Nimmo’s scholarship is marked by an exegetical-dogmatic attentiveness to **Scripture** through the mediations of **Reformed confessions** and **Barth’s Church Dogmatics**. His prose often returns to the concrete grammar of **Christ’s lordship**, **the Spirit’s agency**, and **the Father’s electing love**, refusing to treat **ethics** as a freestanding discipline. He has also been a careful reader of **John Webster**, especially the latter’s insistence on **theological theology**, which anchors human thought in the **triune God’s** self-communication. Nimmo’s voice is pastoral without being pragmatic; he pursues clarity that can serve **catechesis**, **preaching**, and **moral discernment**. Editorially, his leadership in the **Cambridge Companion to Reformed Theology** 2016 and **Oxford Handbook of Karl Barth** 2019 has shaped a generation of discourse by setting the agenda for research on **Reformed dogmatics** and **Barthian ethics**. His projects illustrate an instinct for catholic breadth within confessional depth.

Across his oeuvre, certain conceptual tools recur. The paired notions of **grace and command** define the ethical horizon. The motif of **being-in-action** grants moral ontology a Christological contour. The use of **analogia relationis** keeps creaturely agency tethered to divine fellowship rather than to metaphysical sameness. The insistence on **union with Christ** integrates **justification and sanctification** without collapsing them. The ecclesial setting under **Word and sacrament** frames ethics as a communal vocation. And the doctrinal scaffolding of **election** ensures that assurance and summons coincide. These are not ad hoc emphases but an ordered dogmatics in which the **triune God**, **Christology**, **pneumatology**, **soteriology**, **ecclesiology**, and **ethics** coinhere.

Reformed and WTS evaluation. Nimmo’s Barthian retrieval supplies salutary emphases on **Christocentric grace**, **union with Christ**, and the **third use of the law** as evangelical obedience, which resonate with **Reformed confessionalism**. His stress on **Word and sacrament**, the **Spirit’s agency**, and the simultaneity of **justification and sanctification** guards against both moralism and antinomianism. Yet tensions arise for classical **Reformed orthodoxy** as institutionalized at **Westminster**. First, the **actualistic ontology** inherited from **Barth** risks blurring **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** as classically construed, even when Nimmo seeks to retain their truth in a history-embracing mode. Second, his rejection of **analogia entis** and deep suspicion of **natural theology** safeguard revelational primacy but leave less room for **general revelation** and **natural law** as confessed in **Westminster Confession of Faith** I.1 and I.6. Third, the strong centering of **election in Christ** can eclipse the federal grammar of **covenant of works** and **covenant of grace**, destabilizing the doctrinal architecture that orders **law and gospel** and grounds the **imputation** of Christ’s righteousness in a representative economy. Finally, the Barthian doctrine of **Scripture** that hovers in the background of Nimmo’s method remains a pressure point for **WTS** commitments to **inerrancy** and the **Scripture principle**. A constructive Reformed alternative would temper **actualism** with renewed affirmation of **classical theism**, retrieve **covenant theology** to structure ethics under creation-redemption consummation, and engage **virtue** and **natural law** as subordinate lights ordered by **special revelation**. Even so, Nimmo’s central claim holds real promise: that in Christ, **grace commands** and **command graces**, and thus Christian **being-in-action** is evangelical through and through.
