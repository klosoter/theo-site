### TITLE
Performative Word and Two Kinds of Righteousness: Robert Kolb’s Wittenberg Way for Contemporary Confession

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar American Lutheranism**, transatlantic Wittenberg studies, and early modern **confessionalization** historiography within a globalizing academy.
- Historical-theological **method** that treats doctrine as **rhetorical confession**, centered in the **performative Word** and **catechetical formation**.
- **The Book of Concord** 2000; **Bound Choice, Election, and Wittenberg Theological Method** 2005; **The Genius of Luther’s Theology** 2008; **Martin Luther: Confessor of the Faith** 2009; **Luther and the Stories of God** 2012; **Martin Luther and the Enduring Word of God** 2016.
- **Two kinds of righteousness**, **law and gospel**, **promissio** as divine speech, **baptismal identity**, **vocation**, **verbum efficax**, and the **Wittenberg school**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Robert Kolb, born 1941, emerged as a leading interpreter of **Wittenberg theology** by inhabiting both American Lutheran classrooms and the European archival world. His career at **Concordia Seminary, St. Louis** and earlier stints in Germany placed him at the crossing of confessional church life and critical scholarship. Against the backdrop of postwar **American Lutheran realignment** and the methodological turns of late twentieth-century historiography, Kolb forged a practice of reading **doctrine as confession** and **confession as rhetoric**. He situates theology within living communities shaped by **catechesis**, **liturgy**, and the **performative Word**, deploying the language of *promissio* and **verbum efficax** to insist that God works by saying. In this frame, **systematic theology** becomes a disciplined telling of the gospel that crafts **baptismal identity** and directs **vocation** in the world.

Kolb’s method marries the linguistic sensitivity of modern **Luther-exegesis** with early modern social history. He absorbs insights from **Heiko Oberman** on late medieval contexts and from **Oswald Bayer** on promise-shaped existence, yet he anchors his moves in the texts and school practices of **Wittenberg**. He draws on the historiography of **confessionalization** associated with **Heinz Schilling** and **Wolfgang Reinhard**, not to reduce theology to politics but to show how **confessions** functioned as pedagogical and juridical tools that formed communities. The result is a method that treats **law and gospel** as the church’s grammar, **two kinds of righteousness** as its anthropology, and the **means of grace** as God’s institutional speech.

The editorial project **The Book of Concord** 2000, produced with **Timothy J. Wengert**, exemplifies this method. The volume integrates textual scholarship with doctrinal commentary, presenting the **Augsburg Confession**, the **Apology**, the **Smalcald Articles**, the **Catechisms**, and the **Formula of Concord** as a coherent Wittenberg voice. Kolb models reading **confessions** not as relics but as living instruments of **catechesis**, shaped by the rhetoric of **promise** and the pastoral ordering of **law and gospel**.

#### 2. Key works and consolidation of a Wittenberg grammar
In **Bound Choice, Election, and Wittenberg Theological Method** 2005, Kolb reconstructs **Luther’s** and **Melanchthon’s** distinct but converging approaches to **election** and **freedom**. He argues that Wittenberg’s grammar proceeds from the sinner’s **bound will** under **the law** to the **freedom of the gospel** given in Christ’s **promise**. Election is not first a speculative decree but a **Christologically located promise** proclaimed to the baptized. By framing **predestination** within the performative act of the **Word**, Kolb elevates **proclamation** and **catechetical assurance** over system-building. The **Formula of Concord**’s approach to **election** thus becomes a pastoral doctrine of comfort rather than a metaphysical axiom about divine causality.

Alongside election, Kolb resourced the Wittenberg pair of **two kinds of righteousness** as the structural key to Lutheran anthropology. In **The Genius of Luther’s Theology** 2008, co-authored with **Charles P. Arand**, he presents **passive righteousness coram Deo** as receptive faith that trusts Christ’s alien righteousness and **active righteousness coram mundo** as the Spirit-driven life of love within **vocation**. This duality orders the **law and gospel** dynamic and clarifies how **justification** neither collapses into moralism nor forsakes the world. The **simul iustus et peccator** is here not a puzzle to be solved but a pastoral orientation that locates the Christian at the intersection of the **Word’s promise** and the neighbor’s need.

Kolb extends this grammar into a narrative mode in **Luther and the Stories of God** 2012. He shows how **biblical narratives** served **Luther’s catechesis** to craft Christian identity. The stories are not mere moral exempla but vehicles for God’s **performative speech** that inscribe the baptized into the **economy of grace**. This narratival appropriation of **verbum efficax** yields a pedagogy in which **the creed**, **the Lord’s Prayer**, and **the Ten Commandments** are woven into a living script of **repentance**, **faith**, and **vocation**.

#### 3. The Wittenberg school and Scripture as living voice
Kolb’s **Martin Luther and the Enduring Word of God** 2016 recovers the **Wittenberg school** as a community of interpretation intent on hearing Scripture as **viva vox evangelii**, the living voice of the gospel. He profiles **Melanchthon**, **Bugenhagen**, **Chemnitz**, and **Gerhard**, demonstrating how exegesis, catechesis, and pastoral care intertwined. For Kolb, **sola Scriptura** is not a naked formal principle but a confessional habitus trained to receive Scripture as **God’s address** that creates what it says. This opposes both modern historicisms that lock the text in the past and spiritualisms that detach the Spirit from the text’s public words. The Wittenberg disciplines of **lectio**, **disputatio**, and **praedicatio** display a hermeneutic of **promise** governed by **law and gospel**.

The biography **Martin Luther: Confessor of the Faith** 2009 compresses this vision into a portrait of **Luther** as preacher, exegete, and confessor. Kolb resists any reduction of Luther to a proto-modern individualist or a purely late medieval monk. Instead, he reads **Luther’s reforms** as the recovery of **God’s external Word**, the consolidation of baptismal **identity**, and the reordering of ecclesial life around the **catechisms**. The book thus underwrites Kolb’s conviction that **Reformation** is first a renewal of **proclamation** and **catechesis**, not a political revolution or philosophical shift.

Kolb’s broader historical work, notably **Confessing the Faith: Reformers Define the Church 1530–1580** 1991, situates the **Lutheran confessions** within the contested space of sixteenth-century **ecclesiology**. He traces how **Augsburg** defined the church as the assembly where the gospel is rightly taught and the **sacraments** are rightly administered, and how the **Formula of Concord** consolidated doctrinal boundaries without suffocating **pastoral care**. The conceptual center remains the **means of grace** as the Spirit’s instruments that give Christ.

#### 4. Law and gospel, catechesis, and vocation
Throughout his corpus, Kolb re-centers **law and gospel** as the hermeneutical and pastoral axis of Wittenberg theology. The **law** names God’s alien work that kills self-justification and orders civic life. The **gospel** names God’s proper work that gives Christ’s righteousness and raises faith. Kolb highlights the **proper distinction** not as a once-for-all definition but as a preached discernment enacted in **absolution**, **baptism**, and **the Supper**. The preacher’s task is diagnostic and therapeutic, disclosing sin under the **law** and delivering Christ under the **gospel**, so that **faith** receives and **love** serves.

The **catechetical** dimension is crucial. Kolb reads the **Small Catechism** as a compact curriculum for **identity formation**. The **Decalogue** exposes and directs, the **Creed** narrates the gift of **creation**, **redemption**, and **sanctification**, the **Lord’s Prayer** trains desire, and the **sacramental sections** ground life in the **means of grace**. He demonstrates how **homes**, **schools**, and **parishes** in the Wittenberg orbit formed a network of **catechesis** that generated **confessional culture**. His appropriation of **confessionalization** remains theological by showing how institutionalization served the **Word’s mission** rather than replacing it.

Kolb’s treatment of **vocation** threads **active righteousness coram mundo** back into the heart of Christian discipleship. He insists that Luther’s **two kinds of righteousness** never justify quietism. Instead, the **passive reception** of Christ frees persons for **active service** in estates of **family**, **church**, and **civic life**. Here he resists both works-righteousness and antinomian slippage by grounding good works in **God’s orders** as the neighbor’s claim, while preserving the **law and gospel** distinction so that Christ remains the sole righteousness **coram Deo**.

#### 5. Sacrament, church, and assurance
On the **sacraments**, Kolb emphasizes the **sacramental union** as Christ’s self-giving in and with the elements through the **Word of promise**. The Lord’s Supper is Christ’s testament, a **verbum visibile** that delivers forgiveness. Baptism establishes **baptismal identity**, an eschatological status enacted daily through **repentance** and **faith**. The **church** is where this **Word** sounds and these **sacramental words** are enacted, thus the classic **Augsburg marks** define the church. Kolb’s stress on **assurance** flows from this sacramental-proclamatory matrix. Because God binds himself to the **external Word**, assurance is not mined from introspection but received at the font, pulpit, and table.

Kolb’s reading of **election** coheres with this pastoral frame. By setting **predestination** within the concrete **means of grace**, he follows the **Formula of Concord** in repudiating curiously speculative inferences while affirming that the **gospel’s promise** is God’s chosen means to create faith. The believer’s **bound choice** is loosed not by discovering a decree in the hidden God but by hearing Christ’s voice in the **public Word**. Thus **assurance** is ecclesial and audible.

#### 6. Interlocutors, debates, and contemporary deployment
Kolb engages earlier generations of **Luther research** that emphasized existential hermeneutics. While appreciative of **Gerhard Ebeling** and **Bayer** on **theology as God’s speaking**, he resists dissolving dogma into pure experience by insisting on the **confessional forms** of doctrine. He shares with **Oberman** a commitment to late medieval backgrounds but refuses relativizing outcomes by portraying **Luther** as a medieval theologian without remainder. With **Timothy Wengert**, he has pursued editorial and interpretive work that bridges **LCMS** and **ELCA** scholarly worlds, while maintaining a robust **confessional Lutheran** identity.

Kolb also debates within Lutheranism over the **third use of the law**. Standing with the **Formula of Concord**, he affirms the law’s ongoing role in guiding the regenerate, yet he carefully subordinates this use to **gospel-driven identity** to avoid moralism. He treats **two kingdoms** as a schema for clarifying God’s different works rather than a withdrawal from public ethics. In the ecumenical sphere, Kolb has participated in dialogs where **justification by faith** and the **means of grace** are central, stressing the **public Word** and **sacramental realism** over consensual vagueness.

Kolb deploys this Wittenberg grammar to contemporary questions of **church life**, **mission**, and **catechesis**. He argues that late modern fragmentation requires renewed **catechetical communities** ordered by **law and gospel**, anchored in **baptismal identity**, and feeding on the **Supper**. He treats cultural change not primarily as a threat to be managed but as a context in which the **promising God** continues to create a people through **the preached Word**. His work thus equips pastors and teachers with a coherent Lutheran habit of mind that is both historically informed and missionally attuned.

#### 7. Style, pedagogy, and theological contribution
Kolb writes with a pedagogical clarity that belies substantial archival labor. His prose displays the **rhetorical shape of confession**, showing how **doctrine** both interprets Scripture and instructs believers. The key contribution is the consolidation of a **Wittenberg way** whose axes are **two kinds of righteousness**, **law and gospel**, and the **performative Word**. By casting **assurance**, **election**, **sacrament**, and **vocation** within this matrix, he offers a cohesive and pastorally potent system. The recurring emphasis on **identity** highlights how the **external Word** crafts persons who live in the **freedom of Christ** for the neighbor.

In curricular terms, Kolb’s work functions as a bridge between **historical theology** and **systematic theology**. The **Book of Concord** 2000 serves as a base text for graduate study. **Bound Choice** and **Genius of Luther’s Theology** provide analytic lenses. **Enduring Word of God** maps the Wittenberg ecosystem of exegesis and proclamation. Together these works model how to mine **confessional documents** for normative doctrinal use while staying sensitive to historical nuance. The antireductionist insistence that God acts by **saying** guards theology against psychologizing or pragmatizing tendencies.

Kolb’s role within **LCMS** scholarship adds a confessional firmness that is neither sectarian nor amorphous. His collaborations with **Wengert** and **Arand** show that rigorous confessional identity can sustain charitable dialogue. The practical upshot is a Lutheranism confident in **monergistic grace**, centered on **the means of grace**, and ordered by **catechesis** toward resilient congregational life.

#### 8. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **Westminster** vantage, Kolb’s achievement in retrieving **two kinds of righteousness**, the **performative Word**, and pastoral **law and gospel** is welcome. There is deep alignment on **sola Scriptura**, **monergism**, **original sin**, and **justification by faith**. His ecclesial emphasis on the **external Word** resonates with **means-of-grace** piety and opposes experientialism. Yet principled contrasts remain. First, his election-through-**promise** framing could be judged to insufficiently integrate **decretal election** within a comprehensive **covenant theology**, leaving the relation of the **decree** to the **means** underdeveloped where Reformed dogmatics unites both without collapsing either. Second, the **sacramental union** in the Supper stands over against a **spiritual real presence**, and Kolb’s tight bond of the **Word** to the elements may be seen as an overextension of **verbum efficax** into material ontology, where a Reformed reading would safeguard Christ’s **bodily locality** and the **ascension** while affirming true participation by the Spirit through **faith**. Third, Kolb’s accent on **two kinds of righteousness** functions analogously to **duplex gratia**, yet Reformed theology would press for thicker **union with Christ** language to coordinate **justification** and **sanctification** within the **covenant of grace**, and would press harder on the **third use of the law** for normative guidance. Finally, on **two kingdoms**, Kolb’s differentiation of God’s works is salutary, but a **covenantal** account of public life might resist basing civil ethics primarily in the **law’s first use** and **orders** and would instead articulate a positive **natural law** and **creational-covenantal** normativity under Christ’s mediatorial kingship. None of these tensions negate Kolb’s core contribution. They set the terms for fruitful exchange: a Reformed alternative would receive his **law and gospel** grammar, reframe **election** in explicit **covenantal** terms, articulate **union with Christ** as the bond of **duplex gratia**, and affirm a spiritual Supper while sustaining a robust **means-of-grace** ministry in the church.
