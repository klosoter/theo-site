### TITLE
Creativity as search in conceptual spaces—an interdisciplinary, computational account of mind and culture.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar cybernetics and early AI** within **British analytic philosophy** and the rise of **cognitive science** at **Sussex** in the 1960s.
- **Computational functionalism** with methodological **naturalism**, explanatory **mechanism**, and pluralist allegiance to both **symbolic AI** and **connectionism**.
- **Artificial Intelligence and Natural Man** 1977; **Computer Models of Mind** 1988; **The Creative Mind** 1990, rev. 2004; **Mind as Machine** 2006; **Creativity and Art** 2010; **AI Its Nature and Future** 2016.
- **Conceptual spaces**, **P-creativity versus H-creativity**, **combinational, exploratory, transformational creativity**, **levels and emergence**, **representation and search**.
- Dialogues with **A. M. Turing**, **H. A. Simon and A. Newell**, **D. Dennett**, **J. Searle**, **H. Dreyfus**, **R. Penrose**, and debates on **symbolic vs connectionist** AI.
- Reformed cue—test **computational personhood** and **creativity** against **Creator-creature distinction**, **imago Dei**, **providence and common grace**, and norms of **wisdom and truth**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Margaret A. Boden emerged as a leading theorist of **artificial intelligence** and **cognitive science** amid the ferment of postwar **cybernetics** and **British analytic philosophy**. Born in 1936 and educated in **philosophy and psychology** during the 1950s and early 1960s, she came of age intellectually as **Turing-style computation**, **information theory**, and the **symbolic AI** of **Simon and Newell** were reconfiguring the study of mind. The University of **Sussex** became her long-term base, where she helped institutionalize **interdisciplinary cognitive science** across **psychology**, **philosophy**, **computer science**, and **AI**. Boden’s formative interlocutors included **Gilbert Ryle** and **Michael Polanyi** on tacit knowledge, **Noam Chomsky** on rule-governed generativity, and critics such as **Hubert Dreyfus** who challenged the rationalist optimism of early AI. From this milieu she forged a method that blends **computational functionalism** with historical sensitivity to scientific research programs, anchoring explanation in **mechanisms**, **representations**, and **search** rather than in appeal to opaque faculties.

Boden’s epistemic stance is an explicit **naturalism** with a disciplined openness to **levels of description** and **emergence**. She argues that mental phenomena can be illuminated by **algorithmic models** that articulate the internal organization of thought, language, and action. A program is explanatory when its structures map onto the **conceptual spaces** that agents actually navigate, and when its constraints and heuristics generate novel yet intelligible behavior. Her method is neither eliminativist nor dualist. She affirms the legitimacy of **intentional idioms** alongside **computational realizations**, often invoking **Dennett’s intentional stance** while resisting both **mysterianism** and simplistic reductive materialism. The goal is **mechanistic intelligibility** without denial of complexity, a posture continuous with the best of **philosophy of science** from **Hempel** to **Kitcher** and compatible with a robust account of **scientific explanation** as model-based and multi-level.

#### 2. Major works and consolidations
The early programmatic statement **Artificial Intelligence and Natural Man** 1977 introduced a wide audience to the prospects of **AI** as a window on human cognition, arguing that **computational models** can both simulate and explain aspects of **reasoning**, **language**, and **problem solving**. **Computer Models of Mind** 1988 surveyed **symbolic architectures**, **production systems**, and **search strategies**, while recognizing the rising importance of **connectionism** and **neural networks** in modeling associative and parallel processes. Her signature thesis matured in **The Creative Mind** 1990, revised 2004, which framed creativity not as ineffable inspiration but as **structured exploration within conceptual spaces**. The magisterial **Mind as Machine** 2006 provided a two-volume history and analysis of **computing**, **AI**, and **cognitive science**, synthesizing decades of debate about **representation**, **learning**, and **consciousness**. **Creativity and Art** 2010 applied her framework to **aesthetics**, while **AI Its Nature and Future** 2016 distilled her balanced assessment of **AI’s capacities and limits**, engaging public concerns about **ethics**, **autonomy**, and **societal impact**.

Across these works Boden refines a unified program. She insists that to explain mind we must specify the **space of possibilities** a system inhabits, the **generative rules** that structure that space, the **heuristics** that guide search, and the **constraints** that prune the search tree. Explanation is not mere behavior duplication but **mechanistic reproduction** of the underlying **representational organization**. This is a methodological thesis with ontological restraint. Boden continually clarifies that **strong AI** claims about full **consciousness** or **qualia** are not entailed by success in modeling **creativity** or **intelligence**, though she remains open to the possibility that future architectures might exhibit richer forms of **agency** under stringent criteria.

#### 3. Creativity as conceptual exploration and transformation
Boden’s most influential doctrinal contribution is a tripartite account of **creativity** anchored by the distinction between **P-creativity** and **H-creativity**. **P-creativity** denotes psychological originality for a given individual, the first-time crossing of a conceptual frontier in a personal cognitive history. **H-creativity** denotes historical originality recognized by a community as unprecedented. Both varieties are analyzed through three forms of process. **Combinational creativity** produces novelty by unfamiliar juxtaposition of existing ideas. **Exploratory creativity** searches a predefined **conceptual space** structured by rules or constraints, making novel but permissible moves that were previously unvisited. **Transformational creativity** alters the generative rules themselves, reshaping the **conceptual space** so that previously impossible ideas become possible.

This framework reframes debates about **machine creativity**. If a program can articulate a **conceptual space**, traverse it, and in some cases revise its own generative constraints, then its outputs may count as **P-creative** relative to the program and even as **H-creative** if the sociocultural community judges them valuable. Boden emphasizes that **novelty** is insufficient by itself. **Creativity** includes **value**, that is, norm-guided recognition by competent judges within a tradition. Evaluation is therefore social and historical while production is computational and cognitive. The triad of **combinational**, **exploratory**, and **transformational** creativity gives precise handles for comparative analysis across **mathematics**, **music**, **visual art**, and **scientific discovery**, mapping local rules and global transformations that generate surprise.

#### 4. Representation, search, and the symbolic–connectionist debate
Boden defends a robust conception of **representation** against both **anti-cognitivist** and **eliminativist** critiques. Early **symbolic AI** advocated by **Newell and Simon** offered explicit structures for plans, goals, and inference, yet often suffered brittleness and poor learning. **Connectionist** models introduced **distributed representation**, graceful degradation, and data-driven generalization, but risked opacity about **rule-like competence**. Boden’s alignment is a principled pluralism. She argues that different cognitive tasks recruit different representational economies, and that hybrid models can reconcile **rule-governed generativity** with **statistical learning**. The key is to demonstrate how an architecture encodes the **constraints** of a **conceptual space** and how **search** exploits those constraints to generate novelty with intelligibility.

In sustained dialogue with **Dreyfus**, **Searle**, and **Dennett**, Boden clarifies boundary claims. Against **Dreyfus’s** charge that skillful coping eludes formalization, she points to **subsymbolic** and **probabilistic** methods that recapture context sensitivity. Against **Searle’s** Chinese Room, she emphasizes that the explanatory target is not bare syntax but the entire **system-level organization** that produces **semantics** through embedded practice, world coupling, and appropriate training regimes. With **Dennett** she affirms that intentional idioms are real patterns that track underlying design, even if implemented by **multiple realizability**. The upshot is a defense of **computational explanation** as a family of models fitting diverse cognitive ecologies.

#### 5. Levels, emergence, and the scope of mechanism
A recurring theme in Boden’s corpus is **levels and emergence**. She distinguishes **weak emergence**—novel systemic properties arising from organized interactions—from claims of **strong emergence** that posit ontologically irreducible properties. Her sympathies lean toward **weak emergence** allied with **multi-level explanation**, where psychological regularities supervene on neural dynamics without collapsing into them. She mobilizes examples from **perception**, **learning**, and **creative insight** to show how **constraint satisfaction** in networks can yield global gestalt shifts, while **symbolic re-representation** can open new problem decompositions. This dual-aspect picture resists both **explanatory vacuity** and **reductionist triumphalism**.

Boden’s historical sensibility, displayed in **Mind as Machine**, reads the trajectory from **cybernetics** through **GOFAI** to **machine learning** as a sequence of research programs each disclosing particular **mechanisms**. Her counsel is methodological humility with cumulative ambition—prefer **model pluralism** and **cross-validation** by psychological evidence, computational performance, and phenomenological fit. That stance preserves space for discussing **consciousness**, **intentionality**, and **value** without importing extra-scientific dogmas. It also grounds the plausibility of **machine creativity** in terms of structured **search** rather than mystified genius.

#### 6. Art, science, and the norms of evaluative judgment
In **Creativity and Art** and related essays Boden develops an account of **aesthetic creativity** that unites production and evaluation. A **conceptual space** for an art form is constituted by stylistic rules, genre conventions, and available techniques. Innovation emerges through **combinational** play, **exploratory** elaboration, or **transformational** rule change. Yet whether an artifact counts as **creative** depends on **normative appraisal** by informed communities whose judgments are historically located and corrigible. This duality allows her to acknowledge **normativity** without positing transcendental values. Value is neither purely subjective nor metaphysically absolute but arises from disciplined practices that stabilize **criteria** for excellence.

By applying the same categories to **scientific discovery**, Boden gives an integrated picture in which **mathematical proof strategies**, **heuristics**, and **model building** are understood as movements within and revisions of scientific **conceptual spaces**. The **hunches** of discovery are domesticated as **structured searches** where constraints lead to insight. She shows how **explanation**, **prediction**, and **unification** function as evaluative norms that make scientific creativity more than mere novelty. This account complements her computational commitments by placing **rational appraisal** alongside **algorithmic generation**.

#### 7. Limits, personhood, and the future of AI
In **AI Its Nature and Future** Boden canvasses prospects and perils of **autonomous systems**, **machine learning**, and **societal integration**. She is skeptical of inflated promises about near-term **strong AI**, yet confident that systems can display genuine **creativity**, **learning**, and **adaptation** in narrow domains. Her position on **personhood** is guarded. She distinguishes **intelligence** and **creativity** from **conscious subjectivity**, urging careful criteria before ascribing **moral status**. She stresses the importance of **transparency**, **accountability**, and **value alignment** as engineering and policy norms, positioning **ethics** as an integral constraint on the **conceptual spaces** within which AI develops. The future, on her view, belongs to deepening hybrids that integrate **symbolic reasoning**, **probabilistic methods**, and **neural architectures**, guided by societal norms that shape evaluation of outputs as **valuable** or **dangerous**.

Boden’s overall anthropology is a sophisticated **functionalism**. Human beings are organisms whose mental powers can be partially captured by **computational models**, though not exhausted by any single model. She embraces **embodiment** and **situatedness** as additional constraints on viable explanations, anticipates fruitful exchange with **neuroscience**, and resists both despair and hubris regarding AI. In all of this, the abiding conviction remains that **creativity** is a species of **structured search** rather than an uncaused spark.

#### 8. Theological interface—anthropology, culture, and providence
For systematic theology the Boden corpus is most salient for **theological anthropology**, **creation and providence**, and the doctrine of **culture**. Her account of **creativity as conceptual exploration** resonates with the Reformed doctrine of the cultural mandate under **providence**, where human making unfolds the *ordo creationis* through lawful constraint and inventive labor. The insistence on **constraints** and **generative rules** mirrors older Christian convictions about **created natures** and **secondary causes**. Her **levels and emergence** approach offers tools for articulating **soul-body holism** without resort to Cartesian dualism, supporting a non-competitive account of divine **causality** and creaturely **agency** as ordered *ad extra* by **God’s governance**.

Yet Boden’s **naturalism** also presses theology to clarify **imago Dei**. If **creativity** is explicable as **structured search**, then the distinctiveness of human persons is not the presence of a magical power but a vocation within ordered spaces of possibility under **wisdom**. Theology can appropriate her **conceptual spaces** as created repertoires of form and law, seeing **transformational creativity** as an image, under grace, of the new possibilities opened by **redemption**—a kind of cultural **nova creatio** that reorients norms as well as outputs. At the same time the locus of **value** cannot be grounded finally in communal consensus but in **divine goodness** that disciplines and judges our evaluations. Boden’s coupling of **production** and **evaluation** invites reflection on **tradition** and **canon** as Spirit-formed communities that test novelty by **truth** and **edification**.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical Reformed and WTS perspective, Boden offers substantial **common-grace** insight into **human making**, **lawful order**, and **secondary causality**, yet her methodological **naturalism** leaves unresolved tensions about **normativity**, **personhood**, and **teleology**. First, on **revelation and reason**, her account treats **value** as emergent from communities rather than anchored in **God’s self-attesting truth**. A covenantal epistemology would insist that the **criteria** for judging creative goods derive analogically from **special revelation** and are not reducible to sociological stabilization. Second, on **imago Dei and personhood**, her **functionalism** risks conflating **agency** with **information processing**. A Reformed anthropology affirms holistic **embodiment** and appreciates **algorithmic models** as descriptions of creaturely processes, yet it grounds personhood in **covenantal relation** and **responsibility coram Deo**, not in computational sophistication. Third, on **providence and emergence**, her **weak emergence** can be fruitfully received as a created order of **multi-level laws**, but theology must stress the **Creator-creature distinction** and **asymmetrical dependence**, cautioning against any slide toward ontological **self-sufficiency** of systems. Finally, regarding **machine creativity**, Reformed thought can acknowledge genuine artifact novelty as secondary-cause gift while denying **artifact moral agency** or **spiritual accountability** apart from human authorship. The better alternative in principle is an **analogical ontology** in which **conceptual spaces** are aspects of **creational wisdom**, **transformational creativity** is redirected by **grace** within the **covenant**, and evaluative norms are tethered to **Scripture** and **the mind of Christ** rather than to immanent consensus alone.
