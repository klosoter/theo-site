### TITLE
A self-authenticating New Testament canon grounded in Reformed epistemology and second-century ecclesial history.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary evangelical** setting shaped by debates over **canon, text, and early Christianity**, with the **Bauer–Ehrman thesis** and postcritical **historical method** in the background.
- **Methodological blend** of **Reformed epistemology**, **Van Tilian presuppositionalism**, and rigorous **patristic and manuscript history**, aiming at **autopistia Scripturae** and the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum**.
- **Key works**: **The Heresy of Orthodoxy** 2010, **Canon Revisited** 2012, **The Early Text of the New Testament** ed. 2012, **The Question of Canon** 2013, **Christianity at the Crossroads** 2017, **The Gospel of the Savior** 2005, **A Biblical-Theological Introduction to the New Testament** ed. 2016.
- **Central categories**: **self-authenticating canon**, **divine qualities**, **apostolicity**, **corporate reception**, **providential exposure**, **autopistia Scripturae**, **regula fidei**, **codex culture**, **nomina sacra**, **orthodoxy and heresy**.
- **Interlocutors and influences**: **Calvin**, **Bavinck**, **Van Til**, **Plantinga**, **Larry Hurtado**, against **Bart Ehrman**, **Lee Martin McDonald**, **Harry Gamble**, engaging **Walter Bauer**, **Bruce Metzger**, and **Irenaeus**.
- **Reformed reception**: strong alignment with **WCF 1.4–1.5** on **Scripture’s authority** and **Spirit’s witness**, with questions about **aesthetic criteria**, **Old Testament canon**, and the weighting of **corporate reception**.

### ESSAY
Michael J. Kruger has emerged as a leading contemporary evangelical voice on **canon**, **text**, and **early Christian identity**, operating from a confessional Reformed center while engaging the complex terrain of modern **historical-critical scholarship**. As President of **Reformed Theological Seminary, Charlotte**, and a scholar trained in early Christianity and **manuscript culture**, his work interfaces with the late twentieth and early twenty-first century debates framed by **Walter Bauer’s** legacy and its revival in **Bart D. Ehrman**, the sociological narratives of **Harry Gamble** and **Lee Martin McDonald**, and the patristic turn exemplified by figures like **Larry W. Hurtado**. Kruger’s academic posture is both historical and theological. He reads the second century as the crucible of Christian self-definition, and he rearticulates **autopistia Scripturae** as canon’s intrinsic authority, received by the church through the **testimonium Spiritus Sancti internum**. The result is a model that draws from **Calvin** and **Bavinck**, resonates with **Van Til**, borrows analytic clarity from **Alvin Plantinga**, and presses a robust claim: the canon is **self-authenticating**, not bestowed by church or reconstructed by critical historians, but recognized under divine providence by the people of God.

Kruger’s method can be summarized as theological-historical realism. He presupposes the **triune God’s** self-revelation in Christ and Scripture, contending that the church’s recognition of the **New Testament canon** is an act of discernment rather than conferment. The core of this method appears programmatically in **Canon Revisited** 2012, where he distinguishes ontology and epistemology. Ontologically the canon exists by divine origin and **inspiration**. Epistemologically the canon is known through a proper environment that God provides. He unfolds three coordinated components of this epistemic environment. First, **providential exposure** ensures that the church encounters the right books. Second, the books display **attributes of canonicity**, which he specifies as **divine qualities** such as beauty, harmony, and efficacy, **apostolicity** both authorship and close apostolic association, and **corporate reception** in the catholic church. Third, the **internal testimony of the Holy Spirit** grants the cognitive and moral conditions for right recognition. This is a confessional retrieval of **autopistia Scripturae**, the self-attesting authority of Scripture, bridged to contemporary philosophy by **Reformed epistemology**, which normalizes properly basic beliefs and allows a virtuous rather than vicious circularity. The circle is Scripture attests itself and the Spirit attests Scripture, which mirrors all ultimate authorities, including reason or sense perception, that finally appeal to themselves.

Against the dominant extrinsic accounts of canon formation, Kruger’s **The Question of Canon** 2013 attacks what he calls five status-quo assumptions. He targets the claim that the canon is a late fourth-century artifact, counters the idea that earliest Christians were disinterested in written norms, questions the belief that canon is purely a response to heresy, rejects the notion that criteria like **apostolicity** were late, and refuses the thesis that the concept of canon was alien to the apostolic era. Kruger argues for an intrinsic trajectory. The apostolic proclamation generated apostolic writings with covenantal authority, already being collected, circulated, and treated as Scripture in the second century, with roots in the first. He appeals to **Irenaeus** on the fourfold Gospel, the **Muratorian Fragment** for emerging lists, the role of **Athanasius’s Festal Letter 39** as a late witness but not the constitutive act, and the distinctive **codex culture** of early Christians. Drawing on **Hurtado**, he emphasizes early use of the **codex**, the phenomenon of **nomina sacra**, and the scribal network that reflects a self-conscious textual identity. The canon was thus not forced upon the church by heresy or council but was inherent to the church’s **regula fidei** and mission.

In **The Heresy of Orthodoxy** 2010, coauthored with **Andreas J. Köstenberger**, Kruger offers a sustained critique of the **Bauer–Ehrman thesis** that early Christianity was a cacophony of equal theologies from which orthodoxy later emerged victorious by power. Kruger contends for an early and substantive **orthodoxy** that, while contested, was neither accidental nor late. He brings forward **Irenaeus’s** coherent deployment of the **regula fidei**, the Christological monotheism evidenced in early worship, and the apologetic and pastoral responses to **Marcion**, **Valentinus**, and **Montanus** as acts of preservation rather than innovation. This line continues in **Christianity at the Crossroads** 2017, where Kruger narrates the second century as the decisive period that shaped the church’s future. He discusses the development of leadership patterns, the clarification of the **canon**, the resilience under persecution, the growth of liturgical regularities such as Lord’s Day gatherings, and the intellectual defense of the faith through apologists like **Justin Martyr**. The canon is not the only plotline, but its consolidation is integral to the church’s emergence as a catholic and missionary body formed by a Scripture-centered identity.

Kruger’s interest in manuscripts and textual transmission grounds his canonical claims in material history. As coeditor with **Charles E. Hill** of **The Early Text of the New Testament** 2012, he highlights the early and geographically diverse witnesses to the **New Testament text**, arguing for substantial textual stability and pushing back on narratives that foreground uncontrolled textual plurality. He stresses the significance of early citations, the habits of Christian scribes, and features such as the **nomina sacra** that reflect reverence and theological intentionality. In debate with **Ehrman’s** proposals about corruption and diversity, Kruger maintains that while variation exists, the overall picture exhibits a stable core, an implied canon, and a consciousness of sacred text. His earlier monograph **The Gospel of the Savior** 2005, an analysis of **P.Oxy. 840**, situates apocryphal Jesus traditions in relation to the canonical Gospels, reinforcing the primacy of the canonical four in early reception and clarifying the derivative and often posterior character of extracanonical fragments.

The constructive center of Kruger’s contribution is his articulation of a **self-authenticating canon**. He catalogs and critiques two classes of rival models. Community-determined models, including certain **Roman Catholic** and some **postliberal** accounts, make the church’s decision constitutive of canon. Historically-determined models, often **critical-protestant**, place canonical status at the mercy of reconstructable events like councils, usage statistics, or supposed original authorship. Kruger replies that both are extrinsic and unstable. His alternative argues that God’s act of inspiration and providence supplies the intrinsic authority of the books and the sufficient conditions for their recognition by the church. The key insight is that **attributes of canonicity** are not freestanding criteria that stand over Scripture, but indicators that shine from Scripture and are perceived by a Spirit-renewed community. Thus **divine qualities** cohere with apostolic roots and corporate reception, and their convergence is recognized because the **Spirit** operates through the **Word** in the **church**. This model allows theological confidence without denying the instrumentality of historical evidence.

Kruger’s narrative of canon formation employs careful distinctions. He differentiates between the date a book becomes Scripture and the date it is recognized as such, thereby defusing the late-canon argument by locating authority at inspiration. He uses the second-century evidence to argue for an early canonical core that includes the **four Gospels**, the Pauline corpus as a unified unit already perceived as authoritative, and a growing recognition of Catholic Epistles. He treats outliers like **Hebrews** and **2 Peter** with nuancing attention, noting debates but insisting that dispute does not imply absence of truth or lack of divine authority. He integrates **Irenaeus’s** use of Scripture, the **Muratorian Fragment’s** irregular list, and **Athanasius’s** later clarity into a single providential story. Councils and lists are thus recognitive seals rather than constitutive acts. This posture challenges the appeal to a necessary infallible magisterium and strengthens the Reformation claim that the church is ministerial, not magisterial, to the **Word of God**.

Although the New Testament is his primary focus, Kruger addresses adjacent questions. He interacts with **Roman Catholic** and **Eastern Orthodox** appeals to conciliar authority, arguing that the fourth-century Festal Letter and regional synods like **Hippo** and **Carthage** did not create the canon. He registers sympathy with **Bruce Metzger’s** sober cataloging of patristic evidence while resisting the impression that historical description can ground canonical normativity. He gestures to the **Old Testament canon** debates, affirming Protestant lines that align with the Hebrew Bible and challenging the **deuterocanonical** expansion, though his detailed labors remain on the New Testament side. In pastoral-apologetic mode, he extends this work to the university context, as in **Surviving Religion 101** 2021, where he addresses doubt, pluralism, and skepticism, drawing on the same nexus of **Scripture’s self-attestation**, communal life, and intellectual patience.

The ecclesial implications of Kruger’s work are visible in his editorial and institutional leadership. As editor of **A Biblical-Theological Introduction to the New Testament** 2016, he models a canonical-theological reading that places each New Testament book within the unity of **redemptive history** and the coherence of **covenant theology**. His presidency at **RTS Charlotte** positions his scholarship within a broader project of training pastors who handle Scripture as the **norma normans non normata**, the norming norm. His regular engagement with lay readers, pastors, and scholars exhibits the confidence and tone of an evangelical catholicity centered on Christ, nourished by the fathers, and anchored by the Reformation’s doctrine of the **Word and Spirit**.

Theologically, Kruger’s synthesis offers a compelling answer to the crisis of authority that shadows both the academy and the church. By marrying **historical attentiveness** to **dogmatic clarity**, he invites a recovery of **autopistia Scripturae** without historical naivete. He rejects a reductionist positivism that treats canonicity as a late artifact while eschewing a blunt fideism that refuses to read the sources. His use of **Reformed epistemology** underwrites confidence in **properly basic beliefs** about Scripture, while his **Van Tilian** instincts insist that only under the **covenant Lord** can reason function rightly. The result is a thick account of **canon** as theologically grounded, historically manifested, and ecclesially received, held together by the active agency of the **Holy Spirit**.

Reformed and WTS evaluation. Kruger’s project resonates markedly with **Westminster Confession of Faith 1.4–1.5**, where Scripture’s authority depends wholly on God, and the full persuasion and assurance of its infallible truth arises from the **Spirit’s inward work**. His **self-authenticating canon** coheres with **autopistia Scripturae** and strengthens the ministerial role of the church. Two tensions invite refinement. First, the appeal to **divine qualities** risks aesthetic subjectivism unless more explicitly tethered to the objective **divine perfections** in a robust doctrine of **analogia fidei** and to the covenantal effects of the Word, as in **Vos** and **Bavinck**. Second, the weighting of **corporate reception** as an attribute must avoid slipping into a soft **consensus ecclesiae** principle. A more explicit deployment of **covenantal epistemology** in the line of **Van Til** and **Frame** could clarify that reception is a Spirit-wrought covenantal obedience rather than a sociological majority. On historical matters, New Testament focus leaves **Old Testament canon** questions less fully developed, though his trajectory is soundly Protestant. Overall, the WTS tradition can affirm Kruger’s principial stance on **revelation, canon, and church**, while proposing thicker accounts of covenantal normativity and catholicity that guard against both subjectivism and historicism.
