### TITLE
From creation to new creation: a biblical theology of the royal-priestly imago Dei and the earthly hope of resurrection life

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Jamaican diaspora, Reformational ICS Toronto, Neo-Calvinist milieu** shaping a postcolonial yet Kuyperian biblical theology in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries.
- **Canonical-biblical theology, ANE background studies, worldview analysis, missional hermeneutic** under a chastened critical realism attentive to narrative coherence.
- **The Transforming Vision** 1984, **Truth Is Stranger Than It Used to Be** 1995, **The Liberating Image** 2005, **A New Heaven and a New Earth** 2014, **Abraham's Silence** 2021.
- **Imago Dei as royal-priestly vocation, creation-fall-redemption-new creation, cultural mandate, lament as faithful protest, new earth eschatology** as central categories.
- **Abraham Kuyper, Herman Bavinck, Herman Dooyeweerd, Al Wolters, Brian Walsh, N. T. Wright, John Walton** as principal influences and interlocutors.
- **Reformed reception cue**: strong **creational goodness** and **new earth** emphasis energizes Kuyperian mission, with questions about **covenant centrality**, **federalism**, and **Christological concentration** for classical Reformed dogmatics.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
J. Richard Middleton emerges from the **Jamaican diaspora** into the **Neo-Calvinist** orbit of the Institute for Christian Studies in Toronto in the 1980s, where **Herman Dooyeweerd** and **Al Wolters** provided a reformational grammar for reading Scripture as public truth. The **Kuyperian** conviction that every square inch belongs to Christ supplied the imaginative frame for his later biblical theology of **creation** and **new creation**. Trained in philosophical hermeneutics and then in biblical studies, Middleton learned to move between **worldview analysis** and close exegesis, reading the Bible as a coherent **canonical narrative** from Genesis to Revelation. The context of Caribbean postcoloniality sharpened his sensitivity to **ideology critique**, and the rise of late modern **postmodernity** demanded a credible account of truth without foundationalist triumphalism. This produced a method best described as chastened **critical realism** with a **missional hermeneutic**, where Scripture forms a community to inhabit the true story of the world. By the 2010s he taught at **Northeastern Seminary** in Rochester, embodying a pastoral-academic vocation that aligns with the **Neo-Calvinist** integration of scholarship and mission.

Middleton's methodological signature blends **ANE background studies**, **narrative intertextuality**, and a reformational **creation-fall-redemption-new creation** schema. The biblical drama, not a speculative metaphysic, supplies the scaffolding for doctrine. He refuses a dualism between **exegesis** and **systematics**, insisting that doctrine should be the distilled wisdom of sustained readings of texts like **Genesis 1–2**, **Psalm 8**, **Romans 8**, **1 Corinthians 15**, and **Revelation 21–22**. The resulting stance is neither a narrow **historical-critical** reduction nor a proof-texting biblicism, but a **canonical-biblical theology** ordered to the church's participation in the *missio Dei*. This method foregrounds **creational monotheism**, *creatio ex nihilo*, and an **eschatological telos** in which the earth itself is the arena of God’s **kingdom**.

#### 2. Early program: worldview and postmodernity
Middleton's first phase is collaborative and programmatic. With **Brian J. Walsh**, he authored **The Transforming Vision: Shaping a Christian World View** in 1984, a manifesto articulating a **biblical worldview** against both **modernist scientism** and privatized **pietism**. The work harnesses the **Neo-Calvinist** axiom of **sphere sovereignty** and the reformational critique of **idolatry**, presenting Scripture as a public story that re-educates desire, economics, and politics. The theme of **exile** as a posture of faithfulness, drawn from **Jeremiah 29**, anticipates Middleton's later emphasis on **diasporic mission**. The 1984 book situates the **cultural mandate** of Genesis 1 as neither dominionism nor retreat, but a **royal-priestly** service forming humane culture under God.

A decade later, **Truth Is Stranger Than It Used to Be** 1995 confronts **postmodernity** directly. Middleton and **Walsh** discern in postmodern suspicion both a judgment on **modernity's** idolatrous metanarratives and an opportunity for **narrative witness** grounded in **Scripture**. Their proposal rejects **relativism** by commending the **biblical drama** as a storied account of reality that generates a community of **justice** and **hospitality**. Here Middleton's commitment to **creation-fall-redemption-new creation** becomes explicit as a hermeneutical key. The book's engagement with **popular culture** and **consumer capitalism** demonstrates an early synthesis of **Neo-Calvinist** cultural engagement with a prophetic critique of **empire**, themes that will mature in his later exegesis of **Revelation 21–22** and **Romans 8**.

#### 3. The Liberating Image and the democratic imago Dei
Middleton's landmark monograph **The Liberating Image: The Imago Dei in Genesis 1** 2005 reframes the **imago Dei** through rigorous **ANE comparison** and **canonical exegesis**. Building on royal ideology where kings were images of the deity, he argues that **Genesis 1:26–28** radicalizes and democratizes the motif. Every human being is constituted as God's **royal-priestly** representative, commissioned for **stewardly dominion** that cultivates shalom. He critiques the traditional **substantive** view that locates the image in rational capacities and the **relational** view that reduces it to interpersonal communion, advocating a **functional-royal** account in which vocation, not essence, is primary. Yet he integrates **priestly** temple themes, showing how **Sabbath** and cosmic **sanctuary** imagery bind human vocation to worship.

Theologically, this yields a social ethic. The **imago Dei** confers a sacred dignity that delegitimizes **oppression**, **patriarchy**, and **violence**, a claim Middleton grounds in the textual horizon of **Genesis 9:6** and the democratizing trajectory of **Psalm 8**. He reads the **cultural mandate** as a call to craft humane culture under God, not resource extraction. Here **Neo-Calvinist** insights converge with a **postcolonial** sensitivity, insisting that dominion is cruciform service modeled by the **royal** and **priestly** people. This account illumines **Christology**, since the **second Adam** in **1 Corinthians 15** embodies and restores the fractured **image**, and it informs **ecclesiology** by casting the church as a renewed **royal priesthood**. The imago thesis thus links **creation** to **eschatology**, anchoring Middleton's later work on the **new earth**.

#### 4. Eschatology as new earth hope
In **A New Heaven and a New Earth: Reclaiming Biblical Eschatology** 2014, Middleton mounts a comprehensive case for **earthly eschatology** against what he calls **Platonizing** or escapist visions of heaven. He assembles a canonical argument from **Isaiah 65–66**, **Romans 8**, **1 Corinthians 15**, and **Revelation 21–22**, contending that Scripture promises not annihilation but **renewal**, not evacuation but **resurrection** and **cosmic liberation**. The famous phrase the **glory and honor of the nations** in **Revelation 21:24–26** becomes a clue for cultural **transfiguration**, where human creativity is purged and offered in worship. Middleton treats the **continuity and discontinuity** of the present order with eschatological consummation by deploying images of **refining fire** and the **seed-body** analogy in **1 Corinthians 15**. The aim is neither naive progressivism nor pure rupture, but a grace-wrought transformation of creation to its *telos*.

This eschatology is not speculative. It interacts with **dispensational premillennialism**, **amillennial spiritualizing**, and modern **rapture** theologies, challenging them with the **creational monotheism** that saturates Scripture. He echoes and extends **N. T. Wright** on **resurrection** and kingdom, while retaining **Neo-Calvinist** confidence in **creational goodness** and **cultural calling**. The pastoral force of the book lies in a reorientation of Christian hope toward **embodied life** and **public justice** now as foretaste of the **kingdom**, reframing ethics, work, and politics under the arc of **new creation**. The eschatological horizon integrates **ecology**, **economics**, and **worship** without dissolving them into a secular project.

#### 5. Lament, protest, and theodicy
Middleton's **Abraham's Silence: The Binding of Isaac, the Suffering of Job, and How to Talk Back to God** 2021 develops a biblical theology of **lament** and **faithful protest**. Frustrated by pious readings that valorize quietism in **Genesis 22**, he revisits the **Aqedah** within a canonical network that includes **Genesis 18**, **Job**, **Moses**, **Jeremiah**, and the **Psalms of lament**. His central claim is that authentic **covenantal** faith includes the courage to argue with God, to plead justice against unjust suffering, and to call down **divine righteousness** for the vulnerable. Abraham's silence at Moriah, read against his boldness in Sodom, becomes an index of moral complexity rather than unqualified virtue.

This project is not a **Leibnizian theodicy** but a pastoral summons to truth-telling prayer before the **holy** God. It draws on **imago Dei** dignity to legitimate protest, insisting that royal-priestly humans may litigate their case in prayer. The task is to form a church that prays the **Psalms of disorientation** as a mode of hope, a practice that coheres with **Romans 8** groaning and the eschatological promise of **justice**. In this way, Middleton integrates **theodicy** into the **new creation** horizon, neither explaining evil away nor surrendering to nihilism, but urging **fidelity** through lament until **resurrection** sets the world right.

#### 6. Hermeneutics and interlocutors
Across his corpus, Middleton converses with **ANE scholarship** and **narrative theology** to clarify Scripture's rhetoric. He engages **John Walton** on **Genesis 1**, particularly on cosmic **temple** imagery and functional ontology, while preserving the **imago Dei** as a **royal-priestly vocation** rather than a merely cultic tag. He appropriates **N. T. Wright** on **resurrection** and **new creation**, yet situates Wright's emphases within a broader **Kuyperian** frame of **cultural calling**. He draws from **Herman Bavinck** on the goodness of **creation** and the unity of **nature and grace**, and from **Al Wolters' Creation Regained** on the non-dualistic scope of **redemption**. His method also listens to **liberation** and **postcolonial** voices, correcting **Eurocentric** blind spots with the lived experience of Caribbean and global Christian communities.

In hermeneutical posture, Middleton practices a disciplined **canon-conscious exegesis**, attentive to textual **intertextuality** and the eschatological **telos** of narratives. He resists pluralist dissolutions of meaning by tethering readings to the **Christological** fulfillment of the royal-priestly mission in the **second Adam**. At the same time, he avoids reducing the canon to a flattening harmony, preserving **genre** integrity and allowing lament, wisdom, and apocalyptic to sound their distinctive notes. The result is a hermeneutic that is pastoral, public, and **missionally** engaged, committed to Scripture's authority without scholastic defensiveness.

#### 7. Ethical and ecclesial implications
The doctrinal center of **imago Dei** and **new earth** radiates ethical implications across economics, politics, and ecology. Middleton's democratized kingship implies a radical account of **human dignity** that challenges **racism**, **patriarchy**, and exploitative **economies**. His critique of **consumer capitalism** in the 1995 volume anticipates later reflections on the **glory and honor of the nations**, arguing that redeemed culture is not homogenized but diversified in worshipful plurality. The church, as a **royal priesthood**, anticipates and embodies this future by practices of **justice**, **hospitality**, and **Sabbath** that signal **kingdom** realities in ordinary life. The **diasporic mission** lens, rooted in **Jeremiah 29**, reframes Christian political presence as patient, hopeful, and locally constructive rather than triumphalist.

In this frame, **ecclesiology** is not a retreat to the private sphere. The assembly is the seed of **new creation**, bearing witness to the **resurrection** in sacraments, work, and neighbor-love. Ethics are eschatologically charged, not merely rule-keeping but **virtue** formation for royal service. Here Middleton's **Neo-Calvinist** lineage is clear, yet chastened by **lament** as a check on triumphalism. He invokes **Romans 8** to insist that present suffering and creation's groaning belong within the same horizon as **glory**, thereby teaching a hopeful patience that resists cynicism.

#### 8. Systematic payoffs and doctrinal contour
Although Middleton writes as a **biblical theologian**, his work yields systematic payoffs. On **anthropology**, the **imago Dei** as vocation reframes debates about **nature and grace** by prioritizing office and mission, even as **Christology** secures the image's ontological depth in the **incarnate Son**. On **ecclesiology**, the church is the renewed **royal priesthood**, tasked with a **cultural** and **liturgical** witness patterned by the **Sabbath** and the **temple**. On **eschatology**, he advances a restorative **new earth** model that secures embodied **resurrection**, continuity of identity, and transformation of culture. On **ethics**, he grounds human rights in the **imago** and frames political participation as **stewardship** rather than sovereignty.

Theologically, Middleton's approach pushes against **dualism** and **Platonizing** tendencies by retrieving **creational monotheism**. His constructive proposals align with **Herman Bavinck's** comprehensive grace and with **Kuyper's** public theology, yet they also resonate with **Irenaean recapitulation**, where the end fulfills the beginning. He does not trade in metaphysical systems of **divine simplicity** or **immutability**, but his narrative judgments presuppose a faithful and righteous **covenant Lord**. The accent on **lament** enriches **theodicy**, integrating **Job** and **Psalms** into an eschatological grammar where protest is sanctified speech. In all this, Middleton's hallmark is a pastoral confidence that Scripture's **story** re-narrates the world for mission.

#### 9. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Middleton's theology offers significant gifts and raises disciplined questions. The gifts are clear: a robust **creational goodness**, a compelling **new earth** eschatology grounded in **resurrection**, and a socially weighty **imago Dei** that fuels justice without dissolving piety. These align with **Herman Bavinck's** nature and grace in organic unity and with a **Kuyperian** public theology. Yet tensions appear at key points. First, by centering the **cultural mandate** and the **royal-priestly** vocation, he risks shading the organizing primacy of the **covenant of grace** and **union with Christ**, long axial in classical **Reformed orthodoxy**. The **anthropological** stress on vocation must be explicitly tethered to **federal headship** in Adam and the **imputed righteousness** of the second Adam lest **justification** recede behind cultural sanctification. Second, his eschatological speculation about the **glory and honor of the nations** can overextend the continuity thesis if it implies an unproblematic translation of cultural products into glory; **Westminster** cautions that grace does not canonize culture but renews it under **judgment**. Third, the salutary retrieval of **lament** must be normed by **divine aseity** and **immutability**, ensuring that faithful protest does not collapse into anthropocentric litigation against God but remains covenantal speech under **revelation**. A more explicit **Christocentric** concentration that structures **creation to new creation** through **covenant**, **federalism**, and **sacramental** means would strengthen the Reformed profile while preserving Middleton's exegetical insights. Nevertheless, within the Reformed family, his body of work stands as a persuasive biblical case for **earthly hope**, **royal-priestly anthropology**, and a mission that refuses both quietism and utopianism.
