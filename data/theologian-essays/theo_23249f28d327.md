### TITLE
Universal call and particular grace: Prosper of Aquitaine’s Augustinian synthesis against **Pelagian** and **semi-Pelagian** reductions

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late Roman Gaul to early Roman papacy, navigating the **Pelagian** and **semi-Pelagian** aftermath of **Carthage 418** and the episcopal politics of **Marseilles** and **Rome**, under **Celestine I** and **Leo the Great**.
- A biblico-doctrinal retrieval method: systematic deployment of **Augustine’s** anti-Pelagian categories with rhetorical acuity, canonical exegesis of **Romans 9** and **1 Timothy 2.4**, and deference to **conciliar** authority.
- Key works: **De ingratis** c. 431; **Contra Collatorem** 432; **Responsiones ad capitula Gallorum** 431–432; **Responsiones ad excerpta Genuensium** 431–432; **Epitoma Chronicon** 433, rev. to 455; **Indiculus de gratia Dei** c. 435; **De vocatione omnium gentium** 450–455.
- Central terms: **gratia praeveniens**, **gratia operans et cooperans**, **praedestinatio**, **donum perseverantiae**, **vocatio universalis et specialis**, **massa perditionis**, asymmetrical **reprobatio**.
- Main interlocutors: **Augustine**, **Pelagius**, **Julian of Eclanum**, **John Cassian**, **Vincent of Lérins**, **Faustus of Riez**, with institutional backing from **Celestine I** and **Leo I**.
- Reformed reception cue: template for **total inability**, **unconditional election**, and twofold **calling**, with nuanced reading of **1 Timothy 2.4** anticipating later Reformed distinctions.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Prosper of Aquitaine emerges from late Roman **Aquitaine** around c. 390, educated in classical **rhetoric** and formed by the monastic and episcopal milieu of southern **Gaul**. His life stretches into the 450s, with activity documented through 455 and probable death c. 463, situating him between the condemnations of **Pelagianism** at **Carthage 418** and the consolidation of Latin **Augustinianism** under **Leo the Great**. His context was the turbulent doctrinal aftermath of **Pelagius** and **Julian of Eclanum**, but also the conciliatory, ascetical ethos of **Marseilles**, where **John Cassian** and later **Faustus of Riez** advocated a mitigated account of **grace** and **free will**. Prosper’s method is a biblico-doctrinal retrieval of **Augustine**, coupling terse, epigrammatic rhetoric with close exegesis of **Romans 9**, **Ephesians 2**, and **1 Timothy 2.4**. He treats **gratia praeveniens** and **gratia operans et cooperans** as controlling categories, embeds his arguments within the **regula fidei**, and insists on the Church’s **catholic** consensus, appealing to **councils**, papal letters, and especially to **Augustine’s** late anti-Pelagian writings.

Prosper’s early decades were tied to **Marseilles**, where he perceived that a pious, pastoral concern for monastic edification was shading into **semi-Pelagian** patterns that eroded **predestination** and reduced **grace** to assistance contingent on an autonomous **initium fidei**. The pastoral texture of his writing is thus polemical and parenetic at once: he seeks to defend **gratuitous grace** without encouraging fatalism. His signature contribution is a sustained defense of **unconditional election** joined to a universal preaching of the **gospel** and a serious account of the Church’s **sacramental** life, particularly **baptism** and incorporation into the *communio sanctorum*. The literary forms he adopts range from **chronicle** to **poetry** to **controversial tracts**, all conscripted into the service of an **Augustinian** theology that must be made persuasive in the schools and monasteries of Gaul and later in the Roman **curia**.

#### 2. Catalyzing Augustine’s final anti-Pelagian works
Prosper’s most decisive early act was to carry the Gaulish debate to **Augustine** himself. In 428–429 he and a colleague named **Hilary** sent **capitula** summarizing the objections of monks and clergy in **Gaul**, urging Augustine to clarify the relationship of **predestination** to **grace** and the beginning of **faith**. Augustine’s reply in Epistula 225 and his twin treatises **De praedestinatione sanctorum** and **De dono perseverantiae** 428–429 were direct responses to Prosper’s dossier. The two Augustinian works fixed the thesis that the **initium fidei** is itself the gift of **gratia praeveniens**, and that the perseverance of the saints is a distinct **donum perseverantiae**. Prosper would become the sharpest Latin interpreter of these final **Augustinian** positions, defending their catholicity against the charge of novelty raised by **Vincent of Lérins** and others.

This mediation role displays Prosper’s method: he triangulates between local **monastic** concerns, **episcopal** oversight, and the **doctor ecclesiae** of Hippo, resolving disputes by canonically weighting **Augustine’s** mature teaching. He appeals to **Carthage 418** for the doctrinal frame and to the living **magisterium** of the Roman bishops. His compilation of objections and his structured requests for resolutions anticipate later scholastic quaestiones, but his rhetoric remains classically late antique. The effect was to relocate the debate from Gaulish monasteries to the authoritative court of **Augustine**, and then to press its reception in **Rome** under **Celestine I**, who in 431 supported Augustine’s doctrine in a letter to the bishops of **Gaul**.

#### 3. Verse polemic and the grammar of grace
In the poem **De ingratis** c. 431 Prosper mounted a sustained poetic indictment of **Pelagian** ingratitude, insisting that to attribute the **initium** and **increase** of faith to human powers is to rob **God** of glory. The poem rehearses **original sin**, the collapse of **liberum arbitrium** in the *lapsus*, the necessity of **gratia operans**, and the pastoral consolation that all boasting is excluded, echoing **Romans 3**. Although verse is an unusual theological medium, Prosper’s **Carmen** functions as catechesis in the grammar of **freely given grace**, organized around **Augustine’s** triad of prevenient, operative, and cooperative grace, and the descent of the **Word** for the redemption of the **massa perditionis**.

By adopting verse, Prosper seized the public pedagogy of Roman **rhetoric** to inculcate **Augustinian** dogma. He embeds stock anti-Pelagian arguments into memorable lines, depicting how **concupiscence** enslaves and how **baptism** engrafts, drawing a line from **Adam** to **Christ** that is governed by **gratuitous election**. The poem’s polemic is edged but pastoral: it aims to reorient the monk and the lay believer away from self-initiated holiness and toward a doxology of **grace** that begins, continues, and consummates salvation. The positive doctrinal content is already clear: **faith** is a gift, progress is a gift, and perseverance is a gift, all under **divine predestination**.

#### 4. Semi-Pelagian controversy and precise refutation
Prosper’s prose masterpiece in the Gaulish phase is **Contra Collatorem** 432, a meticulous critique of **John Cassian’s** Conference 13. Cassian had defended the claim that while **grace** is necessary, the first movement to **faith** may arise from the will. Prosper sharpens **Augustine’s** logic: the human will after **Adam** is in bondage; therefore even the first good **voluntas** toward God is caused by **gratia praeveniens**. He exegetes **Romans 9** with care, highlighting the purpose of **God’s** electing mercy that is not based on foreseen **merits** and the asymmetry of **vessels of mercy** and **vessels of wrath**. He insists that God’s hardening is a just non-giving rather than efficient causation of evil, an account of **reprobatio** that safeguards both divine **justice** and the positivity of **grace**.

This work also manifests Prosper’s dialectic between **gratia operans** and **gratia cooperans**: once called and regenerated, the believer truly cooperates, yet the cause of the cooperation remains **grace**. He denies that **predestination** makes exhortation pointless; rather, preaching and exhortation are appointed means under **providence**. By refusing a crude determinism, he positions himself against **fatalism** while defending **unconditional election**. The treatise thereby became a template for later Augustinian responses to **semi-Pelagianism**, and its careful handling of **Romans 9** and **1 Timothy 2.4** anticipates the way he will synthesize the universality of the **call** and the particularity of **grace** in his Roman period.

#### 5. Responsa and the art of doctrinal consolidation
The paired **Responsiones ad capitula Gallorum** and **Responsiones ad excerpta Genuensium** 431–432 show Prosper at his most lawyerly, answering precise objections with **Augustinian** citations and brief arguments. He reaffirms that the **initium fidei** is the result of **gratia praeveniens**, that perseverance is a distinct **donum perseverantiae**, that **baptism** is the ordinary means for infants born under **original sin**, and that **free will** is real yet disabled apart from **grace**. The Responsa format reveals his epistemic stance: doctrine must be both exegetically and ecclesially warranted, drawing on the common stock of authoritative **patristic** texts and the witness of **councils**.

These works also introduce Prosper’s important pastoral refrain drawn from **Augustine**: where reasons run out, we rest in the **inscrutable judgments** of God. He uses this not as an escape from exegesis but as a boundary rule in speaking of **predestination** and **reprobation**. He refuses the suggestion that **foreknowledge** alone accounts for election, arguing instead for the primacy of **grace** specifying the number of the **elect**, even while insisting that the preaching of the **gospel** and the administration of **sacraments** are genuinely offered to many who will not ultimately be **converted**. This articulation of a serious **offer** with particular **efficacy** becomes programmatic for his later treatise on the **vocation of the nations**.

#### 6. Move to Rome, papal service, and instruments of reception
By the late 430s Prosper was in **Rome**, serving first the interests of **Celestine I** and, more prominently, acting as a notarius under **Leo the Great** 440–461. In this role he likely had a hand in papal letters and may have assisted the redaction of the **Tome of Leo** 449, aligning his **Augustinian** soteriology with the **Chalcedonian** Christology of the Roman see. His **Epitoma Chronicon** first to 433 and revised to 455 locates **Pelagian** and **Nestorian** crises within providential history, using the **chronicle** to teach dogmatic memory. The **Indiculus de gratia Dei** c. 435, a dossier of authoritative statements on **grace** and **original sin**, is probably Prosper’s compilation, and it helped shape the canons later received at **Orange 529**.

Rome afforded Prosper a wider stage and a different register. He continued to be polemically anti-**semi-Pelagian**, but his tone is more integrative, coordinating **grace** with the Church’s mission to the **gentes** and the duties of imperial and ecclesial leaders. His Roman work clarifies that the **Augustinian** project is not sectarian austerity but the catholic doctrine of **salvation** and **sanctification**, grounded in **Christ’s** merits and applied through the **Spirit** in the means of grace. This shift sets up his most mature constructive treatise, in which he gathers his earlier positions into a theology of **calling** and **election** shaped by the global horizon of the Church.

#### 7. De vocatione omnium gentium: universal call, particular grace
The two-book **De vocatione omnium gentium** 450–455 is Prosper’s capstone. Book 1 defends the **universal call** to salvation: the **gospel** is truly addressed to all peoples, and God in some real sense wills that **all kinds** of persons be saved, citing **1 Timothy 2.4** and highlighting the salvation of **kings** and **subjects**, **Jews** and **Gentiles**. He denies that this universality is a bare externality; it reflects the sincere goodness of God who bestows diverse **prevenient aids** and **means** across history. Book 2 treats **particular grace** and **predestination**: among those called, God grants **gratia operans** inwardly to those whom he has **elected**, bringing about the **initium fidei** and securing their **perseverance** as a distinct gift.

Throughout the treatise Prosper wields a twofold **vocation**: a **vocatio generalis**, the universal preaching and summons, and a **vocatio specialis**, the internal, efficacious drawing by the **Spirit**. He refuses to flatten **1 Timothy 2.4** into either universalism or a denial of election. Instead, he argues that God wills the salvation of all in the sense of all estates and nations, and he distinguishes God’s benevolent disposition toward the world from his specific decree of **election**. Without using later technical language, he anticipates the distinction between antecedent benevolence and consequent justice, letting **Romans 9** and **Ephesians 1** govern his account of **predestination**, while safeguarding that God does not positively cause the evil of **reprobates**. The result is a principled harmony of **mission** and **election** that would prove seminal.

#### 8. Predestination, reprobation, perseverance, and ecclesial life
Doctrinally, Prosper articulates an asymmetrical **praedestinatio** and **reprobatio**. God positively **predestines** to **life**, granting the gifts of **faith** and **perseverance**, while he does not predestine to sin or unbelief; rather, he justly leaves some in the **massa perditionis**. Hardening is a permissive judgment that withdraws **grace**, not an infusion of evil. This asymmetry is crucial to his defense of divine **goodness** and to his repudiation of fatalistic **determinism**. He is also emphatic that **perseverance** is not reducible to regenerate habit; it is a fresh **donum perseverantiae**, linked to the **intercession of Christ** and applied by the **Spirit** in time.

Prosper’s soteriology is embedded in an ecclesial **ordo salutis**. **Baptism** remits **original sin**, and the catechized believer is nourished by **Scripture**, **preaching**, and the **Eucharist**. Exhortation is not canceled by **predestination**; it is one of its instruments. He commends prayer for **perseverance** and missionary preaching to the **nations**, convinced that the Church’s external ministry is the arena in which **gratia cooperans** elicits genuine human acts that are nonetheless dependent on the antecedent gift. He never grants **assurance** the robust profile it will later have in Reformed thought, but he speaks of a moral certainty grounded in the **promises** of God and the empirical fruits of **sanctification** wrought by **grace**.

#### 9. Interlocutors, opponents, and long reception
Prosper’s immediate interlocutors were **John Cassian**, **Vincent of Lérins**, and the wider **Marseilles** circle, along with the more radical **Julian of Eclanum**. He pressed **Celestine I** to commend **Augustine**, eliciting papal support in 431, and served **Leo the Great**, thereby tethering **Augustinian** soteriology to Roman **magisterial** authority. His influence runs forward to **Caesarius of Arles** and **Orange 529**, whose canons reflect the **Indiculus de gratia Dei** tradition and reproduce Prosper’s insistence on **prevenient grace** and condemnation of the claim that the **initium fidei** is from ourselves. In North Africa and Italy, **Fulgentius of Ruspe** deepened the predestinarian lines with Prosperian vocabulary. In the Carolingian era, debates over **Gottschalk of Orbais** again retrieved Prosper’s asymmetry between **predestination** and **reprobation** to resist both double predestination caricatures and semi-Pelagian retrenchments.

Humanists and Reformers read Prosper as the faithful son of **Augustine**. The **Epitoma Chronicon** remained a source for dating the late empire, and the **De vocatione** became a touchstone for reconciling **mission** with **election**. Reformation theologians mined Prosper’s claim that **God wills all kinds of men to be saved** and his twofold **call**. **Calvin** cites Prosper’s formulae on the universality of the call and the particularity of grace in his commentaries and Institutes, and **Reformed orthodoxy** later standardized his reading of **1 Timothy 2.4**. Jansenist debates likewise invoked Prosper as an Augustinian witness. Across these receptions, Prosper’s identity is stable: he is the disciplined transmitter and strategic consolidator of late **Augustinian** soteriology into the common treasure of the Latin Church.

In Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation, Prosper’s achievement is foundational. He aligns with **total inability** through his account of the fallen will under **original sin**; he articulates **unconditional election** with an **asymmetrical** account of **reprobation**; he stabilizes a twofold **call** that distinguishes **vocatio universalis** and **vocatio specialis**; and he defends **perseverance** as **donum perseverantiae**. His handling of **1 Timothy 2.4** anticipates the Reformed distinction between God’s universal benevolence in the external offer and his effectual decree, without collapsing either. Yet tensions appear. First, the appeal to **inscrutable judgments** sometimes replaces explanatory architecture that later **covenantal** and **redemptive-historical** frames would supply. Second, assurance remains underdeveloped relative to Reformed teaching on the **Spirit’s** sealing and the reflexive acts of **faith**. Third, while Prosper deftly avoids equal ultimacy, his language of divine permission and withdrawal of **grace** could be sharpened by classic Reformed accounts of **decree**, **providence**, and secondary causality. Nevertheless, within classical Reformed orthodoxy, the constructive alternative is not to revise Prosper’s core theses but to locate them explicitly within the **covenant of grace**, to articulate the universal gospel offer as an ordained means within **effectual calling**, and to anchor perseverance in the **intercession of Christ** for his covenant people. On these lines, Prosper stands as a trusted ally, offering categories and exegesis that Reformed theology can receive, refine, and extend.

### RECAP NOTES
- Late Roman **Gaul** to **Rome**, combating **Pelagian** and **semi-Pelagian** currents under **Celestine I** and **Leo the Great**.
- Central problem: safeguarding **gratia praeveniens** and **predestination** while affirming a universal **gospel call**.
- Major works: **De ingratis** c. 431; **Contra Collatorem** 432; **Responsiones** 431–432; **Epitoma Chronicon** 433/455; **Indiculus de gratia Dei** c. 435; **De vocatione omnium gentium** 450–455.
- Distinctives: twofold **vocatio universalis et specialis**; asymmetrical **praedestinatio** and **reprobatio**; **donum perseverantiae**; exegesis of **Romans 9** and **1 Timothy 2.4**.
- Interlocutors: **Augustine**, **John Cassian**, **Vincent of Lérins**, **Faustus of Riez**, with papal support and impact on **Orange 529**.
- Reformed assessment: strong alignment on **total inability**, **unconditional election**, twofold **calling**, and **perseverance**; needs covenantal framing and fuller account of **assurance** within classic **Reformed orthodoxy**.
- Legacy: conduit of **Augustinian** soteriology into the Latin mainstream, shaping **Fulgentius**, **Orange 529**, medieval predestination debates, and later **Reformation** and **Jansenist** appropriations.
