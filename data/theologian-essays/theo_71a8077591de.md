### TITLE
Thinking through technology: from devices to volition, ethics before efficiency.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Cold War technocracy**, **postwar engineering expansion**, and the rise of **STS** form the backdrop, with interlocutions from **Heidegger** and **Ellul** in late modernity.
- A **humanities philosophy of technology** method that is **historical**, **phenomenological**, and **normative**, set over against a merely **instrumentalist** or **engineering** stance.
- **Thinking through Technology** 1994; **Philosophy of Technology: Readings in the Philosophical Problems** 1972; **Technology and the Good Life?** 2000; **Encyclopedia of Science, Technology, and Ethics** 2005; **Ethics and Science: An Introduction** 2012.
- Central categories include technology as **objects**, **knowledge**, **activity**, and **volition**; contrasts of **instrumentalism**, **substantivism**, and **critical theory**; classical terms *techne*, *poiesis*, *praxis*, *episteme*.
- Key influences and interlocutors: **Aristotle**, **Bacon**, **Descartes**, **Comte**, **Marx**, **Heidegger**, **Ellul**, **Jonas**, **Borgmann**, **Ihde**, **Latour**, **Simondon**.
- Reformed cue: dialogue with **Kuyperian cultural mandate**, **Dooyeweerdian modal norms**, and **Van Til’s antithesis** in critiquing **technicism** and articulating **common grace**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Carl Mitcham, born in 1941 and intellectually formed in the late twentieth century, matured in an era of **Cold War technocracy**, expanding **engineering** education, and the emergence of **Science and Technology Studies** as a critical field. His core conviction is that modern cultures must think through the meaning of **technology** before celebrating its power, and that such thinking requires a **humanities philosophy of technology** in conversation with the sciences. Against a climate of confident **instrumentalism**, Mitcham draws on **Heidegger’s** question concerning technology and **Jacques Ellul’s** critique of **la technique** to insist that technology is not a neutral tool but an order of **means** that shapes ends and social imaginaries. The early editorial project **Philosophy of Technology: Readings in the Philosophical Problems** 1972 signaled a vocation to curate cross-disciplinary debate, situating **Aristotle**, **Bacon**, **Descartes**, and **Marx** within a genealogy of modern technoscience. The method is historical and phenomenological, attentive to experience and meaning, yet unapologetically normative about the ethical stakes of **technological culture**.

Mitcham’s professional appointments at institutions like **Catholic University of America** and the **Colorado School of Mines** positioned him at the seam between secular engineering practice and moral inquiry. The resultant method exhibits a double fidelity. On one side, he honors the concrete intelligence of **engineering design** and **applied science**. On the other, he retrieves philosophical resources from **classical virtue ethics** and **continental hermeneutics** to interpret technology as a mode of being-in-the-world. This double fidelity grounds his contrast between **engineering philosophy of technology** and **humanities philosophy of technology**, a contrast elaborated in **Thinking through Technology** 1994. The former asks how to do technology better. The latter asks what technology is and what it does to us. This methodological framing put Mitcham alongside **Albert Borgmann’s** focal things and practices and **Hans Jonas’s** imperative of responsibility, and it set him in contention with any easy **technological determinism**.

#### 2. A fourfold ontology of technology and its genealogy
At the heart of Mitcham’s analysis is a fourfold account of technology as **objects**, **knowledge**, **activity**, and **volition**. Technology is, first, a world of **artifacts and devices**. It is, second, a structured **technical knowledge** akin to *techne* and informed by *episteme*. It is, third, a set of **productive activities** of *poiesis* ordered to fabrication and use. It is, fourth, a pattern of **volition**, desire, and will that channels aspiration, risk, and power. The emphasis on **volition** is decisive. It moves beyond the device and the workshop into the realm of culture-forming loves, policies, and projects, where the normativity of technological action is disclosed. This fourfold avoids mere **instrumentalism** which reduces technology to neutral means, and resists pure **substantivism** that treats technology as a fate. It instead provides an analytic map for discerning how different layers of technological life bear ethical weight.

Mitcham overlays this ontology with a genealogy of modernity. He reads **Francis Bacon** as inaugurating the experimental alliance of knowledge and power, **René Descartes** as articulating the ideal of mathematical mastery over nature, **Auguste Comte** as codifying a positivist sociology of progress, and **Karl Marx** as revealing ambivalences in the forces and relations of production. He also registers **Heidegger’s** claim that modern technology enframes reality as standing-reserve and **Ellul’s** thesis of technique’s autonomous expansion. Yet he refuses despairing fatalism. By drawing on **Aristotle’s** account of *phronesis* and **Jonas’s** emphasis on responsibility toward the fragile future, Mitcham situates technological judgment within **practical wisdom** rather than ontological doom. The fourfold analysis becomes a grammar for normative deliberation.

#### 3. Thinking through Technology and the shaping of contemporary debate
The signature work **Thinking through Technology** 1994 synthesizes decades of debate into a single map. Mitcham defines and contrasts **engineering philosophy of technology** and **humanities philosophy of technology**, reviews technophilosophical positions from **instrumentalism** to **critical theory**, and sets forth the fourfold of **objects**, **knowledge**, **activity**, and **volition** as a comprehensive lens. The book displays a wide reading of **continental philosophy** and **Anglo-American ethics**, with attention to **Ellul**, **Heidegger**, **Borgmann**, **Don Ihde**, and **Bruno Latour**. It places special stress on the relation between **technological volition** and social institutions such as markets and states, raising questions about **technocratic governance**, **democratic deliberation**, and **moral responsibility**. The work refuses reduction to slogans such as technology is neutral, and it complicates easy praise or blame by showing how different dimensions of technological life exhibit distinct moral profiles.

Within this framework Mitcham challenges simplistic notions of **technological determinism** and alleged value neutrality. He introduces distinctions between **micro-ethics** of individual engineering practice and **macro-ethics** of public policy and systemic design. He argues for a primacy of ethical inquiry with respect to efficiency, insisting that **prudence** should govern **optimization**. This argument entails a hierarchy of ends in which human flourishing, communal goods, and ecological integrity check the expansion of technique. By defending the legitimacy of humanistic evaluation inside a largely technical academy, the book became a point of reference for programs in **engineering ethics** and **STS**, and a foil for more celebratory narratives of **innovation** and **disruption**.

#### 4. Editorial labors, ethics of science, and public engagement
Mitcham’s editorial work broadened the field. The volume **Philosophy of Technology: Readings in the Philosophical Problems** 1972 built a canon that legitimized the philosophy of technology for an English-speaking audience. The massive **Encyclopedia of Science, Technology, and Ethics** 2005 coordinated contributions on **bioethics**, **environmental ethics**, **information technology**, and **nanotechnology**, giving scholars and practitioners a common vocabulary. These projects institutionalized humanistic attention to the **ethics of technoscience**, now standard in universities and policy circles. 

Beyond editorial labors, Mitcham coauthored **Ethics and Science: An Introduction** 2012 with **Adam Briggle**, expanding from engineering to the emergent moral terrain of contemporary research. The work addresses issues like **dual-use research**, **responsible conduct**, **peer review**, and the entanglement of **industry and academia**. Throughout, Mitcham maintains that the power of science and engineering must be met by the power of ethical reasoning and civic responsibility. This insistence places him near **Hans Jonas’s** call for a new ethics adequate to the magnitude of human action and near **Borgmann’s** plea for focal practices that resist the device paradigm.

#### 5. Dialogue with religion and the theological turn
Although not a confessional theologian, Mitcham’s project invites and at points cultivates dialogue with **religion**, especially through his engagement with **Ellul** and **Heidegger** and his long service at **Catholic** institutions. His emphasis on **limits**, **responsibility**, and **virtue** resonates with **Catholic social thought**, especially the preference for the **common good**, the practice of **prudence**, and critique of **technocratic paradigms**. The retrieval of *techne*, *poiesis*, and *praxis* opens paths for theological accounts of **vocation**, **Sabbath**, and **stewardship**. His fourfold ontology, culminating in **volition**, places moral agency and love at the center of technological discernment, a move that nourishes theological reflection on **desire** and **idolatry**.

In essays and lectures on **engineering ethics**, Mitcham repeatedly asks what kind of persons engineers must become in order rightly to exercise technical power. This anthropological question aligns with the theological grammar of **virtue**, **sin**, and **grace**, even when articulated in secular terms. Points of convergence appear in concerns about **transhumanism**, the commodification of the body in **biomedical technologies**, and the erosion of **place** under the **device paradigm**. His stress on civic virtues and the ethical primacy of the common good provides a bridge for theologians who seek to move from doctrine to **public theology** in matters of technology assessment and design.

#### 6. Interlocutors, schools, and contested terrains
Mitcham maps the philosophy of technology as a plural field. He credits **Borgmann** with diagnosing the **device paradigm** and recovering **focal practices**, and places **Don Ihde** as a leader in **postphenomenology** that examines human-technology relations and multistable embodiments. He reads **Bruno Latour** as a theorist of **actor-network** assemblages that redistribute agency, and he notes the resources of **Gilbert Simondon** on the **individuation** of technical objects. He remains attentive to **critical theory**, especially the tools for analyzing **ideology** and **systemic power**, while resisting an unremittingly negative assessment of modernity. His approach is synthetic, guided by a conviction that multiple lenses clarify technology’s layered reality.

Key debates center on **determinism versus choice**, **neutrality versus shaping**, and **progress versus responsibility**. Mitcham’s contribution is not to choose one horn, but to differentiate domains. He grants contingency at the level of **engineering design**, recognizes shaping at the level of **social institutions**, and argues for responsibility at the level of **volition**. He balances **Ihde’s** relational analysis with **Borgmann’s** normative recovery of practices, and he challenges **Latour’s** flattening of agency by reasserting the ethical primacy of human purposes. This balancing is visible in his careful separation of **micro-ethics** and **macro-ethics**, a distinction that disciplines both classrooms and policy tables.

#### 7. Normativity, prudence, and the governance of technoscience
Mitcham advances a normative vision organized by **prudence**, **responsibility**, and the **common good**. He calls for institutional forms of **technology assessment**, interdisciplinary **deliberative forums**, and a culture of **virtue** among engineers and scientists. His ethic is neither deontological alone nor consequentialist alone. It is a virtue-oriented practical reason that tests technical ambitions against goods such as **human dignity**, **ecological integrity**, and **justice**. He affirms innovation as a human excellence while warning against **technicism**, the ideology that makes technique the arbiter of value. In this he echoes **Ellul’s** diagnosis, yet avoids fatalism by affirming the possibility of morally formative design and governance.

On specific frontiers such as **nanotechnology**, **information infrastructure**, and **biomedicine**, Mitcham recommends slow thinking, public participation, and anticipatory **ethics-by-design**. He promotes **macro-ethical** reflection by engineers who typically occupy **micro-ethical** roles. He exposes the insufficiency of compliance-only regimes and calls for formative education that fosters **practical wisdom**. This normativity remains porous to religious contributions without presupposing any confessional starting point. It invites theologians to contribute substantive conceptions of the good, while challenging them to translate doctrine into public reasoning.

#### 8. Anthropological and theological implications
Though crafted within philosophy, Mitcham’s project implies a rich **theological anthropology**. By elevating **volition**, he foregrounds the love-shaped nature of human action. Technology embodies ordered love or disordered desire, which means devices are crystallizations of **praxis** and habit as much as objects. This insight resonates with Christian accounts of **sin** as misdirected love and of **virtue** as habituated orientation toward the true good. His stress on **finitude**, **risk**, and **responsibility** mirrors doctrines of **creation** and **providence**, where human agents are vicegerents under a higher moral order. Even when the transcendent is not named, the moral shape of reality is treated as intelligible and binding.

Mitcham’s retrieval of *techne* and *poiesis* also intersects with theological discussions of **culture-making** and the **imago Dei** as creative stewardship. The fourfold grammar helps theologians discern how artifact, knowledge, activity, and will each require redemptive scrutiny. For instance, technology as **knowledge** raises questions about truthfulness and epistemic humility. Technology as **activity** tests labor practices and sabbath rest. Technology as **objects** presses toward design that honors creaturely limits and enables focal practices. Technology as **volition** addresses idolatrous devotion to control and security. These correlations make his work a fertile matrix for public theology of **technology and culture**.

#### 9. Limits, tensions, and prospects
Critics press Mitcham on several fronts. Some accuse the **humanities philosophy of technology** of romanticizing premodern life or overstating the pervasiveness of **technological determinism**. Others judge the ethical vision underdeveloped at the level of first principles, since it trades on prudential judgments without a disclosed metaphysic. Postphenomenologists such as **Ihde** sometimes fault him for insufficient attention to the fine-grained mediations discovered in use, while network theorists like **Latour** challenge the privileging of human volition over distributed agency. Mitcham answers with pluralism and differentiation. He defends the fourfold as a scaffold for ethical clarity and reaffirms that the priority of **prudence** is not a failure of theory but a recognition that technological life is a field for cultivated judgment.

A further tension concerns political economy. Mitcham criticizes **technocracy** and calls for **democratic deliberation**, yet his program depends on institutions willing to slow down and submit to moral scrutiny. Where markets and states elevate **disruption** and **competitiveness**, the space for prudence narrows. He attempts to widen that space through education, public scholarship, and cross-sector dialogue. The editorial work in the **Encyclopedia of Science, Technology, and Ethics** and curricula in **engineering ethics** are instruments of that widening. The durability of this project depends on whether cultures will value moral wisdom more than speed.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical Reformed and WTS vantage, Mitcham’s project is a salutary provocation to repent of **technicism** and to recover **prudence** within the **cultural mandate**. His fourfold of **objects**, **knowledge**, **activity**, and **volition** aligns with a **Kuyperian** intuition that technology is a creational possibility with multiform normativity under God’s law. His insistence on **macro-ethics** honors **common grace** structures that govern societies and their artifacts. Yet the project finally lacks a covenantal teleology anchored in **special revelation**. The governing norms are articulated as the **common good** and **responsibility**, but the measure and end of these remain indeterminate without the **Lordship of Christ** and the canonical narrative of **creation, fall, redemption, consummation**. The priority of **prudence** is right, yet prudence must be tutored by **covenantal law**, disciplined by the **antithesis** that exposes idolatrous **volition**, and energized by **redemptive grace** that reorients cultural labor toward Sabbath and shalom. Mitcham’s reservations about **technological determinism** parallel a Reformed confidence in human agency under providence, but his resistance to robust metaphysics undercuts sustained critique of **power** and **principalities** at work in technique. A Reformed alternative would absorb his analytic fourfold into a **Dooyeweerdian** account of creational norms, deploy **Van Til’s** epistemic critique to expose the myth of neutrality, and specify **sphere sovereignty** as the polity for resisting technocratic overreach. In sum, Mitcham helps the Reformed tradition think technology morally before technically. The Reformed tradition, in turn, can supply the doctrinal depth that Mitcham’s prudential humanism presupposes but does not name.
