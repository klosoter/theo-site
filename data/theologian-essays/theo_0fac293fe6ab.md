### TITLE
Worship as participation in the Son’s priestly communion with the Father by the Spirit, grounding covenant in grace rather than contract.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Mid-to-late twentieth-century Scotland**, **University of Aberdeen**, **Church of Scotland** liturgical renewal, under the shadow of **Barthian ressourcement** and retrieval of **Athanasius** and **Calvin**.
- **Trinitarian-doxological method**, **Christological mediation**, and a pastoral hermeneutic shaped by **vicarious humanity** and **union with Christ**.
- **Worship, Community and the Triune God of Grace** (1996); seminal essays such as **Covenant or Contract?** (1970s) and **The Incarnation and Worship** (1976); Festschrift **Christ in Our Place** (1989).
- Central terms: **covenant vs contract**, **priesthood of Christ**, **participation by the Spirit**, **objective reconciliation**, **union with Christ**, **one baptism**, **eucharistic thanksgiving**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **John Calvin**, **Athanasius**, **Karl Barth**, **T. F. Torrance**, and the **federal Reformed scholastics** such as **Cocceius**, **Witsius**, and **Owen**.
- Reformed reception cue: applauded for **Trinitarian worship** and **pastoral assurance**, queried over **federal theology**, **imputation**, and the **extent of atonement**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Born in 1923 and dying in 2003, **James B. Torrance** stands as a distinctive Scottish Reformed voice whose teaching at the **University of Aberdeen** and ministry within the **Church of Scotland** framed a lifelong project: to retrieve a **Trinitarian-doxological theology** of worship and covenant. Raised in a missionary family and shaped by the same intellectual currents that formed his brother **T. F. Torrance**, he appropriated **patristic** and **Reformation** sources not as antiquarianism but as resources for ecclesial reformation. His method fused **biblical exegesis**, historical theology, and pastoral dogmatics, with a clear preference for a **Christological mediation** of all soteriology and ecclesiology. Against pragmatic or rationalist reductions, he insisted that **theology is doxology**, which locates knowing God in participation in the Son’s **priestly communion** with the Father through the **Holy Spirit**. This epistemic stance centers on the **vicarious humanity of Christ**, a theme that turns questions of faith, repentance, and obedience into acts first performed by Christ for us, and only then by us in him.

The intellectual context of the mid-twentieth century made **J. B. Torrance** conversant with **Karl Barth’s** critique of natural theology and with ecumenical currents concerned for **liturgy and sacrament**. Yet he refused to oppose doctrine and worship: for him, dogmatics culminates in **eucharistic thanksgiving**. Influenced by **Athanasius’s** *homoousios* and **Calvin’s** **union with Christ**, he framed the church’s life as participation, by the Spirit, in Christ’s filial relation, thus resisting what he called an **I-it** posture of religion and advancing an **I-Thou** orientation of **covenant communion**. The pastoral payoff lay in assurance: because the Gospel’s center is **God’s covenant of grace** rather than a **religious contract**, believers rest in Christ’s perfect worship, rather than anxiously establishing their own.

#### 2. Major works and themes
The accessible but programmatic **Worship, Community and the Triune God of Grace** appeared in 1996 and crystallizes J. B. Torrance’s principal claims: Christian worship is inherently **Trinitarian**, **incarnational**, and **eschatological**, because it is the **Son’s priestly offering** in which the church participates by the Spirit. In this framework, the church’s prayer is grounded in **Christ’s intercession**, the church’s confession echoes **Christ’s vicarious confession**, and the church’s obedience flows from Christ’s **vicarious obedience**. Earlier essays like **Covenant or Contract?** from the 1970s and **The Incarnation and Worship** in 1976 excavate the conceptual fault line he discerned in post-Reformation debates: a shift, as he narrates it, from a **biblical-unconditional covenant** of grace to a **bilateral contract** that conditions grace upon human performance. This shift, he argued, created a liturgical and pastoral deficit: worship became a human offering to secure divine favor rather than sharing in the **Triune life of grace**.

The **covenant vs contract** contrast frames his sustained reading of Reformed sources. J. B. Torrance claimed that **Calvin** centered salvation and worship in **union with Christ**, with faith as the Spirit’s instrument integrating believers into Christ’s **priesthood**, whereas later **federal theology** sometimes functionalized covenant into a juridical pact. He drew a line of development from **Cocceius** and **Witsius** to strands of **Puritan scholasticism** where, in his judgment, **faith and repentance** became conditions of the covenant rather than gifts within it. His point was not to abandon **federal theology**, but to relocate covenantal thinking within **Christ’s mediation**, thereby preserving assurance and grounding ethical response in **grace-first** ontology.

#### 3. Christology, mediation, and vicarious humanity
At the doctrinal core is a **high Christology** that centers **the vicarious humanity of Christ**. In J. B. Torrance’s telling, the priestly work of Jesus does not end at the cross; it continues as the risen and ascended Lord who prays, obeys, and worships on behalf of the church. This emphasis builds on **Athanasius** and the soteriological axiom that what is not assumed is not healed, and it resonates with **Barth’s** account of **Christ’s active obedience**. For J. B. Torrance, Christ’s baptism, faith, prayer, and obedience are not merely paradigmatic; they are **representative and substitutive** acts in which believers share through the **Spirit’s uniting**. Thus, the call to repentance is an invitation to participate in Christ’s own **metanoia** for us, and the call to holiness is an outworking of union with the **Holy One**.

This **vicarious mediation** reframes classic loci. In **atonement**, he stressed **objective reconciliation** accomplished once for all in Christ, resisting both **subjectivism** that makes salvation dependent on human response and **legalism** that smuggles conditions into the Gospel. In **election**, he welcomed the **Christocentric** orientation where Jesus Christ is both the electing God and elected man, thereby safeguarding assurance and disallowing speculation into a **decretal abyss**. In **pneumatology**, the Spirit is the **Spirit of adoption** who incorporates believers into the Son’s **Abba**-cry, so that sanctification is the Spirit’s enabling of filial participation rather than mere moral improvement. These doctrinal moves secure a doxological horizon: worship is **thanksgiving and intercession in Christ**, not self-generated religious achievement.

#### 4. Liturgy, sacraments, and ecclesial practice
J. B. Torrance’s ecclesiology is inseparable from his **sacramental theology**. On **baptism**, he appealed to **one baptism** into the death and resurrection of Christ, contending that baptism is first a **divine act and naming**, before it is our confession. Hence he resisted reducing baptism to a public vow or ordinance, and directed the church to see baptism’s efficacy in **union with Christ** rather than in human sincerity. On the **Eucharist**, he fought **Zwinglian memorialism** by retrieving the **patristic** and **Calvinian** emphasis on real participation by the Spirit in the ascended Christ. The Lord’s Supper is **eucharistic thanksgiving** where the church is lifted by the Spirit into the Son’s **priestly offering** to the Father, a movement that safeguards both **transcendent realism** and **pastoral assurance**.

His appropriation of **covenant** also reshaped church life. He argued that when covenant is construed as **contract**, preaching becomes moral exhortation and sacraments become human pledges. When covenant is recovered as **communion**, preaching becomes **gospel proclamation** and sacraments are **means of grace**. His advocacy within the **Church of Scotland** for liturgical reform pressed toward **Trinitarian doxology**, explicit confession of **Christ’s mediation**, and prayers that recognize the **Spirit’s agency**. This pastoral turn is not optional but intrinsic to the dogmatic claim that Christ’s priesthood continues, and that the church’s worship is secondary, derivative, and participatory.

#### 5. Reading Calvin, reading the federal tradition
J. B. Torrance read **John Calvin** as a theologian of **union with Christ** whose doctrine of **the Lord’s Supper** and **prayer** is suffused with **Trinitarian-doxological** accents. He loved to note Calvin’s insistence that in prayer we are borne along by **Christ’s intercession**, and that the Supper is a **spiritual feeding** on the living Christ. From this vantage he reread the seventeenth-century **federal divines**, distinguishing between trajectories that preserved a **Christocentric covenant of grace** and those that drifted toward **contractualism**. He engaged figures such as **Owen** and **Witsius**, not to caricature, but to warn against turning **faith** and **repentance** into preconditions that jeopardize **sola gratia**. He thus called for a critical ressourcement that keeps the **pactum salutis** and **covenant of grace** only insofar as they safeguard the primacy of **Christ’s mediation**.

This reading led him into conversations with **Karl Barth** and into debates over the place of **imputation** and **penal substitution**. He did not deny **penal dimensions** of the cross, but he set them within a larger **Trinitarian** and **representative** economy, where the Son answers the Father on our behalf. His use of **vicarious faith** at times seemed to some critics to blur the **forensic** clarity of **justification by faith**, yet for him the point was not to erase the human act of believing, but to ground that act in **Christ’s perfect faithfulness**. The direction of his argument is consistent: the grammar of the Gospel begins with **the indicative of grace** before the **imperative of obedience**, and the **Spirit** unites us to the Son so that our obedience is the fruit of **participation**, not the condition of acceptance.

#### 6. Objective reconciliation and the scope of salvation
J. B. Torrance’s emphasis on **objective reconciliation** raised immediate questions about the **extent of atonement** and the reach of salvation. While he resisted **universalism**, he was sharply critical of **limited atonement** construed as a restriction in Christ’s priestly office. The cross, for him, is **sufficient for all and efficient in the church** through the Spirit’s uniting work. In his lectures and in **Worship, Community and the Triune God of Grace** he regularly distinguished between the **ontological inclusion** of humanity in Christ’s **assumptio carnis** and the **personalizing work** of the Spirit who brings men and women into effective participation through **faith**. This preserved both a robust **evangelical proclamation** to all and a sober insistence that salvation is **in Christ**, not apart from him.

Here his pastoral concern is evident: preaching announces what **God has done in Christ** and calls for response that is itself the Spirit’s gift. The **church-world** relation is not symmetrical; yet the church’s posture is one of invitation because the **Triune God of grace** has established a covenant that is fundamentally **God’s promise**. Assurance flows from the **objective center** in Christ, not from the oscillations of human experience. This emphasis shaped his treatment of **baptismal identity**, catechesis, and **Eucharistic** practice, all calibrated to prevent the resurgence of **contractual religion**.

#### 7. Education, community, and social implications
Although not a political theologian in the conventional sense, J. B. Torrance extended **covenant as communion** into spheres of **education** and **community**. In essays allied to **Covenant or Contract?**, he pressed that learning is dialogical and covenantal, not merely transactional. This had implications for Christian formation: catechesis is participation in **the school of Christ**, and ecclesial community is ordered by **gift and gratitude**, not by **exchange and merit**. He drew on **biblical covenants** to frame ecclesial belonging and mutual responsibility, arguing that the **Triune** pattern of gift and response reconfigures **authority** and **service** in the church. The social resonance of his work lies less in policy than in a theological anthropology: persons are defined by **relation in communion**, which echoes the **Trinitarian** life and relativizes **contractual individualism**.

This extension of dogmatics into practice remains integral to his method: the **rule of worship** is the rule of faith, and so the shape of the liturgy tutors the conscience of the people of God. Recovering **Trinitarian prayer**, explicit confession of **Christ’s mediation**, and dependence on the **Spirit’s agency** reforms the church’s ethos. To that end, J. B. Torrance’s lectures, articles, and pastoral engagements sought not simply to clarify doctrines but to reshape the church’s **habitus** toward **thanksgiving**, **intercession**, and **praise** grounded in the **covenant of grace**.

#### 8. Interlocutors and legacy
J. B. Torrance’s theological conversation partners span centuries: **Athanasius** and the **Nicene** tradition for a robust **incarnation** and **homoousios**; **Calvin** for **union with Christ** and sacramental realism; **Karl Barth** for **Christocentric election** and the rejection of **natural theology**; the **federal Reformed scholastics** for critical engagement over **covenant**; and contemporary liturgical theologians over **worship**. His 1989 Festschrift, **Christ in Our Place**, signals the recognition of his contribution: centering the doctrines of grace in **Christ’s priestly mediation** and integrating ecclesial practice with dogmatics. The widespread adoption of **Trinitarian language** in worship guides and the renewed appreciation for **participatory liturgy** owe much to his clear, accessible expositions.

A generation of pastors and scholars found in **Worship, Community and the Triune God of Grace** a compact thesis for catechesis and practice. By tracing the missteps and resources of **post-Reformation** theology, he helped reorient Reformed churches toward **doxological theology**. His legacy also lives in ongoing debates about **atonement**, **election**, **sacraments**, and the precise nature of **federal theology**. Whether hailed or questioned, his insistence that worship begins with **God’s action** remains a needed counter to modern voluntarism and ecclesial busyness.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed/WTS evaluation
From within classical **Reformed orthodoxy**, including a Westminsterian sensibility, J. B. Torrance’s achievement is both salutary and debatable. Positively, his retrieval of **Trinitarian worship**, **Christ’s priesthood**, and **union with Christ** corrects reductionist pieties and grounds assurance in **grace alone**. His critique of **contractualism** exposes the pastoral dangers of turning **faith** and **repentance** into subtle works, and his sacramental theology recovers **means of grace** as divine acts rather than human pledges. Yet tensions surface. His sharp **covenant vs contract** polarity risks flattening the diversity of **federal theology**, where many divines maintained unconditional **election** and monergistic **regeneration** alongside a serious **ordo salutis**. By foregrounding **vicarious faith** and **objective reconciliation**, he sometimes appears to underemphasize the **forensic imputation** of Christ’s righteousness and the particularity of **effectual calling**. His resistance to **limited atonement** and accent on ontological inclusion in Christ could blur the church-world boundary and complicate the grammar of **justification by faith** as an instrument, not an outcome of union. A Westminster alternative would affirm his **Trinitarian doxology** and **Christic mediation** while retaining a robust **covenantal federalism**: the **covenant of works** clarifies law-gospel relations, the **pactum salutis** secures the unity of the divine decree and Christ’s obedience, **imputation** preserves the forensic dimension of grace, and **particular redemption** guards divine initiative in history. On this reading, the best synthesis would integrate J. B. Torrance’s vision of **participatory worship** with classical **forensic soteriology**, keeping divine grace both **personal and covenantal**, and also **just and particular**.
