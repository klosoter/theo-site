### TITLE
From origins to orthodoxy: a cumulative, analytic case for God, Christ’s divinity, and the resurrection

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Contemporary Asian setting of analytic theology and public apologetics amid **New Atheism** and the **Early High Christology** debate, with institutional work in Hong Kong and wider global networks in **philosophical theology**.
- Method marked by **analytic philosophy**, **abductive inference**, selective **Bayesian confirmation**, and historical **critical realism** oriented to **public evidence** rather than private fideism.
- Key works: **A Kryptic Model of the Incarnation** 2014, **The Origins of Divine Christology** 2017, **God and Ultimate Origins** 2017, **Investigating the Resurrection of Jesus Christ** 2020, **The Origin of Humanity and Evolution** 2022, **The Teleological and Kalam Cosmological Arguments Revisited** 2022.
- Central categories: **kalam cosmological argument**, **fine-tuning teleology**, **Principle of Sufficient Reason**, **early high Christology**, **kryptic incarnation**, **Chalcedonian dyophysitism and dyothelitism**, **collective religious experience**, **genealogical Adam**.
- Main interlocutors and influences include **William Lane Craig**, **Richard Swinburne**, **Alexander Pruss**, **Graham Oppy**, **Paul Draper**, **Larry Hurtado**, **Richard Bauckham**, **Bart D. Ehrman**, **Dale C. Allison**, **N. T. Wright**, **Joshua Swamidass**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong alignment with **conciliar Christology** and **resurrection historicity**, but debated use of **natural theology**, **PSR**, and **genealogical Adam** in light of **federal headship** and **presuppositional** concerns.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Andrew Loke has emerged as a leading contemporary voice in **philosophical theology**, working from Asia while engaging a global guild shaped by **analytic philosophy** and public apologetics in the era of **New Atheism**. Trained first in the empirical disciplines and later in theology and philosophy, he exemplifies a cross-disciplinary temperament that places **public evidence**, explanatory rigor, and argumentative clarity at the center of his project. The intellectual environment includes debates over **natural theology**, analytic **Christology**, and the historical study of Christian origins, especially the so-called **Early High Christology** discussion. Loke’s writing consistently aims at what he calls a transdisciplinary approach that binds **metaphysics**, **history**, and **exegesis** into a cumulative case oriented to rational evaluability.

Methodologically, Loke draws on **abductive inference** and selective **Bayesian confirmation theory** alongside classical metaphysical tools such as the **Principle of Sufficient Reason** and causal analysis familiar from the **kalam cosmological argument**. Within the historical disciplines he works under a construct of **critical realism**, affirming that past events can be known probabilistically by converging indicators and by the superior explanatory scope of certain hypotheses. His tone is unapologetically public, aiming to furnish noncircular grounds for commitment to **classical theism**, **Chalcedonian Christology**, and the **bodily resurrection**. This methodological profile unifies his defense of **God and ultimate origins**, the divinity of **Jesus as YHWH**, and the reality of the **Easter events** as historical and theological foci.

Loke’s approach is dialogical and combative in the best scholarly sense. He frames arguments to meet principled critiques by **Graham Oppy** and **Paul Draper** on the cosmological front, by **Bart D. Ehrman** and **James D. G. Dunn** on the historical Jesus and early Christology, and by contemporary metaphysicians on the coherence of **incarnation** and **Trinity**. At the same time he integrates insights from **Richard Swinburne** on probabilistic theism, from **Alexander Pruss** on **PSR**, and from **Larry Hurtado** and **Richard Bauckham** on **early high Christology**. The result is an increasingly unified program across multiple monographs that are best read together as a sustained case for the rationality and truth of **Nicene-Chalcedonian** Christianity.

#### 2. God and ultimate origins
In **God and Ultimate Origins** 2017 and later in **The Teleological and Kalam Cosmological Arguments Revisited** 2022, Loke revisits the **kalam cosmological argument** and the fine-tuning **teleological argument**, seeking to upgrade their premises against recent objections. He defends a refined causal principle grounded in a modest **PSR**, arguing that contingent reality as a whole requires an explanation in a necessary being whose existence is not contingent upon external factors. He interacts with **Oppy** and **Draper**, contending that brute facts about the cosmos lack the modal and explanatory resources to complete the story of origin. Loke deploys the now familiar arsenal against actual infinite pasts, including roadblocks from the **Grim Reaper** family of paradoxes and entropy considerations, while granting that physics is underdetermined and must be interpreted in a larger metaphysical frame.

The move from a necessary being to **classical theism** is handled by adducing the breadth and unity of explanatory features such a being would possess, including **aseity**, **immutability**, and **omnipotence**, construed in ways compatible with creation ex nihilo and creaturely freedom. Loke appreciates **Swinburne’s** probabilistic case for theism but pushes further metaphysically, arguing that a necessary foundation satisfying **PSR** more elegantly accounts for the depth of order and lawlike regularities in the world. His teleological argument incorporates contemporary **fine-tuning** literature to claim that the life-permitting values of cosmic parameters are neither trivial nor inevitable on naturalism but flow naturally from the intellect of a **creator God**. Across these works Loke presents **natural theology** not as exhaustive demonstration but as making theism rationally preferable within a cumulative abductive framework.

#### 3. Early divine Christology
The central historical claim of **The Origins of Divine Christology** 2017 is that the earliest Christians intentionally identified Jesus with **YHWH** rather than merely attributing to him derivative divine agency. Loke contends that a robust **early high Christology** can be shown to be present in the very earliest strata of Christian tradition, including pre-Pauline hymnic material and the Pauline letters of the 50s. Engaging **Hurtado** and **Bauckham**, and confronting skeptical voices such as **Ehrman**, he argues that devotion and worship directed to Jesus are best explained if the earliest communities understood him as sharing the **divine identity** rather than as a promoted angel or functional vicegerent. Loke surveys key texts such as 1 Corinthians 8:6 and Philippians 2, advocating that these are not later developments but intentional invocations of **Shema**-shaped monotheism where Jesus is included within the **one God**.

A distinctive feature of Loke’s case is his deployment of intent-sensitive historiography. He scrutinizes the logic of early confession as a web of commitments rather than a mere aggregation of titles. The claim is that once the earliest communities confessed Jesus as the decisive revealer and the Lord to whom prayer and cultic devotion are rendered, they crossed the conceptual threshold into **divine Christology**. Loke resists evolutionary models that move from adoptionist low Christology to later ontological deification, instead positing that high Christology was present from the beginning and subsequently expressed with greater metaphysical precision at **Nicaea** and **Chalcedon**. The argument integrates **historical bedrock** and theological coherence, reinforcing a trajectory that culminates in **conciliar orthodoxy** as a faithful explicitation rather than a departure.

#### 4. Incarnation and Chalcedonian coherence
In **A Kryptic Model of the Incarnation** 2014 Loke develops a metaphysical account of the **hypostatic union** intended to secure **Chalcedonian dyophysitism** and **dyothelitism** while explaining Christ’s conscious limitations. The distinctive proposal is that the eternal Son assumed a human nature such that certain divine cognitive contents were veiled or non-accessible in the human mode of consciousness, which Loke calls a **kryptic** arrangement. He aims to secure the integrity of Christ’s human knowledge and growth while preserving the Son’s full **omniscience** in the divine mode, avoiding straightforward **kenoticism** that would divest the Son of divine attributes. The model shares family resemblances with **two-minds** accounts but is regimented by analytic distinctions concerning modes of awareness and ranges of access that are supposed to be metaphysically coherent under **Chalcedon**.

To guard against Nestorian partition, Loke argues that the subject of all Christ’s acts is the one **divine person** who subsists in two natures, with human experiences genuinely His yet not exhausting the divine life. He retrieves **anhypostasia** and **enhypostasia** as conceptual tools to mark that the humanity of Christ has no subsistence apart from the person of the Word and is fully personalized in Him. In this frame, Christ’s ignorance of specific times or facts and authentic temptations are explicable without reducing divinity, since ignorance is predicated of the human cognitive range while the Son’s divine knowledge remains intact in a kryptically veiled mode relative to His human consciousness. Loke therefore claims fidelity to **Chalcedonian Christology** in a way that conforms to **conciliar** boundaries while benefitting from analytic precision.

#### 5. Resurrection and public evidence
With **Investigating the Resurrection of Jesus Christ** 2020 Loke presents a transdisciplinary case for the **bodily resurrection** as the best explanation of historical data and religious experience. He critiques minimalist strategies that underplay the specific content of early experiences and the shape of early proclamation, yet he retains the idea of **historical bedrock** such as the postmortem appearances and the origin of the disciples’ resurrection faith. Loke adds a layer of **collective religious experience** as public evidence, arguing that communal experiences and sustained patterns of testimony have analogues in legal and social epistemology that can, in principle, supply publicly accessible data. The resurrection hypothesis is then compared with naturalistic rivals such as hallucination theories and conspiracy models, with Loke concluding that only a real encounter with the risen **Christ** fits the breadth and irreducibility of the evidence.

Loke interacts with **Dale C. Allison** on the phenomenology of visionary experiences and with **N. T. Wright** on the Jewish context for resurrection belief, synthesizing their insights into his abductive framework. He also addresses methodological naturalism in historical inquiry, contending that exclusion of **miracle** a priori is question-begging. In place of a closed historical method, he commends a critical openness guided by criteria such as explanatory scope, internal coherence, and fit with background knowledge supplied by **natural theology**. The unity of his project becomes clear here, since prior arguments for **classical theism** supply the live possibility of divine action, which in turn rationally primes the historian to treat the resurrection hypothesis as a genuine candidate explanation.

#### 6. Human origins, evolution, and Adam
In **The Origin of Humanity and Evolution** 2022 Loke attempts to reconcile **evolutionary biology** with Christian doctrine concerning **Adam and Eve** and **original sin**. He engages proposals such as **genealogical Adam**, drawing on population genetics to argue that a historical couple within a larger population could, within a manageable timescale, become universal genealogical ancestors. This permits retention of historical Adamic headship without denying polygenic biological ancestry at the genetic level. Loke situates this within a broader theological frame that includes **imago Dei**, moral responsibility, and the universality of sin, seeking to avoid both concordist literalism and reductive naturalism.

On hamartiology, Loke explores modes of transmission of **original sin** compatible with a historical pair, assessing **federal headship** models and realist accounts without collapsing into biological essentialism. The aim is a layered account where theological claims about the **fall** address covenantal and spiritual realities while leaving the mechanics of genetic ancestry to empirical science. He thus advances a mediating position, aimed at respecting Scripture, **conciliar orthodoxy**, and mainstream science. Interlocutors include **Joshua Swamidass** on genealogical models and critics of theological monogenesis, with Loke arguing that Christian theism possesses the conceptual flexibility to honor both doctrinal commitments and scientific data without special pleading.

#### 7. Unity of the cumulative case
What ties Loke’s diverse corpus together is his self-conscious use of a **cumulative case** in which **metaphysics**, **history**, and **exegesis** interact. The trajectory often begins with **natural theology**, moves through the historical Jesus and **early high Christology**, and culminates in the **resurrection** as God’s vindicating act that seals the identity of Jesus as **YHWH** incarnate. The metaphysical pieces from **PSR** and the **kalam** supply an ontological backdrop that makes divine action antecedently plausible. The historical Jesus research and the early **divine Christology** thesis then position Jesus as the proper locus of revelation. The **kryptic incarnation** model and **Chalcedonian** commitments ensure coherence of doctrine, and the **resurrection** argument renders the core events publicly defensible. Loke intends each step to reinforce the others, so that rational assent to Christian theism does not rest on a single pillar but on a mutually supportive architecture.

This architecture also shapes his rhetorical posture. Loke does not claim apodictic demonstration of the entire system, but he aims for rational preferability over live competitors such as classical **naturalism**, religious pluralism, or low Christology reconstructions. His engagement with **Oppy**, **Ehrman**, and **Allison** illustrates a willingness to inhabit their strongest objections and then show, within shared canons of argument, why **Nicene-Chalcedonian** Christianity still stands. The result is a characteristic blend of **analytic philosophy**, **historical-critical** scholarship, and doctrinal retrieval, offered as a single case for the truth of the **Gospel** in a late-modern context.

#### 8. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary standpoint, Loke’s project invites both commendation and caution. Positively, his defense of **classical theism**, **creation ex nihilo**, **conciliar Christology**, and the **bodily resurrection** resonates with confessional commitments, and his affirmation of Christ as **YHWH** aligns with **Calvin’s autotheos** and the unity of the **Trinity**. His appeal to **abductive inference** and a **cumulative case** can be welcomed as common-grace reasoning that exposes the instability of **naturalism**. Yet a Vantilian concern arises regarding principia, since Loke leans on **natural theology**, **PSR**, and allegedly neutral **public evidence** in ways that may understate the noetic effects of sin and the role of **revelation** as principium cognoscendi. A Reformed alternative would foreground the **sensus divinitatis**, the **self-authenticating** authority of Scripture, and **covenant epistemology**, allowing natural theology a ministerial rather than magisterial role.

On Christology, Loke’s **kryptic incarnation** commendably safeguards **Chalcedon**, though Reformed scholasticism might prefer classical accounts of **communication of idioms** and the **extra Calvinisticum** to avoid any hint of internal compartmentalization of the Logos. On anthropology, his use of **genealogical Adam** can harmonize with **federal headship**, yet Reformed dogmatics will press for clarity that Adam is not only a universal genealogical ancestor but the unique federal representative whose one trespass imputed guilt to all. On providence, Loke’s reliance on **PSR** and his openness to multiple historical-causal pathways should be tethered explicitly to **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and exhaustive **providence** lest the metaphysical ground of explanation drift toward semi-autonomous structures. In short, Reformed theology can embrace much of Loke’s constructive achievement while re-centering the principled primacy of **revelation** and clarifying the doctrines of **federal headship**, **providence**, and the **extra** within a robustly confessional frame.
