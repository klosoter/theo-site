### TITLE
Correlating existential human questions with Christian symbols through an ontology of the **ground of being**.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Formed amid **German Idealism** and the **Weimar crisis**, war chaplain in 1914 to 1918, exiled by **National Socialism** in 1933, and re-rooted at **Union Theological Seminary**, **Harvard**, and **Chicago**.
- Epistemic stance: **method of correlation**, **ontological analysis of being**, **symbol theory of revelation**, and **existential hermeneutics** engaging **culture** and **philosophy**.
- Key works: **Systematic Theology** vol. 1, 1951; vol. 2, 1957; vol. 3, 1963; **The Courage to Be**, 1952; **Love, Power, and Justice**, 1954; **Dynamics of Faith**, 1957; **The Protestant Era**, 1948; **Theology of Culture**, 1959; **The Religious Situation**, 1926 German, 1932 English; **The Socialist Decision**, 1933.
- Central terms: **being-itself** and **ground of being**, **ultimate concern**, **New Being**, **estrangement**, **Protestant principle vs Catholic substance**, **theonomy**, **demonic**, **symbol and myth**.
- Interlocutors: **Kant**, **Hegel**, **Schelling**, **Heidegger**, **Kierkegaard**, **Troeltsch**, **Rudolf Otto**, with dialogue and contrast to **Karl Barth** and kinship yet difference from **Rudolf Bultmann**.
- Reformed cue: tension with **creator-creature distinction**, **personal triune Lord**, **special revelation and Scripture**, **sin as guilt** vs **estrangement**, and **justification** vs existential **acceptance**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and context: crisis, culture, and an ontology under pressure
Paul Tillich’s early life and career were forged in the crucible of **modern crisis**, from his upbringing in conservative Lutheranism to the shock of **World War I** where he served as a chaplain and absorbed the collapse of **Kulturprotestantismus**. In the 1920s he navigated the intense ferment of **Weimar intellectual life**, influenced by **German Idealism** under **Kant**, **Hegel**, and especially **Schelling**, and by **existentialism** as deepened by **Kierkegaard** and **Heidegger**. He taught in Berlin, Dresden, Leipzig, and at **Frankfurt**, developing a program of **religious socialism** and a theology of the *Kairos*, the opportune moment of the **New Being** breaking through history. Branded politically suspect by **National Socialism**, he was dismissed in 1933 and emigrated to New York. At **Union Theological Seminary** from 1933 to 1955, alongside **Reinhold Niebuhr**, and then at **Harvard** and **Chicago**, he reshaped American Protestant thought. Across this trajectory, a core motive took shape: to respond to modern **estrangement** by correlating existential questions arising from **culture** with the healing symbols of the **Christian message**, reframed through an **ontology of being**.

#### 2. Method: correlation as disciplined reciprocity between question and answer
Tillich’s celebrated **method of correlation** provides the architectonic of his **Systematic Theology**. The method pairs existential questions that arise from an analysis of **human existence** with theological answers drawn from the symbols of **revelation**. Questions are elicited through **ontological analysis** of the human condition, treating categories such as **finitude**, **anxiety**, **freedom and destiny**, and **individualization and participation**. The answers are not imposed as alien dictates but are interpreted as the **content of the Christian kerygma** that speaks to precisely those fault lines. In programmatic contrast to **Karl Barth**’s **dialectical theology**, which insists on a unilateral invasion of **revelation** that judges all human questions, Tillich insists on a disciplined reciprocity that honors both the depth of **human questioning** and the norming power of **the message**. The method allows doctrinal loci to be unfolded in order, from **reason and revelation** to **being and God**, **existence and Christ**, **life in the Spirit**, **history**, and **the Kingdom**, all under the grammar of **symbol** and **participation**.

#### 3. Being and God: the ground of being and the God beyond theism
At the heart of Tillich’s system is the claim that God is **being-itself**, the **ground of being**, not a highest instance within the **order of beings**. Any depiction of God as a being among beings is idolatrous **theism**, a projection subject to the same finitude and contingency as creatures, which for Tillich is the mark of the **demonic**. Under the influence of **Schelling’s philosophy of freedom** and **Heidegger’s Seinsfrage**, he undertakes an **ontological analysis** of the structures of being and nonbeing. Anxiety discloses nonbeing as threat, elaborated in **The Courage to Be** as the triad of fate and death, guilt and condemnation, emptiness and meaninglessness. Courage receives being from the **power of being** that affirms the self despite nonbeing. Tillich’s God is the **Unconditioned**, the inexhaustible depth and inexhaustible meaning of reality, the **God beyond the God of theism**, a phrase culminating in the *courage to accept acceptance*. He refuses the **theistic proofs**, yet he rehabilitates the **ontological argument** as an existential pointer to the depth dimension of reality. Unlike **analogia entis** proposals, his **analogy of being** is not a metaphysical bridge from created perfections to divine attributes but a disclosure of depth where all finite being participates in the **ground**.

#### 4. Christology and salvation: the New Being in Jesus as the Christ
Tillich’s soteriology radiates from the center of **Christ as the New Being**, a reality that **heals estrangement** between essence and existence, God and humanity, self and others. In **Systematic Theology, volume 2, 1957**, the figure of Jesus is interpreted as the concrete bearer of the **Spirit**, where transparency to the **ground of being** is manifested under the symbol of **Incarnation**. The doctrine of **atonement** is not framed by **penal substitution** or metaphysical satisfaction but by the overcoming of **existential estrangement** on the Cross, where the depth of abandonment is met by the **power of being** that conquers nonbeing. The **resurrection** is a symbol of the victory of the New Being rather than a biological miracle, a disclosure of **ultimate concern** rather than a violation of natural causality. The classical **Trinity** functions in his work as a symbolic triad of **power of being**, **New Being**, and **Spiritual Community**, mapping depth, event, and communal presence rather than emphasizing **ontic hypostases**. In **Love, Power, and Justice**, he binds the divine predicates around a relational metaphysic where **agape** actualizes **power** as the possibility of being for the other and **justice** orders power toward community, all as expressions of the **New Being**.

#### 5. Sin, grace, and Spirit: estrangement and acceptance in the Spiritual Community
Humanity’s tragic condition is **estrangement**, a condition Tillich distinguishes in three dimensions: separation from **God** as ground of being, alienation within the **self**, and fracture in **community**. He marks the ontological polarity of **essential and existential being**, where essence discloses our structure directed to the **Unconditioned**, while existence is marked by **finitude** and **fallen ambiguity**. **Sin** is not first a legal status but the refusal of one’s depth, a curving in upon the conditioned powers that become **demonic**. **Grace** is the acceptance that precedes our acceptance, an ontological gift of participation in **being-itself** that heals anxiety. Tillich’s reading of **justification** and **sanctification** demythologizes juridical and moralistic construals and reframes them as existential reconciliation and **Spiritual power**. The **Church** is the **Spiritual Community**, the community of those who have been grasped by **ultimate concern**, bearing the ambiguous signs of the **Kingdom** in **sacraments** understood as **bearing the power of being** through symbols of water, bread, and wine. Under the **Spirit**, the dimensions of culture that manifest creativity and justice are seen as anticipations of the **theonomy** of the Kingdom.

#### 6. Revelation, symbol, and Scripture: the Protestant principle and demonic critique
Tillich’s doctrine of **revelation** is structured by **ecstasy** and **miracle**. Ecstasy names the state where finite forms are transparently open to the **Unconditioned**, and miracle names an event that points beyond itself without violating the structures of being. All revelation is mediated through **symbol**, and symbols **participate** in the reality they signify. Symbols are born and die; they cannot be absolutized without becoming **demonic**, a central thrust of the **Protestant principle** which insists that no finite expression can be equated with the **Unconditioned**. In works like **The Protestant Era** and **Theology of Culture**, Tillich contrasts the **Protestant principle** with the **Catholic substance**, the latter preserving the sacramental fullness of being, the former incessantly critiquing idolatrous enclosure. **Scripture** is a privileged symbol of revelation, norming because of its witness to the **Christ event** as **New Being**, yet Scripture remains within history’s ambiguity and requires **existential interpretation**. In proximity to **Rudolf Bultmann**, Tillich affirms **demythologization** as necessary hermeneutical labor, yet he retains more ontological metaphors than Bultmann’s kerygmatic minimalism, grounding proclamation in an account of **being and nonbeing**.

#### 7. Ethics, politics, and culture: theonomy amid autonomy and heteronomy
Tillich’s ethics unfolds the polarity of **autonomy** and **heteronomy** under the higher synthesis of **theonomy**, where the **law of being** becomes translucent in **Spirit**. In the interwar period, his commitment to **religious socialism** and the critique in **The Socialist Decision**, 1933, attempted to navigate between ideologies by exposing the **demonic** sacralization of nation, race, and economic powers. Later, in **Love, Power, and Justice**, he argues that **agape** without **power** is sentimentality, **power** without **justice** is domination, and **justice** without **love** ossifies into legality. Sermons in **The Shaking of the Foundations** display his capacity to translate **depth ontology** into pastoral proclamation, addressing **anxiety**, **meaninglessness**, and **guilt** with the message of **acceptance**. His aesthetics and cultural theory affirm that **art**, **psychoanalysis**, and **philosophy** can bear revelatory depth insofar as they break open the **surface** to the **ground of being**, a claim elaborated in **Theology of Culture** and later essays on **religion and culture**. The critical principle remains that every finite bearer is ambiguous and must be tested by the **Protestant principle**.

#### 8. Major works and system development: from The Religious Situation to Systematic Theology
Tillich’s intellectual biography can be traced through key publications that mark transitions in emphasis while maintaining a unified **ontological-theological project**. **The Religious Situation**, 1926 German, 1932 English, diagnoses modernity’s **crisis of meaning** and the fragmentation of **culture**. **On the Boundary**, 1936, narrates his intellectual itinerary and the vocation of theology between **church** and **academy**. The three volumes of **Systematic Theology** appear in 1951, 1957, and 1963, respectively, laying out the architectonics of **method of correlation**, **God as ground of being**, **Christ as New Being**, **Spirit and life**, and **history and Kingdom**. **The Courage to Be**, 1952, appropriates **Heideggerian** analysis to address **anxiety** before **nonbeing**, culminating in the **God above the God of theism**. **Love, Power, and Justice**, 1954, reframes **ethics** ontologically. **Biblical Religion and the Search for Ultimate Reality**, 1955, insists that **biblical symbols** uniquely answer ultimate questions. **Dynamics of Faith**, 1957, defines faith as **ultimate concern** and clarifies the **symbolic** character of all religious language. Late essays in **Theology of Culture**, 1959, and **Morality and Beyond**, 1963, continue to explore **theonomy** beyond mere moralism. His final lectures at **Chicago** press deeper into the relation of **religion** and **culture**, maintaining the same **Protestant principle**.

#### 9. Interlocutors and debates: dialogical construction under modern pressures
Tillich’s constructive labor is deeply dialogical, engaging **Kant** on the limits of reason and **the moral law**, **Hegel** on the unity of **spirit** and history, and **Schelling** on **freedom** and the **ground**. With **Heidegger**, he shares the project of **ontological analysis**, though Tillich resists an exclusively **phenomenological** register by insisting on the **Unconditioned** as depth. From **Kierkegaard**, he draws the crisis of **existence** and **decision**, yet he seeks synthesis rather than pure paradox. He converses with **Rudolf Otto** on the **numinous** and holiness and with **Ernst Troeltsch** regarding the **historical-relativist** challenge. Against **Barth**, Tillich defends the **method of correlation** as faithful to the **incarnational** logic of mediation, insisting that the **message** meets the **question** in history and culture rather than obliterating it. In relation to **Bultmann**, Tillich welcomes **demythologization** but retains a thicker **ontological** account than a mere kerygma. His claim that God is **being-itself** elicited criticism from analytic philosophers of religion who defended a **personal theism**, yet Tillich maintained that the **personal** is a symbol that must be preserved within, not against, **the depth of being**.

#### 10. Eschatology and history: eternal now and the ambiguity of the finite
Tillich’s eschatology revolves around the **eternal now**, the irruption of the **Unconditioned** into time as **kairos**, a category he had already used in his Weimar writings on **history**. The **Kingdom of God** is present as **anticipation** within the **Spiritual Community**, never possessed, always breaking judgment upon our **idolatrous** closures. The ambiguity of history persists because the finite cannot bear the infinite without distortion; hence the necessity of the **Protestant principle** to judge every **church**, **state**, and **culture**. In **Systematic Theology, volume 3, 1963**, he elaborates on **history and the Kingdom**, tying **eschatology** to the fulfillment of the **New Being** in the universal **reconciliation** of estranged realities. The last word is not progress but **transcendence**, not a temporal extension but the depth-transformation of time by the **ground of being**. Death remains the boundary, yet **courage** receives even death as traversed by the **power of being**. The eschatological horizon thus sanctions engaged **ethics** without utopianism and offers **hope** grounded not in historical inevitability but in the **Unconditioned** that sustains and judges history.

#### 11. Reception and legacy: American reconfiguration and global religious studies
In the United States, Tillich became a public intellectual whose **Theology of Culture** shaped seminaries and the wider **academy**, connecting **religion**, **psychoanalysis**, **art**, and **politics**. Colleagues like **Reinhold Niebuhr** sharpened debates on **nature and grace** and the limits of social hope, while philosophers and theologians debated **being-itself** in light of **personal theism** and analytic **philosophy of religion**. Later **death-of-God** theologians such as **Thomas Altizer** radicalized the **God beyond theism** theme, though Tillich himself insisted on the **Unconditioned** rather than a literal negation of **God**. His proposals were received with interest by **comparative religion** and shaped conversations on **symbol** that stretched into **semiotics** and **hermeneutics**. Even critics found the **method of correlation** fruitful for preaching to modern **anxiety** and **meaninglessness**, and pastors drew on **Dynamics of Faith** to clarify **ultimate concern**. At the same time, ecclesial theologians worried that a depth-ontology displaced the **historical concreteness** of **Israel** and **Christ**, a concern that would become central in later **postliberal** and **narrative** theologies that sought to re-center **Scripture** and **community**.

#### 12. Reformed and WTS evaluation: revelation, ontology, and the gospel’s objectivity
From a classical **Reformed** and **WTS** vantage, Tillich’s project exposes both salutary insights and profound tensions. Positively, the **Protestant principle** offers a stern critique of **idolatry**, warning the church against identifying any finite form with the **Unconditioned**, a caution consonant with the **second commandment** and a Reformed suspicion of **culture-church conflations**. His attentiveness to **general revelation** in **culture**, along with the moral clarity of **Love, Power, and Justice**, can enrich a **common grace** account of cultural life. Yet at the level of principle, Tillich’s identification of God as **being-itself** risks dissolving the **creator-creature distinction** into an ontological continuum of **participation** in the **ground**, thereby subverting the biblical confession of the **personal triune Lord** who freely speaks and covenants. By recasting **revelation** as depth-ecstasy and by treating **Scripture** as an ambiguous symbol rather than **norming norm**, the **method of correlation** bends the divine **Word** toward the measure of existential **questions**, inverting the Reformed commitment that **special revelation** canonically norms the questions themselves. His **sin as estrangement** underplays **guilt and corruption** before a holy God, and his **grace as acceptance** sidelines **justification by the imputed righteousness of Christ**. The **resurrection** as symbol rather than event weakens the **gospel’s objectivity** in **redemptive history** and eclipses the **covenantal** plot wherein God acts in promise and fulfillment. A Reformed alternative would preserve Tillich’s prophetic critique of the **demonic** while grounding **correlation** not in an ontology of **being-itself** but in **covenant revelation**, where **general revelation** sets the stage and **special revelation** in **Christ and Scripture** defines the drama, so that **culture** is engaged under the lordship of the **personal triune God** who is both transcendent Creator and faithful Redeemer.
