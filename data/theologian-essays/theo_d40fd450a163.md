### TITLE
Retrieving Nicaea as a doxological and soteriological grammar of the Trinity, ordered to deification through the cross

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth and early twenty-first century **ressourcement Catholicism** engaging the patristic renewal and the **pro-Nicene consensus** in dialogue with modern Trinitarian theologies.
- Historically rigorous **dogmatic retrieval** that integrates **theological exegesis**, **liturgy and doxology**, and a participatory **soteriological ontology** guided by the **rule of faith**.
- **Athanasius: The Coherence of His Thought, 1998**; **Athanasius, 2004**; **Retrieving Nicaea: The Development and Meaning of Trinitarian Doctrine, 2011**; **Deification through the Cross: An Eastern Christian Theology of Salvation, 2020**.
- Core categories include **homoousios**, **relations of origin**, **inseparable operations**, **theologia and oikonomia**, **theosis**, **divine simplicity**, and **paschal deification**.
- Converses with **Athanasius, the Cappadocians, Augustine**, and modern voices like **Karl Rahner, Karl Barth, Hans Urs von Balthasar**, while aligning with patristic historians **Lewis Ayres** and **Michel Barnes** against the **de Régnon thesis**.
- Reformed cue: strong convergence on **classical theism** and **Nicene grammar** with points of tension around **nature and grace**, **theosis**, and the soteriological weighting of **sacrament and liturgy**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
The work of **Khaled Anatolios** arises within a broad post Vatican II **ressourcement** that has re-centered Christian doctrine around the **patristic imagination** and the **rule of faith**, yet with a distinctive aim to weld historical precision to dogmatic clarity. His setting is the contemporary academy where the **pro-Nicene consensus** has been reconstructed by historians like **Lewis Ayres** and **Michel Barnes** as a richly unified yet pluriform tradition. Against both thin historicism and free-floating systematics, Anatolios advances a **dogmatic retrieval** that treats the early councils as living grammar for the Church’s confession. Methodologically, his hallmark is a disciplined interplay of **theological exegesis**, **liturgy and doxology**, and **metaphysical grammar** that he names a **soteriological ontology**. This approach presumes that the doctrine of the **Trinity** is the doctrine of **salvation**, and that every claim about **divine simplicity** or **homoousios** is finally about the shape of creaturely participation in the **triune life**.

Anatolios’s epistemic stance is catholic and ascetical. He reads Scripture within the **canon, creed, and cultus** as mutually implicating witnesses. The classical distinction of **theologia and oikonomia** is not a barrier but a hinge, for the missions of the Son and Spirit manifest the processions, while the processions ground the missions. He affirms **Rahner’s rule** in a patristically chastened register, insisting that the **economic Trinity** is truly the **immanent Trinity**, yet only as confessed under the constraints of **divine simplicity** and **apophatic reserve**. The upshot is a habitus of **doxological reasoning**, where the Church’s prayer to the Father through the Son in the Holy Spirit is the hermeneutical cradle of all talk about **relations of origin** and **inseparable operations**.

Anatolios also positions himself against modern dichotomies that oppose **metaphysics** to **biblical narrative** or **dogma** to **spirituality**. His method counters this by retrieving the patristic sense that **metaphysical predicates** like **impassibility**, **simplicity**, and **immutability** are liturgical guardrails for confessing the **identity of God** made known in Christ. The early councils are not museum pieces; they render the grammar by which the **paschal mystery** is confessed without collapse into either **subordinationism** or **tritheism**. Thus his method exemplifies a catholic synthesis of **exegesis**, **dogmatics**, and **contemplative practice**, grounded in **Nicene** faith and ordered to **theosis**.

#### 2. Early focus: Athanasius and soteriological ontology
Anatolios’s first major study, **Athanasius: The Coherence of His Thought, 1998**, established the axis of his future work. He argues that **Athanasius** is not a polemicist improvising under pressure but a coherent theologian whose **anti-Arian** polemics, **Trinitarian confession**, and **soteriology of deification** form a single **soteriological ontology**. The stakes of **homoousios** are soteriological: only if the Son is consubstantial with the Father can the economy of salvation effect genuine **participation** in the divine life. In this frame, the *theologia* of the Son’s eternal generation is internally ordered to the *oikonomia* of the Son’s incarnate mediation.

A key contribution is his treatment of **Athanasius’s epinoia** discourse, the notion of conceptualizing the divine through saving acts. Anatolios shows that for **Athanasius**, the many names and titles of Christ are anchored in the real identity of the Son who enacts **inseparable operations** with the Father. **Creation ex nihilo** functions as a boundary marker that secures the ontological divide between Creator and creature while also grounding the gratuitousness of **grace**. This yields a metaphysical map in which salvation is not moral improvement but **ontological participation** by **adoption** into the filial relation, fulfilled in the Spirit. The profound coherence of **incarnation** and **deification** not only answers **Arian** reduction but articulates how **divine simplicity** makes possible the unity of divine act toward creatures.

Anatolios’s later primer, **Athanasius, 2004**, distilled these insights for broader readership while maintaining the patristic density of terms like **ousia**, **hypostasis**, and **energeia**. He continues to display how **Athanasius** reads Scripture under the **rule of faith**, demonstrating that theological judgments about **Trinity** and **Christology** arise from ecclesial worship rather than speculative curiosity. The coherence he uncovers lays the foundation for his signature claim that **Nicene dogma** is an integrated **soteriological grammar**.

#### 3. Retrieving Nicaea: the pro-Nicene pattern
The synthesis of his historical and dogmatic labors arrives in **Retrieving Nicaea: The Development and Meaning of Trinitarian Doctrine, 2011**. Here Anatolios sets forth the **pro-Nicene consensus** as a theological pattern, not a slogan, whose salient features can be named and reinhabited. He identifies convergent commitments in **Athanasius**, the **Cappadocian Fathers**, and **Augustine** that secure the unity of God and the distinctions of the **divine persons**. These commitments include **divine simplicity**, **relations of origin** as the only real distinctions in God, and **inseparable operations** by which the one undivided divine act is manifest in the economy in modes proper to Father, Son, and Spirit.

Crucially, Anatolios rejects the **de Régnon thesis** that posits a structural opposition between East and West, where the East supposedly begins with persons and the West with essence. He shows instead that both **Cappadocians** and **Augustine** treat **ousia** and **hypostasis** as correlative and that both articulate **inseparable operations** while allowing for **appropriations** that pastorally instruct the faithful. He further contends that the **homoousios** is not a metaphysical moniker detached from Scripture but the creedal crystallization of biblical confession, particularly the **Johannine** presentation of the Son as the one who does what only God does.

This retrieval prioritizes **scriptural exegesis** as the matrix of doctrine. Anatolios rehearses pro-Nicene readings of key texts, showing for instance how **John 5** and **John 10** underwrite claims of consubstantial unity, and how **Romans 8** grounds triune agency in sanctification. The goal is to reactivate a **doxological hermeneutic** in which confessing **one God the Father** entails confessing the Son and Spirit as sharing the single divine **energeia**, such that the economy becomes the transparent enactment of the eternal **processions**.

#### 4. Simplicity, missions, and the shape of confession
A recurring pillar in Anatolios’s corpus is the claim that **divine simplicity** is the enabling grammar of **Trinitarian confession** rather than an obstacle to personal differentiation. Borrowing from both **Cappadocian** metaphysics and **Augustinian** insight, he clarifies that the one divine essence is numerically single and wholly possessed by each divine person according to **relations of origin**. From this it follows that the Father, Son, and Spirit perform **inseparable operations**, such that every external work of God is one undivided act of the triune God.

Anatolios integrates this grammar with **Rahner’s rule** by mapping the **missions** of the Son and Spirit onto their eternal **processions**. The visible mission of the **incarnation** and the invisible mission of the **Spirit** are not ad hoc events but reveal the eternal **generation** and **spiration**. Yet because of **apophatic reserve**, the revelation gives true knowledge without exhausting the mystery of the intra-divine life. Hence he resists forms of **social trinitarianism** that treat the persons as three centers of consciousness, and he distances himself from any **functional subordinationism** in which the Son’s obedience in the economy is projected into eternal hierarchy. The resulting confession sustains **monotheism** without modal collapse and preserves **personal distinction** without tritheist dispersion.

This grammar also shapes prayer. Following pro-Nicene practice, Anatolios places doxology at the center of theology, directing **liturgical prayer** to the Father, through the Son, in the Holy Spirit. Such **appropriation** is pedagogical rather than ontological division, cultivating a sense for the unity of operation and the personal modalities of grace. His portrayal of **baptismal** and **eucharistic** life as participation in the triune missions renders metaphysics as an undergirding of **ecclesial mystagogy**.

#### 5. Deification through the Cross: paschal participation
With **Deification through the Cross, 2020**, Anatolios extends his Trinitarian grammar into a comprehensive **soteriology**. The controlling thesis is that **theosis** is irreducibly **paschal**, that the Church’s union with God is mediated through the crucified and risen Christ in the Spirit. He resists any bifurcation of **atonement** and **deification**, arguing for an integrated account that gathers **Christus Victor**, **satisfaction and sacrifice**, and **recapitulation** into the one action of the **triune God**. In this rendering, the cross is not an addendum to participation but its very form, the place where the Son’s filial obedience opens creaturely existence to **adoptive sonship**.

Drawing deeply on **Athanasius**, **Cyril of Alexandria**, **Maximus the Confessor**, and **John of Damascus**, Anatolios frames sin as a disordering of participation that encloses the self in non-communion. The cruciform obedience of the incarnate Son, enacted through **inseparable operations**, repairs this rupture by reconfiguring human life to the **relation of origin** that characterizes the Son. The Spirit then assimilates the Church to Christ’s filial relation, which is to say that **sanctification** is the Spirit’s mission to enact in us what belongs to the Son by nature. The grammar of **divine simplicity** protects the unity of this saving act, while the distinct missions ensure that participation preserves **personal differentiation** rather than absorbing the creature into the divine essence.

Liturgically, Anatolios reads the **eucharist** and the **Paschal Triduum** as the Church’s ongoing incorporation into the **paschal mystery**. The repetition of anamnesis is not mere recollection but a sacramental intensification of **communion** in the triune life. This sacramental emphasis coheres with his earlier insistence that doctrine is doxological. The **cross** is the axis of Christian existence, and **deification** is cruciform, welcoming the believer into the pattern of death and resurrection that the Son shares with the Father in the Spirit.

#### 6. Scripture, canon, and theological interpretation
Anatolios resists any driving wedge between **Scripture** and **dogma** by exemplifying **theological exegesis** under the **rule of faith**. He offers close readings of **Johannine** Christology, where the Son does the works of the Father, and of **Pauline** texts like **Philippians 2** and **Romans 8**, in which the kenotic descent of the Son and the adoptive work of the Spirit emerge within a robust monotheism. For Anatolios, *kenosis* is the economic manifestation of the Son’s eternal **fromness**, not a compromise of **divine impassibility** but its redemptive mode in the economy by which God in unchangeable love heals the mutable creature.

This exegetical posture is not anti-critical but post-critical. He appreciates historical-critical insights while insisting they be subsumed beneath the canonical unity that the **creed** articulates. The **pro-Nicene consensus** provides a set of reading rules that forestall both **Arianizing historicism** and speculative fantasia. By demanding that exegesis respect **inseparable operations** and **relations of origin**, Anatolios shows how Scripture yields a **Trinitarian pattern** without proof-texting. The result is a model of doctrinal exegesis where the **Trinity** is not superimposed on the text but is the Church’s confession of what the text narrates.

#### 7. Interlocutors and contemporary positioning
Anatolios regularly stages conversation with modern giants like **Karl Rahner**, **Karl Barth**, and **Hans Urs von Balthasar**, as well as with recent debates around **analytic trinitarianism** and **social models**. From **Rahner**, he appropriates the imperative to correlate **economy** and **immanence** while refusing to flatten the mystery of **relations of origin** into economy-only predicates. With **Barth**, he shares the resolve to center **revelation** in Jesus Christ, though Anatolios resists reducing the doctrine of the Trinity to a function of the doctrine of revelation by recovering the **patristic metaphysics** of **simplicity**. He appreciates **Balthasar’s** paschal imagination, reframing it within the precise grammar of **homoousios** and **inseparable operations** to avoid any suggestion of intratrinitarian rupture.

Anatolios also engages the historiography of trinitarian doctrine through dialogue with **Lewis Ayres** and **Michel Barnes**, endorsing their critique of the **de Régnon thesis**. He is wary of **social trinitarianism** that inductively projects human communality into the Godhead. Likewise, he critiques thin accounts of **divine passibility** that make God a fellow sufferer without retrieving the older distinction between **impassible nature** and **passible flesh** in the **communicatio idiomatum**. The force of his positioning is constructive moderation, restoring **classical theism** without neglecting the **economy** and affirming the **mystery** without silencing **soteriology**.

#### 8. Doctrinal synthesis and pastoral telos
Across his corpus, Anatolios offers a doctrinal synthesis in which the **Trinity**, **Christology**, and **soteriology** interlock. **Divine simplicity** guarantees that God is one, the **relations of origin** differentiate the three, **inseparable operations** name the one action of God in creation and redemption, and **theosis** names the end of that action in creaturely participation. The Church confesses this in the **rule of faith**, celebrates it in **liturgy**, and reads it in **Scripture**. All of these are not separate trajectories but integrated aspects of the same **doxological life**.

The pastoral telos is explicit. Doctrine serves **discipleship**, not in a utilitarian sense but by training the community to inhabit the shape of **filial obedience** and **Spirit-led sanctification**. Anatolios portrays Christian existence as participation in the Son’s relation to the Father, which is to say that holiness is nothing other than sharing in the **missions** that manifest the **processions**. The Church’s catechesis thus orients believers toward a **cruciform theosis**, holding together **atonement** and **participation** under the sign of the **paschal mystery**.

#### 9. Contribution and critique in the field
In the wider field of **systematic theology**, Anatolios’s enduring contribution is to have shown that the **pro-Nicene consensus** is a usable past for constructive dogmatics. He clarifies that **homoousios** is doxologically necessary, that **divine simplicity** is soteriologically fecund, and that **inseparable operations** anchor a robust sense of triune agency. He demonstrates that the East and West, typified by the **Cappadocians** and **Augustine**, coincide at the deepest levels of Trinitarian grammar. This coherence offers a prophylaxis against recurring pathologies, from **subordinationism** to **tritheism**, and equips churches to confess the **Trinity** in a manner that is both biblically faithful and metaphysically responsible.

Yet his retrieval is not static. By insisting on **theological exegesis** and **liturgical doxology**, Anatolios presses beyond mere repristination toward a contemporary inhabitation of the doctrine. His account of **deification through the cross** is a notable advance, integrating divergent atonement motifs within a thoroughgoing **participatory ontology**. In an era wary of metaphysics, he exemplifies how metaphysical commitments serve evangelical proclamation and ecclesial formation.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed and Westminster** vantage, Anatolios offers substantial common ground and real points of tension. There is strong convergence on **classical theism**, **divine simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, and the **pro-Nicene consensus**, along with agreement that **homoousios** is soteriologically decisive and that **Rahner’s rule** must be constrained by **relations of origin**. Reformed theology can affirm his emphasis on **Scripture under the rule of faith**, his caution toward **social trinitarianism**, and his insistence that **economy** reveals rather than constructs the **immanent Trinity**. The tensions arise chiefly around the **nature and grace** schema and the form of **theosis**. Where Anatolios’s **paschal deification** is sacramentally thick and ontologically participatory, the Reformed tradition, while embracing **union with Christ**, accents **covenant** and the **forensic-transformation** duplex under **federal headship**, thereby guarding **grace** as a redemptive reconstitution rather than elevation of an intrinsic capacity. A Reformed critique would also register caution toward any reading of **kenosis** or **passibility** that risks diluting **immutability**, even as it can welcome his Chalcedonian insistence that the **impassible** God truly suffers in the flesh of the Son. On balance, Anatolios’s retrieval of **Nicene grammar** and **cruciform theosis** provides substantial resources that Reformed orthodoxy can appropriate, so long as participation is explicitly tethered to **covenantal union with Christ** and framed by **autotheos** and the primacy of **revelation** in the Word written and preached.
