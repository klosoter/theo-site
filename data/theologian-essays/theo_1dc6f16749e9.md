### TITLE
Retrieving classical Reformed doctrine of God and the cross for contemporary evangelical theology through a rigorous defense of penal substitution and divine impassibility.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth to early twenty-first century British evangelicalism**, intersection of **postliberal** and **open theist** challenges, and retrieval of **Reformed scholasticism** within ministerial training.
- **Text-driven dogmatics** wedded to **confessional realism**, privileging **Sola Scriptura**, **analogia fidei**, and **inseparable operations** over revisionist metaphysics.
- Key works: **Penal Substitution: A Response to Recent Criticisms** Themelios 2006; essay in **The Atonement Debate** 2008; **His Love Endures Forever** 2016; essays on **John Owen** and **definite atonement** c. 2012–2013.
- Central terms: **penal substitutionary atonement**, **divine impassibility**, **immutability**, **divine simplicity**, **triune harmony**, **covenant curse**, **autotheos** and **eternal generation**.
- Interlocutors: **Steve Chalke**, **J. D. G. Dunn and N. T. Wright** on atonement framing, **J. Denny Weaver**, **Joel Green and Mark Baker**, **Jürgen Moltmann**, **Robert Jenson**, **open theism** figures like **Clark Pinnock** and **Greg Boyd**; influences include **Calvin**, **Turretin**, **John Owen**, **John Murray**.
- Reformed reception: warmly received in **confessional Reformed** and **WTS** circles for defending **classical theism** and **PSA**, with discussion on exegetical tone and metaphysical specificity.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and orientation
Garry J. Williams emerges within late-twentieth and early-twenty-first century **British evangelical** theology as a disciplined exponent of **classical Reformed orthodoxy**. Educated in the universities of the United Kingdom and long associated with London Seminary’s **John Owen Centre** for Theological Study, his work moves along two axes: retrieval of **Reformed scholastic** precision and pastoral provisioning for ministers. That institutional nexus shapes his method. He stands against revisionist trajectories from **open theism**, **process-adjacent passibilism**, and **social trinitarianism**, while engaging analytic clarity without surrendering the **confessional realism** of **divine simplicity** and **immutability**. Historical interlocutors include **Calvin** and **Turretin**, with theological instincts decisively marked by **John Owen** on the atonement, **John Murray** on the accomplishment-application nexus, and the **Westminster Standards** on **triune operations**. His distinctive register is not that of the research monograph alone but of the dogmatic essay and the carefully targeted book, crafted for the contested public of evangelical theology.

At the level of posture, Williams’s theological impulse is that exegesis must be indwelt by dogmatics. He rejects a modern bifurcation that opposes biblical theology to systematic theology, insisting on **analogia fidei** as hermeneutical guardrail and on **inseparable operations** as the grammar of Scripture’s depiction of the **triune God**. He insists that metaphysical predicates like **simplicity** and **immutability** clarify rather than obscure Scripture’s witness to **divine love** and **wrath**, because they protect God’s aseity and ensure that salvation is from God alone. This is a classic **Sola Scriptura** stance that is not biblicist. It assumes that the catholic dogmatic tradition furnishes tested rules of reading where **classical theism** calibrates the reading of anthropopathic texts, retaining analogical truth while refusing creaturely passions in God.

#### 2. The atonement debates and penal substitution
The public frame for Williams’s central contribution is the British atonement controversy catalyzed by **Steve Chalke’s** 2003 characterization of **penal substitutionary atonement** as “cosmic child abuse.” Williams’s response appears early and decisively in Themelios as **Penal Substitution: A Response to Recent Criticisms** 2006 and then in the collected volume **The Atonement Debate** 2008. He confronts both rhetorical caricatures and exegetical objections, ranging from the claim that retributive justice is sub-Christian to the suggestion that penal substitution fractures the **Trinity**. His strategy is twofold. First, he demonstrates that penal substitution is not an isolated metaphor but a canonical nexus grounded in the **covenant curse** motif Deuteronomy 27 to 30, **Isaiah’s Servant** Isa 52 to 53, and **Paul’s law-court** and **curse** idioms Galatians 3:13 and Romans 3:21 to 26. Second, he argues that the substitution is triune in origin and execution, thereby safeguarding **triune harmony** and the unity of will ad intra.

The battering ram of his argument is that divine **wrath** is not an unprincipled divine affect but the settled judicial opposition of God’s **holiness** to sin. That holiness, in his account, is coordinated with **divine love** rather than contrasted with it. Hence the cross is not God overcoming his own arbitrary rage but the **God-man** bearing the just sanction of the law in love. He retrieves **Owen’s** logic that the atonement must have an internal teleology and definite efficacy or else it cannot be a true satisfaction; that frame links **retributive justice** to **covenant mercy**. Williams destabilizes the revisionist claim that **Christus Victor** and **penal substitution** are alternatives. He argues instead that victory over the powers requires the juridical disarming of their claim, a disarming only achieved through penal satisfaction. The cross is thus victoriously juridical.

Against pacifist or non-violent atonement proposals from **J. Denny Weaver** and against **Joel Green** and **Mark Baker’s** reduction of atonement plurality to a narrative hermeneutic, Williams insists on a controlled pluralism under a juridical center. He points to the New Testament’s use of **hilastērion** and to sacrificial typology in Leviticus as suffused with notions of liability, cultic cleansing, and satisfaction. The lineaments of **federal theology** surface as he reads Adamic and Mosaic structures through the lens of the Last Adam who bears the sanction of broken covenant for his elect. His analytic watchword is that the cross addresses the problem that Scripture actually sets: guilt in foro Dei, the divine court.

#### 3. Divine impassibility, immutability, and love
If penal substitution is Williams’s great apologetic cause, his most architectonic dogmatic contribution is the defense of **divine impassibility** as integral to **divine love**. In his short but pointed book **His Love Endures Forever** 2016 he argues that the biblical confession of God’s **immutability** and **simplicity** secures, rather than jeopardizes, the constancy and freedom of God’s love. He clarifies the distinction between passions as passively received, creaturely alterations and **affections** properly predicated of God analogically. Love in God is not a fluctuating psychological state but the living plenitude of his perfect will. Therefore God’s love cannot be heightened by suffering nor diminished by sin. The payoff is dogmatic and pastoral: because God is impassible, his covenant **hesed** endures forever Psalm 136, and because he is simple and immutable, he is never internally at war with himself at the cross.

A core move in Williams’s case is to anchor metaphysical rules in Scripture itself. He reads **Exodus 34:6-7** with **James 1:17** to align the Lord’s name as abounding in steadfast love with the confession that in God there is no variation or shadow due to change. He acknowledges anthropopathic speech yet refuses to corral it into bare metaphor. Instead he argues for **analogical predication** guided by the Creator-creature distinction. Here **classical theism** is not philosophy grafted onto the Bible but the grammar of the Bible’s God. Against **open theism** and **Jürgen Moltmann’s** and **Robert Jenson’s** various passibilisms, Williams insists that God’s free decree and eternal act ad intra secure the history of salvation ad extra, without collapsing God into history. The cross is then a temporal manifestation of the eternal will of love, not a historical event that alters the divine life.

This dovetails with his trinitarian emphasis on **inseparable operations** and the one simple will of God. Because the Father, Son, and Spirit act undividedly in every external work, the giving of the Son to bear wrath is the concerted act of triune love. The specter of **patripassianism** is dismissed not by denying cost to the Father but by denying creaturely passion in God altogether. He leans on **autotheos** in **Calvin** rightly framed alongside **eternal generation** to maintain both the aseity of the Son as to essence and the order of subsistence. This equilibrium prevents both social trinitarian partitions and modalist collapses and so clears a path for a penal substitution that is fully trinitarian.

#### 4. Owen, definite atonement, and retrieval
Williams’s retrieval instinct finds a natural exemplar in **John Owen**, whose **The Death of Death in the Death of Christ** provides the classic Reformed case for **definite atonement**. Williams appropriates and rearticulates Owen’s ordo of intent, extent, and application, arguing that the satisfaction rendered by Christ is of such a nature that it cannot be indeterminate in scope. Satisfaction grounds the infallible salvation of a people, not the bare possibility of salvation. While recognizing the polyvalence of atonement language in the New Testament, he stresses that the logic of substitution coupled with the **priestly office** of Christ requires efficacy indexed to election. This is not a scholastic curiosity but a biblical implication once the atonement is set within the covenantal and priestly structures of Scripture.

In his essays and lectures on Owen’s **trinitarian soteriology**, Williams highlights the pactum salutis, the pretemporal counsel of peace, as the background that makes sense of the Son’s mission and work. The Father sends the Son to secure a people given to him, the Son willingly offers himself through the eternal Spirit, and the Spirit applies that purchased redemption to the elect. Under this grammar, talk of atonement intent is not reductionist arithmetic but participation in the Bible’s own doxological description of the **triune economy**. Williams pushes back on contemporary discomfort with definiteness by noting that pastoral proclamation gains traction, not loses it, when grounded in a cross that actually saves rather than one that hypothetically might.

Alongside Owen, Williams frequently cites **Turretin** and **Murray** to demonstrate continuity across **Reformed orthodoxy**. He treats Murray’s distinction between the accomplishment and application of redemption as protective of penal substitution’s efficacy and as hermeneutically vital to avoid conflating the ordo salutis. The accent is on a disciplined soteriological grammar, one that resists collapsing justification into transformation while still affirming the indivisible Christ who is both righteousness and sanctification.

#### 5. Scripture, hermeneutics, and triune action
Methodologically, Williams refuses the fragmentation of theological disciplines. He urges pastors and scholars to practice what might be called canonical dogmatics. He deploys **analogia fidei** and **Scripture interprets Scripture** to thread Old Testament legal and sacrificial categories through the Gospels and Paul. His interpretive center is not a single text but a network: **Levitical cult**, **Isaianic Servant**, **Pauline justification**, and **Hebrews’ priesthood**. This canonical web supports penal substitution while illuminating other motifs such as **Christus Victor**, **recapitulation**, and **new exodus**. The web is held together by the doctrine of **inseparable operations**. Without this trinitarian anchor, the New Testament’s manifold descriptions of Christ’s work risk being treated as competing metaphors rather than coinhering descriptions of one saving act of the **triune God**.

Williams’s hermeneutical caution regarding reductionist narrative approaches is evident in his engagement with **N. T. Wright** and **Joel Green**. He applauds the retrieval of biblical-theological frameworks like exile and return but criticizes the tendency to recast forensic concepts into covenantal relation without juridical content. For Williams, covenant is a juridical relation as well as a communion, and law is not a foil to grace but the theater wherein grace triumphs through the satisfaction rendered by Christ. Consequently, sanction, curse, and justice are not antithetical to love; they are the dark relief that displays love’s blazing hue.

#### 6. Pastoral theology, churchly purpose, and public apologetic
The institutional location of Williams’s work in a pastors’ academy shapes his prose: dense but usable. Thematically, **divine impassibility** is not an academic ornament but the basis for pastoral assurance. An impassible God is a stable rock in suffering. He contends that comforting the bereaved or emboldening the tempted requires proclamation of the God whose **immutability** and **simplicity** secure his ever-fresh mercy. Likewise the clarity about **penal substitution** equips preaching to address guilt and shame with real absolution rather than with therapeutic uplift. He worries that atonement revisions trim the gospel to fit the plausibility structures of late-modern sentiment, whereas the apostolic kerygma aims to transform those structures by announcing a crucified and risen **Lord**.

Publicly, Williams argues that apologetics should not relieve the scandal of the cross but explain it. The cross is a stumbling block because it names sin as guilt under a holy God, and it answers by the self-giving of that same God in the flesh. In this frame, the church’s catechesis must restore basic dogmatic grammar. Terms like **simplicity**, **inseparable operations**, **hypostatic union**, and **satisfaction** are not scholastic distractions but bearings without which the church will drift into either moralism or mysticism. His study of **Owen** is in service of that catechesis, modeling how historic theology can be re-inhabited rather than merely quoted.

#### 7. Interlocutors and contested borders
Williams’s polemical edge is sharp yet measured. Against **Moltmann** and **Jenson**, he contends that divine **passibility** instrumentalizes God’s life to secure empathy at the cost of aseity and the Creator-creature distinction. The argument is that a God who needs the world to be love loses the freedom to love sinners. Against **open theism** represented by **Pinnock** and **Boyd**, he insists that real fellowship with God does not require divine temporality or ignorance. Rather, fellowship with the living God depends on the divine lordship and omniscience that make covenant promises unbreakable. Against **atonement revisionists** like **Weaver** and **Green**, he argues that expunging retributive justice from the cross removes the very ground on which reconciliation and victory stand. The result of these engagements is not a bunker but a guarded catholicity: he affirms multiple atonement motifs while securing their juridical core, welcomes renewed trinitarian interest while corralling it within **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** grammar, and embraces biblical theology while refusing its deployment against **forensic justification**.

His constructive counter-claims are consistent. The doctrine of **eternal generation** framed within **autotheos** protects both the full deity of the Son and the order of personal subsistence. The rule of **inseparable operations** secures trinitarian unity against partitioned agencies. The attributes of **simplicity** and **immutability** secure **impassibility**, which secures **covenant love**. And the nexus of **penal substitution**, **priestly representation**, and **definite atonement** secures the unbreakable link between cross and application so that preaching can promise salvation, not merely offer it as a possibility.

#### 8. Consolidation and profile
The through-line of Williams’s oeuvre is the conviction that theology must say what Scripture says with the church’s best words. This yields a signature profile. He is a defender of **penal substitutionary atonement** against its most potent contemporary criticisms. He is a resourcer of **classical theism** against the allure of modern **passibilism**. He is an interpreter of **John Owen** and a custodian of **Reformed scholastic** clarity. He is a pastor-theologian who aims his work at the pulpit and the catechism rather than at the fashionable seminar. The body of work spans essays of 2006 and 2008 on atonement, a 2016 book on **divine love and immutability**, and ongoing lectures and publications on **Owen**, **definite atonement**, and the doctrine of God. The intellectual style is retrievalal yet contemporary, with an eye for clean distinctions held in service of gospel proclamation.

Reformed and WTS evaluation. Williams’s project stands squarely within **classical Reformed orthodoxy** and aligns strongly with **WTS** emphases on **Sola Scriptura**, **covenant theology**, and a robust **doctrine of God**. Strengths abound: the insistence on **inseparable operations** as the nonnegotiable trinitarian grammar of the cross, the refusal to pit **divine love** against **divine justice**, and the pastoral deployment of **impassibility** in service of assurance. Tensions arise where the pressure of polemics risks under-describing the intensional richness of biblical anthropopathisms; here **Bavinck’s** richly textured account of God’s analogical condescension may supplement Williams by showing how immutable love genuinely engages historical sin without internal change. Likewise, in defending **definite atonement**, the line between intent and extent can be pastorally nuanced by emphasizing the **free offer** of the gospel as the ordained means by which the definite accomplishment reaches its objects. None of these are contradictions, only invitations to thicken what Williams already affirms. In principle, his synthesis of **classical theism**, **federal soteriology**, and **canonical exegesis** offers a compelling alternative to modern revisions, honoring revelation’s primacy, covenantal structure, sovereign grace, and the unity of God’s action in history.
