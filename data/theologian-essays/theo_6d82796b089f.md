### TITLE
The religion of technology as secularized eschatology: engineers as a modern priesthood promising salvation by technique.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Cold War technocracy**, **postwar corporate capitalism**, and the rise of **science-technology studies** frame **David F. Noble** as a historian of technology reading modern invention through the lens of **Christian millenarianism** and **American civil religion**.
- A genealogical and materialist **method** blending **Weberian ideal-types**, **Marxist labor history**, and polemical **iconoclasm**; epistemic stance: unmasking the **soteriological myths** sacralizing **technique**.
- **America by Design** (1977); **Forces of Production** (1984); **A World Without Women** (1992); **The Religion of Technology** (1997); **Progress Without People** (1995); **Digital Diploma Mills** (2001); **Beyond the Promised Land** (2005).
- Central categories: **religion of technology**, **secularized eschatology**, **engineers as priesthood**, **corporate capitalism**, **automation and control**, **clerical culture**, **civil religion of progress**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Max Weber**, **Karl Marx**, **Jacques Ellul**, **Lewis Mumford**, **Hans Blumenberg**, **Charles Taylor**; debated by **medievalists**, **feminist historians of science**, and **STS constructivists**.
- Reformed reception cue: strong resonance with **idolatry of technique**, **Romans 1** demythologizing of **progress**, but friction with **creation mandate**, **common grace**, and a Reformed theology of **vocation and science**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
The enduring thesis of **David F. Noble** is that modern technoscience is animated by a **religion of technology**, a secularized **eschatology** in which engineers and scientists serve as a priesthood promising redemption through invention. Born in 1945 and active as a public intellectual until his death in 2010, Noble emerged in the crucible of **postwar technocracy**, the **Cold War research university**, and the consolidation of **corporate capitalism**. By the late 1970s he was teaching in the science, technology, and society milieu associated with places like **MIT**, and he later spent decades at **York University** in Toronto. The intellectual climate was shaped by **Weberian** analyses of rationalization and **Marxist** histories of labor and production, alongside cultural critics such as **Lewis Mumford** and **Jacques Ellul** who warned of the autonomy and totalizing pressure of **technique**. Noble absorbed this climate but gave it a distinctive historiographical form: a **genealogy of invention** that traces modern projects back into medieval **monastic asceticism**, **millenarian hopes**, and early modern **Christian humanism**.

Methodologically, Noble combined **archival labor history** with a polemical **demythologization** of sacred narratives surrounding innovation. He rejected the neutrality of **science and technology** as ideology, claiming that the design, funding, and deployment of systems answered to **control imperatives** baked into **capitalist management** and the **national security state**. At the same time he argued that these imperatives wear a halo of meaning drawn from **religious imaginaries** that translate sin into ignorance, salvation into mastery, and eschaton into **progress**. If **Weber** announced the disenchantment of the world, Noble contended that it never occurred in the promised way. The world was re-enchanted by a **civil religion of progress** that retained the **Christian soteriological structure** while displacing God with **human divinization** through technology.

#### 2. America by Design and the corporate capture of science
Noble’s first major book, **America by Design: Science, Technology, and the Rise of Corporate Capitalism** (1977), established his reputation as a historian of **institutional capture**. He traced how late nineteenth and early twentieth century **corporations** domesticated the emerging research enterprise, reconfiguring **universities**, **patents**, and **professional societies** for accumulation and control. The creation of the **industrial research laboratory**, the alignment of **engineering curricula** with corporate goals, and the consolidation of **intellectual property regimes** all functioned as instruments of **capital discipline**. The myth that science is a **value-neutral** pursuit serving the common good was, on his telling, a modern **civil catechism** that cloaks the class character of innovation.

This analysis is thick with **Weberian legitimation** themes and **Marxist labor process** insights. For Noble, **Taylorist scientific management** and the professionalization of **engineering** served the same end: transfer of discretion from skilled workers to managerial and design elites. **America by Design** exposes the structural co-dependence of **knowledge production** and **corporate power**, showing how a purportedly universal **Wissenschaft** became a strategic auxiliary to **capital**. From the beginning, then, Noble’s historiography married **material interests** to **moral meanings**, treating the corporate ordering of science as an enacted **theology of mastery**.

#### 3. Automation as a soteriological project
In **Forces of Production: A Social History of Industrial Automation** (1984), Noble offered a canonical case study of **automation** as the technological codification of **managerial control**. He reconstructed the postwar development of **numerically controlled machine tools**, sponsored by **Pentagon** contracts and refined in places like **MIT’s Servomechanisms Laboratory**, arguing that technical choices were guided less by efficiency than by the drive to deskill labor and impose **managerial sovereignty**. The promise that automation would deliver a world beyond toil, a kind of secular **Sabbath of plenty**, masked the displacement of shop-floor **craft knowledge** by algorithmic discipline. The hallmark of this project was an **ascetical ideal**: a purified production process free of contingency, error, and human dependence, echoing monastic aspiration.

Here Noble surfaces his theological leitmotif: **automation** is not merely a set of machines but a **soteriology of control**. The metaphorical charge is deliberate. As **Ellul’s la technique** had argued, technical systems seek self-expansion under the sign of necessity. Noble’s contribution was to show that necessity is narrated as **deliverance**. The social harms of unemployment and alienation are rendered acceptable by a liturgy of **progress** in which sacrifice today secures the **eschatological** tomorrow. Thus **automation** is intelligible only when read as a political economy infused with **religious hope**.

#### 4. Clerical culture and the prehistory of scientific masculinity
With **A World Without Women: The Christian Clerical Culture of Western Science** (1992), Noble pressed his genealogy backward into the **medieval** and **early modern** periods. The thesis was blunt: early Western science took shape within a **clerical culture** marked by **celibacy**, **misogyny**, and a perceived spiritual superiority of mind over flesh. The resulting **masculinist asceticism** valued intellectual abstraction, purity, and withdrawal from ordinary domestic life, traits that later reappeared in the personas and institutions of modern **scientific elites**. Noble connected late medieval **scholasticism**, **monastic learning**, and early **natural philosophy** to a clerisy that policed boundaries between the sacred and the profane in ways that excluded women and feminized domains of knowledge.

The book is controversial. Critics charged **selective reading** and **overextension** from theological texts to social practice, while sympathetic readers saw an incisive exposure of how **clerical forms of authority** survived secularization. For Noble, the point was not to demonize Christianity per se but to register how a particular **clerical ideal** bequeathed a habitus of disembodied rationality to the modern **scientific vocation**. That habitus, he argued, predisposed technoscience to **gnostic** inflections that depreciated the body and promised redemption as escape through **knowledge**.

#### 5. The religion of technology: secularized eschatology unmasked
Noble’s signature synthesis appears in **The Religion of Technology: The Divinity of Man and the Spirit of Invention** (1997). He narrates a long arc from **Joachim of Fiore’s** millennial imaginings, through **Francis Bacon’s** **New Atlantis** and **Renaissance magi**, to the **space program**, **artificial intelligence**, **cryonics**, and **nanotechnology**. The through-line is a **secularized eschatology**: the **imago Dei** is recast as the capacity to remake the world, *imitatio Dei* becomes technological creation, the **fall** is ignorance and limit, and **salvation** is unlimited mastery culminating in godlike powers. The slogan of modernity is thus a theological exodus: from **transcendent grace** to **immanent technique**, from ecclesial sacrament to **laboratory experiment**, from the **beatific vision** to the **cosmic frontier**.

Noble insists that these are not merely metaphors affixed after the fact. They live inside the imaginations of practitioners. He reads the rhetoric of **astronautics** as an ascent to heaven, **AI** as the extrusion of mind from mortal flesh, **biotech** as a reversal of **mortality**, and **cryonics** as a parody of **resurrection** without judgment. The animating force is a devotional **apotheosis of man**, the halo of which legitimates massive resource allocation and social risk. This is the **civil religion of progress**, a sacred canopy transposing **Christian soteriology** into the key of **Promethean humanism**.

#### 6. Progress, higher education, and civil religion
The social and institutional corollaries of his thesis are explored in **Progress Without People** (1995), **Digital Diploma Mills** (2001), and **Beyond the Promised Land** (2005). In **Progress Without People**, Noble critiques the rhetoric of **inevitability** that accompanies job-destroying innovations, calling for democratic **counterpower** and a politics of **labor dignity** against the idols of productivity. With **Digital Diploma Mills**, he targets the **automation of higher education**, arguing that distance learning platforms and administrative **managerialism** commodify pedagogy, severing **teacher-student presence** and reducing knowledge to content delivery. The book is an anatomy of **academic capitalism**, where the **university** is refashioned as a site of **surveillance, metrics, and intellectual property** rather than as a community of learning.

In **Beyond the Promised Land**, Noble offers a wider critique of **American civil religion**, exposing how the biblical **exodus** motif was nationalized into a myth of **manifest destiny**, now technologized as boundless **innovation**. The prophetic edge is unmistakable. He identifies liturgies, catechisms, and saints of **progress** that coordinate policy, funding, and aspiration. Together these works display the coherence of his project: a seamless move from **genealogical theology** to **institutional critique**, always under the sign of the **religion of technology**.

#### 7. Interlocutors, convergences, and disputes
Noble’s conceptual neighborhood includes **Jacques Ellul’s** analysis of **la technique** and **Martin Heidegger’s** account of **Gestell** as the enframing of beings for use. Like **Ellul**, he treats technique as a quasi-autonomous order advancing under the logic of efficiency, though he foregrounds **class conflict** and **institutional power** more overtly. Like **Weber**, he tracks **rationalization**, but he denies that modernity is simply **disenchanted**; rather, it is **re-enchanted** by a disguised **soteriology**. His work also sits alongside **Lewis Mumford’s megamachine**, while in a different key than **STS constructivists** such as **Bruno Latour**, who resist macro-sociological moralization in favor of **actor-network** descriptions. In intellectual history, Noble anticipates themes later elaborated by **Charles Taylor’s** account of the **secular age**, though Taylor emphasizes **immanent frames** rather than an explicit **technical soteriology**.

Criticism has been vigorous. Scholars of **medieval Christianity** accused Noble of reading sophisticated **eschatology** through a reductive lens of **masculinist clericalism**. Historians of **science and gender** questioned whether the trope of a **world without women** can carry the burden of explaining complex institutional trajectories. Others argued that Noble underweights **internalist** technical constraints and overreads **teleology** into design decisions. Defenders reply that his aim is not exhaustive causation but **apocalyptic unmasking**, showing the spiritual stakes disguised as pragmatism.

#### 8. Conceptual architecture and theological stakes
At the conceptual level, Noble’s architecture rests on three linked claims: first, that modern technical projects are **morally narrativized** by an implicit **eschatology**; second, that the institutions of invention are structured by **control imperatives** answering to **capital and state**; third, that the convergence of narrative and imperative constitutes a **religion of technology**. The first claim is genealogical, the second sociological, the third theological. Critics sometimes press the gap between these registers. Does the presence of **millenarian language** in **Bacon** or **NASA** prove that practice is spiritually driven, or does it merely supply a **rhetorical garnish** to decisions driven by **economics and geopolitics**?

Here Noble’s analogies matter. He does not argue that technologists consciously replicate **Christian doctrine**, but that the **symbolic structure** of Christian **soteriology** survives secularization as a **cultural grammar**. Where **Hans Blumenberg** proposed that modernity is a legitimate **self-assertion** rather than a parasitic **secularization**, Noble counters that its animating **telos** remains soteriological, and therefore vulnerable to **idolatry**. By foregrounding the **apotheosis of man**, he presses theologians to see modern technics not as tools alone, but as **liturgical forms** that train desire toward a **technological eschaton**.

#### 9. Style, rhetoric, and moral posture
Noble wrote with a jeremiad’s cadence, marrying **archival detail** to **prophetic denunciation**. The tone is deliberate: he means to interrupt the catechism of **progress** by disenchanting its sacramentals. His portraits of **engineering priesthoods**, **university hierophants**, and **corporate liturgists** are stylized in order to render the worship visible. The moral posture is **solidarity with labor**, **suspicion of elites**, and **skepticism toward inevitability** claims. He refuses the consolation of **technological determinism** and insists on **political contestation** of design. In this sense, **Noble** is a theologian of the **saeculum** by indirection, reading a culture’s gods from its machines.

The risk of this strategy is **over-totalization**. Not every technical program is liturgical; not every laboratory is an altar. Yet the payoff of Noble’s drama is clarity. He reveals **technological soteriology** where pieties of the **neutral tool** still dominate public discourse. He also reopens old Christian questions about **means and ends**, **dominion and service**, and the nature of **hope** versus **optimism** within a world that sacralizes **innovation**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed** and **WTS** vantage, Noble’s unmasking of the **religion of technology** deserves careful assent. His exposure of **secularized eschatology**, with its **apotheosis of man**, mirrors the biblical diagnosis of **idolatry** in **Romans 1** and resonates with a **Van Tilian antithesis** between covenantal **worship of the Creator** and autonomous **worship of creation**. His critique of the **civil religion of progress** accords with a **two-ages** eschatology that refuses to collapse the **kingdom of God** into historical **innovation**. Yet Noble’s analysis risks a **reduction of theology to function**, flattening Christian doctrine into a social **epiphenomenon** and caricaturing the **imago Dei** as sheer will to mastery. Classical **Reformed orthodoxy** would nuance his account by retrieving a **creation mandate** under **divine providence**, where **science and craft** are gifts of **common grace** ordered by **vocation** for neighbor-love, not engines of self-deification. It would sharpen his warnings with a richer doctrine of **sin’s noetic effects**, explaining why **technique** drifts toward **domination**, while also permitting qualified affirmation of **technological arts** under **Christ’s lordship** and within **covenantal limits**. In short, Noble helps Reformed theology name the **idolatrous liturgies** of technicism; Reformed theology can, in turn, supply the **teleology of creation, fall, and redemption** that distinguishes faithful **stewardship** from a counterfeit **technological eschaton**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Postwar **technocracy**, **corporate capitalism**, and **Cold War** research; historian of technology reading modernity through **Christian millenarian** residues.
- **Core problem**: The rise of a **religion of technology** that retools **soteriology** as **progress** and recasts engineers as a **priesthood** mediating salvation by **technique**.
- **Major works**: **America by Design** (1977) on **corporate capture**; **Forces of Production** (1984) on **automation and control**; **A World Without Women** (1992) on **clerical culture**; **The Religion of Technology** (1997) on **secularized eschatology**; **Progress Without People** (1995); **Digital Diploma Mills** (2001); **Beyond the Promised Land** (2005).
- **Distinctive positions**: Demythologizing **neutrality**; genealogy from **monastic asceticism** to **AI** and **spaceflight**; critique of **academic capitalism** and **civil religion of progress**.
- **Conceptual tools**: **Genealogy of invention**, **managerial control**, **apotheosis of man**, **automation as soteriology**, **engineers as priesthood**.
- **Interlocutors**: Converges with **Ellul’s la technique** and **Weberian** rationalization; contrasts with **Blumenberg’s legitimacy of modernity** and **STS constructivism**; debated by **medievalists** and **gender historians**.
- **Reformed evaluation**: Strong alignment on **idolatry of technique** and **anti-progressivist eschatology**; critique of his **reductive secularization thesis** and lack of **creation mandate**, **common grace**, and **vocation**; Reformed frame offers **stewardship under Christ** against **technological eschaton**.
- **Legacy**: A persistent lens for reading **technoscience** as **liturgical** and **eschatological**, shaping debates in **theology of technology**, **STS**, and critiques of **academic and corporate power**.
