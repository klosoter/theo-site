### TITLE
The church as a truthful, peaceable polity whose practices witness to Christ against liberal modernity’s imperial claims

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth-century American mainline crisis and post-Vietnam disillusionment; **postliberal Yale**, **Notre Dame** and **Duke** settings; revival of **virtue ethics** after **MacIntyre** and the critique of **Christendom**.
- Method as **ecclesiocentric, narrative, virtue-based practical reason**; **tradition-constituted rationality** with **Wittgensteinian** attention to language and **Aquinas** on habitus; suspicion of **liberal autonomy** and proceduralism.
- Key works: **Character and the Christian Life** 1975; **A Community of Character** 1981; **The Peaceable Kingdom** 1983; **Suffering Presence** 1986; **Resident Aliens** 1989; **After Christendom?** 1991; **Sanctify Them in the Truth** 1998; **With the Grain of the Universe** 2001; **The Hauerwas Reader** 2001; **Matthew** 2006.
- Central terms: **church as polis**, **peaceableness and nonviolence**, **witness and martyrdom**, **sanctification and holiness**, **truthful speech**, **Constantinianism**, **resident aliens**.
- Interlocutors: **John Howard Yoder**, **Karl Barth**, **Alasdair MacIntyre**, **George Lindbeck**, **Thomas Aquinas**, **Augustine**; critics **Jeffrey Stout**, **Oliver O Donovans political theology**, **Nigel Biggar on just war**, and the **Niebuhr** tradition.
- Reformed cue: Shared stress on **pilgrim identity**, **church discipline**, and the **antithesis with liberalism**; tensions over **two kingdoms**, **just war and magistracy**, **sola Scriptura versus ecclesial hermeneutics**, and **natural law**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Stanley Hauerwas, born in 1940 to a Texas bricklayer, crafted a theology from the shop floor to the seminar room, marrying **Methodist holiness** to a stern critique of **American liberalism**. Trained at **Yale** in the 1960s under figures like **James Gustafson** and amid the ferment that produced **George Lindbeck’s** postliberal turn, he began teaching at **Notre Dame** in 1970 and moved to **Duke Divinity School** in 1983. The ideological backdrop was Vietnam, civil rights, the fracturing of **mainline Protestant** authority, and the reemergence of **virtue ethics** in the academy. Hauerwas absorbed **Wittgenstein** on language as social practice and **Alasdair MacIntyre** on tradition-constituted rationality, while turning to **Karl Barth** and **John Howard Yoder** for a non-Constantinian ecclesiology. From these sources he forged an **ecclesiocentric method**: Christian truth is known as the church embodies the story of **Jesus Christ** through disciplined **practices** that form **virtues**, especially **peaceableness** and **truthful speech**. The question is not first what Christians should do in public policy, but what kind of people the **church as a polis** must become to be a truthful witness.

Hauerwas’s program takes shape as an ethics oriented by *telos* rather than rules alone, indebted to **Aquinas’s account of habitus**, yet transposed by **Wesleyan sanctification** into an ecclesial pedagogy of holiness. The practices of **baptism**, **Eucharist**, **confession**, and the hard disciplines of reconciliation school a people whose speech and lives fit the **Gospel’s narrative**. He regularly opposes **liberal proceduralism**, **autonomous selves**, and the imperial notion of **Constantinian Christendom**, arguing that modern nation-states train citizens to die and kill for false liturgies. The church, conversely, trains people to live and sometimes to die in the **peaceable kingdom** inaugurated by the crucified **Lord**.

#### 2. Early works and the turn to character
Hauerwas first made his mark with **Character and the Christian Life** 1975, where he framed **Christian ethics** as the formation of character, not the adjudication of discrete moral dilemmas. The book draws from **Aristotle** through **Aquinas** yet insists that **Jesus** determines the virtues Christians must embody. There he develops the moral grammar of **truthful speech** and the practice-based nature of **practical reason**, insisting that moral concepts are intelligible only within communities shaped by specific **narratives**. This early move away from **decisionism** and toward **formation** seeded his later ecclesiology: the church does not simply possess an ethic; the **church is its social ethic**.

The consolidation came with **A Community of Character** 1981 and **The Peaceable Kingdom** 1983. The former announces the famous claim that the **church as polis** is itself a political presence. The latter, subtitled A Primer in Christian Ethics, sets nonviolence at the center of the **Christian moral life**, not as a technique but as a truthful response to the **Christological revelation** that the world is reconciled by the **cross**. The strategy is deeply **Barthian** and **Yoderian**. From **Barth** he draws the axiom that **Christology** governs all loci, disallowing any appeal to independent **natural law** as a moral foundation detached from the **Gospel**. From **Yoder** he learns that the **politics of Jesus** is not an impossible ideal but the concrete way the **kingdom** appears in history through nonviolent witness.

#### 3. Church, politics, and the end of Christendom
Against the background of declining **Christendom**, Hauerwas and **Will Willimon** wrote **Resident Aliens** 1989 to announce that the church exists as a colony of heaven within late modern empire. They recommend retrieval of **catechesis**, **discipline**, and **liturgy** rather than pursuit of influence through **democratic liberalism**. The following **After Christendom?** 1991 explicates how freedom, justice, and a Christian nation as public ideals deform the church’s *habitus*. The heart of the proposal is an unapologetic **ecclesiocentrism**. The **Eucharist** is not only sacrament but politics; it rehearses a truthful economy of gift and reconciliation that unmasks the coercive liturgies of the **nation-state**.

This reframing entails a critique of **American civil religion** and a refusal to reduce Christianity to **rights discourse**. Hauerwas challenges the sacred canopy of **liberal neutrality**, arguing with **MacIntyre** that all rationality is **tradition-constituted** and so Christians ought to be straightforwardly Christian rather than adopting a thin moral Esperanto. The church’s calling is not to manage the state but to witness by producing a people whose **sanctification** is visible in works of mercy, patient endurance, and nonviolent practices. By being a peculiar polity, the church becomes good news for the world.

#### 4. Scripture, doctrine, and the ecclesial imagination
While not a biblical scholar, Hauerwas cares deeply about **Scripture** as a communal canon whose interpretation is embedded in **creed** and **liturgy**. In **Unleashing the Scripture** 1993 he argued that the Bible must be freed from captivity to **American individualism** and the idol of **historical-critical** mastery, advocating local **ecclesial interpretation** governed by the rule of faith. The **Methodist** accent surfaces in **Sanctify Them in the Truth** 1998, where he displays holinesstraining as a communal project under the prayer of **John 17**. His **Brazos commentary on Matthew** 2006 dwells on the **Sermon on the Mount**, especially **truthful speech**, oath-taking, and enemy love, using the lectionary to guide the church’s habit formation.

The doctrinal posture is intentionally modest yet resolutely **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian**. He resists turning doctrine into a deductive system and places dogmatic clarity in service of **ecclesial performance**. Following **Lindbeck’s** cultural-linguistic model, doctrine regulates Christian speech and practice, safeguarding the narratival coherence of the **Gospel**. Hence **atonement**, **Trinity**, and **creation** function canonically as the shape of the story into which Christians are baptized and trained, rather than as free-standing metaphysical theses. This does not deny metaphysics but disciplines it under **Christological rule**.

#### 5. Natural theology and the grain of the universe
The **Gifford Lectures**, published as **With the Grain of the Universe** 2001, brought Hauerwas into classic debates on **natural theology**. He reads **William James** as a moral psychologist of religion and **Reinhold Niebuhr** as a paradigmatic Christian realist who accommodated **liberal modernity**, then commends **Barth** for reconfiguring natural knowledge of God under **revelation**. Hauerwas contends natural theology is unavoidable but must be done rightly, that is, Christologically. The universe’s grain is peaceable because creation is ordered by the **Lamb who was slain**. To live nonviolently is not to defy reality but to be trained to perceive and inhabit it truthfully.

This proposal sharpened his resistance to **foundationalism** and to appeals to **universal reason** as a neutral court. Critics charged inconsistency, alleging that he both refuses natural law and posits a creational grain. Hauerwas answers that the claim is not a freestanding ontology but **evangelical proclamation** that shapes practical reason. The metaphysical bite of the thesis is derivative from **Christology** and eschatology, yielding an **apocalyptic politics of patience** rather than a blueprint for public consensus. Peace is not strategy but participation in God’s **eschatological** reconciliation already beginning in the **church**.

#### 6. Suffering, medicine, and truthful presence
A notable extension of his ecclesial ethic addresses medicine and disability. In **Suffering Presence** 1986 and later in **Living Gently in a Violent World** 2008 with **Jean Vanier**, Hauerwas argues that the church’s primary response to suffering is not control but **presence**. The grammar of **care** is learned in communities that tell the truth about pain, death, and dependency, resisting the technocratic aims of late modern bioethics. The virtue of **truthful speech** reappears as a hallmark of holiness. In praying and confessing, as modeled in **Prayers Plainly Spoken** 1999, Christians learn to speak truthfully to God and one another, rejecting the managerial idioms of therapeutic culture.

Here his **Wesleyan holiness** leans into practices of sanctification that cultivate patience, forgiveness, and reconciliation. The church embodies **corporal works of mercy** not as adjuncts to mission but as its very substance. The **sacraments** inscribe this truthful presence into the body, rehearsing peace in a world of violence. Hauerwas’s essays collected in **The Hauerwas Reader** 2001 and **Performing the Faith** 2004 frequently insist that sanctification is a communal pedagogy rather than a solitary achievement. Holiness is ecclesial craft, like bricklaying, done over time, with care and truthfulness.

#### 7. Interlocutors, debates, and charges of sectarianism
Hauerwas’s rejection of **just war** and of the moral sufficiency of **democratic liberalism** has drawn sustained critique. **Jeffrey Stout** worries that his refusal of liberal public reason imperils democratic solidarity, while **Nigel Biggar** and **Oliver O Donovan** argue that pacifism misconstrues the **magistrate’s** vocation and Christian charity to the vulnerable. Hauerwas replies that the church’s first politics is the **Eucharist** and that the best service to the world is to refuse participation in coercive violence, thereby exposing the false necessity of war. When accused of **sectarianism**, he answers with the claim that a people formed by the **Beatitudes** is rendered intelligible and leaven-like precisely by its difference.

He also contests the **Niebuhr** tradition of Christian realism for baptizing national projects under the rhetoric of **responsibility**, and he disputes **human rights** talk insofar as it operates as liberal grammar that substitutes for ecclesial description of the good. Hauerwas does, however, engage dialogue partners seriously, often producing readings that reappropriate **Augustine**, **Aquinas**, and **Barth** for his constructive vision. His exchanges display a consistent methodological wager: rationality is internal to practices, so the most rational Christian politics is to be the **church as polis**.

#### 8. Doctrinal contour: Christ, church, sanctification, and peace
Substantively, Hauerwas’s doctrinal contours are ordered by **Christology** and **ecclesiology**. The **incarnation** narrates a politics of vulnerability; the **cross** reveals God’s rule as noncoercive reconciliation; the **resurrection** inaugurates a people whose common life is a foretaste of the **kingdom**. **Sanctification** rather than **justification** serves as the leading soteriological edge, not to deny the latter but to insist that salvation is participation in a people taught to speak truth and refuse violence. This is a distinctly **Wesleyan** accent, though chastened by **Barth’s** heavy Christological center and **Yoder’s** insistence on concrete obedience.

The sacraments function as political acts that render the church a visible **alternative polis**. **Baptism** inducts into a new citizenship. The **Eucharist** is the peaceable feast that trains memory away from the nation’s sacrificial liturgies. The church’s mission is thus described as **witness and martyrdom**, a truthful publicity rather than a managerial strategy. Eschatology animates patience, the refusal to secure outcomes by force, trusting that God’s **apocalyptic victory** in Christ orders time toward reconciliation. The result is a coherent, if intentionally un-systematized, vision in which **holiness**, **nonviolence**, and **truthful speech** are interwoven marks of catholic faithfulness.

#### 9. Late work, self-description, and enduring contribution
In **With the Grain of the Universe** 2001 and subsequent essays, Hauerwas matured a theological self-description as a Christian who learned to write theology by being apprenticed to the church rather than to the academy’s desire for influence. His memoir **Hannah’s Child** 2010 displays how biography and belief interpenetrate, making clear that his talk of **practices** and **habits** is not metaphor but craft, reminiscent of his bricklaying youth. Later essays in **The Work of Theology** 2015 reiterate that theology is an activity of a people under the discipline of **Scripture**, **creed**, and **worship**. His writings on war, such as **War and the American Difference** 2011, extend his critique of the state’s liturgical demand for sacrifice, contrasting it with the **church’s** refusal to kill.

Hauerwas’s distinctive gift is to have made **ecclesiology** do the work of **ethics** without dissolving theology into activism. By retrieving **virtue ethics**, aligning it with **Wesleyan sanctification**, and placing it under **Christological** and **Barthian** governance, he offered a counter-liberal account of Christian rationality. The language of **resident aliens**, **church as polis**, and **peaceable kingdom** has reshaped the imaginations of pastors and scholars across denominational lines. Even critics admit that he forced the question of whether Christians can be a people of peace in a world at war.

Reformed and Westminster evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster vantage, Hauerwas’s stress on **pilgrim identity**, **church discipline**, and the **antithesis** with liberal modernity is salutary. His **Barthian** refusal of neutral **natural theology**, his insistence on the primacy of **Christology**, and his critique of **civil religion** all resonate with Calvin’s prophetic posture against idolatrous powers. Yet significant tensions remain. First, his **ecclesiocentrism** can obscure the primacy of **Scripture’s** self-authenticating authority, since he sometimes speaks as if the church’s practices constitute rather than receive the canon’s norm. A **Van Tilian** approach would share his denial of neutrality but intensify the principled priority of the **Word** over communal performance. Second, his absolutizing of **pacifism** conflicts with **two-kingdoms** and **magistracy** doctrines that affirm the legitimate, limited use of the sword under **Romans 13** and a chastened **just war** tradition. Third, the soteriological accent on **sanctification** risks shading the **law-gospel** distinction and the centrality of **justification by faith alone** as the engine of holiness. Finally, his suspicion of **natural law** and common **grace** forecloses Reformed accounts of creational order that can underwrite principled cooperation in the civic realm while guarding the church’s unique commission. A Reformed alternative would appropriate his call to truthful, sacramentally formed witness, yet re-anchor it in a clearer order of authority in which **Scripture** norms the church, **covenant theology** sets the redemptive frame, **two-kingdoms** clarifies the church’s pilgrim politics, and **just war** remains a tragic but possible work of neighbor love.

### RECAP NOTES
- Texas-born 1940, trained at **Yale**, taught at **Notre Dame** and **Duke**; postliberal and post-Vietnam milieu with revived **virtue ethics** and critique of **Christendom**.
- Core problem: how the **church as polis** can embody **truthful speech** and **peaceableness** against **liberal modernity** and **Constantinianism**.
- Major works: **Character and the Christian Life** 1975; **A Community of Character** 1981; **The Peaceable Kingdom** 1983; **Suffering Presence** 1986; **Resident Aliens** 1989; **After Christendom?** 1991; **Sanctify Them in the Truth** 1998; **With the Grain of the Universe** 2001; **Matthew** 2006.
- Distinctives: **ecclesiocentric ethics**, **narrative rationality**, **virtue and sanctification**, **nonviolence**, **Eucharistic politics**, critique of **rights talk** and **liberal neutrality**.
- Interlocutors and debates: influenced by **Yoder**, **Barth**, **MacIntyre**, **Lindbeck**, **Aquinas**; contested by **Stout** on democracy, **O Donovan** and **Biggar** on **just war**, and the **Niebuhr** realist legacy.
- Reformed reception: appreciates **pilgrim identity**, **discipline**, and anti-neutrality; questions his **pacifism**, ecclesial priority over **Scripture**, muted **justification**, and dismissal of **natural law** and **two kingdoms**.
- Legacy: catalyzed a generation to see the **church** as a visible, peaceable polity; enduring influence on **ecclesiology**, **ethics**, and practices of **catechesis**, **liturgy**, and **public witness**.
