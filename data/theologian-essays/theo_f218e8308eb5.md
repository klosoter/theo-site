### TITLE
Reading Christ in the Old Testament through covenantal, canonical, and typological lenses for pastoral proclamation

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Emerging from late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century evangelical Old Testament studies, **Richard P. Belcher Jr.** works within confessional Presbyterianism and engages **postcritical hermeneutics** and **canonical approaches** in a pastoral academy setting.
- Methodologically he combines **historical-grammatical exegesis**, **canonical shaping**, and **redemptive-historical typology** under **progressive revelation** and a robust **doctrine of Scripture**.
- Key works: **The Messiah and the Psalms: Preaching Christ from all the Psalms** (2006); **Genesis: The Beginning of God’s Plan of Salvation** (2006); **Job: The Mystery of Suffering and God’s Sovereignty** (2017); **Finding Favour in the Sight of God: A Theology of Wisdom Literature** (2018); **The Fulfillment of the Promises of God: An Explanation of Covenant Theology** (2020).
- Central categories: **covenant of works**, **covenant of grace**, **covenant of redemption**; **messianic psalmody**; **typology versus allegory**; **fear of the Lord**; **Davidic covenant**; **authorial intent** and the **divine author** dimension.
- Interlocutors and influences include **Geerhardus Vos**, **Meredith Kline**, **John Calvin**, **Brevard Childs**, **Sidney Greidanus**, and critical foils such as **Walter Brueggemann**, **John Goldingay**, and **dispensational** and **new covenant** frameworks.
- Reformed reception: welcomed within **Westminster Standards** circles for clear **covenant theology** and **Christocentric hermeneutics**, with debates over **Mosaic republication** and the scope of **messianic readings**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
Situated in the late modern evangelical academy yet firmly planted in confessional Presbyterian soil, **Richard P. Belcher Jr.** serves at **Reformed Theological Seminary** and writes as a scholar-pastor who unites **philological rigor** with pulpit-sensitive **biblical theology**. The historical setting of his work is defined by the aftermath of twentieth-century **historical criticism**, the rise of **canonical interpretation** inspired by **Brevard Childs**, and the consolidation of a **Reformed retrieval** that foregrounds **Geerhardus Vos** on **progressive revelation** and **Meredith Kline** on the **covenantal architecture** of Scripture. Belcher’s method is consciously **historical-grammatical**, but he refuses the hermeneutical reductionism that isolates original context from the canon’s **final form** and the church’s **rule of faith**. He advances a Christocentric reading through **typology**, disciplined by **authorial intent** as extended through the **divine author** and the Bible’s own **intertextual** patterns.

Belcher’s epistemic stance presupposes the **clarity, sufficiency, and authority of Scripture**, articulated confessionally in the **Westminster Confession of Faith**, while recognizing the complexity of **genre**, **poetry**, and **wisdom**. A hallmark of his method is the repeated contrast between **typology and allegory**: typology is grounded in God’s actual acts in history, tethered to **covenant promises** and to the **Davidic royal line**, while allegory slides into subjective ingenuity. Within this framework, he insists that **messianic psalmody** arises from historical **Davidic kingship** and the **Abrahamic-Davidic promises**, not from later church projection. This blend of careful exegesis, **canonical shape**, and **redemptive-historical unity** yields a hermeneutic at once conservative and generative.

#### 2. Early work: Psalms and the Christological horizon
Belcher’s formative monograph, **The Messiah and the Psalms: Preaching Christ from all the Psalms** (2006), sets out a taxonomy for reading the Psalter’s **messianic witness**. Against both minimalist critics like **John Goldingay** who restrict messianism to explicit predictions and maximalist allegorists who dissolve history into symbol, Belcher proposes layered messianic categories: direct **royal-messianic psalms** grounded in the **Davidic covenant** such as **Psalm 2** and **Psalm 110**; **typological psalms** where the experience of David as an anointed king figures Christ’s suffering and exaltation, as in **Psalm 22** and **Psalm 69**; and **wisdom or torah psalms** that frame the righteous king’s way under **God’s law**, such as **Psalm 1** and **Psalm 19**. The controlling doctrinal anchors are **covenant theology**, **progressive revelation**, and the **two levels of authorship**: David as human author and the **Spirit** as divine author.

The method is neither a simple appeal to **sensus plenior** disconnected from the text, nor a refusal of **New Testament** rereadings. Belcher reads the **Apostolic hermeneutic** in texts like **Acts 2** and **Hebrews 1** as authoritative demonstrations of how the Psalms’ royal and priestly motifs culminate in **Christ’s kingship and priesthood**. He shows how **Psalm 110’s Melchizedek motif** intersects with **Genesis 14**, how **Psalm 72** universalizes **Abrahamic blessing**, and how **Psalm 22’s righteous sufferer** anticipates the cruciform path of the Davidic Son. Thus **messianic psalmody** becomes a matrix for preaching the **gospel** without severing first-order historical meanings. The pastoral payoff is explicit: a discipline for **Christocentric preaching** rooted in **textual and covenantal** realities.

#### 3. Wisdom literature: fear of the Lord and canonical coherence
Turning to Israel’s sapiential texts, Belcher’s **Finding Favour in the Sight of God: A Theology of Wisdom Literature** (2018) argues that the unifying principle of **Proverbs**, **Job**, and **Ecclesiastes** is the **fear of the Lord** understood as covenantal reverence, moral submission, and epistemic humility. Against readings that play **Job** and **Ecclesiastes** off against **Proverbs**, Belcher frames a canonical dialogue: Proverbs articulates an ordered **creation theology** and **retribution principle** in seed form; Job probes the limits of retribution in a fallen world and presses toward **eschatological vindication**; Ecclesiastes confronts **hebel** with an under-the-sun realism that only the **gift of God** can overcome. The doctrine of **providence** and the Creator-creature distinction govern the whole.

Belcher’s constructive move is to read the wisdom corpus within **covenant theology**. The wise life is not generic morality but **covenantal obedience** that anticipates **Christ as wisdom** and the Spirit’s re-creation of moral agency. By resisting both **skeptical wisdom** reductionism and pietistic harmonizations, he integrates **fear of the Lord**, **ethical formation**, and **eschatological hope**. Interlocutors include **Walter Brueggemann** on Israel’s testimony, **Tremper Longman** on sapiential theology, and **Craig Bartholomew** on wisdom’s theological thickness. Belcher maintains a confessional line: wisdom flows from **Torah**, is normed by **covenant**, and matures in **Christ crucified and risen** as the telos of **creation and redemption**.

#### 4. Commentary work and narrative-theological synthesis
Belcher’s commentaries, such as **Genesis: The Beginning of God’s Plan of Salvation** (2006) and **Job: The Mystery of Suffering and God’s Sovereignty** (2017), incarnate his method in sustained exegesis. In **Genesis** he highlights **creation**, **imago Dei**, and the **Abrahamic covenant** as the hinge of **seed** and **land** promises, coordinating **Genesis 12, 15, 17** with the **new covenant** trajectory. He emphasizes the **protoevangelium** of **Genesis 3:15**, the typological arc from **Noah** to **Abraham** to **Joseph**, and the way **blessing and kingship** converge in **Judah** and the expectation of **Shiloh**. The method is covenantal and canonical, with **Meredith Kline’s** insights on treaty forms illuminating structure, yet disciplined by **historical-grammatical exegesis** and **literary sensitivity**.

In **Job** Belcher explores the bounds of the **retribution principle**, the role of **divine speech** in re-situating human wisdom, and the cruciform pattern of righteous suffering that adumbrates **Christ the Just Sufferer**. He resists moralistic readings that make Job a mere exemplar and also resists skeptical conclusions that relativize divine justice. The **theophany** speeches re-anchor Job’s epistemology in **creation theology**. Across narrative and poetry alike, Belcher’s synthesis holds: **covenant promises** direct history, **typology** bridges epochs, and **Christological fulfillment** is neither an imposed gloss nor a late ecclesial invention but the canonical horizon set by the **Spirit**.

#### 5. Covenant theology: structure, unity, and disputation
The capstone, **The Fulfillment of the Promises of God: An Explanation of Covenant Theology** (2020), is a crisp yet comprehensive tour of **Reformed federal theology**. Belcher articulates the classic triad: the **covenant of redemption** as the *pactum salutis* among the **Trinity** grounding salvation; the **covenant of works** with **Adam** promising life upon perfect and personal obedience; and the **covenant of grace** administered through **Noahic**, **Abrahamic**, **Mosaic**, **Davidic**, and **new covenant** economies. He presses the unity of the **people of God** against **dispensational discontinuities** and critiques **new covenant theology** for flattening the moral law and neglecting the confessional categories of **administration** and **substance**.

A signature issue is the **Mosaic covenant**. Belcher affirms with the **Westminster Confession of Faith** that the Mosaic is an administration of the **covenant of grace** while acknowledging a typological **works principle** oriented to **land tenure** and **national blessing**, drawing on and nuancing **Meredith Kline**. He distinguishes between the **moral law** as perpetually binding, the **ceremonial law** as fulfilled in **Christ**, and the **civil law** as expired save for **general equity**. The typological overlay explains Israel’s exile as covenant curse without collapsing Israel’s economy into a second soteriological works covenant. He argues for **baptism’s continuity** with **circumcision** as covenant sign and defends **Lord’s Day** practice within the **Sabbath** command, positioning Reformed worship and ethics inside the covenantal map.

#### 6. Scripture’s unity: authorial intent, canon, and ecclesial use
Belcher’s construal of **authorial intent** includes a dual agency: the human authors wrote in concrete settings under the **Spirit**, and the **divine author** superintended a canonical coherence that stretches beyond individual horizons. This yields three regulative claims: first, **historical-grammatical exegesis** is irreducible; second, the **canonical form** bears normative theological meaning; third, the **New Testament’s** use of the **Old** is authoritative and paradigmatic. This triangulation is especially visible in his treatment of **messianic psalmody** and **wisdom’s Christological fulfillment**, where he tracks how **apostolic citation** and **echo** unveil deeper patterns already resident in the text’s covenantal structure.

In contrast to **allegory**, Belcher’s **typology** is anchored in promissory structures like the **Abrahamic** and **Davidic covenants**, in cultic-prophetic patterns such as **priesthood** and **sacrifice**, and in creation motifs of **image**, **rest**, and **dominion**. The logic is that of **divine providence**, not reader-constructed symbolism. Homiletically he aligns with **Sidney Greidanus** and **Dennis Johnson**, but his distinctive contribution is a more granular set of criteria for discerning **messianic textures** in the Psalter and a sustained fusion of **sapiential** and **covenantal** themes for pastoral formation. His work thus provides a template for the church’s **catechesis** and **preaching**, rooted in a thick **doctrine of Scripture**.

#### 7. Interlocutors and debates
Belcher’s scholarship is dialogical. With **Brevard Childs**, he appreciates **canonical shape** yet resists an approach that sidelines historical reference. He pushes back on **Walter Brueggemann’s** dialectic of Israel’s testimony when it renders the theological center too pluralized, by re-centering **covenant promises** and **divine kingship**. He engages **John Goldingay** and allied minimalists on the Psalms, contending that **Davidic expectation** licensed broader messianic readings already in Israel’s royal theology. In wisdom studies he situates himself between **Tremper Longman’s** existential-theological emphases and more strictly sapiential approaches by rooting wisdom in **fear of the Lord** as covenantal reverence with **ethical** and **eschatological** dimensions.

Within evangelical debates on **Christ in the Old Testament**, Belcher positions against both **Christotelic** reduction, which confines the Old Testament to a non-Christological preparatory role, and unchecked **allegorism**. He strengthens **Geerhardus Vos’s** insight that **eschatology precedes soteriology** by showing how **Davidic** and **wisdom** motifs already signal **kingdom** and **new creation** trajectories. He also participates in intra-Reformed conversations on **republication** at Sinai, aligning with a typological works principle limited to Israel’s theocratic administration rather than a parallel soteriological covenant, thereby mediating between **John Murray’s** continuity and **Meredith Kline’s** stronger works codicil.

#### 8. Doctrinal themes in pastoral register
Across genres and testaments, Belcher’s doctrinal center is **covenant communion with the triune God** realized in **Christ**. The **creation mandate**, refracted through **Sabbath** and **kingdom**, is taken up in the **Abrahamic** and **Davidic** oaths, concentrated in the **new covenant**, and consummated in **new creation**. The **law-gospel** relation is configured through the **covenant of works** broken in Adam and fulfilled by **Christ’s active and passive obedience**, with the **covenant of grace** admitting believers and their children by **promise** and **faith**. Belcher’s wisdom theology contributes an anthropology of **finite, dependent knowing** that guards against **autonomous reason**, while his Psalms theology contributes a robust **Christological piety** that legitimates lament, imprecation, and praise within a kingdom horizon.

At the level of hermeneutics, the **divine author** category underwrites his account of **sensus plenior** as canonically and apostolically bounded, not speculative. At the level of ecclesial practice, he defends **means of grace** and **Lord’s Day** rhythms as covenantal enactments. His exegesis of **Genesis**, **Psalms**, and **Job** converges in a dogmatic commitment to **providence**, **holiness**, **divine kingship**, and the **immutability of God’s promise**. The results are deployed for **preaching**, **catechesis**, and **pastoral care**, exhibiting the Reformed conviction that **doctrine and doxology** are inseparable.

#### 9. Contribution and distinctiveness
What distinguishes **Richard P. Belcher Jr.** among contemporary Reformed Old Testament theologians is the integration of three features: a defined **federal theology** that orders the canon’s storyline, a textured account of **messianic psalmody** that opens the Psalter for **Christocentric preaching**, and a theologically thick **wisdom synthesis** that stabilizes sapiential tensions within **covenant fear** and **eschatological hope**. His program models how to read the Old Testament with **canonical depth** and **textual discipline**, balancing traditional **confessional categories** with acute attention to literary form and genre.

Interdisciplinarily, Belcher manages to be conversant with **critical scholarship** while remaining unapologetically aligned with **Reformed orthodoxy**. By foregrounding **authorial intent** and the **divine author**, he offers a principled way to adjudicate between atomistic exegesis and free-floating theologies of the canon. For pastors shaped by **Westminster Standards**, his work supplies clear rails for **Christocentric preaching** that are neither reductionistic nor speculative, honoring both the **earthiness of Israel’s history** and the **transcendent unity** of God’s promise from **Abraham** to **Christ**.

#### 10. Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Belcher’s corpus is a strong ally for **Scripture’s unity**, **covenant theology**, and **Christocentric hermeneutics**. Strengths include his careful demarcation of **typology versus allegory**, his confessional clarity on the **covenant triad** of works, grace, and redemption, and his nuanced handling of the **Mosaic economy** with a measured **republication** that secures both the **gospel’s gratuity** and Israel’s **theocratic particularity**. Two tensions merit ongoing discussion. First, some WTS voices might press for either a more robust **Klinean** works principle at Sinai or a more **Murray-like** rejection of republication to protect the catechetical simplicity of the **covenant of grace**; Belcher’s mediating stance invites refinement on how **law-covenant** functions typologically without soteriological leakage. Second, in **messianic psalmody**, his expansive but disciplined taxonomy will provoke questions about the outer boundary of **Christological readings**, especially in wisdom or lament texts where apostolic citation is sparse; here an alternative emphasis on **Apostolic exegesis as canonically sufficient** could narrow the field, while a **Calvinian** insistence on the Psalter as a mirror of the soul may broaden pastoral application. Nevertheless, by rooting interpretation in **revelation**, ordered by **covenant**, and oriented to **Christ’s kingdom**, Belcher offers an exemplary model within classical **Reformed orthodoxy** for preaching and teaching the **Old Testament**.
