### TITLE
Revelation as God’s self-communication climactically manifest in the risen Christ and rendered credible within history

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Post–Vatican II Roman Catholic renewal**, Australian Jesuit at the **Pontifical Gregorian University** amid **ressourcement** and **aggiornamento** currents.
- **Fundamental theology** oriented by **Dei Verbum**, a **personalist and historical-critical method** balancing **motiva credibilitatis** with lived **experience of grace**.
- Key works: **Christology** (1995; 2nd ed. 2009), **The Tripersonal God** (1999), **Jesus Our Redeemer** (2007), **Jesus: A Portrait** (2008), **Rethinking Fundamental Theology** (2011), **Believing in the Resurrection** (2012), **A Concise Dictionary of Theology** (1991).
- Central terms: **God’s self-communication**, **resurrection as revelatory event**, **fundamental theology**, **Trinitarian soteriology**, **motives of credibility**, **sacramentality of revelation**.
- Interlocutors: **Karl Rahner**, **Bernard Lonergan**, **Hans Urs von Balthasar**, **Joseph Ratzinger**, **Wolfhart Pannenberg**, critical engagement with **Rudolf Bultmann** and **Edward Schillebeeckx**.
- Reformed cue: welcomes **resurrection historicity** and **high Christology**, questions on **sola Scriptura**, **penal substitution**, and **nature-grace** integration.

### ESSAY
Gerald O’Collins SJ stands as a major contemporary Roman Catholic voice who integrated **post–Vatican II fundamental theology** with a robustly historical **Christology** centered on the **resurrection**. Born in 1931 in Australia and trained as a **Jesuit**, he spent decades teaching at the **Pontifical Gregorian University** in Rome from the early 1970s until 2006, forming generations of seminarians and scholars amid the ferment unleashed by **Dei Verbum** and **Lumen Gentium**. His career unfolds within the twin movements of **ressourcement**, retrieving patristic and biblical sources, and **aggiornamento**, renewing method and idiom for contemporary culture. That ecclesial setting, together with the legacy of **Karl Rahner** and **Bernard Lonergan**, prepared a method that is at once confessional and critical, deeply scriptural yet attuned to modern historical sensibilities, and intensely interested in the credibility and communicability of **revelation** as God’s **self-communication**.

At the level of method, O’Collins reclaims and reconfigures **fundamental theology** as the disciplined reflection on the possibility, actuality, and recognition of divine **revelation**, drawing on **Dei Verbum 2’s** linkage of **events and words** and its insistence on God’s personal **self-disclosure**. He retrieves the classical **motiva credibilitatis** without reverting to a rationalist apologetics, arguing that God’s saving acts furnish fitting **signs** that both invite and enable faith. Theologically, he moves with a **personalist** emphasis that stresses encounter and **grace**. Epistemically, he employs a **critical-realist** posture, acknowledging the theory-laden nature of historical inquiry yet insisting on the public accessibility of the gospel’s core claims, especially the **resurrection**. This approach borrows from **Lonergan’s** analysis of cognitional structure and from **Rahner’s** notion of **supernatural existential**, but O’Collins is less transcendental and more narratival, deploying Scripture, liturgy, and historical reasoning as convergent witnesses to the **economy of salvation**.

The focal point of his fundamental and systematic project is the **resurrection** as revelatory event. In works culminating in **Believing in the Resurrection** 2012, O’Collins contends that the **empty tomb** and **appearance traditions** form mutually reinforcing historical data whose best explanation is that God raised Jesus in a transformed, embodied mode. Against **Rudolf Bultmann’s** *Entmythologisierung* that collapses Easter into kerygma, and qualifiedly against **Edward Schillebeeckx**, who at points prioritized Easter faith over event, O’Collins maintains the simultaneity of **event and interpretation**. The **resurrection** is not simply intrapsychic conversion but a divinely effected transformation inaugurating the eschaton. He appeals to **multiple attestation**, **embarrassment**, and the origin of **Sunday worship** as historical indicators, while stressing the intersubjective character of the appearances and the Spirit’s role in enabling their recognition. Theologically, Easter constitutes God’s vindication of **Jesus’ filial identity**, the decisive disclosure of divine **self-communication**, and the foundation of Christian **credibility**.

Out of this Easter center, O’Collins constructs his **Christology**. In the widely used **Christology** 1995, he advances a synthetic account that begins with the **biblical witness** to Jesus’ proclamation of the **kingdom**, **miracles as signs**, and distinct **Abba-consciousness**, moves through the **conciliar dogmas** of **homoousios** and **hypostatic union**, and culminates in a contemporary confession of **Jesus as Lord**. He positions himself against **adoptionism** and simplistic **kenoticism**, upholding the classic **communicatio idiomatum** while giving careful attention to Jesus’ human **knowledge and freedom**. Drawing on **Hebrews** and **John**, he argues for the preexistence of the **Logos** who assumes a full humanity, living a filial obedience that reaches its apex on the cross and is confirmed by resurrection. O’Collins is deliberately non-speculative in metaphysical detail, preferring to anchor metaphysics in the narrative arc from **incarnation** to **paschal mystery**, yet he does not shy away from concise doctrinal claims on **two natures** and the unity of the **divine person**.

The doctrine of the **Trinity** receives a programmatic treatment in **The Tripersonal God** 1999, where O’Collins labors to secure the unity of the **economic** and **immanent Trinity** without collapse. He interprets Scripture’s economy as reliably expressive of the **immanent relations** of Father, Son, and Spirit, refraining from speculative extension beyond what the **economy** warrants. The **filioque** is handled with a conciliatory note that attends to East-West convergence on the Spirit’s procession through the Son. O’Collins insists that Christian faith is inherently **Trinitarian**, and that **soteriology** must be rendered as an invitation into the triune **communio**, with the **Spirit** mediating participation in the risen **Christ** to the glory of the **Father**. He emphasizes the performative knowledge of the Trinity in **liturgy**, **baptismal confession**, and **doxology**, arguing that worship supplies both the grammar and the horizon for interpreting **revelation**.

In **Jesus Our Redeemer** 2007, O’Collins offers a constructive **soteriology** that is both biblical and patristically textured. He advances a **kaleidoscope of atonement metaphors** rather than a single controlling theory, weaving together **sacrifice**, **ransom**, **victory over the powers**, **representative obedience**, **satisfaction**, and **justification** within a Trinitarian frame. He is appreciative yet critical of **penal substitution** when construed in isolation or in ways that threaten intra-Trinitarian unity, preferring language of vicarious **representation** and restorative **justice**. The cross is interpreted as the climactic enactment of **self-giving love**, and the **resurrection** as God’s affirmation and effective communication of that love as salvation. This **soteriology** leans on the unity of the **incarnation**, life, death, and resurrection, thus resisting any partition of the saving work from the identity of the **Son**. The universal scope of salvation is affirmed, while the decisive mediation of **Christ** and the necessity of **grace** are maintained.

O’Collins’ most programmatic statement on method comes in **Rethinking Fundamental Theology** 2011, where he revisits the **motives of credibility** post–Vatican II. He argues that miracles, preeminently the **resurrection**, are authentic **signs** that cohere with the personal nature of **God’s self-communication**, thus requiring a hermeneutics of trust shaped by the **Church’s tradition** and an openness to historical inquiry. He elaborates the **sacramentality of revelation**, proposing that the Church’s **liturgy**, the saints, and the **sensus fidelium** function as historically extended **signs** that witness to the truth of Christ. While receptive to **ecumenism** and **interreligious dialogue** under **Nostra Aetate** and **Lumen Gentium 16**, he resists dissolving the uniqueness of **Christ** or the definitiveness of **revelation**. He engages **religious experience** but anchors it in the objective **economy**, pressing back against existentialist reductions and underscoring the unity of **Scripture and Tradition** under the **magisterium**.

A hallmark of O’Collins’ contribution is a deft handling of **Scripture** within a Catholic **hermeneutic** shaped by **Dei Verbum**. He embraces the **historical-critical method** as a service to **faith**, while urging a **canonical** and **liturgical** reading that situates exegesis within the Church’s confessional practice. In **Christology** and the more popular **Jesus: A Portrait** 2008, he models a reading that employs criteria such as **multiple attestation** and **dissimilarity**, yet is alert to their limits, giving sustained weight to the narrative unity of the **Gospels** and to the early **creedal** summaries embedded in **Paul** and **Acts**. The goal is not a neutral reconstruction but a reasoned confession shaped by the **analogia fidei**. The **resurrection** functions here too as interpretive key, reframing the memory of Jesus’ ministry as the self-disclosure of the **Son** and grounding the Church’s proclamation.

O’Collins’ work also exhibits a pastoral and catechetical tact without sacrificing academic rigor, as seen in **A Concise Dictionary of Theology** 1991 coauthored with **Edward G. Farrugia**, which distills complex loci like **divine simplicity**, **grace**, **sacrament**, and **eschatology** into accessible entries. That volume reflects his conviction that systematic clarity serves the Church’s **mission**, and that doctrinal precision aids **evangelization** by presenting **revelation** as both intelligible and life-giving. His style exemplifies the **ressourcement** ideal: retrieval for renewal, biblical fidelity allied with dogmatic stability, and a refusal of both rationalist reduction and fideist retreat. The theological school at the **Gregorian University** under his tenure became a bridge between Anglophone and Roman scholarship, giving his **Christology**, **Trinity**, and **fundamental theology** a wide reception.

In dialogue with contemporaries, O’Collins engages **Wolfhart Pannenberg** on the public character of the **resurrection** and the theology of history, appropriating Pannenberg’s insistence on historical rationality while retaining a thicker sacramental ontology. With **Joseph Ratzinger**, he shares a high **Christology** and a conviction about the unity of **Scripture and Tradition**, though O’Collins’ tone remains pastorally irenic. He profits from **Hans Urs von Balthasar** on the aesthetic and dramatic character of revelation while maintaining his own emphases on **credibility** and historical grounding. His critiques of **Rudolf Bultmann** argue that demythologization fails to do justice to the objectivity of **revelation**, while his engagement with **Edward Schillebeeckx** presses for a more ontological account of Easter. He converges with **N. T. Wright** on the bodily-transformed character of **resurrection**, welcoming corroboration from biblical historiography. Throughout, he positions **Christ’s resurrection** as the hinge on which both method and dogma turn.

Taken as a whole, O’Collins offers a coherent theological vision in which **God’s self-communication** culminates in the **incarnate Son**, is vindicated in the **resurrection**, is interpreted within the **Trinitarian** economy, and is mediated by the **Church’s sacramental life**. His method renews **fundamental theology** by wedging open the space between apologetic demonstration and fideist fiat through historically responsible **motives of credibility**. His **Christology** secures the classic dogma while re-situating it in a narrative and liturgical horizon. His **soteriology** is Trinitarian, multivalent in metaphors, and careful in its account of **grace**. For Roman Catholic theology after **Vatican II**, he represents a synthesis that is biblically grounded, doctrinally stable, and missionally oriented, with the **resurrection** as the axis of both knowledge and salvation.

For a Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary evaluation, several convergences and tensions emerge. Positively, O’Collins’ defense of the historical, bodily **resurrection**, his high **Christology**, and his insistence that **revelation** is God’s initiating **self-communication** resonate with classical **Reformed orthodoxy** and its commitment to the public truth of the gospel. Yet important differences persist in principle. First, his rehabilitation of **motiva credibilitatis** and a **sacramental epistemology** risk softening the Reformed insistence that Scripture is **self-authenticating** through the **Spirit’s** internal testimony, with historical evidences as ministerial but never constitutive supports. Second, his nature-grace synthesis and **sacramentality of revelation** sit uneasily with a Reformed **covenantal** alternative that frames general and special revelation under **covenant** rather than a graded ontology of nature and **grace**. Third, his atonement **kaleidoscope** tends to marginalize **penal substitution** as central to **justification**, a point at issue for confessional Reformed soteriology that reads the cross primarily as **vicarious penalty-bearing** within the pactum salutis. Finally, his account of the unity of **Scripture and Tradition** under the **magisterium** diverges from **sola Scriptura**, which grounds doctrinal normativity in the canon alone, normed by the **analogia fidei** within the Church but never by an extrinsic magisterial authority. A Reformed appropriation would retain O’Collins’ Easter-centered **Christology** and historical seriousness while recentering epistemology on Scripture’s principium, retrieving **penal substitution** as dogmatic load-bearing, and reframing sacramentality within the **covenant of grace**.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Australian **Jesuit**, long-time professor at the **Pontifical Gregorian University**, working in the wake of **Vatican II** and the **ressourcement** movement.
- **Core focus**: Renewed **fundamental theology** and **Christology** grounded in the historical and revelatory centrality of the **resurrection**.
- **Major works**: **Christology** 1995, **The Tripersonal God** 1999, **Jesus Our Redeemer** 2007, **Jesus: A Portrait** 2008, **Rethinking Fundamental Theology** 2011, **Believing in the Resurrection** 2012, **A Concise Dictionary of Theology** 1991.
- **Distinctives**: **God’s self-communication**, retooled **motives of credibility**, **sacramentality of revelation**, **Trinitarian soteriology**, historically responsible **resurrection** apologetic.
- **Interlocutors**: Engagement with **Rahner**, **Lonergan**, **Balthasar**, **Ratzinger**, **Pannenberg**, critical responses to **Bultmann** and **Schillebeeckx** on Easter.
- **Reformed critique**: Affirm **resurrection** and **high Christology**; challenge **motiva credibilitatis**, **magisterial** authority, nature-grace synthesis, and the relative downplaying of **penal substitution**.
- **Legacy**: Widely used textbooks, durable synthesis of **biblical**, **dogmatic**, and **liturgical** theology; influential in shaping post–Vatican II **fundamental theology** and contemporary **Christology**.
