### TITLE
Retrieving **Herman Bavinck** for a coherent **neo-Calvinist public theology** that integrates **creation and redemption** into Reformed discipleship

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century **Reformed** setting at **Calvin Theological Seminary**; Anglophone reception of **Dutch neo-Calvinism** and the wider retrieval of **Herman Bavinck** and **Abraham Kuyper**
- Historically grounded **dogmatic ressourcement** with a Kuyperian **world-formative ethic**, privileging **dogmatics** as architectonic over social theory while engaging **economics** and **public theology**
- Key works: general editor, **Herman Bavinck, Reformed Dogmatics** 2003–2008; author, **A Free Church, a Holy Nation?** 2001; **Economic Shalom** 2013; **Bavinck on the Christian Life** 2015; editor, **Herman Bavinck, Reformed Ethics** 2019–2024; co-editor, **Essays on Religion, Science, and Society** 2008
- Central categories: **union with Christ**, **duplex gratia**, **common grace and antithesis**, **sphere sovereignty**, **creation order**, **principled pluralism**, **economic shalom**
- Interlocutors and influences: **Bavinck**, **Kuyper**, **Calvin**; debates with **two-kingdoms** proposals, **liberation theology**, **Anabaptist** withdrawal, and **evangelical pietism**
- Reformed reception: widely received as a leading **Bavinck** interpreter whose constructive **Kuyperian** ethics aim to stabilize **Reformed orthodoxy** in public life

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
John Bolt is a contemporary **Reformed** theologian whose career at **Calvin Theological Seminary** consolidated the English-language renaissance of **Dutch neo-Calvinism**, especially through his editorial leadership of **Herman Bavinck**. Working at the intersection of **systematic theology** and **social ethics**, Bolt framed his task as dogmatic retrieval for public discipleship: a return to the thick theological metaphysics of **creation**, **Trinity**, and **Christology** in order to discipline the church’s engagement with **culture** and **economics**. The intellectual context includes the post-1970s Anglophone reassessment of **Karl Barth**, renewed attention to **Reformed orthodoxy**, and the broad evangelical impulse toward cultural engagement. Against both thin **evangelical pragmatism** and historicist **liberation theology**, Bolt insists on the priority of **dogmatics** to ethics, grounding public theology in the *ad intra* life of the **Triune God** and the *ad extra* works of **creation and redemption**.

Methodologically, Bolt champions a confessional **ressourcement** approach: the recovery of **Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics** as a canon within the Reformed canon, where **Scripture** norms all theology and **catholicity** is prized. His interpretive center is **union with Christ** and **duplex gratia**, the double grace of justification and sanctification, which he deploys to recalibrate ethics away from voluntarist activism and toward **pilgrim holiness** in history. He argues that the **grace restores nature** axiom does not weaken **antithesis** but instead sets **common grace** within God’s **eschatological** patience, locating the church’s cultural labor within the already and the not yet. The upshot is a consistent **Kuyperian** affirmation of creaturely structures and **sphere sovereignty**, tempered by the Reformed insistence on **sin** and the need for **redemption**.

Bolt’s setting in the **Christian Reformed Church** and its institutions also shaped his dialogical posture. He engages the North American debates over **two-kingdoms** theology, **natural law**, and the scope of **civil society**, while remaining loyal to a **confessional** and **creational** outlook. He is wary of collapsing theology into sociology, yet he learns from modern social analysis to clarify the limits and responsibilities of **state**, **market**, and **church**. His method is not correlational in the classic **Tillichian** sense but covenantal: **special revelation** governs, **general revelation** is real but fallen, and **wisdom** is cultivated within the people formed by **Word** and **sacrament**.

#### 2. Editorial labor and the Bavinck renaissance
Bolt’s most enduring contribution is his stewardship of the English **Herman Bavinck, Reformed Dogmatics** translation, published in four volumes from 2003 to 2008 by **Baker Academic**. As general editor, he did more than shepherd a text; he curated a reception. The set reintroduced the Reformed world to Bavinck’s synthesis of **catholic orthodoxy** and **Reformed specificity**, including Bavinck’s robust accounts of **divine simplicity**, **Scripture’s organic inspiration**, **creation ex nihilo**, and **Christ’s two natures**. Bolt’s introductions, indices, and editorial decisions projected a vision in which **dogmatics** governs ethics and in which **Reformed catholicity** navigates between **sectarianism** and **liberal accommodation**. By placing **Bavinck’s organic motif** and his doctrine of **common grace** before Anglophone readers, Bolt created a grammar for contemporary Reformed engagement with **modernity** that is simultaneously appreciative and critical.

The editorial project widened with **Essays on Religion, Science, and Society** in 2008, co-edited by Bolt, which showcased **Bavinck’s** cultural and scientific essays. Here Bolt highlighted the Kuyperian themes of **sphere sovereignty**, the integrity of **science** under **Christ’s lordship**, and the role of **worldview** without surrendering theological primacy. He also spearheaded the publication of **Herman Bavinck, Reformed Ethics** beginning in 2019, bringing to light Bavinck’s previously unpublished moral theology. Across three volumes released by 2019, 2021, and 2024, Bolt framed **Reformed ethics** as the outworking of **union with Christ**, attending to **virtue**, **law**, **conscience**, **sanctification**, and the *ordo salutis* within an **eschatological** pilgrimage. The editorial through-line is clear: ethics flows from **dogmatics**, the **church’s catechesis** is the seedbed of public renewal, and the **creation order** is neither self-sufficient nor dispensable.

#### 3. Public theology: church and civil society
Bolt’s **A Free Church, a Holy Nation? Abraham Kuyper’s American Public Theology** 2001 functions as his constructive manifesto for **church and state**. Mining **Kuyper’s** distinction between the church as **institute** and as **organism**, Bolt argues for **principled pluralism** under **sphere sovereignty**. The **state** should not sacralize itself through civil religion; **church** should resist Erastian co-optation; and **civil society** mediates between family, congregation, and political authority. The free church witnesses to a **holy nation** not by nationalizing the gospel but by sustaining **worship**, **catechesis**, and **discipline** that generate a people capable of public charity. In Bolt’s reading, **Calvin’s** doctrine of **magistracy**, sweetened by **Kuyper’s** theory of social spheres, protects communal differentiation and the freedom of the **conscience** while affirming the **lordship of Christ** over all of life.

This **neo-Calvinist** political ethics is neither quietist nor revolutionary. Bolt repudiates **liberation theology’s** hermeneutic of class struggle and **Anabaptist** withdrawals from responsibility, even as he interrogates **Constantinianism**. The eschatological tension refracts as vocation: a pilgrim people render **public justice** while refusing **eschatological overreach**. In this idiom, the ministries of **diaconal mercy**, **education**, and **work** become forms of creaturely obedience sustained by **Word and sacrament**. Bolt’s public theology is thus sacramentally charged yet civically restrained, operating within the **two-ages** structure without adopting a **two-kingdoms** dualism that would diminish the church’s organismal presence in culture.

#### 4. Economic ethics: shalom and the market
In **Economic Shalom: A Reformed Primer on Faith, Work, and Human Flourishing** 2013, Bolt develops a **Reformed social ethics** that places **work** and **vocation** under **creation** and **kingdom**. The controlling biblical theme is **shalom**, comprehensive well-being under God’s **covenant**. Bolt defends the moral legitimacy of market coordination, emphasizing **property**, **exchange**, and **subsidiarity** as creational goods guarded by **justice**. Yet he rejects market absolutism. The **Sabbath**, the **poor**, and the limits of **commodification** mark boundary lines for economic life. The book reads as a catechesis in **wisdom**: prudential judgment informed by **Proverbs**, chastened by **fallenness**, animated by **charity**, and framed by **eschatological hope**.

Bolt’s distinctive contribution is to embed **economic reasoning** within **dogmatics**. The human person is a covenantal **image-bearer**, not a utility maximizer; the **law** is an expression of God’s moral will, not an instrument of social engineering; and **common grace** enables cooperation across confessional lines without erasing **antithesis**. He appropriates select insights from modern economics while subjecting them to **Christological** and **ecclesiological** disciplines. The result is neither **libertarianism** nor **statism** but a **Kuyperian** civil-society pluralism that privileges intermediate associations, **diaconal** responsibility, and the moral formation of desire through **sanctification**.

#### 5. Moral theology through Bavinck: union and pilgrimage
Bolt’s **Bavinck on the Christian Life: Following Jesus in Faithful Service** 2015 crystallizes his reading of **union with Christ** as the shape of Christian existence. He argues that **duplex gratia** links **justification** and **sanctification** inseparably. The believer’s status and transformation are coordinates of one **covenantal** union, so that ethics emerges as grateful obedience rather than autonomous virtue. Bolt highlights **Bavinck’s** organic view of the **church**, relating household, school, and civic life to worship and discipline. The Christian life is a **pilgrimage**, not a utopian project; the **antithesis** is real in thought and conduct; and **common grace** opens space for cultural cooperation without compromising confessional integrity.

Here Bolt combats two distortions. First, against **pietist** reduction of discipleship to inwardness, he insists on **vocation** and **culture** as sanctified arenas. Second, against **moralism**, he anchors obedience in **Christ’s merits** and the Spirit’s **regeneration**. The eschatological horizon shapes the virtues: **patience**, **hope**, **charity**, and **courage** are trained by liturgical time and the cross’s paradox. This **Augustinian** undertone keeps Bolt’s **neo-Calvinism** from triumphalism, while his confessional commitments keep it from dissolving into generic **evangelicalism**.

#### 6. Scripture, doctrine, and catholicity
Although best known for public-theological work, Bolt labors to keep **Scripture** and **dogma** central. In the **Reformed Dogmatics** project he foregrounded **Bavinck’s** doctrine of **revelation**, affirming the organic inspiration of **Scripture** and its self-attesting authority. Bolt’s pedagogical essays and editorial framing emphasize **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** as ecumenical goods, resisting modern attempts to loosen classical attributes in the name of relationality. He draws on **Calvin’s autotheos** to underscore the self-existence of the **Son** and the full **Trinitarian** communion that grounds redemption’s efficacy. This classical retrieval underwrites his ethics: without a stable **Creator-creature distinction**, the order of **creation** either tyrannizes grace or is romanticized. In Bolt’s hands, **catholic Reformed** dogmatics furnishes a metaphysical base for sober cultural labor.

Bolt’s insistence on **catholicity** extends to the moral tradition. By editing **Reformed Ethics**, he reintegrated **virtue** analysis and **conscience** formation into the Reformed conversation, complementing the Reformation’s **law-gospel** grammar. He contends that **virtue ethics** can be baptized where **union with Christ** and **grace** govern, avoiding Pelagian drift. This move strengthens **catechesis** for public faithfulness, equipping the church to form stable households, truthful speech, and just dealings. Here Bolt’s alignment with **Bavinck** shows the promise of a confessional Protestant moral theology that is neither repristinating nor derivative of secular theory.

#### 7. Nature, grace, and culture: common grace and antithesis
At the center of Bolt’s constructive program stand the twin **Kuyperian** poles of **common grace** and **antithesis**. He reads **creation** as ordered and teleological, but never neutral; he reads **redemption** as restorative, but never annihilating. Thus **common grace** is not an abstract neutrality but the Spirit’s preserving and distributing goodness in a fallen world, enabling the just use of **law**, **custom**, and **art** outside the church. The **antithesis** remains a noetic and moral rupture between **regenerate** and **unregenerate**, guarding the church’s proclamation and worship. Bolt refuses to allow either pole to swallow the other, and he resists both a **natural-law** triumphalism and an **antithetical** separatism.

This dialectic disciplines his judgments about **politics** and **economics**. He exhorts Christians to inhabit **vocational** callings with competence and charity, to honor **sphere sovereignty** by respecting institutional limits, and to pursue **justice** and **mercy** as fruits of sanctification. He also warns against messianic politics that confuses penultimate goods with the **kingdom of God**. The regulating image is **pilgrim citizenship**: fully engaged, never assimilated; publicly helpful, never idolatrous. Bolt hereby offers a grammar for sustained faithful presence that is dogmatically thick and prudentially realistic.

#### 8. Interlocutors and debates
Bolt’s work enters contested terrains. With **two-kingdoms** theologians he shares a concern to avoid **ecclesiocracy** and to honor the **regulative** specificity of the church’s ministry, but he criticizes strong dualisms that reduce **culture** to common and **church** to redemptive. With **liberation theology** he shares a passion for **justice**, but he rejects its **hermeneutic** of suspicion as a governing dogma and its temptation to politicize soteriology. With **Anabaptist** critiques of power he shares warnings about coercion, yet he rejects their ecclesial withdrawal from **civil society**. He appropriates insights from **Reformed philosophers** like **Dooyeweerd** and **Wolterstorff** while maintaining that **dogmatics** must lead, not follow, in ordering public life. These interlocutions sharpen Bolt’s insistence that **Scripture**, **confession**, and **churchly practice** are the non-negotiable matrix for **public theology**.

In these debates Bolt also converses, implicitly or explicitly, with **Van Til’s** emphasis on **antithesis** and presuppositional critique. He adapts that apologetic posture by embedding it within **Bavinck’s** thicker ontology of **creation** and **common grace**, steering between rationalist **natural theology** and fideistic retreat. The end result is a nuanced **Reformed epistemology** in practice: affirming **general revelation** and creational norms, denying autonomous reason, and directing all knowledge toward **doxology**.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed/WTS evaluation
From a confessional **Reformed** and **WTS** vantage, Bolt’s achievement is impressive and strategic. He has reintroduced **Herman Bavinck** as a master in **dogmatics**, retrieved **union with Christ** and **duplex gratia** as ethical centers, and supplied a careful account of **common grace** and **sphere sovereignty** for **public theology**. His editorial labor on **Reformed Dogmatics** and **Reformed Ethics** has enriched the church’s **catholic** memory and disciplined evangelical activism under **Trinitarian** and **Christological** norms. Yet tensions remain. First, the **creation order** and its prudential elaborations sometimes function in Bolt’s proposals with a quasi-**natural law** clarity that Reformed critics, especially in the **Van Tilian** stream, will warn risks softening the **antithesis** and introducing a moral ontology insufficiently chastened by **noetic** effects of sin. Second, his **Kuyperian** account of **market** and **civil society**, while far from libertarian, can seem to confer too much normative weight to institutional differentiation and not enough to the church’s pilgrim distinctiveness under the **means of grace**. Third, relative to **two-kingdoms** appeals, Bolt’s single-kingdom Christology could profit from sharpened articulation of **law-gospel** and **common-redemptive** distinctions to guard the church’s ministry from diffuse cultural mandates. The alternative within classical **Reformed orthodoxy** is not withdrawal but a stricter covenantal ordering of revelation and vocation: sustain robust **antithesis** in epistemology, defend real **common grace** in providence, locate cultural labor under the penultimate rule of **law**, and reserve the final word of **grace** to the church’s proclamation of **Christ crucified**. Within that frame, Bolt’s corpus remains an indispensable guide, provided it is read in tandem with a vigilant **Westminster** accent on the church’s spirituality and the apologetic critique of **autonomous** reason.
