### TITLE
Sanctification flows from union with Christ by faith, not from legal striving.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Post-Restoration English Puritanism**, **Protestant scholastic federalism**, and the **neonomian–antinomian debates** frame Walter Marshall's ministry and argument.
- **Pastoral casuistry** ordered by **scholastic distinctions**, Scripture as **principium externum** and the Spirit as **principium internum**, a **Christocentric federal method**.
- **The Gospel Mystery of Sanctification** 1692, published posthumously by nonconformist colleagues in London.
- **Union with Christ**, **duplex gratia** justification and sanctification, **law–gospel distinction**, **covenant of grace** versus **covenant of works**, **instrumental faith**, **mortification and vivification**.
- Engages **Richard Baxter** and **neonomianism**, draws from **Thomas Goodwin** and **John Owen**, anchored in **Westminster Confession** and **Puritan divinity**.
- Received within **Reformed orthodoxy** as a lucid pastoral synthesis of **federal theology** and **experiential piety**, prized by later **evangelical Reformed** circles.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and context
Walter Marshall, 1628 to 1680, labored as an English **Puritan** pastor forged by the turmoil of the **Civil War**, the **Interregnum**, and the **Restoration** under Charles II. His ministry unfolded under the shadow of the **Act of Uniformity** and the ejection of 1662, within the marginal but resilient world of English **nonconformity**. His intellectual soil was **Protestant scholasticism**, catechized by the **Westminster Confession** and the **Shorter and Larger Catechisms**, steeped in **federal theology** that distinguished the **covenant of works** from the **covenant of grace**. Marshall absorbed the pastoral casuistry of the **Puritan practical divines** while inhabiting the codified distinctions of **Reformed orthodoxy**, so that conscience care and scholastic precision converged. The late seventeenth century also sharpened the polarity between **neonomianism**, often associated with **Richard Baxter**, and **antinomianism**, associated in polemic with **Tobias Crisp** and his heirs. Marshall's mature contribution engages this double threat by deploying a robust **law–gospel distinction** within a covenantal and Christocentric frame, ordered to the sanctification of sinners.

#### 2. Method, posture, and epistemic stance
Marshall's method is the **pastoral application of scholastic doctrine**, a disciplined casuistry that tests the conscience by **Scripture** as the **principium externum** and leans upon the Spirit as the **principium internum** of saving knowledge. He writes as a churchman, not a speculative metaphysician, yet his argument is full of **ordo salutis** grammar and **causal analysis**. The Spirit is the **efficient cause** of sanctification, **Christ's righteousness and life** provide the **meritorious and material cause** of holiness, **faith** is the **instrumental cause**, and new obedience is the **formal expression** of renewed nature. This coherent causal account is placed within **duplex gratia**, the twin gift of **justification and sanctification** in union with Christ. Marshall's epistemic posture is the **theologia practica** of the Puritans, an axiological priority that doctrine must heal the conscience and strengthen godliness. He critiques **introspective moralism** and the overreliance on the **practical syllogism** for assurance, insisting that the conscience must be reoriented by the **indicatives of the gospel** before the **imperatives of the law** can be enacted in truth.

#### 3. The Gospel Mystery of Sanctification, 1692
Marshall's sole classic, **The Gospel Mystery of Sanctification**, appeared in 1692, twelve years after his death, and it distills twenty years of pastoral wrestling with the **power of indwelling sin**. The treatise is organized as a sequence of **directions**, each a doctrinal thesis turned toward practice, and the cumulative effect is a spiritual itinerary from **union with Christ** to **mortification and vivification**. The book addresses the sore conscience that oscillates between **legal striving** and **lawless despair**, arguing that sanctification is a **benefit of the covenant of grace** as surely as justification is, and not a project of nature aided by law. Marshall pleads for **faith's direct act** upon Christ, not the reflex act of sifting evidences, as the animating principle of holiness. The language of **federal headship**, **Spirit-wrought participation**, and **promise-driven obedience** anchors each direction, yielding a work that feels both exegetically Puritan and architecturally scholastic, yet with the heat of **experiential divinity**.

#### 4. Union with Christ as the fountain of holiness
At the core of Marshall's doctrine lies **mystical union with Christ**, the Spirit's bond by which the believer shares in Christ's death and life, *unum mysticum* for the restoration of the image of God. Marshall insists that the believer is **dead to the law as a covenant of works**, yet **alive to God under the covenant of grace**, a dialectic essential to the **third use of the law**. The law remains a perfect **rule of life**, while its condemning covenantal voice is silenced by **imputation** and **adoption**. Out of this union, by **duplex gratia**, the believer receives both **forensic acceptance** and **transformative renewal**, inseparable yet distinct. Faith is not a general confidence in improvement, but the **instrumental reception of Christ himself**, the vine who communicates sap and fruit to the branches. Marshall's framing echoes **Calvin's unio cum Christo** and anticipates later emphasis on the **definitive aspect of sanctification**, yet he relentlessly tethers union to the **means of grace** and to the **promises** that create what they command.

#### 5. Law, gospel, and the shape of evangelical obedience
Marshall's most volatile polemical edge addresses **neonomianism**, the revision of the gospel into a milder **new law** with conditions proportioned to human ability. He argues that the **law–gospel distinction** is not a dispensational curiosity but the grammar of **covenantal soteriology**, in which the **covenant of works** demands and condemns, while the **covenant of grace** gives what it requires through **Christ's mediatorial obedience**. Commands, considered as law, cannot impart the **new principle** of life, they only expose the want of it. The gospel, considered as **promise in Christ**, under the Spirit, gives the **habitus** and **act** of holiness. Thus evangelical obedience is the **fruit of faith**, not the condition of access to Christ. Marshall nevertheless upholds the **third use of the law**, so that the same decalogue that smote the conscience now serves as **normative guidance**, and the **imperatives** are to be embraced precisely because the **indicatives** have been believed. His pastoral watchword is that grace does not run against the law, grace runs beneath it.

#### 6. Mortification, vivification, and assurance
Within this unionist and covenantal frame, Marshall treats **mortification and vivification** as a single rhythm of sanctification under the **Spirit's agency**. Sin is not eradicated, but it is dethroned, and the believer practices a **gospel mortification**, striking at sin through a stronger love for **Christ's sufficiency** rather than through bare self-denial. He richly deploys **Romans 6** as the template, the believer reckoning himself dead to sin and alive to God in **Christ Jesus**, a reckoning that is not make-believe but **participation**. Assurance, for Marshall, is not the capstone of elite piety, it is the **ordinary fruit of faith** as it beholds **Christ crucified and risen**. He does not abolish the **practical syllogism**, the reflective inference from sanctification to justification, but he refuses to make it the foundation. The direct act of faith on **Christ's promises**, sealed in **baptism and the Lord's Supper**, is both the spring of holiness and the basal ground of quieted conscience within **Reformed experiential piety**.

#### 7. Interlocutors and debates, pastoral warfare on two fronts
Marshall's constructive labor is unintelligible apart from his pastoral duel with **Baxterian moralism** and the specter of **antinomianism**. He acknowledges **Richard Baxter's** zeal for holiness but contends that Baxter's **neonomian drift** turns the gospel into an achievable rule, sapping the soul of evangelical joy and power. On the other flank, he rebukes **antinomian misreadings** that invert grace into **licentiousness**, reminding readers that **union with Christ** entails actual **conformity to Christ**. He finds better companions among the **Westminster divines**, and among Puritans such as **Thomas Goodwin** and **John Owen**, whose doctrines of **union**, **Spirit**, and **mortification** supply deep resources. Yet Marshall's idiom is more hospitable, more directly curative of the anxious soul, less preoccupied with polemic system-building than with **healing by doctrine**. His book arrives into the later **neonomian controversy of the 1690s**, though he died earlier, and it quickly functioned as a **middle course** that was neither Baxterian nor Crispian, a **federal evangelicalism** that could be prayed and obeyed.

#### 8. Exegetical threads and sacramental economy
Marshall's exegesis expands upon **Romans**, **Galatians**, and **John 15**, texts where **union**, **faith**, and the **law–gospel antithesis** are clearest. He underscores **Romans 8** on the **Spirit of adoption**, showing that filial status drives filial likeness, and that **adoption** is no mere forensic afterthought but the relational matrix of holiness. He reads **Galatians 2.20** as the grammar of sanctification, I live by faith in the Son of God, so that life in the flesh is life by **pistis Christou** as trust in the faithful Christ. The sacraments, **baptism** and **the Supper**, are means by which the **Spirit** seals the **promises**, strengthening faith's bond to **Christ's person** and **benefits**. Marshall's federal logic ensures that sacramental grace is neither automatic nor extrinsic, but that the **sign seals the thing signified** to believers, consolidating the **gospel order** in which reception precedes performance. These exegetical threads reveal a disciplined **Reformed hermeneutic**, one that refuses to let apostolic indicatives be annexed by law, and one that anticipates later retrievals of **Christological sanctification** in **Reformed orthodoxy**.

#### 9. Reception and legacy in Reformed piety
The reception of **The Gospel Mystery of Sanctification** within **Reformed churches** has been steady rather than spectacular, its influence carried in pastoral schools and devotional reading more than in academic footnotes. Eighteenth- and nineteenth-century **evangelical Calvinists** found in Marshall a balance against both **quietism** and **moralism**, a way to preach commandments as **gospel obedience**. His language about the **promissory nature of sanctification** echoed in later emphases on **definitive sanctification**, and his stress on **union with Christ** prepared the way for modern **union-centric** retrievals. He stands alongside **Owen** for mortification, alongside **Goodwin** for union, and serves as a pastoral counterpoint to **Baxter** for the shape of duty. In contemporary **Reformed and Presbyterian** circles, Marshall is often commended as a classic of **experiential Calvinism**, a text that catechizes the conscience into **promise-driven holiness**. His legacy is thus a school of **gospel method**, an ordered path by which **federal theology** becomes **practical godliness**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
Evaluated within classical **Reformed orthodoxy**, including the sensibilities of the **Westminster Standards** and a **WTS** trajectory that prizes **Scripture**, **covenant**, and **Christocentric exegesis**, Marshall's project is remarkably sound. He rightly makes **union with Christ** the structural principle of sanctification, preserves the **law–gospel distinction** without denying the **third use**, and refuses both **neonomian conditionalism** and **antinomian license**. Two tensions remain in need of careful handling. First, the robust priority of **indicatives** can be misheard as softening the sharpness of **imperatives**, so preaching must ensure that the **law as norm** is not swallowed by the **gospel as promise**. Second, the insistence on **faith's direct act** for assurance can be skewed either toward **pietistic introspection** if detached from **Word and sacrament**, or toward a **psychological voluntarism** if faith is construed as naked resolve rather than Spirit-given reception. A WTS-shaped alternative accent would reinforce Marshall's insights by integrating more explicitly the **historia salutis** with the **ordo salutis**, rooting sanctification in the once-for-all accomplishment of **Christ's death and resurrection** and the eschatological gift of the **Spirit**, while clarifying that the **means of grace in the covenant community** are the Spirit's ordinary workshop of holiness. Within those calibrations, Marshall remains a trustworthy guide to **grace-fueled obedience**.
