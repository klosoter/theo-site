### TITLE
Being as Communion: Trinitarian personhood, Eucharistic ontology, and the Church as eschatological hypostasis

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Historical context**: Greek Orthodox theologian of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, shaped by **neopatristic ressourcement** and ecumenical dialogues after **Vatican II**.
- **Method**: A **eucharistic ontology** grounded in liturgical experience, interpreted through **patristic metaphysics** of **hypostasis**, **ousia**, and **communion**.
- **Key works**: **Being as Communion** 1985; **Eucharist, Bishop, Church** 1965 Greek, 2001 English; **Communion and Otherness** 2006.
- **Central terms**: **personhood as event**, **monarchy of the Father**, **communion ontology**, **eschatological prolepsis**, **theosis**, **hypostatic freedom**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Cappadocians** and **Maximus the Confessor**; modern personalists like **Buber** and **Levinas**; critics including **Lucian Turcescu** and **Paul McPartlan**; conversation with **Moltmann** and **Gunton** on **social trinitarianism**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: Questions over **principium theologiae**, **divine simplicity**, **autotheos** and **filioque**, and the priority of **Word over sacrament** in **ecclesiology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and neopatristic method
John D. **Zizioulas** 1931â€“2023 emerged within the Greek **Orthodox** world marked by the twentieth-century **neopatristic synthesis** of **Georges Florovsky** and **Vladimir Lossky**, a movement that rejected historicist **Hellenization** theses and returned to the **Cappadocian Fathers**, **Maximus the Confessor**, and **Palamas** as living sources. Educated in **Thessaloniki** and **Athens**, and shaped by study with **Florovsky** in the early 1960s, he completed his doctoral work on the unity of the Church in the divine **Eucharist** and the **bishop** in 1965, later published as **Eucharist, Bishop, Church**. His career spanned appointments in **Thessaloniki**, **King's College London**, and **Glasgow**, and he was consecrated **Metropolitan of Pergamon** in 1986, integrating academic **dogmatics** with ecclesial oversight. The ecumenical climate after **Vatican II** and the **Faith and Order** agenda of the **World Council of Churches** provided a horizon for his signature project: articulating a **eucharistic ontology** in which **being as communion** describes both **Trinity** and **Church** as a single theological vision.

The hallmark of Zizioulas's method is a retrieval of **hypostasis** over **substance** and of **communion** over **individuality**, expressed programmatically in **Being as Communion** 1985. He argues that the **Cappadocians** effected a decisive ontological shift by locating **being** in **personhood**, so that **relation** is constitutive, not accidental. This hermeneutic is not a mere historical claim but a constructive **ontology** that places **liturgy**, **episcopacy**, and **synodality** at the center of knowing God. The method is intentionally **apophatic** in the **Losskian** sense, yet it affirms a positive knowledge of God through the **ecclesial event**, especially the Eucharistic **anaphora** where the Spirit constitutes the Body of **Christ**. The result is a theological epistemology in which **experience** is not private intuition but the historically embodied **koinonia** of the Church that mediates **eschatological** reality.

#### 2. Being as communion: personhood, otherness, and freedom
In **Being as Communion**, Zizioulas develops three linked claims on **personhood**, **otherness**, and **freedom**. First, the ultimate ground of being is the **hypostasis** of the **Father**, the **monarchy of the Father** from whom the Son is eternally begotten and the Spirit eternally proceeds. This monarchy is not subordinationism but the personal source of **homoousian** communion, a claim intended to secure both **otherness** and **unity** without collapsing into **substance** monism. Second, he defines person as ecstatic being, **ekstasis**, whose identity is constituted in **relation**, not in an isolated **nature**; hence, person is an event of **communion** rather than an individual essence. Third, he links personhood with **freedom**, not the arbitrariness of choice but the freedom of being-from-the-Other, a mode epitomized in the **Trinitarian** relations where the Son receives being from the Father without necessity and without inferiority. These theses together construct an ontological grammar in which **communion and otherness** are mutually implicative, later elaborated in **Communion and Otherness** 2006, where he responds to worries that relational ontologies dissolve **alterity**.

This architecture recasts classic **patristic** distinctions between **ousia** and **hypostasis**. Zizioulas insists that **nature** is universal and abstract, while **hypostasis** is concrete and relational, and that the **Church** exists as a corporate hypostasis by the **Spirit** in the **Eucharist**. He draws on **Maximus the Confessor** for the idea of personalizing **nature** and for the eschatological transformation of the **logoi** of creation in the **Logos**, and he engages modern personalists such as **Buber** and **Levinas** to highlight **face-to-face** alterity while rejecting their lack of an ontological foundation in **Trinity**. The point is ontological, not just anthropological: **being** is communion, and only communion can defeat the necessity of **death** that imprisons biological hypostases.

#### 3. Eucharistic ecclesiology: bishop, local church, and catholicity
Zizioulas's earliest major work, **Eucharist, Bishop, Church** 1965, argues historically and theologically that the **local church** gathered around the **bishop** manifests the fullness of the **catholic** Church. Drawing on early patristic sources such as **Ignatius of Antioch**, he maintains that the unity of the Church is realized in the local **Eucharist** as an eschatological **synaxis**, not as a federation of communities under a juridical head. The **bishop** presides as the icon of **Christ** and the principle of unity, representing the one Church in each place and thereby grounding catholicity in the concrete **Eucharistic** assembly. This early thesis becomes a pillar of his wider **eucharistic ontology**, where the Church's being is constituted by the **Spirit** through the anamnetic and epikletic dynamic of the **liturgy**.

From this basis he develops a multi-level **primacy** within **synodality**. In ecumenical dialogues, notably the **Ravenna** 2007 document, he argues for primacy at local, regional, and universal levels as a canonical expression of **communion**, while rejecting centralized **juridicism**. His contributions to the **Joint International Commission for Theological Dialogue** with the **Roman Catholic Church** display a consistent attempt to imagine a universal **primacy** compatible with **Orthodox** synodality. Critics such as **Paul McPartlan** have pressed him on the balance of **Eucharist** and **episcopate**, and on the risk of **Eucharistic** self-sufficiency that fails to secure the visibility of universal structures, but his ecclesiology remains rooted in the **local-church** ontology of communion.

#### 4. Pneumatology and eschatological ontology
Zizioulas's **pneumatology** is theologically decisive. He resists a merely Christomonistic ecclesiology by assigning the **Holy Spirit** the role of bringing the **eschaton** into history. The Spirit, he says, constitutes the Church from the **future**, a claim resonant with motifs in **Moltmann** and **Pannenberg**, yet articulated within an **Orthodox** horizon of **eschatological prolepsis**. The **Eucharist** is thus an event of the **last things**, where creatures are hypostasized not by biological necessity but by **gracious** communion. This allows him to radicalize the **already-not yet** structure of Christian existence: baptism and **chrismation** form an ecclesial hypostasis that anticipates the resurrectional mode of being, while still vulnerable to sin and death. The **Spirit's** role maintains **otherness** by preventing the assimilation of churchly identity to sociological or natural determinations.

In this eschatological light he addresses **soteriology** and **theosis**. Salvation is not primarily juridical acquittal but the personalizing of nature through participation in **Trinitarian** life, a process grounded in **Christology** and actualized by the **Spirit**. The **theosis** motif, learned from **Maximus** and **Palamas**, becomes the soteriological correlate of **being as communion**. Thus, the Church is not simply an instrument but the sacramental locus of **deification**, a claim that raises questions among Western interlocutors about the relation of history, sacrament, and grace. Zizioulas insists that this does not disparage history; rather, it frees history from the tyranny of necessity by opening it to **eschatological** freedom.

#### 5. Person and nature: anthropology, ethics, and creation
Zizioulas extends the person-nature distinction into **anthropology**. Human beings, he argues, are not individuals defined by **nature** but persons constituted by **communion**. The tragic condition of fallen existence is the imprisonment of the person in **biological** hypostasis, where death dictates identity and relation. In **Communion and Otherness** 2006 he explores how **otherness** is affirmed without rivalry: to be is to be-in-communion, yet not to be absorbed. He employs the language of **hypostatic freedom** to argue that human personhood is a gift from the **eschaton**, mediated by **ecclesial** life, a claim he grounds in the **sacraments** of initiation and in the ascetical ethos.

This anthropology yields an ethical and ecological program. In essays on **ecology** and creation, he proposes a **eucharistic asceticism** in which the world is received as gift and offered back in **anaphora** to God, resisting consumption and instrumentalization. The bishop's presidency at the **Eucharist** becomes a paradigm for a eucharistic **cosmology**, where the priestly offering of creation anticipates its **transfiguration**. His ethic of **communion** critiques both collectivist and individualist paradigms and calls for a **koinonic** politics. Some have read an implicit universalism into his ontology of communion; Zizioulas denies this, affirming the possibility of self-exclusion from **communion**, though his emphasis on the ecclesial hypostasis drives a soteriological confidence in the Church's **catholicity**.

#### 6. Historical claims and critical reception
Zizioulas's claim that the **Cappadocians** effected an ontological primacy of **person** over **nature** has provoked intense scrutiny. Scholars such as **Lucian Turcescu** and **Andrew Louth** question whether **Basil**, **Gregory of Nyssa**, and **Gregory Nazianzen** ever made person ontologically ultimate, or whether Zizioulas reads a modern relational personalism back into **patristic** texts. Others, including **John Behr**, warn against separating **hypostasis** and **ousia** in a way alien to **pro-Nicene** metaphysics. Zizioulas replies that he offers a faithful retrieval, not a mere repristination, and that **dogmatic development** warrants conceptual sharpening. His robust defense of the **monarchy of the Father** likewise draws critique from Western theologians worried about threats to **consubstantial equality** and to the **autotheos** of the Son. He insists that monarchy is personal causality, not priority in nature, and is essential to affirm both **otherness** and **communion**.

The reception of his **eucharistic ecclesiology** has been wider and more positive. **Roman Catholic** theologians like **Paul McPartlan** appreciate his stress on local catholicity and **synodality**, while pressing him on universal structures. **Protestant** ecclesiologists have drawn from his **being as communion** to correct individualism and sacralize the communal, with figures like **Colin Gunton** adapting his critique of **substance metaphysics** for a Reformed **social trinitarianism**. Zizioulas himself resists flattening the **Trinity** into a social analogy, warning that the **Cappadocian** grammar cannot be directly applied to human community without attention to **transcendence** and **apophatic** reserve. The debates reveal both the fecundity and the limits of his programmatic thesis.

#### 7. Trinity, filioque, and doctrinal balances
Zizioulas's **Trinitarian** teaching is structured by the **monarchy of the Father**, the **homoousios** communion of the Three, and the safeguarding of **otherness** from absorption. He rejects the **filioque** as a deformation of **pneumatology** that compromises the Spirit's distinct otherness and risks a dual-causality that undermines the personal monarchy. He proposes instead that the Son's eternal manifestation of the Spirit be articulated economically and relationally without attributing **ekporeusis** to the Son. His mode of exposition stresses the **Spirit's** role in constituting the **Church** and in realizing eschatological communion, a stress that sometimes appears, to Western readers, to tilt toward **pneumatocentrism**. Yet he insists on **Christology** as essential, arguing that the **Logos** is the content of communion while the **Spirit** is its mode, an ordering learned from **Maximus**.

Doctrinal balances are evident across his work. The **divine simplicity** of the one **ousia** is preserved by denying any tritheistic outcome to his personalism; at the same time, he is explicit that **simplicity** cannot be construed as the primacy of abstract essence over concrete **hypostases**. The **immutability** of God is not static timelessness but the unchanging faithfulness of personal communion. Although not a metaphysician in scholastic terms, he interrogates Western **substance metaphysics** from the standpoint of **ecclesial** experience, urging a metaphysics of **communion** that can bear the weight of Scripture's doxological witness and the Church's liturgical life.

#### 8. God, history, and Scripture
Zizioulas's constructive project is intentionally ecclesial and liturgical, yet he frames it as fidelity to **Scripture** read in the communion of the saints. His biblical exegesis is indirect, mediated by **patristic** dogmatics. The story of Israel and the **Christ-event** is received through the **Eucharist** as the interpretive center where **anamnesis** makes past events present and **epiclesis** opens them to the **eschaton**. He does not elevate private mystical experience but the communal hearing of the **Word** within the **liturgy**, so that the **Church** as **koinonia** becomes the hermeneutical subject. This yields a distinct approach to **revelation**, where the canon is inseparable from Christ's Body, and where **tradition** is not a set of propositions but the living continuity of **communion**.

His engagement with modernity is mediated by a critique of **individualism** and an embrace of **personalism** purified by **Trinitarian** ontology. He acknowledges insights from **Buber** and **Levinas** on **otherness**, yet he relocates them christologically and sacramentally. He interacts with **Moltmann**'s eschatological trinitarianism and **Pannenberg**'s history-centered theology, but insists that **ontology** cannot be derived from salvation history alone; rather, **ecclesial** participation in the **Trinity** grants a metaphysical horizon within which history is redeemed. In these moves Zizioulas exhibits the confidence of the **neopatristic** program: a catholic, eschatological **metaphysics** capable of hospitable engagement with the modern West.

#### 9. Integrated Reformed or WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and Westminster Theological Seminary vantage, Zizioulas offers salutary correctives yet raises principial concerns. Positively, his ontology of **communion** resists atomizing **individualism**, his **Trinitarian** focus on **personhood** guards relationality, and his **Eucharistic** stress recalls the Church's concrete **koinonia**. Yet his epistemic center of gravity appears to rest on the **ecclesial event**, risking the displacement of the principium of **Scripture alone** as the norming norm. A Reformed account would insist that the **Word** constitutes the **Church** and its **sacraments**, not vice versa, and would hesitate to treat the **Eucharist** as the site that ontologically constitutes ecclesial being. Doctrinally, the **monarchy of the Father** as causal principle can appear to jeopardize the Reformed emphasis on the **autotheos** of the Son and the **filioque** as a guardrail of consubstantial communion, while his relational articulation of **divine simplicity** can be read as softening the classical affirmation that God is one simple essence. Likewise, the construal of **theosis** as personalizing nature within **communion** risks occluding the forensic clarity of **justification** by faith and the covenantal structure of grace. A Reformed alternative would anchor communion in **covenant** and **union with Christ** by the **Spirit** through the **Word**, affirm **divine simplicity** and **aseity** as metaphysical safeguards of God's freedom, confess the **filioque** as expressive of the Spirit's eternal communion with Father and Son, and articulate **ecclesiology** such that **preaching** enjoys primacy as the ordinary means by which the **Triune** God creates and sustains faith, with the **supper** as a divinely appointed means of grace but not the ontological ground of being.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Greek **Orthodox**, postwar **neopatristic** context, episcopal theologian engaged in **ecumenical** dialogues and modern **personalism**.
- **Problem**: Recasting ontology as **being as communion**, integrating **Trinity**, **Eucharist**, and **Church** under an **eschatological** horizon.
- **Works**: **Being as Communion** 1985; **Eucharist, Bishop, Church** 1965/2001; **Communion and Otherness** 2006; essays on **pneumatology** and **ecology**.
- **Doctrines**: **Monarchy of the Father**, **person over nature**, **eucharistic ontology**, **theosis**, **eschatological prolepsis**, relational **divine simplicity**.
- **Interlocutors**: **Cappadocians**, **Maximus**, **Lossky**, **Florovsky**, with critique by **Turcescu** and engagement with **Gunton** and **Moltmann**.
- **Reformed critique**: Priority of **Scripture** over ecclesial event, classical **simplicity** and **autotheos**, **filioque**, covenantal **union with Christ** over eucharistic ontology.
- **Legacy**: A generative **communion** theology reconfiguring **ecclesiology** and **Trinity**, widely influential across **Orthodox**, **Catholic**, and **Protestant** conversations.
