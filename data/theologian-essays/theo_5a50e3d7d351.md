### TITLE
Historicizing early Christian diversity through critical editions and canonical-apocryphal comparison

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth and early twenty-first century British biblical scholarship** situated in a post-Bultmann, post-Streeter landscape that retools **Formgeschichte** and **Redaktionsgeschichte** with renewed attention to **manuscript evidence** and **Second Temple Judaism**.
- **Historical-critical method** that integrates **textual criticism**, **source and redaction analysis**, and *Überlieferungsgeschichte* to map **kerygmatic development** and **community formation** behind texts.
- **Community, Law and Mission in Matthew’s Gospel** 2004; **The Writings of the Apostolic Fathers** 2007 ed.; **The Non-Canonical Gospels** 2008 ed.; **The Apocryphal Gospels: A Very Short Introduction** 2009; **The Gospel of Peter: Introduction, Critical Edition and Commentary** 2010.
- **Synoptic problem**, **Matthean community**, **Apostolic Fathers**, **canon formation**, **apocryphal gospel typologies**, **textual pluriformity**, **criterion of multiple attestation**, *ipsissima vox* versus *ipsissima verba*.
- Interlocutors include **B. H. Streeter**, **Christopher Tuckett**, **John Kloppenborg**, **James D. G. Dunn**, **Richard Bauckham**, **Larry W. Hurtado**, **Bart D. Ehrman**, **J. K. Elliott**, **Kurt Niederwimmer**.
- Reformed cue: use of **historical-critical reconstruction** aids historical awareness, yet presses against **self-authenticating Scripture** and **redemptive-historical unity**, raising questions for **inerrancy** and **canon**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, setting, and method
The intellectual profile of **Paul Foster** emerged within the consolidating milieu of late twentieth-century British New Testament scholarship, where the heirs of **Rudolf Bultmann** and **B. H. Streeter** recalibrated **Formgeschichte** and **Redaktionsgeschichte** in conversation with **Second Temple Judaism** and with a renewed respect for **textual criticism**. Working from the University of Edinburgh’s **New College** tradition, Foster’s research inhabits the deliberately modest claims of the **historical-critical method**, seeking to recover *Sitz im Leben* and to describe the trajectories of **tradition history** *Überlieferungsgeschichte* that produced both canonical and non-canonical gospel literature. His methodological instincts are descriptive rather than speculative: reconstruct the earliest recoverable text, identify source relations, and trace editorial profiles that reveal the theological self-understanding of communities. Behind this stands a restrained confidence in categories such as **criterion of multiple attestation**, **criterion of coherence**, and the distinction between *ipsissima vox* and *ipsissima verba*, coupled with a textual sensibility that foregrounds **manuscript witnesses** and **textual pluriformity**.

That methodological posture places Foster downstream from **Christopher Tuckett** on the **Synoptic problem**, adjacent to **John Kloppenborg** on **Q**, and in critical conversation with **James D. G. Dunn** and **Richard Bauckham** on memory and **eyewitness testimony**. It also aligns him with **Larry W. Hurtado** and **Bart D. Ehrman** in attending to material and documentary realities: codices, fragments, and editorial accretions. In this environment, **Foster’s historical-epistemic stance** is cautious: he does not claim access to uninterpreted origins, only to increasingly well-specified textual and redactional layers whose theological profiles can be mapped. The payoff is double. It grounds theological claims in demonstrable literary phenomena, and it reframes debates about **orthodoxy and heresy** after **Walter Bauer** by attending to actual texts across the second century and beyond.

#### 2. Matthew, community, and Torah
Foster’s early monograph, **Community, Law and Mission in Matthew’s Gospel** 2004, is an exercise in **redaction criticism** and **Second Temple Jewish context** that probes how the **Matthean community** negotiated **Torah**, identity, and **universal mission**. Reading **Matthew** through the lens of fulfillment and community boundary markers, Foster argues that the evangelist’s **halakhic discourse** in the **Sermon on the Mount** and the **antitheses** functions as communal catechesis for a group both within and in dispute with the synagogue. The book underscores **Matthew’s fulfillment formulae** and **Emmanuel Christology** as theological engines that authorize reconfigured obedience to **Torah** under the lordship of **Jesus as Kyrios**. The **Great Commission** universalizes mission without dissolving the continuity of **Israel’s Scriptures**, producing a coherent yet contested ecclesial identity.

This line of argument positions Foster among interpreters like **Ulrich Luz**, **Graham Stanton**, and **Anthony Saldarini**, for whom **Matthean redaction** reveals real communal pressures and aspirations. It also touches doctrinal loci indirectly. **Ecclesiology** appears as **binding and loosing**, **teaching** and **discipline**; **Christology** as Emmanuel, Davidic sonship, and **Son of Man** authority; **missiology** as Gentile inclusion under the rubric of **fulfillment**. The method remains constant: text-critical stability, source-sensitive reading, and sustained attention to the **editorial voice** that shapes inherited **Jesus tradition** for communal formation.

#### 3. The Apostolic Fathers as early reception and ecclesial formation
If Matthew gave Foster a canonical base, the **Apostolic Fathers** furnished him with an early reception history and comparative ecclesiology. As editor of **The Writings of the Apostolic Fathers** 2007, Foster curates the textual, historical, and theological profiles of **1 Clement**, **Ignatius of Antioch**, the **Didache**, the **Epistle of Barnabas**, and related corpora. The volume situates these texts within **late first and early second-century** Christianities, attending to **textual transmission**, **provenance**, and **genre**. The **Didache** emerges as a catechetical manual of **two ways** instruction with vivid directives on **baptism**, **fasting**, **eucharistia**, and itinerant prophets. **Ignatius’s letters** display a polemical high **Christology** and advocacy for **monepiscopacy**, signaling consolidation of **church order** in the face of **docetism** and schism. **1 Clement** witnesses to Roman mediation and a scripture-saturated appeal to **order and concord**.

Foster’s work here complements the editions by **Michael W. Holmes** and **Bart D. Ehrman**, coinciding with their attention to **text-type variants**, **recensional layers**, and intra-Christian conflicts. Theologically, the Apostolic Fathers function in Foster’s hands as laboratories for observing the early development of **ecclesiology**, **sacramental practice**, and **moral catechesis**. They also demonstrate the porous boundary between **Scripture and tradition** in the era when **canon formation** remained fluid. By placing these writings adjacent to Matthew and the other Gospels, Foster enables diachronic comparisons that complicate linear narratives of doctrinal stabilization while showing discernible patterns of **tradition consolidation**.

#### 4. Non-canonical gospels and the shape of diversity
Foster’s editorial and authorial voice reaches a wide audience in **The Non-Canonical Gospels** 2008 and **The Apocryphal Gospels: A Very Short Introduction** 2009. These works synthesize a century of scholarship on apocryphal materials, classify them by **genre** and **theological profile**, and locate them within the larger story of **early Christian diversity**. The typology is instructive: **infancy gospels** such as the **Protoevangelium of James** with Marian concerns and fulfillment tropes; **passion-resurrection narratives** like the **Gospel of Peter** with expanded drama and polemic; **sayings or dialogue gospels** like the **Gospel of Thomas** with sapiential esotericism; and **Jewish-Christian gospels** that complicate our sense of **Torah** and **Christ** for communities near the synagogue.

Against romanticized appeals to hidden Christianities, Foster emphasizes **textual provenance**, **date**, **language**, and **manuscript evidence**. He explains the fragmentary state of witnesses such as **P. Oxy.** papyri and the **Akhmim** codex, instructs readers in reading **critical editions**, and cautions against constructing sweeping theologies from sparse data. While receptive to the **Bauer thesis** that orthodoxy and heresy are historically negotiated, he resists simplistic inversions of the canonical judgment by weighing **intertextual dependencies** and **redactional techniques**. The result is a disciplined account of **canon formation** as a historical process in which communities evaluated competing texts by **apostolicity**, **orthodoxy**, and **catholic usage**, all discernible through careful **textual criticism** and **source analysis**.

#### 5. The Gospel of Peter and passion tradition
Foster’s most focused contribution to apocryphal studies is **The Gospel of Peter: Introduction, Critical Edition and Commentary** 2010. Built on the **Akhmim** discovery of 1886 to 1887 and subsequent papyrological finds, this volume offers a **critical text**, **translation**, and sustained **commentary** on a passion-resurrection narrative that displays both literary artistry and theological polemic. Foster reconstructs the fragile text, catalogues **variant readings**, and proposes a **second-century** date, likely postdating and dependent upon **Matthew** and **Mark**, with some knowledge of **Luke**. He devotes particular attention to the memorable **walking and talking cross** episode and to a **resurrection** scene more spectacular than the canonical accounts.

As a core argument, Foster challenges **John Dominic Crossan’s** proposal of a pre-canonical **Cross Gospel** embedded in the Gospel of Peter and serving as a source for the canonical evangelists. Through **intertextual analysis**, he demonstrates Petrine dependence upon canonical narrative seams, vocabulary, and plot sequencing. He catalogs **anti-Jewish polemic** sharpened beyond the canonical textures and identifies **docetic** resonances that occasioned later ecclesial suspicion. The **commentary’s** strengths lie in its combination of **text-critical discipline**, **source criticism**, and historically-sensitive **theological description**. In dialogue with **Raymond E. Brown**, **Craig A. Evans**, and **J. K. Elliott**, Foster repositioned the Gospel of Peter from hypothetical source to derivative witness, thus mapping the **passion tradition** as a site of both fidelity and polemical innovation in the second century.

#### 6. Canon, Christology, and the economy of diversity
Across these studies, Foster traces the contours of **canon formation** and **Christological development** without collapsing either into a simple trajectory. The canonical Gospels articulate convergent **kyrios** Christology, with **Matthew** deploying **Emmanuel** motifs and **Son of Man** authority; the **Apostolic Fathers** such as **Ignatius** escalate **christological confession** in polemical settings; the **Gospel of Peter** intensifies **resurrection** spectacle and modulates **soteriological** emphases. Foster’s descriptive synthesis resists both a straight line to **Nicaea** and a cynical reduction of **high Christology** to late theological imposition. Here his work intersects with **Larry Hurtado’s** thesis on **early high Christology**, though Foster allows for greater **pluriformity** and for the diverse **Sitz im Leben** of communities to register in the textual surface.

Soteriologically, the comparison of **atonement motifs** across texts shows a move from **narrative proclamation** in the canonical Gospels to **martyrological** and **sacrificial** categories in early second-century literature. The **Apostolic Fathers** yield a richer picture of **ecclesial practice** with **baptism** and **eucharistia** configured as identity and boundary. Foster describes these developments without importing confessional teleology, thereby letting the documents display how **paradosis** is received, intensified, or redirected. The benefit for systematic theology is a more textured horizon of **doctrinal emergence**, stocked with concrete textual data and sensitive to **community formation**.

#### 7. Memory, orality, and the limits of criteria
Foster is attentive to methodological debates that have reshaped Gospel studies. He dialogues with **Richard Bauckham’s** **eyewitnesses thesis**, testing claims of controlled tradition against demonstrable **redactional** and **intertextual** phenomena. He appropriates **James D. G. Dunn’s** focus on **oral tradition** and the **Jesus memory**, but insists on the primacy of **written sources** and **editorial shaping** for the artifacts we possess. While not rejecting the traditional **criteria of authenticity**, he notes their limited probative value and the risk of atomizing **pericopae** detached from **narrative-theological** contexts. He invites instead a composite method where **source criticism**, **redaction analysis**, and **memory studies** coinhere, anchored in **textual criticism** and the production history of documents.

Linked to this is Foster’s interest in **Wirkungsgeschichte** or reception history. By placing canonical and apocryphal gospels alongside the **Apostolic Fathers**, he follows **tradition** into its early interpretations, liturgical uses, and polemical redeployments. This move does not surrender historical priority to later texts, but it does help identify the **hermeneutical horizon** of second-century readers for whom **Scripture** and **tradition** mutually informed identity. The practical upshot is a chastened confidence in reconstructing origins, joined to a robust account of how **theological discourse** crystallized in concrete communities.

#### 8. Pedagogy, editorial work, and scholarly posture
Beyond monographs and commentaries, Foster’s editorial work and **introductory syntheses** have shaped how students and scholars approach early Christian literature. By producing accessible yet academically rigorous overviews like **The Apocryphal Gospels: A Very Short Introduction**, he normalizes serious engagement with **non-canonical texts** without either sensationalism or apologetic fearfulness. His edited collections such as **The Non-Canonical Gospels** assemble voices across the field, stage debates over **dating**, **provenance**, and **dependence**, and model the discipline of arguing from **manuscript evidence**. This pedagogical contribution is methodological: it instructs readers in using **critical editions**, consulting **apparatus criticus**, and weighing **textual variants** as a basis for further theological interpretation.

This scholarly posture has public significance. It tempers cultural claims about lost Christianities by re-centering attention on what our texts actually say and how they came to be. It simultaneously disallows ecclesial complacency by exposing the **contested processes** of **canonization** and the breadth of early **Christological** and **ecclesiological** experimentation. The abiding feature of Foster’s contribution is not a grand theory but a durable habit: describe, compare, and locate, then step back to let the map of early Christian literature inform theological judgment.

#### 9. Integrated significance and Reformed evaluation
For systematic theology, the significance of **Paul Foster** lies in the disciplined re-description of origins that he enables. By clarifying the **Matthean redactional profile**, by editing the **Apostolic Fathers**, and by producing critical work on the **Gospel of Peter** and other **apocryphal gospels**, he offers a textured portrait of **early Christian diversity** that refuses both homogenization and fragmentation. This portrait sharpens doctrinal conversation by showing which **Christological** claims are already embedded in first-century proclamation and which **ecclesial** practices consolidate in the second century. It also refocuses debates about **canon**, locating the church’s recognition of Scripture within a recognizable history of **usage**, **apostolicity**, and **orthodoxy**, rather than in pure abstraction. The net effect is a thicker account of the soil from which classical dogma grew, with **textual criticism** and **source analysis** functioning as guardians against anachronism.

From a **Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary** vantage point, the gains are genuine yet bounded. The gain is a chastened, more historically-aware reading of Scripture and early tradition that can fortify **catholicity** and sober confidence in **providence** guiding the church through messy processes of **canon formation**. The boundary is methodological: Foster’s **historical-critical method** operates with methodological naturalism and bracketed **theological claims**, which tend to fragment **redemptive-historical unity** and pressure **inerrancy** by privileging **source and redactional hypotheses** over the canon’s final form. A classical Reformed alternative would welcome Foster’s **textual and historical clarifications** while insisting on **Scripture’s self-authenticating authority** and **organic inspiration** as the horizon for interpretation. It would appropriate his mapping of **apocryphal literature** as evidence of derivative and often polemical receptions, not as coequal witnesses, and would read the **Apostolic Fathers** as edifying but non-normative echoes of apostolic truth. The principial contrast concerns **revelation and history**: Foster’s reconstruction is history to theology, whereas a Reformed stance is revelation ordering history. The wisest course draws on his meticulous **philology** and **source criticism** to refine exegesis within a **covenantal canon** that supplies the norm for weighing all early Christian voices.
