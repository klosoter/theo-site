### TITLE
A cruciform, open, and warfare-shaped vision of God that re-reads Scripture and providence through the cross

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Late twentieth-century American evangelicalism**, Minnesota pastoral setting, and the **open theism controversy** in the 1990s and 2000s frame Boyd’s project.
- Method anchored in a **cruciform hermeneutic**, **narrative theology**, analytic engagement, and **Anabaptist nonviolence**, with an epistemic stance of **faith seeking understanding** that rejects certainty as an idol.
- Key works: **Trinity and Process** 1992, **Letters from a Skeptic** 1994, **God at War** 1997, **God of the Possible** 2000, **Satan and the Problem of Evil** 2001, **Is God to Blame** 2003, **The Myth of a Christian Nation** 2005, **The Jesus Legend** 2007, **Benefit of the Doubt** 2013, **The Crucifixion of the Warrior God** 2017, **Cross Vision** 2017, **Inspired Imperfection** 2020.
- Central terms: **open theism**, **dynamic omniscience**, **warfare worldview**, **cruciform hermeneutic**, **Christus Victor**, **divine accommodation**, **redemptive withdrawal**, **libertarian freedom**, **nonviolence**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Charles Hartshorne**, **Clark Pinnock**, **John Sanders**, **William Hasker**, **Thomas Oord**, critics like **John Piper**, **Bruce Ware**, **D. A. Carson**, patristic resources like **Origen** and **Athanasius**, cultural theorist **René Girard**.
- Reformed reception cue: debate over **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, **meticulous providence**, and **inerrancy**, with comparison to **Westminster Confession** and **analogia fidei**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and methodological posture
Gregory A. Boyd emerges within late twentieth-century **evangelicalism**, shaped by conversion from skepticism, graduate study at **Yale Divinity School** and **Princeton Theological Seminary**, and early teaching at **Bethel University** in Minnesota. In 1992 he co-founded **Woodland Hills Church** in St. Paul, a congregation that became a laboratory for his **Anabaptist** emphasis on **nonviolence** and **discipleship**. His ministry unfolds amid the **open theism** debates that gripped the Evangelical Theological Society in the late 1990s and early 2000s, as well as the culture-war moment following 2001 and 2004. Methodologically Boyd is intentionally **Christocentric** and **cruciform**, claiming that the **cross** is the supreme revelation of God and the hermeneutical key for all Scripture. Epistemically he advances what he later calls in **Benefit of the Doubt** a renunciation of coercive certainty, encouraging an honest faith that confronts ambiguity, while maintaining a robust commitment to **biblical inspiration** read through **divine accommodation** and narrative coherence.

Boyd’s theological idiom combines analytic clarity about **providence** and **freedom**, patristic instincts for **theologia crucis**, and pastoral energy drawn from **Anabaptist nonviolence**. He refuses **meticulous providence** and instead constructs a **warfare worldview** in which creation is contested by **angelic powers and principalities**, echoing **Irenaeus** and a modern **Christus Victor** model. This posture sets the stage for his signature advocacy of **dynamic omniscience** within **open theism**, his re-appropriation of **Origen’s** spiritual exegesis in service of a **cruciform hermeneutic**, and his critique of American civil religion in **The Myth of a Christian Nation**.

#### 2. Trinity against process, and the early constructive impulse
Boyd’s first major monograph, **Trinity and Process** 1992, directly engages **Charles Hartshorne’s** **process theism**. He criticizes process metaphysics for undermining **creatio ex nihilo**, divine freedom *ad intra*, and the sovereignty of **triune love** *ad extra*. While rejecting classical **impassibility** as indifference, he distances himself from process denials of **aseity** and **immutability** rightly understood, arguing for a relationally dynamic but ontologically stable **Trinity** whose perichoretic love grounds divine responsiveness without metaphysical dependence. The book’s constructive proposal is a social-trinitarian articulation of God’s loving relationality that anticipates his later **open theism** without capitulating to **process** metaphysical commitments.

This early work signals two abiding themes. First, his insistence that **God’s character is cruciform love**, which must govern metaphysics and exegesis. Second, his willingness to reconfigure long-standing debates by drawing fresh lines of contrast, as when he opposes **classical theism and process theism** alike at certain points and proposes a third way centered on **trinitarian personhood**. Already present is Boyd’s pattern of drawing on **patristic** insights, like **Athanasius** on divine condescension, alongside analytic distinctions about **foreknowledge and freedom** that will shape his mature **warfare theodicy**.

#### 3. The warfare worldview and the problem of evil
With **God at War** 1997 Boyd advances his signature **warfare worldview**. He contends that both Scripture and experience portray a cosmos in which **Satan** and **principalities** oppose God’s purposes, and that much evil arises from creaturely agency rather than divine decree. This model reframes theodicy: the question is not why a meticulously controlling God ordains atrocities, but how a sovereign yet risk-taking God wisely governs a world where **libertarian freedom** among humans and angels is real. The narrative sweep of **Old and New Testaments** is read as a protracted holy war, culminating in **Christus Victor** at the cross, where God’s power is revealed as self-sacrificial love rather than coercion.

The follow-up, **Satan and the Problem of Evil** 2001, extends this into a formal **trinitarian warfare theodicy**. Boyd insists that **omnipotence** does not entail causal determination of all events, and that **omniscience** does not require that future free acts be eternally fixed as definite truths. He develops the notion of **divine wisdom** guiding providence through flexible strategies, resourceful adaptation, and **redemptive withdrawal**, a judgment motif in which God at times withdraws protective presence and allows agents to reap consequences. The cross, enacted in the face of demonic and human resistance, displays God’s wise victory through suffering love, thereby clarifying the character of divine governance across redemptive history.

#### 4. Open theism and dynamic omniscience
Boyd’s most publicized contribution is to **open theism**, popularized in **God of the Possible** 2000 and defended exegetically and philosophically across articles and debates. He argues for **dynamic omniscience**: God knows all reality as it is, including the future insofar as it is partly open, consisting of branching possibilities grounded in **libertarian free agency**. Prophetic specificity is explained through **conditional prophecies**, **divine intentions** that are certain because they depend solely on God’s will, and providential steering without exhaustive foredetermination. Scriptural depictions of God deliberating, grieving, and changing course are interpreted not as anthropomorphic mere appearances, but as bona fide indicators of God’s relational life within time while remaining the eternal triune Lord.

In this framework **providence** is purposive but not meticulous. God’s sovereignty is understood as the unrivaled capacity to achieve eschatological goals despite creaturely contingencies, a mastery of history through love rather than a **decretal blueprint**. Boyd differentiates his view from **Molinism**, rejecting **middle knowledge** as metaphysically problematic and biblically thin, and from **process theism**, rejecting any denial of God’s ability to unilaterally act or to raise the dead. The hallmark claim is that **exhaustive definite foreknowledge** of libertarianly free acts is incoherent, and that biblical revelation commends a living God who takes real risk for love.

#### 5. Cruciform hermeneutics and the violent divine portraits
The capstone of Boyd’s hermeneutical project is **The Crucifixion of the Warrior God** 2017, with its popular digest **Cross Vision** 2017. Here he proposes a **cruciform hermeneutic** that treats the **cross** as the definitive disclosure of God’s character. All Scripture bears witness to Christ, but many **Old Testament** portraits depict God commanding or performing violence. Boyd’s answer is a multilayered account of **divine accommodation** in which God stoops to bear the sin of his people and allows his character to be negatively mirrored in the violent images they project. Drawing on **Origen’s** spiritual exegesis and a theology of the cross grounded in **self-emptying love**, he contends these texts function as what he calls literary crucifixes, distancing devices that reveal God by hiding him beneath the mask of enemy-loving condescension.

To operationalize this he articulates several **cruciform principles**. The **principle of cruciform accommodation** describes God’s willingness to be portrayed in ways that reflect human fallenness as part of covenantal solidarity. The **principle of redemptive withdrawal** interprets many judgments as God withdrawing protection, allowing destructive agents to act. The **cosmic conflict principle** credits **Satanic** and demonic agencies with destructive power that Scripture often attributes to God by **Semitic idiom of agency**, a linguistic convention that ascribes ultimate responsibility to God as Lord. The end effect is a hermeneutic in which **nonviolence** and **enemy-love** are not one ethical strand among others but the controlling revelation of who God is and how Scripture must be read.

#### 6. Public theology, ecclesial practice, and epistemic humility
Boyd’s polemic against American civil religion reaches a wide audience with **The Myth of a Christian Nation** 2005. He contrasts the **power-over** logic of empire with the **power-under** love of the kingdom, arguing that the church must renounce coercive politics for cruciform witness. This **Anabaptist** ethical vision coheres with his **Christus Victor** soteriology and his **warfare worldview**, where the church’s armor is truth, righteousness, and prayer rather than the sword. The pastoral shape of his work is also evident in **Repenting of Religion** 2004 and **Present Perfect** 2010, which press toward spiritual formation in **enemy-love** and practicing the presence of God.

In **Benefit of the Doubt** 2013 he proposes an epistemic posture that distinguishes **covenantal trust** from Cartesian certainty. Faith clings to the crucified Christ, not to a putatively error-proof web of propositions. That stance undergirds his later **Inspired Imperfection** 2020, where he argues that the Bible’s human limitations, including internal tensions and ancient cosmology, are part of its **God-breathed** character precisely as accommodated revelation. Here Boyd explicitly challenges **Chicago-style inerrancy**, preferring **trustworthy infallibility** indexed to God’s saving intent rather than to modern precision. The same cruciform logic governs his view of Scripture’s form and function.

#### 7. Apologetics and historical Jesus work
With **Letters from a Skeptic** 1994, coauthored with his father Edward Boyd, he commends a conversational apologetic that wrestles with **evil**, **hell**, **biblical reliability**, and the uniqueness of **Jesus**. Later, together with **Paul Eddy**, he authors **The Jesus Legend** 2007 and its abridgement **Lord or Legend** 2007, contesting **mythicist** claims and defending the historical credibility of the **Synoptic** tradition. The arguments draw on **Second Temple Judaism**, criteria of authenticity, and narrative coherence, situating Jesus within the Jewish apocalyptic and messianic horizon while maintaining the canonical portrait of the crucified and risen Lord. This apologetic corpus integrates with his **cruciform hermeneutic** by aiming to show that the Jesus of history and the Christ of faith converge in the same enemy-loving revelation of God.

Boyd’s credibility among lay evangelicals has been enhanced by this accessible apologetic work, even as his **open theism** and **cruciform** readings have prompted controversy. The 2002 to 2003 debates at **Bethel University** and within the **Evangelical Theological Society**, where efforts to expel **Clark Pinnock** and **John Sanders** for alleged violations of inerrancy failed, formed the context of his contested standing. Public exchanges with **John Piper**, **Bruce Ware**, **Norman Geisler**, and **D. A. Carson** sharpened fault lines over **providence**, **foreknowledge**, and **hermeneutics**. Through all this Boyd maintained a pastoral accent and a scholarly tone, advancing arguments that bind **theology**, **exegesis**, and **spiritual formation** into one cruciform whole.

#### 8. Doctrinal synthesis and ongoing influence
Taken together, Boyd’s oeuvre advances a cohesive vision of **God**, **Scripture**, and **salvation** centered on cruciform love. Metaphysically he holds **open theism** with **dynamic omniscience** and **libertarian freedom**, rejecting both **determinism** and **process necessity**. Hamartiologically he highlights **cosmic conflict**, ascribes significant causal agency to demonic powers, and reads human sin within an apocalyptic drama culminating in **Christus Victor**. Soteriologically he emphasizes substitutionary dimensions without centering **penal substitution**, preferring a multi-faceted atonement whose organizing frame is the victory and revelation of cruciform love. Ecclesiologically he champions **Anabaptist nonviolence** and the separation of the church’s cruciform witness from the coercive tools of the state.

Hermeneutically, Boyd’s **cruciform hermeneutic** is both a theological claim about the canon’s center and a methodological claim about reading difficult texts. The twin notions of **divine accommodation** and **redemptive withdrawal**, combined with the **Semitic idiom of agency**, yield a re-reading of violent portraits that safeguards his conviction that God is eternally and unchangeably enemy-loving. Theologically he aligns with **Irenaean** themes of maturation and warfare, with **Origenist** spiritual interpretation, and with modern emphases on narrative and mission. His influence persists in ongoing debates about **open theism** and in a younger evangelical Anabaptist cohort that embraces **nonviolence** and **cruciform** biblical interpretation.

In a Reformed and Westminster-like evaluation, Boyd’s project exhibits both admirable Christocentric focus and serious doctrinal tensions. Positively, his insistence that the **cross** is the climactic revelation, his protest against civil religion, and his pastoral attention to the **problem of evil** commend a doxological seriousness that many **Reformed** theologians share, especially where **Christus Victor** is integrated with substitution. Yet his **open theism** conflicts with **Westminster Confession** teaching on **divine decree**, **immutability**, and **providence**, where God ordains whatsoever comes to pass while remaining pure and not the author of sin, and where **compatibilist freedom** safeguards responsibility without collapsing into libertarian indeterminacy. His **cruciform hermeneutic** risks a canon within the canon that subordinates **Scripture’s self-attesting authority** to a prior construct of divine nonviolence, diverging from **analogia fidei** and the organic unity of **Old and New Testaments** in covenant. The account of **inspiration** in **Inspired Imperfection** departs from **confessional inerrancy**, where accommodation concerns mode not truth value. A Reformed alternative would preserve the centrality of the **cross** within a comprehensive **covenant theology**, affirm **divine simplicity** and **immutability** while stressing God’s *ad extra* condescension, parse judgment texts through progressive revelation and typology without reassigning agency away from God’s holy justice, and reconcile **meticulous providence** with creaturely freedom through **concursus**, **secondary causality**, and a duplex account of will, decretive and preceptive. In short, Boyd’s passion for cruciform love is a needed pole in evangelical theology, but his metaphysic and hermeneutic, judged by Reformed standards, overcorrect and obscure the full biblical witness to the Holy One who is simple, wise, sovereign, and steadfast in mercy and justice.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Contemporary American **evangelicalism**, Minnesota pastorate, controversies over **open theism** and **inerrancy** in the 1990s and 2000s.
- **Core problem**: Reconciling **God’s cruciform love** with **evil**, violence in Scripture, and the dynamics of **providence** and **freedom**.
- **Major works**: **Trinity and Process** 1992 against **process theism**, **God at War** 1997 and **Satan and the Problem of Evil** 2001 on the **warfare worldview**, **God of the Possible** 2000 on **dynamic omniscience**, **The Myth of a Christian Nation** 2005 on **nonviolence**, **The Crucifixion of the Warrior God** 2017 on a **cruciform hermeneutic**, plus apologetics like **The Jesus Legend** 2007.
- **Distinctive positions**: **Open theism**, **libertarian freedom**, **cruciform hermeneutic** with **divine accommodation** and **redemptive withdrawal**, **Christus Victor**, **Anabaptist nonviolence**.
- **Defining tools**: Appeal to **Semitic agency idiom**, emphasis on **cosmic conflict**, use of **Origenist** exegesis, focus on **cross** as hermeneutical rule.
- **Interlocutors**: Dialogue with **Pinnock**, **Sanders**, **Hasker**, critique from **Piper**, **Ware**, **Carson**, early engagement with **Hartshorne**, retrieval from **Origen** and **Athanasius**.
- **Reformed critique**: Tensions with **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, **meticulous providence**, and **confessional inerrancy**, with an alternative in **covenant theology**, **concursus**, and **analogia fidei**.
- **Legacy**: Durable influence on debates over **open theism** and **nonviolence**, a robust **warfare worldview**, and a provocative **cruciform** reading of Scripture that continues to shape evangelical conversations.
