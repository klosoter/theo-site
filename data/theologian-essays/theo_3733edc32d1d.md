### TITLE
Reading Matthew as Israel’s Scriptures Fulfilled: Evangelical exegesis that weds historical rigor, narrative theology, and already-not-yet kingdom eschatology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Contemporary evangelical context** shaped by postwar biblical theology, the **Second Temple Judaism renaissance**, and the **Gospels renaissance** in English-language scholarship.
- **Integrative method**: **historical-grammatical exegesis** enriched by **narrative criticism**, **intertextual fulfillment**, and a chastened use of **redaction criticism** under **evangelical inerrancy**.
- **Key works**: **Matthew, BECNT** 2008; **Cornerstone Biblical Commentary: Matthew** 2005; **Israel’s Last Prophet: Jesus and the Jewish Leaders in Matthew 23** 2015; **Interpreting the Gospels and Acts: An Exegetical Handbook** 2019.
- **Central categories**: **already-not-yet kingdom**, **Matthean Christology** as **Emmanuel–Son of David–Son of Man**, **eschatological Torah**, **covenant lawsuit**, **church as discipled Israel**, **mission to the nations**.
- **Interlocutors and influences**: **R. T. France**, **D. A. Carson**, **Craig Blomberg**, **Ulrich Luz**, **Davies and Allison**, **Darrell L. Bock**, **George Eldon Ladd**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: Strong resonance with **redemptive-historical exegesis** and **Vos–Ridderbos** on the **kingdom**, with debate over **Israel–church continuity** and the scope of **typological fulfillment**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
David L. Turner is a contemporary American evangelical New Testament scholar whose career has been devoted to the **Gospel of Matthew**, the **hermeneutics of the Gospels**, and the **kingdom of God** as an integrative theological motif. Working in the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries amid the renewal of **Second Temple Judaism** studies and the rise of **narrative criticism**, Turner crafted an exegetical posture that deeply engages historical context while insisting on **canonical unity** and **evangelical inerrancy**. His classroom and public scholarship, especially through **Grand Rapids Theological Seminary**, formed pastors and scholars who read the Gospels as **theological historiography**. He stands in critical but appreciative dialogue with **redaction criticism**, appropriating its attention to **Matthean distinctives** without conceding to skeptical **form-critical** dissolutions of the Gospel tradition. In this, he shares with **George Eldon Ladd** and **Craig Blomberg** a chastened historical realism and an **already-not-yet kingdom** framework, while echoing **R. T. France** in his careful attention to **Old Testament fulfillment** in the Matthean narrative.

Turner’s method is integrative. He moves along three coordinates: the **historical-grammatical method** attentive to *Sitz im Leben* and the lexicon of the Greek text; **narrative criticism** that tracks character, plot, and discourse structure across Matthew’s five-discourse architecture; and **intertextual exegesis** of Matthew’s **fulfillment formulae** as typological rereading of **Israel’s Scriptures**. What results is an evangelical hermeneutic where *pleroun* or fulfillment does not reduce to proof-texting but names a **typological escalation** that centers on Jesus as **Emmanuel** and **Son of David**, enacting the **new exodus**. Turner’s work also displays pastoral intent: the Gospels are not only historical witnesses but **catechetical texts** that shape a **discipleship community** under the **Great Commission**. His commitments yield a disciplined openness to insights from **Ulrich Luz** and **Davies and Allison** on Matthew’s literary theology, without yielding the claim that Matthew provides a **trustworthy historical portrait** of Jesus.

#### 2. Matthew as theological historiography: structure and Christology
Turner’s major work, **Matthew in the Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament** 2008, presents the Gospel as **theological historiography** that narrates Jesus as the **climactic embodiment of Israel’s story**. He highlights Matthew’s architecture of five major discourses which frame Jesus as a **new Moses** whose words and deeds constitute **eschatological Torah** for the **ekklesia**. Turner’s treatment of **Matthean Christology** unfolds in a set of titles and narrative arcs: **Emmanuel** in 1:23 signals the **presence of God** among his people; **Son of David** anchors messianic royal identity; **Son of Man** positions Jesus within **Danielic apocalyptic** and the **judicial authority** to forgive sins and rule. The infancy narrative’s fulfillment citations introduce a **typological reading** where **Egypt, exodus, wilderness, and land** culminate in Jesus’ faithful recapitulation of Israel. In Turner's reading, Matthew’s theology is not imposed upon the historical Jesus but derived from a **witness-shaped memory** that faithfully transmits Jesus’ self-understanding and vocation.

Turner consistently parses Matthew’s use of the Old Testament with a **canonical sensibility** that moves beyond atomistic cross-references. When Matthew cites Hosea 11:1 in 2:15, Turner reads **out of Egypt I called my son** as typology rather than prediction, the **corporate son** Israel foreshadowing the **unique Son** who embodies and completes Israel’s vocation. This typological method recurs with **Isaiah’s Servant** and **Zechariah’s humble king**. The cumulative case yields a **Matthean Christology** that is high, even if not stated via later **Nicene** idiom, with Jesus exercising **divine prerogatives**, receiving **worship**, and mediating **eschatological judgment**. The Christological center grounds Matthew’s teaching on the **kingdom**, **discipleship**, and **mission**, themes that Turner integrates rather than isolates.

#### 3. The Sermon on the Mount and eschatological ethics
On the **Sermon on the Mount**, Turner’s exegesis foregrounds **eschatological righteousness** as the telos of **Torah** within the **already-not-yet kingdom**. Matthew 5:17–20 is read as Jesus’ assertion that he has come not to abolish but to fulfill the Law and the Prophets, where fulfillment means both the **completion** and the **eschatological embodiment** of God’s will in and through the Messiah. The **antitheses** are not antinomian replacements but **intensifications** and **internalizations**, relocating the locus of obedience to the **heart** under the kingdom’s inbreaking power. Turner ties *dikaiosyne* or **righteousness** in 5:6, 5:20, and 6:33 to a whole-life alignment with the **Father’s perfection** 5:48, not as Pelagian achievement but as **discipleship shaped by grace** in the presence of **Emmanuel**.

Turner’s sermon exegesis resists both **legalistic moralism** and **cheap grace**, instead presenting a **virtue-ethic of the kingdom** rooted in the **Beatitudes**. The **Lord’s Prayer** becomes a microcosm of kingdom piety orienting the church toward God’s **name, kingdom, and will**, while the imperatives about **almsgiving, prayer, and fasting** reframe *halakhah* for a community under eschatological promise. Central to his reading is the **eschatological horizon** of judgment and reward, a theme that binds the Sermon to the **Olivet Discourse** and to **Matthew 25**’s parables of accountability. Turner’s pastoral angle surfaces in his commentary’s move from text to **homiletical traction** without flattening the Gospel’s apocalyptic tension between **present discipleship** and future **consummation**.

#### 4. Kingdom parables, apocalyptic discourse, and the dual horizon
Turner’s treatment of the **kingdom parables** in Matthew 13 translates **already-not-yet eschatology** into narrative pedagogy. The **sower**, **weeds**, **mustard seed**, and **leaven** articulate the paradox of **present hiddenness and future manifestation**, relativizing triumphalism and despair alike. Turner appropriates **George Eldon Ladd’s** model that holds together **inauguration** and **consummation**, a framework he then deploys in reading the **Olivet Discourse** 24–25. There he argues for a **dual-horizon** exegesis: certain elements bear directly on the **fall of Jerusalem in AD 70**, while others reach to the **final parousia**. The exhortations to watchfulness, readiness, and faithful stewardship are thus pressed upon the church across the age, refusing **date-setting** while intensifying **ethical vigilance**.

Against both preterist reduction and futurist displacement, Turner maintains the **tensive realism** of apocalyptic speech. He tracks intertexts with **Daniel**, **Zechariah**, and **Isaiah**, arguing that Matthew’s Jesus stands as **Israel’s eschatological prophet** who both forecasts near judgment and promises **cosmic restoration**. The authority claim of 28:18–20 binds these themes together: the **risen Son of Man** now bears all authority, and the church’s mission flows from the **eschatological victory** already secured, even as the **end of the age** remains future. This eschatology is not merely charted; it is embedded in how the **ekklesia** prays, forgives, disciplines, and bears witness.

#### 5. Ecclesiology in Matthew: Peter, the keys, and the discipled nations
Turner’s ecclesiological reading of **Matthew 16** and **18** resists both **Roman primacy** and **anti-institutionalism**. Peter is the **representative confessor** whose faith and role are foundational, yet the **keys of the kingdom** and **binding and loosing** are extended to the **community** in chapter 18. Turner interprets the keys via **rabbinic halakhic authority**, now transposed into **apostolic teaching** that opens and guards access to the **kingdom** through the gospel. The church is thus visible and disciplinable, entrusted with the hard work of **reconciliation** and **forgiveness** 18:15–35 under the pattern of the **merciful king**. The **Great Commission** 28:18–20 becomes the capstone of Matthew’s ecclesiology: **disciple the nations**, baptizing into the **Triune Name**, and teaching Jesus’ **commands**, confident in **Emmanuel’s presence** until the **consummation**.

Turner situates Matthew’s **ekklesia** as **renewed Israel**, neither an abrogation of Israel nor a mere sociological sect. The **missionary universalism** of the closing scene is the proper outcome of the **Abrahamic promise**, read through **Davidic kingship** and **Isaianic servant** motifs. This stance positions him as a bridge figure between classical **covenant theology** and **progressive dispensationalism**: there is real continuity of promise and people, yet also discernible progress and **newness** with the **inaugurated kingdom** and the **apostolic community**. The **sacramental note** of baptism in the Triune Name receives brief but serious theological attention, not as a developed sacramentology but as a sign that Matthew’s narrative is already **trinitarianly potent**.

#### 6. Israel’s Last Prophet: covenant lawsuit and Jewish-Christian relations
In **Israel’s Last Prophet: Jesus and the Jewish Leaders in Matthew 23** 2015, Turner focuses exegetically and theologically on the **seven woes** as a **covenant lawsuit**. Drawing on the prophetic *rib* tradition, he reads Jesus as **Israel’s final prophet** who pronounces judgment upon **scribes and Pharisees** not as anti-Jewish invective but as **covenantal summons**. The woes are rhetorical strategy in a context of **intra-Jewish dispute**, clarifying the stakes of **hypocrisy**, **misguided halakhah**, and **spiritual blindness**. Turner pays careful attention to terms like *hodegountes typhloi* blind guides and *krisis* judgment, tying them to Matthew’s broader depiction of **misrecognition** and **rejection**. The lament over Jerusalem frames judgment within **divine compassion**, positioning Jesus as both **judge** and **gathering shepherd**.

Turner leverages this focus to address the fraught issue of **supersessionism**. He warns against an **anti-Judaic reading** of Matthew while recognizing the Gospel’s testimony that leadership failure precipitates **covenant crisis**. In his wider Matthean theology, the **church** is not a gentile replacement but a **messianic remnant** expanded to the nations. This allows affirmation of **continuity in promise** and room for an eschatological hope consonant with **Romans 11**, even if Matthew does not foreground it. Theologically, the **covenant lawsuit** theme safeguards **divine faithfulness** and **moral seriousness**, while keeping Christ’s **prophetic office** in a central, pastorally incisive position.

#### 7. Interpreting the Gospels and Acts: hermeneutical synthesis for the church
Turner’s **Interpreting the Gospels and Acts: An Exegetical Handbook** 2019 crystalizes his methodological commitments in service of pastors and students. He treats the **Gospels** as **ancient biography** with theological aims, urging close attention to **genre**, **intertextual echoes**, and **narrative coherence**. The handbook gives practical guidance for moving from **text to sermon**, integrating **historical background**, **literary structure**, and **theological claims** without collapsing exegesis into homiletics. Turner grants careful space to **textual criticism**, **synoptic relationships**, and the role of **tradition history**, while firmly urging readers to resist any method that severs the **Jesus of history** from the **Christ of faith**.

Complementing this are his earlier works, including **Cornerstone Biblical Commentary: Matthew** 2005, which distills scholarly insights for church use. Across these resources, Turner models **evangelical critical realism**: confidence that the canonical Gospels tell true history, combined with literacy in **Greco-Roman biography**, **Jewish backgrounds**, and **modern literary method**. Theologically, he keeps the **kingdom of God**, **Christology**, and **discipleship** central, arguing that the **Gospel genre** uniquely coordinates doctrine and story, and that **theological interpretation** is not optional but the proper end of **grammatical-historical work**.

#### 8. Doctrinal threads: Scripture, Christ, kingdom, mission
Though a New Testament exegete, Turner’s work presses doctrinal claims of interest to **systematic theology**. On **Scripture**, he embodies an **evangelical inerrancy** that is text-sensitive, genre-aware, and opposed to reductionist harmonization. Matthew’s **fulfillment** is a case study in **canonical sensus plenior** rightly ordered by authorial intention and **redemptive-historical typology**. On **Christology**, his reading shows Jesus exercising **divine prerogatives**, receiving **proskynesis** worship, and enacting functions of **YHWH** such as forgiving sins and subduing the storm, all within Matthew’s narrative frame. On **kingdom**, Turner aligns with **inaugurated eschatology**, where the **messianic reign** is present in veiled form and awaits **consummation** at the **parousia**.

Ecclesiologically, Turner’s **Matthew 16–18–28** triad yields a church that is **confessional**, **disciplined**, and **missional**, ordered by **apostolic teaching** and animated by **Emmanuel’s presence**. Ethically, the **Sermon on the Mount** functions for him as an **eschatological moral catechism**, a third-use inflection of the **law** that forms kingdom virtues rather than a program of national legislation. Missiologically, the **Great Commission** binds **Trinitarian baptism** to **teaching obedience**, resisting both bare decisionism and mere activism. In all this, Turner’s rhetoric and footnotes reveal constant dialogue with **France**, **Carson**, **Blomberg**, **Luz**, and **Davies and Allison**, positioning his voice among the leading interpreters of Matthew in the period 1980–2020.

#### 9. Engagements and distinctives within evangelical scholarship
Turner’s distinctives appear most clearly where he negotiates scholarly fault lines. Against tendencies to sever **history** from **theology**, he insists Matthew is both **reliable testimony** and **theological proclamation**. Against **supersessionist** or **replacement** readings that flatten the Old Testament, he emphasizes **typological fulfillment** that upholds **continuity and escalation**. Against readings of the **Sermon on the Mount** as purely interim ethics, he contends for **present discipleship ethics** shaped by **eschatological grace**. In **Olivet** exegesis he refuses both exhaustive preterism and unmoored futurism, maintaining a **dual horizon** and the necessity of **watchful fidelity**.

Turner’s pragmatic pastoral posture also marks his work. His **BECNT Matthew** and **Exegetical Handbook** continually move from **text-critical and grammatical analysis** to **theological synthesis** and **homiletical application**, modeling a kind of **theological interpretation of Scripture** that is still credibly **historical**. If there is a confessional inflection, it is that of broad **evangelical orthodoxy** rather than denominational distinctives. He shares intuitions with **progressive dispensationalism** on **kingdom inauguration** and a measure of **Israel–church distinction**, though he often frames these matters with **Vos–Ridderbos-like** redemptive-historical vocabulary that sits comfortably within **Reformed catholicity**.

#### 10. Reformed and Westminster evaluation
From a **Reformed/WTS** vantage, Turner’s contributions are strikingly consonant with **redemptive-historical exegesis** in the line of **Geerhardus Vos** and **Herman Ridderbos**. His deployment of the **already-not-yet kingdom**, **typological fulfillment**, and **covenant lawsuit** matches well with a **biblical-theological** reading that sees Christ as the **telos legis** and the **eschatological Adam–Israel**. His handling of the **Sermon on the Mount** readily admits a **third use of the law** within a grace-driven discipleship, and his ecclesial focus on **confession, discipline, and mission** resonates with **Reformed ecclesiology** oriented by **Word and sacrament**. Yet two tensions invite discussion. First, his sympathetic proximity to **progressive dispensationalism** can leave the **Israel–church relation** more bifurcated than classical **covenant theology** would allow; a **covenantal unity of the people of God** with one **Abrahamic promise** fulfilled in Christ provides a tighter frame for Matthew’s **people-of-God** theme. Second, while his **dual-horizon** reading of **Olivet** is salutary, a more explicit appeal to **covenant sanctions** and **intrusion ethics** in **Meredith Kline** could better integrate AD 70 judgment within the **typological economy** of the old covenant’s exhaustion in Christ. These are refinements rather than rejections. On the whole, Turner’s Matthew is a rich ally for **confessional Reformed** preaching and theology: Scripture read as **unfolding covenant history** centered on Christ, the **kingdom present in power**, and the **church sent to the nations** under the **Triune Name**.
