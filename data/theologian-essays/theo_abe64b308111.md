### TITLE
Gospel-centered evangelical theology, a canonical and creedal dogmatics organized by the euangelion of the crucified and risen Lord

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Australian evangelical context**, post-British evangelicalism and global **New Testament studies**, Ridley College in Melbourne and collaboration with **N. T. Wright**, late twentieth to early twenty-first century.
- **Method of gospel-centered dogmatics**, canonical and creedal, deploying **historical-critical tools** within a confessional **evangelical** stance that privileges the **rule of faith** and *kerygma*.
- **Key works**: **The Saving Righteousness of God** 2007, **Introducing Paul** 2008, **Evangelical Theology** 2013 and 2020, **The Gospel of the Lord** 2014, **How God Became Jesus** 2014, **Romans, Story of God Commentary** 2016, **An Anomalous Jew** 2016, **What Christians Ought to Believe** 2016, **Jesus the Eternal Son** 2017, **The New Testament in Its World** with **N. T. Wright** 2019, **Seven Things I Wish Christians Knew about the Bible** 2021.
- **Central categories**: **euangelion as organizing principle**, **union with Christ**, **justification**, **participatory and forensic soteriology**, **early high Christology**, **canon and creed**, **missional ecclesiology**.
- **Main interlocutors**: **N. T. Wright**, **James D. G. Dunn**, **Richard Bauckham**, **Tom Schreiner**, **John Piper**, **Bart D. Ehrman**, **Kevin J. Vanhoozer**, with resonance to **John Stott** and the broader **Great Tradition**.
- **Reformed reception cue**: appreciative of **Nicene orthodoxy**, **penal substitution within a kaleidoscope of atonement motifs**, and **union with Christ**, yet debated on **imputation**, **inerrancy formulation**, **women in ministry**, and nuances of **covenant theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation and context
Michael F. Bird, born 1974, emerged from the distinctive milieu of **Australian evangelicalism** that mediates British conservatism and global critical scholarship. His early academic trajectory in **New Testament studies** was shaped in Brisbane and Scotland, then consolidated at **Ridley College, Melbourne**, where he has taught since the 2010s and helped foster a confessional yet critically alert culture. Bird’s tenure at **Highland Theological College** in the Scottish Highlands widened his encounter with **Reformed** and **Anglican** traditions, while his public engagement with figures like **Bart D. Ehrman** located him within the broader apologetic and historical Jesus debates. The resulting profile is that of a pastorally oriented scholar who speaks fluent **biblical theology**, draws judiciously on **historical-critical** tools, and writes for the church with the tone of an educator of catechumens under the **Apostles’ Creed**.

Bird’s context is a late modern evangelicalism grappling with **post-Christendom** realities, contested public space, and the legacy of the **New Perspective on Paul**. He navigates the tensions between **confessional orthodoxy** and **academic criticism** by prioritizing what he calls the **gospel as organizing principle**, a judicious retrieval of the **regula fidei**, and a broadly **Nicene and Chalcedonian** dogmatic core. The result is an evangelical voice that seeks catholicity without erasing Protestant distinctives, that maintains **scriptural authority** while narrating the Bible’s story in dialogue with **Second Temple Judaism** and **Greco-Roman** contexts.

#### 2. Method, stance, and the gospel principle
Bird’s most distinctive methodological thesis appears in **Evangelical Theology, A Biblical and Systematic Introduction**, first edition 2013, revised 2020. There he argues that the whole of dogmatics should be architected by the **euangelion**, defined by 1 Corinthians 15 and Romans 1 as the royal announcement that **Jesus the Messiah** has died for sins, is raised, and reigns as **Lord**. Rather than beginning with a neutral **prolegomenon**, or a philosophical **theism**, Bird starts with the **kerygma** and the **rule of faith**, which together shape Christian knowing. This gives his method a canonically grounded and narratival frame, attentive to the economy of **Father, Son, and Spirit** and to the story of **Israel and the nations**.

Epistemically, Bird blends **mere evangelical** confidence in revelation with a chastened use of **historical-critical** exegesis. He refuses **positivist inerrantism** yet affirms **inspiration** and **infallibility** with robust historical reliability. He places the **creeds** as hermeneutical aids, not authorities above Scripture, and he adopts an **analogia fidei** hermeneutic that reads texts within the whole canonical witness. His tone stands between **Barthian christocentrism** and **Reformed scholastic** prolegomena, locating normativity in the **gospel’s apostolic deposit** rather than in a metaphysical system or a modern evidentialist grid.

#### 3. Major works and their theses
Bird’s corpus exhibits range and coherence. **The Saving Righteousness of God**, 2007, presents an integrative account of **justification** that is both **forensic** and **participatory**, arguing that **dikaiosyne theou** entails God’s covenant faithfulness and saving action that incorporates believers into **Christ**. **Introducing Paul**, 2008, orients students to **Pauline mission**, **eschatology**, and **church formation** with union with **Christ** as a pervasive motif. **The Gospel of the Lord**, 2014, examines the **formation of the Gospels**, arguing for early **traditioning**, stable *Jesus* memory, and the communal authority of the evangelists as **pastor-theologians** rather than anonymous compilers.

With **N. T. Wright**, Bird coauthored **The New Testament in Its World**, 2019, a single-volume synthesis that frames **Second Temple Judaism**, the **historical Jesus**, **Pauline theology**, and the **canonical sweep** for pastors and students. **What Christians Ought to Believe**, 2016, catechizes doctrine via the **Apostles’ Creed**, an exercise in ressourcement that shows Bird’s retrieval of the **Great Tradition**. **Jesus the Eternal Son**, 2017, counters adoptionist reconstructions by defending **early high Christology** grounded in **divine identity** claims and resurrection exaltation. His **Romans** volume in the **Story of God Commentary**, 2016, presents a pastoral and narrative reading that integrates **justification**, **union**, and **new creation** themes. Finally, **Seven Things I Wish Christians Knew about the Bible**, 2021, articulates a sane doctrine of **Scripture** for lay readers, steering between **fundamentalism** and **skepticism**.

#### 4. Scripture, canon, and authority
Bird’s doctrine of Scripture is classical at its core and chastened in its articulation. He affirms **inspiration**, **divine concurrence**, and **canonical authority**, while resisting reduction of truth to rigid **inerrancy formulations** detached from genre and ancient rhetoric. In **Seven Things I Wish Christians Knew about the Bible** he urges the church to own both the Bible’s **divine authorship** and its richly human mediation, to receive the **canon** as providentially recognized by the church, and to read the Bible through **creedal lenses** that summarize the **rule of faith**. The canon functions as the Spirit-formed horizon within which **Christ** is known and the **gospel** norm is discerned.

Bird positions **tradition** as a ministerial authority, not magisterial, turning frequently to **Nicene** and **Chalcedonian** consensus as guardrails for interpretation. His approach to **hermeneutics** is integrative, drawing on **Richard Bauckham’s** work on **testimony**, the **Gospels** as historically rooted yet theologically interpreted witnesses, and on **Kevin Vanhoozer’s** emphasis on the dramatic performance of the **canonical script**. The aim is an **evangelical rationality** that is doxological, ecclesial, and missionally oriented rather than a bare academic historicism.

#### 5. God, Trinity, and the shape of dogmatics
Within the gospel-organized dogmatics of **Evangelical Theology**, Bird confesses **classical theism** with **Nicene Trinitarianism**, affirming **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** in their classical senses, while emphasizing the missions of the **Son** and **Spirit** in salvation history. The doctrine of **God** is not constructed from abstract attributes but from the revelation of the **Father** through the **Son** in the **Spirit**, which then warrants and clarifies talk of attributes. Bird’s prose underscores the distinction between *ad intra* processions and *ad extra* missions, preserving **creature Creator distinction** and the gratuity of grace.

The Trinity’s economic unveiling grounds a robust **Christology** and **pneumatology** for the church’s confession and life. Bird insists that **worship** and **prayer** are doctrinal locations, not merely applications, such that the lex orandi confirms the lex credendi. Here his use of the **Apostles’ Creed** and **Niceno-Constantinopolitan Creed** functions pedagogically to form evangelical piety in a catholic pattern, a strategy evident also in **What Christians Ought to Believe** where doctrinal exposition tracks the creed’s rhythm from **creation** to **resurrection of the body**.

#### 6. Christology, incarnation, and atonement
Bird’s Christology reflects the best of **early high Christology** scholarship. In **Jesus the Eternal Son** he rejects modern adoptionist proposals and argues that the earliest Christian confession integrated **preexistence**, **incarnation**, **resurrection exaltation**, and **divine identity** into a single mosaic. Following **Richard Bauckham**, he reads New Testament claims about **Kyrios** as inclusion of Jesus in the unique divine sovereignty and worship due to **YHWH**. The virgin birth, true humanity, and true deity are treated as confessional non-negotiables that guard the **homoousios** and uphold the integrity of **Chalcedon**.

On the cross, Bird presents a **multi-faceted atonement**. He affirms **penal substitution** as a biblically warranted dimension of the **atonement**, especially within **Romans** and **Isaiah’s Servant** texts, while also integrating **victory over the powers**, **recapitulation**, and **sacrificial cult** fulfillment. The resurrection is the declarative **vindication** of Jesus and the inauguration of **new creation**, which shapes an eschatologically charged **soteriology**. Bird often frames the atonement through the **gospel announcement** itself, insisting that soteriology flows from the **euangelion’s** proclamation that Jesus is **Lord** who saves.

#### 7. Paul, justification, and union with Christ
Bird’s **Pauline** theology is deliberately integrative with respect to **justification**. In **The Saving Righteousness of God** and in his **Romans** commentary he argues that **dikaiosyne** involves both God’s **covenant faithfulness** and his judicial verdict that acquits the ungodly by grace through faith. He emphasizes **union with Christ** as the matrix within which **imputation** is to be understood, thus refusing a stark dichotomy between **forensic** and **participatory** categories. The believer is righteous in Christ, declared righteous on account of Christ, and transformed by participation in Christ, an account that seeks to mediate between **Reformed old perspective** articulations and **New Perspective** emphases.

On the **pistis Christou** debate, Bird exhibits sympathy for **faithfulness of Christ** nuances while retaining the necessity of human **faith** as instrumental reception. The ecclesial dimension of **justification**, inclusion of Gentiles within the one people of God, and the role of **Torah** in salvation history are highlighted without collapsing justification into mere boundary markers. His construal thus preserves **substitutionary logic** and **forensic acquittal** while embedding them in the wider **eschatological** and **ecclesial** horizon of **new creation**.

#### 8. Church, sacraments, mission, and public theology
Bird’s ecclesiology is unabashedly **missional**, built on the **gospel** as royal proclamation that forms a people who embody **kingdom life**. He reads church order through the lens of **gifts** and **mission**, and he defends **women in ministry** arguing from **Romans 16** and the practice of the **early church** that women labored in leadership and proclamation. His **Anglican** location inclines him toward a moderate sacramental realism, treating **baptism** and **the Lord’s Supper** as means of grace and signs of **covenant** that unite believers to Christ and to one another, while preserving the primacy of faith and the centrality of the **Word**.

In public theology, Bird resists both **culture war** reduction and **quietism**. He reads **empire** themes in **Paul** and the **Gospels** with a soft anti-imperial accent, insisting that the lordship of **Christ** relativizes all earthly powers while commanding neighbor-love and civic virtue. The church’s political posture is cruciform and hopeful, grounded in **resurrection** and practiced through hospitality, justice, and truth-telling. This pastoral register, evident across works like **Romans** and **Evangelical Theology**, aims to furnish congregations with doctrinal ballast for late modern pluralism.

#### 9. Gospels, history, and apologetic engagement
The reliability and formation of the **Gospels** are central to Bird’s contribution. In **The Gospel of the Lord** he advances a robust account of **oral tradition**, apostolic authority, and the evangelists as **theologians of memory**. He argues that the early church curated and transmitted Jesus traditions with corporate care, that the written **Gospels** emerge from living communities under recognized teachers, and that harmonizing impulses must give way to respect for each evangelist’s **theological artistry**. This vision resists skeptical claims about late invention while avoiding harmonistic flattening.

Bird’s public-facing volume **How God Became Jesus**, 2014, replies to **Bart Ehrman** by defending **incarnation** and early **divine Christology** with historical rigor. He deploys **Second Temple Jewish** patterns of exegesis, **Greco-Roman** categories of divinity, and **worship evidence** to show that high Christological claims are primitive. He extends this apologetic to practical catechesis in **What Christians Ought to Believe**, where he uses the **Apostles’ Creed** to connect **Scripture**, **dogma**, and **discipleship**. His approach models a retrieval of **tradition** that invites critical scholarship to serve ecclesial formation rather than erode it.

#### 10. Integrated Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed and Westminster** vantage, Bird’s gospel-centered dogmatics offers significant common cause and a few strategic tensions. There is deep alignment on **Nicene and Chalcedonian orthodoxy**, on **Trinity and Christology**, on **penal substitution** as integral to the **atonement**, on the centrality of **union with Christ**, and on **Scripture’s authority** and sufficiency. His use of the **rule of faith** to norm exegesis resonates with **analogia fidei**, and his insistence that **dogmatics** flows from the **euangelion** echoes classical **Reformed** prioritizing of **covenant of grace** and **redemptive history**. Yet Bird’s refusal to bind doctrine of **Scripture** to stricter **inerrancy** formulations will invite Westminster-style concerns about the clarity and stability of his epistemic ground, since confessional Reformed theology often anchors inspiration in strongly stated **verbal plenary inerrancy**. His integrative view of **justification**, while affirming forensic acquittal, sometimes marginalizes the discrete dogmatic weight of **imputation of Christ’s righteousness**, risking a loss of the precise **federal** logic prized in **Reformed orthodoxy**. His advocacy for **women in preaching and leadership**, as well as an **Anglican** sacramentalism that may soften polemics over **baptismal subjects** and eucharistic **real presence**, will sit uncomfortably with some **Westminster** convictions on **church order** and **sacraments**. Finally, his constructive openness to **historical-critical** method is laudable in courage yet requires a more explicit covenantal epistemology of **revelation and reason** to guard against method drift. A Reformed alternative would sharpen the priority of **special revelation** in a confessional **prolegomenon**, secure **imputation** within **union with Christ** as a constitutive component rather than a corollary, articulate **inerrancy** in concert with genre but without dilution, and ground ecclesial practice in **covenant theology** that orders **sacraments** and **office**. Even with these tensions, Bird’s principal contribution remains a welcome model of evangelical dogmatics disciplined by the **gospel**, nourished by the **canon and creed**, and in fruitful dialogue with the **academy** for the service of the **church**.
