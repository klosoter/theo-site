### TITLE
Analytic evangelical theism that defends God’s sovereignty and moral realism while mapping the many faces of evil

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Postwar American evangelicalism**, the rise of **analytic philosophy of religion**, and the 1990s debates over **open theism** form the historical stage.
- Method marked by **grammatical-historical exegesis**, **propositional revelation**, and an **analytic realist epistemology** disciplined by **inerrancy**.
- Key works: **Continuity and Discontinuity, 1988**, **Ethics for a Brave New World, 1993 and 2010**, **No One Like Him, 2001**, **The Many Faces of Evil, 1994 and 2004**.
- Central categories include **meticulous providence and compatibilism**, **modified classical theism**, **graded absolutism in ethics**, and **dispensational-covenantal continuity**.
- Interlocutors include **Alvin Plantinga**, **Clark Pinnock and Gregory Boyd**, **Charles Hartshorne**, **Craig Blaising and Darrell Bock**, and **Reformed classical theists**.
- Reformed cue: strong alignment on **sovereignty, inerrancy, and anti-open theism**, with tensions over **divine simplicity, impassibility, temporality**, and **dispensational hermeneutics**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
John S. Feinberg emerges within the institutional consolidation of **postwar American evangelicalism**, shaped by the scholarship of his father **Charles L. Feinberg** and by the rise of an English-speaking **analytic philosophy of religion** that pressed clarity and argument into theology. Trained in biblical and philosophical theology and later long-time chair at **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School**, his career spans the apologetic and doctrinal battles over **inerrancy** in the 1970s, the public quarrels about **open theism** in the 1990s, and the evangelical retrieval of **classical theism** in the early twenty-first century. He writes as a pastorally savvy academic committed to **propositional revelation**, **grammatical-historical exegesis**, and the conviction that **systematic theology** must articulate a coherent **worldview** accountable to Scripture and reason. As general editor of the **Foundations of Evangelical Theology** series with Crossway, 2001 onward, Feinberg models evangelical dogmatics that is exegetically saturated and philosophically literate, aiming to equip the church to confess **divine sovereignty**, **human responsibility**, and the reliability of the **doctrine of God** amid the pressure of secular and revisionist options.

Feinberg’s method is unapologetically **analytic**, but not rationalist. He prizes careful definitions, distinctions, and deductive clarity while insisting on **Sola Scriptura** and the priority of **canonical exegesis**. He treats philosophy as ancilla theologiae, a handmaid that clarifies terms like **omniscience**, **impassibility**, and **compatibilism** rather than a master that legislates content. His epistemic stance is a chastened **realism** rooted in **inerrant Scripture** and unembarrassed about **propositional truth**, yet alert to the noetic effects of sin and the need for intellectual virtue. Against postliberal appeals to narrative-only renderings and against revisionist projects in **process theism** or **open theism**, Feinberg insists that Christian confession requires ontological claims about **God’s aseity**, **immutability**, **holiness**, and **triune life**, articulated with conceptual precision that holds together biblical data and metaphysical grammar.

#### 2. The doctrine of God and a measured classical theism
Feinberg’s programmatic contribution is **No One Like Him, 2001**, a thousand-page articulation of the **doctrine of God** for evangelical readers in the wake of challenges from **open theism**, **process theology**, and neo-scholastic retrievals. The book defends a robust **classical theism** that affirms **aseity**, **simplicity** in a qualified sense, **immutability**, **omniscience**, **omnipotence**, and **omnibenevolence**, yet recalibrates certain scholastic formulas to account for the biblical depiction of divine relationality. Against **open theism** he argues that the God of Scripture knows all truths including future contingents, grounds meticulous **providence**, and cannot be surprised or defeated, thus safeguarding **divine omniscience** and **efficacious foreordination**. Against **process theism** he denies any metaphysical dependence of God upon the world, reasserting **creatio ex nihilo** and **act of will** as the only explanation for the world’s existence and continued being under **divine conservation**.

At the same time, Feinberg advances what he calls a **modified classical theism**. He questions extreme readings of **divine simplicity** that collapse all distinctions into identity, preferring a model that preserves the **real harmony** and inseparability of divine perfections without construing them as strict identity claims that erase meaningful attributions. On **impassibility** he contends that God is not vulnerable to involuntary suffering or passible pathos, yet Scripture warrants a robust account of **divine affections** as sovereign, holy, and unchanging expressions of God’s perfect nature. Concerning **divine eternity and temporality**, Feinberg defends the thesis that God is timeless *ad intra* without creation and temporal *ad extra* in relation to a created order with genuine tensed facts, a position he treats as necessary to account for **covenantal history**, **incarnation**, and God’s responsive involvement in **redemptive acts** without compromising **immutability of essence and purpose**.

#### 3. Sovereignty, freedom, and providence
In his dogmatics Feinberg takes up the classic debate about **divine sovereignty** and **human freedom** and argues for **compatibilism** that secures both moral responsibility and meticulous providence. Against libertarian accounts associated with some **Arminian** and **open theist** models, he contends that Scripture depicts human actions as encompassed by **divine decree** and moved by **providence** while remaining free in the morally significant sense of willing according to one’s strongest motives and character. This **compatibilist freedom** allows genuine accountability because the will is not coerced even as it is ordered within God’s comprehensive plan. He challenges **Molinism’s middle knowledge** as an unnecessary and unstable attempt to preserve libertarian freedom, arguing that positing true counterfactuals of creaturely freedom independent of God’s decree compromises **aseity** and does not finally secure moral responsibility.

Feinberg’s providence is meticulous rather than merely general. He maintains **concursus** between primary and secondary causes, uses a **concurrence model** to underwrite creaturely integrity, and deploys **asymmetric explanations** to disentangle divine ordination from moral culpability. Evil events are encompassed by God’s plan for **greater goods** that include **redemptive history**, **sanctification**, and the display of **divine glory**, while God remains morally perfect and holy in all his ways. The outcome is a doctrinal synthesis that aims to preserve the full sweep of biblical affirmations about **God’s sovereignty** and the lived reality of **human responsibility**, delivered in an analytic register that anticipates objections and supplies carefully stated replies.

#### 4. Mapping and answering the problem of evil
Feinberg’s signature monograph on theodicy, **The Many Faces of Evil, 1994 and revised 2004**, offers an unusually comprehensive cartography of how theological systems handle **moral and natural evil**. He distinguishes a **defense** aimed at undercutting the logical or probabilistic force of arguments from evil from a **theodicy** that ventures explanatory goods, drawing on **Alvin Plantinga** for the defense template while expanding the theological range beyond philosophical minimalism. He subjects **Augustinian-Calvinist**, **Arminian**, **Molinist**, **open theist**, and **process** strategies to sustained analysis, highlighting how competing commitments to **divine foreknowledge**, **freedom**, and **providence** shape their respective problem-solving capacities. His verdict is that robust **Calvinist compatibilism** supplies the most integrated answer because it can affirm that God has good reasons for permitting evil, grounds hope in sovereign redemption, and protects **eschatological justice** without sacrificing divine control.

As for positive construction, Feinberg articulates a **multicomponent theodicy**. He emphasizes the role of **stable natural laws** in securing a habitable world while acknowledging that such regularities entail natural risks, thus adopting a version of the **natural law theodicy** proffered within a biblical frame. He integrates elements of **soul-making** insofar as suffering can serve sanctification under divine providence, yet he refuses to anchor meaning in creaturely growth alone, prioritizing **God’s glory in Christ** as the teleological horizon. He argues that the cross supplies the definitive proof of **divine justice and mercy**, turning the worst moral evil into the site of the greatest good, and he commends **pastoral theodicy** that merges doctrinal clarity with spiritual care rather than abstract speculation. The result is both a scholarly resource and a guide for ministry in a world scarred by **tragedy and loss**.

#### 5. Moral theology in a technological age
With his brother **Paul D. Feinberg**, John authored **Ethics for a Brave New World, 1993, revised and expanded 2010**, a thorough evangelical treatment of **moral theology** focused on contemporary bioethical and social dilemmas. They defend a **divine command theory** anchored in the moral character of God and the authority of **inerrant Scripture**, supplemented by prudential reasoning about empirical facts that bear upon **casuistry** and application. The Feinbergs argue for the **sanctity of life** in debates over **abortion**, **euthanasia**, **assisted reproduction**, and **genetic technologies**, articulate a chastened **retributive justice** for **capital punishment**, and deploy a carefully specified **just war** framework for statecraft and military ethics. Their analysis exhibits the same analytic habit seen in John’s doctrine of God, with precise definitions of **personhood**, **viability**, **intention and foresight**, and **proportionality**.

On the perennial issue of moral conflicts, the Feinbergs commend a form of **graded absolutism** or **hierarchicalism**. They argue that divine commands sometimes generate real conflicts in a fallen world and that Scripture itself exemplifies **higher-law priorities**, allowing the conscientious believer to obey the weightier demand without moral guilt. This stance aims to avoid both **situationism** and an implausible claim that conflicts never occur, and it insists that the hierarchy is not ad hoc but divinely revealed in the canon’s moral order. Throughout, the book’s case studies tie exegetical arguments about **creation, covenant, and law** to policy-relevant judgments, modeling a public-facing **evangelical moral realism** attuned to human dignity and common grace.

#### 6. Scripture, hermeneutics, and the Israel-church question
Feinberg’s editorial work in **Continuity and Discontinuity, 1988** maps evangelical debates concerning **dispensationalism** and **covenant theology**. He positions himself as a mediating voice that honors **grammatical-literal exegesis**, defends **inerrancy**, and seeks to account for both discontinuities in covenantal administration and profound soteriological continuity in **redemptive history**. He highlights **kingdom** and **new covenant** themes to explain how Old Testament promises find both present fulfillment in **Christ and the church** and a future realization in God’s purposes for **Israel**, a posture often aligned with **progressive dispensationalism**. His hermeneutic resists reduction to typology alone while welcoming typological fulfillment that keeps faith with textual authorial intent.

This approach feeds back into his doctrine of God and providence by underscoring God’s **faithfulness** across economies of revelation. The same **meticulous sovereignty** that orders individual salvation also orders corporate history toward **eschatological consummation**, where the integrity of **covenant promises** and the unity of the **people of God** are maintained without collapsing distinct roles. By stabilizing hermeneutics with analytic clarity and a high view of Scripture, Feinberg seeks a constructive path through intramural evangelical disputes, trading polemic for proposals that can be tested by **exegesis** and coherent **systematics**.

#### 7. Interlocutors and controversies
Across his corpus Feinberg engages leading figures and schools. From **Alvin Plantinga** he learns the power of the **free will defense** as a strictly logical reply to the inconsistency charge, yet he maintains a **compatibilist** account rather than embracing libertarianism. He disputes **Clark Pinnock**, **Gregory Boyd**, and **John Sanders** over **open theism**, arguing that Scripture and classical theism require exhaustive divine foreknowledge and that the risks of the open view undercut **prophecy**, **providence**, and **assurance**. He critiques **Charles Hartshorne** and other **process theologians** for violating **aseity** and **immutability**, commending instead a God who is richly personal without metaphysical dependence on the world. He also converses with evangelical **classical theists** such as **Bruce Ware** and **John Frame**, advancing friendly debate about **simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **temporality** while sharing a common front against revisionism.

Methodologically, Feinberg embodies the **analytic turn** in evangelical theology that prizes tight argument and conceptual hygiene. He often translates scholastic and Reformed claims about **simplicity**, **immutability**, and **concursus** into contemporary idioms, providing students with bridges to historic doctrines without demanding mastery of medieval metaphysics. His polemics are measured, typically presenting the strongest version of an opposing view before critiquing it, and he consistently returns to the **canonical narrative** of creation, fall, redemption, and consummation as the frame within which metaphysical judgments should be made. The result is a theologian who refuses both a biblicist reduction and a purely speculative system, working instead for a **biblically normed metaphysics**.

#### 8. Doctrinal synthesis and constructive emphases
Taken together, Feinberg’s work displays a coherent suite of commitments that can be called **analytic evangelical theism**. He affirms **triune orthodoxy**, treats the divine perfections with reverent exactness, and ensures that **providence** and **sovereignty** are not swallowed by modern anxieties about autonomy. He offers a **modified simplicity** that preserves unity without collapsing all predication, an **impassibility rightly qualified** that rejects divine suffering as involuntary weakness while affirming holy affections, and a relational account of **divine eternity** that is timeless with respect to God’s essence and decree and temporal with respect to God’s covenantal actions in history. In ethics he develops a **divine command moral realism** vivid enough to adjudicate modern bioethical dilemmas while resisting both legalism and laxity through a calibrated **hierarchy of duties**.

Feinberg’s constructive voice is pastoral as well as academic. He writes as someone who has seen suffering up close, applying his theodicy in the presence of **terminal illness** and loss, thereby grounding **the problem of evil** not only in syllogisms but in the reality of Christian hope. He insists that **Christology** is the linchpin of theodicy and providence, for in the **incarnation** and **atonement** the God who ordains history enters it to defeat sin and death. He calls students to confess a God who is truly **no one like Him**, immeasurably beyond us in **aseity and glory**, yet covenantally near in **mercy and truth**. This is evangelical dogmatics forged at the intersection of canonical exegesis, analytic argument, and the church’s worship.

#### 9. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed and Westminster** vantage, Feinberg’s achievements are substantial. His defense of **inerrancy**, **meticulous providence**, **compatibilist freedom**, **creatio ex nihilo**, and the reality of **divine attributes** stands squarely with **classical Reformed orthodoxy** and serves the church well against **open theism** and **process** proposals. His careful use of **concursus**, his insistence on **primary and secondary causation**, and his clear repudiation of **Molinist middle knowledge** preserve **aseity** and align with the confessional grammar of **divine decree**. Yet tensions remain. His **modified simplicity** and qualified **impassibility** risk softening **pure actuality** and the confessional identity of **essence and attributes** retrieved by **Turretin** and **Bavinck**, and his advocacy of **temporal relationality** for God since creation will trouble those who hold **timeless eternity** without qualification as necessary to safeguard **immutability** and **impassibility**. Ethically, **graded absolutism** sits uneasily beside the Reformed use of **natural law**, **general equity**, and classic **casuistry**, and his **progressive dispensational** leanings diverge from a **covenantal** reading that sees stronger structural unity of **Israel and the church** within the **Westminster Standards**. A Westminster alternative would reaffirm robust **divine simplicity**, a strictly **impassible** account of divine affections as analogical predications of stable perfections, a fully **timeless eternity**, a covenantal hermeneutic that subsumes Israel-church distinctions under **one covenant of grace**, and an ethic that resolves moral conflicts by deeper specification rather than graded hierarchy. Even so, Feinberg’s analytic evangelical theism remains a valuable ally, and his lucid arguments constitute a serviceable platform from which confessional Reformed theology can both learn and offer principled corrections.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Late twentieth-century **evangelicalism** shaped by analytic philosophy, **inerrancy** debates, and challenges from **open theism** and **process theism**.
- **Core focus**: A rigorous **doctrine of God** that integrates **sovereignty, providence, and moral realism** while addressing **the problem of evil**.
- **Major works**: **Continuity and Discontinuity, 1988**, **Ethics for a Brave New World, 1993 and 2010**, **No One Like Him, 2001**, **The Many Faces of Evil, 1994 and 2004** with distinctive stances on **compatibilism**, **graded absolutism**, and **modified classical theism**.
- **Defining tools**: **Analytic method**, **propositional revelation**, **compatibilist providence**, **qualified simplicity and impassibility**, and a nuanced account of **divine eternity and temporality**.
- **Interlocutors**: Engages **Plantinga** on defenses, contests **Pinnock and Boyd** on **open theism**, critiques **Hartshorne** on **process**, and debates evangelical **classical theists** on **simplicity** and **impassibility**.
- **Reformed assessment**: Strong alignment on **inerrancy** and **sovereignty**, with tensions over **simplicity, impassibility, temporality**, **graded absolutism**, and **dispensational hermeneutics** when measured by **Westminster** norms.
- **Legacy**: A leading voice in **analytic evangelical theism**, shaping curricula through **Foundations of Evangelical Theology** and providing durable resources on **providence, theodicy, and ethics** for church and academy.
