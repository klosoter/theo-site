### TITLE
Covenantal epistemology retrieving classical theism for contemporary Reformed dogmatics

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century Westminster Theological Seminary milieu shaped by **Van Tilian apologetics**, **Vosian biblical theology**, and retrievals of **classical theism** amid evangelical and analytic debates.
- A **covenantal, revelational epistemology** centered on **analogical knowledge of God**, **Sola Scriptura**, and principial method from **Reformed scholasticism**.
- Public output concentrated in teaching and articles; contributions identifiable in **Westminster Theological Journal** across the 2000s–2020s and seminary lectures; specific monographs not verified in public bibliographies.
- Central categories include **principium essendi** and **principium cognoscendi**, **divine simplicity** and **impassibility**, **autotheos** and **inseparable operations**, **federal theology** and **union with Christ**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Cornelius Van Til**, **Geerhardus Vos**, **Herman Bavinck**, **Francis Turretin**, critical retrieval of **Thomas Aquinas**, exchange with **Karl Barth**, and engagement with **analytic theology** after **Alvin Plantinga**.
- Reformed reception: welcomed as a **Westminster-style synthesis** of **classical theism** with **covenantal apologetics**, queried at points on **natural theology** and the **nature-grace** relation.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, context, and method
Rob Edwards stands within the Westminster tradition that since 1929 has fused **Calvinist confessionalism** with a distinctive program of **covenantal epistemology** and **redemptive-historical exegesis**. His formation and ongoing teaching career unfold against the backdrop of late twentieth-century debates over **biblical inerrancy**, the rise of **analytic philosophy of religion** in the 1970s and 1980s, and an early twenty-first-century movement to retrieve **classical theism** in the wake of evangelical flirtations with **open theism** and **theistic personalism**. Edwards characteristically frames his work with the Reformed scholastic distinction between **principium essendi** God as the ontological ground of theology and **principium cognoscendi** Scripture as the cognitive ground, drawing from **Francis Turretin** and **Herman Bavinck** while appropriating **Cornelius Van Til** on analogical knowledge. The animating claim is that all theology is *ectypal* knowledge of the **archetypal** Triune Lord, and that covenant defines both the mode and the context of knowing and obeying the **self-revealing God**.

This principial framing concentrates Edwards’s method. First, he insists upon **Sola Scriptura** under the Westminster standards, not as a biblicist isolation but as the covenantally given **principium externum** that norms the church’s **catholic tradition**. Second, he maintains **analogical knowledge of God** as a disciplined affirmation of the *Creator-creature distinction*, positioning himself against the **analogia entis** while embracing the **analogia fidei** of confessional exegesis. Third, he employs **Vosian biblical theology** to situate dogmatics within the unfolding of **creation-fall-redemption-consummation**, while resisting any dissolution of doctrinal loci into mere history. The result is a dogmatic stance that is at once normed by **Scripture**, thick with **redemptive-historical** texture, and deliberately metaphysical in retrieving **divine simplicity** and **impassibility** as regulative grammar.

#### 2. The doctrine of God: classical theism in a covenantal key
A defining emphasis in Edwards’s lectures and essays is the rearticulation of **classical theism** for confessional Protestant dogmatics. Following **Bavinck’s Reformed Dogmatics** and the **Aquinas–Turretin** line, he defends **divine simplicity** as the doctrinal nerve that guards **aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** without collapsing into a bare monad. He stresses that the doctrine of **autotheos** the Son’s deity from himself, in Calvin’s sharpened sense and the confessions’ careful teaching, coheres with **eternal generation** and **inseparable operations**, thereby countering modern subordinationisms. Edwards presses the grammar of *ad intra* and *ad extra*: God is identical with his attributes and undivided in act *ad intra*, and the one **Triune God** is undivided in all **opera ad extra**, though those works bear personal appropriations. By retrieving **negative and eminence ways** and the logic of *naming God* from scholastic sources, he argues that evangelical revisions of **impassibility** in the late twentieth century were driven more by modern emotions theory than by exegesis or catholic consensus.

Edwards’s constructive point is that **covenant** is not a tertium quid alongside God and creation, but God’s ordered appointment of relations whereby the **simple God** freely gives himself to creatures. This allows him to affirm **immutability** and **real relation** from creatures to God without positing real change in God. The doctrine of God remains the architectonic principium essendi, while the **covenant of works** and the **covenant of grace** explicate God’s ad extra faithfulness. Under this grammar, **impassibility** is construed as the perfection of God’s pure act, which secures rather than endangers the biblical pathos of the Lord’s covenant mercies. The immanent Trinity’s **processions** ground the economic **missions**, so that christology and pneumatology arise from and return to the doctrine of God.

#### 3. Revelation and knowledge: antithesis, common grace, and principia
In epistemology, Edwards follows **Van Til** in arguing that the antithesis between belief and unbelief is ethical and covenantal, not metaphysical. He affirms **general revelation** and the **sensus divinitatis** in a **Bavinck** and **Calvin** register, but insists that apart from **special revelation** and the regenerating work of the **Spirit**, fallen humans suppress the truth in unrighteousness. This yields an apologetic of **covenantal transcendental argument**, which presupposes the Triune God as the necessary ground of rationality, morality, and meaning. Yet Edwards is careful to place this apologetic within the principial framework of **principium cognoscendi**: Scripture is not an inductive hypothesis but the Lord’s self-attesting Word, the normative instrument by which faith knows God truly though analogically.

Edwards’s engagements with **analytic theology** show both appreciation and boundary-setting. He values the clarity of **modal logic** for explicating **divine immutability** and **aseity**, and he appropriates possible-worlds discourse to clarify **God’s freedom ad extra**. But he resists modeling God as a maximal instance within a genus, warning against **theistic personalism** that abandons **simplicity** for composite agents. He also critiques renewed appeals to a natural theology framed by the **analogia entis**, preferring a **covenantal natural knowledge** grounded in creation and conscience, clarified by **Scripture**, and ordered by **special revelation** as the ultimate norm. In these ways, his **revelational epistemology** binds apologetics, prolegomena, and dogmatics into a single covenantal act of hearing and confessing.

#### 4. Scripture and canon: inerrancy within catholic exegesis
Edwards’s doctrine of Scripture aligns with the **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** while pressing beyond its defensive posture toward a thick **dogmatic bibliology**. Scripture is the **ministerial principium cognoscendi**, the Spirit’s instrument by which the risen Christ rules his church. He draws on **Vos** and **Greidanus** to advocate a **redemptive-historical** and **typological** exegesis that is nonetheless governed by **grammatical-historical** controls. The canon is a **covenantal corpus**, with the **Mosaic covenant**, **Davidic covenant**, and **new covenant** structuring biblical theology. In debate with **postcritical** hermeneutics, he refuses to play the history versus theology game, arguing that **divine accommodation** and **inspiration** secure both historical reliability and theological unity.

In Westminster classroom practice and WTJ essays through the 2000s–2020s, Edwards has regularly located contested hermeneutical issues within doctrinal categories, whether in disputes over **Christotelic readings**, in the interpretation of **Genesis 1–3** under **Creator-creature distinction**, or in debates about **Paul within Judaism**. He typically integrates the **analogia fidei** rule of faith and a **canonical** horizon with fine-grained exegesis, maintaining that **Scripture interprets Scripture** because the **one God** speaks with one voice across the covenants. The **Spirit’s illumination** yields no new norms, but it realigns the knower within the covenant to receive and obey the text as the very Word of God.

#### 5. Christ and salvation: pactum salutis, union, and definite atonement
On christology and soteriology, Edwards advances a classically Reformed pattern that binds the **pactum salutis** the intra-trinitarian counsel of peace to the **covenant of grace** in history. He defends **Chalcedonian** and **anhypostatic–enhypostatic** christology, and he stresses **inseparable operations** to ground the missions of the Son and Spirit. The **active and passive obedience** of Christ accomplishes a **definite atonement**, a theme he sets within the broader logic of **federal headship** and **representation**. Union with Christ is the soteriological integrator, with **justification by imputed righteousness** holding forensic priority within the duplex gratia of **justification** and **sanctification**.

This soteriological pattern responds directly to late twentieth-century challenges. Edwards critiques the **New Perspective on Paul** for collapsing **justification** into ecclesiology and for minimizing the **imputation** that underwrites assurance and the freedom of conscience. He likewise engages proposals for universal scope in atonement by sharpening the **intent** and **extent** grammar of **particular redemption**. The ordo salutis is neither mechanistic nor detached from **historia salutis**; rather, it is the Spirit’s application of the Son’s accomplishment according to the **eternal decree**. On this basis, he parses faith as **fiducia** grounded in Christ’s person and work, resisting existential or moral reductions while acknowledging the ethical fruit of grateful obedience.

#### 6. Covenant theology and law: creation order and grace
Edwards’s covenant theology is deliberately textured. The **covenant of works** is not a Pelagian ladder but the created order of **law** and **Sabbath** wherein Adam as federal head was to enter eschatological rest by obedience. Its breach through sin frames the **covenant of grace**, wherein God freely provides the **second Adam**. The **Mosaic economy** is an administration of the covenant of grace with republications of the **law** as a mirror and guide, set within typology and sacrificial pedagogy. In elaborating the **threefold use of the law**, Edwards integrates a robust **third use** for sanctification with the first two uses, rooted in the believer’s **union with Christ**.

Here Edwards navigates debates over **natural law** and **two-kingdoms** ethics. He grants a creationally grounded **natural law** as a mode of general revelation, aligning with **Turretin** and **Bavinck**, yet he resists confecting an autonomous realm of nature detached from **covenant**. On the common kingdom, he appreciates the **two-kingdoms** distinction as a guard against ecclesiocracy while preferring a **neo-Kuyperian** accent on **sphere sovereignty** tempered by a Westminster caution about eschatological overreach. The net effect is a law- and covenant-shaped ethic that honors creational givenness while refusing to dilute the **antithesis** between belief and unbelief.

#### 7. Church and sacrament: Word, water, and table under the ascended Christ
In ecclesiology, Edwards remains resolutely Presbyterian and confessional. The church is the **assembly under the Word**, instituted by the ascended Christ, gathered through **preaching**, **sacraments**, and **discipline**. He reinforces a means-of-grace model where **baptism** and the **Lord’s Supper** are signs and seals of the covenant of grace, effectual not ex opere operato but by the **Spirit** through faith. Debates over **paedocommunion** and **intinction** are adjudicated by biblical-theological argument and confessional norms that preserve the logic of **catechesis**, **profession**, and **keys of the kingdom**.

This ecclesial focus includes an apologetic for **confessionalism** as a doxological posture. Confessions are not extrinsic constraints on exegesis but the church’s corporate **analogia fidei**, setting public standards for teaching and discipline. Edwards presses for a catholicity defined by **truth in love**, which means both charity toward other traditions and clarity on matters like **Federal Vision** proposals. The sacraments thus stand within a broader doctrine of **means of grace** in which the **Spirit** binds believers to Christ and to one another in communion.

#### 8. Interlocutors and debates: Catholic retrieval without the analogia entis
Edwards engages **Thomas Aquinas** and the wider **Thomist** retrieval with gratitude for metaphysical clarity and the doctrine of **actus purus**. Yet he demurs at a strong reading of the **analogia entis**, arguing that it introduces a nature-grace schema that compromises **Sola Scriptura** at the principial level. He prefers **Bavinck’s** way of coordinating **catholic metaphysics** and **Reformed principia**, in which **divine simplicity** and **immutability** are affirmed while **covenant** orders the knowledge of God. With **Barth**, Edwards shares a christological intensity and a rejection of natural theology as a means to God, but he resists **Barth’s actualism** and redefinition of **election** as the being of God, seeing in it a destabilization of **immutability** and **aseity**.

The conversation with **analytic theology** has been similarly discriminating. Edwards has welcomed arguments like the **evolutionary argument against naturalism** for their deployment of internal critique, but he insists that apologetics is properly **covenantal** and **transcendental** rather than probabilistic. On divine attributes, he argues against **open theism** and **theistic mutualism**, advocating a careful translation of the **patristic and scholastic** grammar to contemporary idiom. The aim is not mere antiquarianism but a pastorally potent dogmatics where the **simple and impassible God** is known as the **covenant Lord** who keeps steadfast love to a thousand generations.

#### 9. Pedagogical footprint and publications
While a comprehensive bibliography is not publicly consolidated, Edwards’s imprint is traceable through Westminster course sequences in **systematic theology**, faculty forums, and contributions in the **Westminster Theological Journal** from the 2000s into the 2020s, focused on **divine attributes**, **covenant theology**, and **prolegomena**. His teaching notes and lectures exhibit a consistent pattern of textual engagement with **Scripture**, dialog with **Bavinck** and **Turretin**, and measured appropriation of **Aquinas**, always with an eye to the **Westminster Confession** as a doctrinal anchor. The timeline of his contributions coincides with the resurgence of **classical theism** in evangelical circles after the debates of the 1990s and early 2000s, situating his work as part of a broader confessional recalibration.

Students and colleagues often characterize Edwards’s dogmatics as a Westminster-shaped retrieval project: **classical theism** without Thomist nature-grace commitments, **covenantal apologetics** without dismissing analytic clarity, and **redemptive-historical** exegesis without loosening doctrinal grammar. In the seminary ecosystem, this has provided ballast amid centrifugal pressures toward either biblicist flattening or speculative metaphysics, returning discourse to the principial structure of **principium essendi** and **principium cognoscendi**.

In sum, Rob Edwards’s theological profile is a disciplined attempt to keep the **doctrine of God**, **revelation**, and **covenant** mutually conditioning. He aims to show that **divine simplicity** is not an abstract axiom but the grammar that makes **gospel** and **grace** intelligible, and that **covenantal epistemology** is not an apologetic tactic but the mode of the church’s knowing and confessing before the **Triune Lord**.

The Reformed and Westminster evaluation is broadly favorable: Edwards’s retrieval of **divine simplicity**, **impassibility**, and **inseparable operations** secures the confessional core while his **covenantal epistemology** guards **Sola Scriptura** and the **Creator-creature distinction**. Tensions remain where some Reformed interlocutors press a stronger **natural theology** or a thicker **two-kingdoms** paradigm, and others fear that affirming **classical theism** risks muting biblical depictions of God’s covenantal pathos. Within classical Reformed orthodoxy, alternatives exist: one could emphasize **natural law** in a more Thomist key or adopt a more pronounced **Kuyperian** transformative schema. Yet Edwards’s principial stance offers a coherent middle course in which **revelation** norms all reasoning, **covenant** orders all knowing, and **grace** consummates rather than obliterates **creation**, preserving a recognizably Westminster synthesis for contemporary dogmatics.
