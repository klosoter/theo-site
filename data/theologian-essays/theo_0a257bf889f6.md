### TITLE
Reframing Reformed Orthodoxy: Continuity, Diversity, and Scholastic Method in Early Modern Theology

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Historical context**: Late twentieth- and early twenty-first-century reassessment of **Post-Reformation Reformed orthodoxy** within the legacies of **Heiko Oberman** and **David C. Steinmetz**; response to **Barthian** and **neo-Puritan** narratives.
- **Method**: Historico-philological retrieval of **scholastic method** as a technical tool, careful use of **Latin sources**, attention to **genre, dispositio, and loci communes**, and anti-anachronistic refusal of **central-dogma** reconstructions.
- **Key works**: **Dictionary of Latin and Greek Theological Terms** (1985; rev. 2017); **Christ and the Decree** (1986); **God, Creation, and Providence in the Thought of Jacob Arminius** (1991); **Post-Reformation Reformed Dogmatics** 4 vols. (1987–2003); **The Unaccommodated Calvin** (2000); **After Calvin** (2003); **Calvin and the Reformed Tradition** (2012); **Divine Will and Human Choice** (2017); **Grace and Freedom** (2020).
- **Central categories**: **archetypal and ectypal theology**, **principium essendi** and **principium cognoscendi**; **cognitio Dei insita/acquisita**; **divine simplicity** and **immutability**; **pactum salutis**, **covenant of works**, and **covenant of grace**; **infralapsarianism/supralapsarianism**; **ordo salutis**; **compatibilist freedom** and **synchronic contingency**.
- **Interlocutors**: Against **R. T. Kendall** and **Brian Armstrong** on the **Calvin versus the Calvinists** thesis; in dialogue with **Barth** and **T. F. Torrance**; conversation with **Turretin**, **Voetius**, **Owen**, **Perkins**, and **Aquinas** as sources rather than foils.
- **Reformed reception**: Widely received among confessional **Reformed** seminaries as a corrective to **decline narratives**, with debates over **natural theology**, **method**, and **Van Tilian** epistemology.

### ESSAY
Richard A. Muller has become the decisive cartographer of **Post-Reformation Reformed orthodoxy**, redrawing the map of early modern **Reformed scholasticism** with a historian’s rigor and a theologian’s ear. Formed in the crucible of the **Duke University** school under **David C. Steinmetz**, a student of **Heiko A. Oberman**, Muller inherited an agenda of historical retrieval aimed at dismantling simplistic genealogies of **Reformation** thought. His teaching career at **Calvin Theological Seminary** in Grand Rapids sharpened his focus on the internal grammar of confessional **Reformed** theology, particularly its **Latin** scholastic sources. Across a corpus that includes the **Dictionary of Latin and Greek Theological Terms** and the four-volume **Post-Reformation Reformed Dogmatics**, his signature thesis is that early modern **Reformed orthodoxy** exhibits structural continuity with the medievals, genuine internal diversity, and a coherent, non-rationalist **scholastic method** that serves rather than supplants Scripture’s authority.

Muller’s historiographical method opposes the twin distortions of **Whig progressivism** and **decline narratives**. Against claims that **Protestant scholasticism** betrayed the **Reformation** by subordinating Scripture to **Aristotelian metaphysics**, he demonstrates that scholasticism is a pedagogical and argumentative technique, not a doctrinal master. The key is genre attention: the **commonplace book** and **disputation**, the architecture of **loci communes**, and the **dispositio** of prolegomena to soteriology. He mines **Latin** titles, marginalia, and disputations to reconstruct the architectonics of **archetypal and ectypal theology**, the careful distinctions of **principium essendi** in God and **principium cognoscendi** in Scripture and the Spirit, and the multiple senses of **cognitio Dei insita et acquisita**. In this way he restores the classical **Reformed** texture of **divine simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** as exegetically oriented dogma that employs metaphysical tools while remaining normed by the **analogia fidei**.

Two early studies set the trajectory. In **Christ and the Decree** 1986, Muller argues that later **Reformed** theologians did not reduce theology to **predestination** but integrated decree and **Christology** within a broader **covenant** and **Trinitarian** framework. By tracing **Calvin**, **Beza**, **Perkins**, and **Polanus**, he refutes a certain **central-dogma** reading and shows a spectrum of supra- and infralapsarian positions functionally compatible with robust **preaching** and **sacramental** practice. In **God, Creation, and Providence in the Thought of Jacob Arminius** 1991, he situates **Arminius** within the same scholastic grammar, highlighting the use of **middle knowledge** and a distinctive account of **divine concurrence**, thereby correcting polemical caricature while clarifying substantive soteriological divergence over **grace** and **freedom**.

The monumental **Post-Reformation Reformed Dogmatics** 1987–2003 establishes the standard reference for early modern **Reformed** dogmatics. Volume 1 on **Prolegomena** recovers the layered doctrine of principia, distinguishing **principium essendi** in **God** as the ontic ground of theology from **principium cognoscendi externum** in **Scripture** and **internum** in the **Spirit**. Muller maps the **Scripture principle** as more than proof-texting: a technical doctrine of inspiration, canon, **exegesis**, and **hermeneutics** framed by a complex account of **traditio** and **confessiones**. Volume 2 on **Holy Scripture** details the doctrine of **claritas**, **sufficiency**, and **authority**, including the distinction of **autographa** and **apographa**, thereby showing that early modern **Reformed** defended the reliability of received **textual** forms without collapsing them into a naive **biblicism**. Volumes 3 and 4 on **The Divine Essence and Attributes** and **The Triunity of God** track the retrieval of **actus purus**, **simplicity**, **immensity**, and **aseity**, together with **Calvin’s autotheos**, all elaborated by **Turretin**, **Voetius**, and **Owen** within the **archetypal-ectypal** distinction that guards the Creator-creature divide while underwriting the possibility of true knowledge by **accommodation**.

Muller’s middle-period work consolidates this picture while taking aim at the **Calvin versus the Calvinists** thesis. In **The Unaccommodated Calvin** 2000, he contests romanticized portraits of **Calvin** as an existential biblical theologian bereft of **scholastic** distinctions. By reading Calvin’s 1559 **Institutes** alongside his **commentaries** and **disputations**, Muller shows that Calvin’s thought is neither univocally anti-metaphysical nor directly identical to later orthodoxy; it is the seedbed of a tradition that develops through varied **loci** and **methods**. **After Calvin** 2003 then replaces the linear narrative with a field of multiple trajectories: **Ramist** codifications in **Perkins**, **covenantal** synthesis in **Ames**, juridical precision in **Turretin**, and pastoral scholasticism in **Voetius** and the **Nadere Reformatie**. The result is a complex but discernible **Reformed** identity formed by confessions, schools, regional contexts, and disputational cultures rather than by a single **master idea**.

A distinctive contribution concerns **freedom**, **contingency**, and **providence**. In **Calvin and the Reformed Tradition** 2012 and **Divine Will and Human Choice** 2017, Muller traces the early modern taxonomy of **libertarian**, **compatibilist**, and **determinist** accounts, refusing anachronistic projections of analytic categories onto the seventeenth century. He recovers the **synchronic contingency** account, whereby events are contingent in relation to secondary causes and certain in relation to **God’s decree**, under the formal act of **simple intelligence** and **free knowledge**. He shows how **Aquinas**, **Scotus**, and **Suarez** shaped the idiom, and how **Perkins**, **Ames**, **Turretin**, and **Owen** articulated compatibilist freedom consistent with **moral responsibility** and **secondary causation**. **Grace and Freedom** 2020 deepens the analysis by locating **William Perkins** as a key mediator of scholastic categories, revealing how discussions of **habitus**, **faculties**, and the **ordo salutis** were deployed to secure both divine **efficacy** and human **agency** without collapsing into **Pelagianism** or **fatalism**.

Crucial to Muller’s portrait is the place of **covenant theology** within the older doctrinal architecture. He refutes the idea that **federal theology** displaced **Trinity** and **decree** as a novel central dogma. Rather, the **pactum salutis**, **covenant of works**, and **covenant of grace** function as biblical-legal coordinates that mediate the decree in the economy and integrate **Christology**, **justification**, and **sanctification** under **duplex gratia** and **unio mystica**. The framework accommodates debates over **hypothetical universalism** in **Amyraut** and English divines while preserving the confessional **particularism** of redemption. In this arrangement, **predestination** remains doctrinally important but is never the architectonic apex; it is coordinated with **Christ** and the **covenants** by a careful **ordo docendi** designed for preaching, catechesis, and ecclesial discipline.

Muller’s account of **natural theology** and **theological epistemology** is a flashpoint of his reception. He argues that early modern **Reformed orthodoxy** affirmed a circumscribed **cognitio Dei insita et acquisita** grounded in the **imago Dei** and the **semen religionis**, employing arguments for **God’s existence** and **attributes** as part of prolegomena. Yet this **natural theology** is subordinated to the **principium cognoscendi** of **Scripture** and corrected by the **Spirit’s** internal testimony. He shows that the older **Reformed** did not adopt the **analogia entis** as a theological principle in the later **Barthian** sense, though they used **analogia attributionis** and **analogia proportionalitatis** as metaphysical tools. The resulting doctrine of **God** secures **simplicity** and **aseity** while underwriting a robustly expository **exegesis** committed to canonical coherence and the **sensus literalis**.

The detail-work of Muller’s scholarship depends on his reconstruction of scholastic genres and technical vocabulary. The **Dictionary of Latin and Greek Theological Terms** equips readers to parse **quaestio**, **responsio**, **determinatio**, **distinctio**, and the careful modulation of **causa efficiens**, **formal**, **material**, and **final** causes in doctrinal argument. In tracing **res** and **signa**, the distinction between **theologia as habitus** and **doctrina**, and the role of **syllogistic** inference within biblical **lectio**, he shows how theological schools governed the flow from **Scripture** to **loci** with scholarly discipline. This ribbed structure explains the capacity of early modern **Reformed** theology to address **heresy**, to adjudicate **intra-Reformed** debates, and to sustain the catechetical ministries of churches bound to the **Three Forms of Unity** and the **Westminster Standards**.

Muller’s polemical edge emerges in his engagement with modern critics. He takes aim at **R. T. Kendall** and **Brian Armstrong**, whose **Calvin versus the Calvinists** thesis casts later orthodoxy as a rationalist betrayal. By careful contextual reading, he shows that alleged shifts in **assurance**, **faith**, and **atonement** are often misreadings of genre or of the distinction between **ordo salutis** and **historia salutis**. He also queries **Barth** and **T. F. Torrance**, not by refuting their constructive programs directly, but by challenging their historiography of the **Protestant scholastics** as metaphysical captives. In parallel, Muller’s retrieval of **Aquinas** and **Scotus** as positive conversation partners corrects modern **Reformed** allergy to medieval **metaphysics**, even as he emphasizes the confessional distinctives of the **Reformed** settlement.

Recent work extends the reach of his core thesis into broader intellectual history. **Divine Will and Human Choice** correlates early modern debates over **freedom** with parallel developments in **Jesuit** and **Thomist** schools, reframing **Molinism** and **Bañezianism** as live options within the ambient scholastic ecosystem that **Reformed** theologians navigated. Mullers attention to **synchronic contingency** revises analytic oversimplifications and situates **Turretin’s** compatibilism within a carefully qualified doctrine of **voluntas ordinata** and **voluntas absoluta**. In so doing, he highlights the versatility of **Reformed scholasticism**, capable of integrating insights from **medieval** and **Renaissance** sources while maintaining fidelity to **Scripture** and confessional norms.

Mullers overall contribution is thus a threefold reorientation: the demonstration of genuine **continuity** with the medievals, the articulation of real but bounded **diversity** within **Reformed orthodoxy**, and the rehabilitation of **scholastic method** as a theological instrument serving the **Scripture principle**. The result is not a nostalgic repristination but a carefully charted resource for contemporary **dogmatics**, especially on **divine attributes**, **Trinity**, **covenant**, and **providence**. By teaching readers to read the sources in **Latin**, to honor **genre**, and to refuse **anachronism**, Muller furnishes a durable grammar for constructive theology that is historically responsible and confessionally lucid.

From a Reformed and WTS evaluation, Mullers retrieval is a salutary correction to modern **biblicist** and **Barthian** misreadings, aligning with the **Westminster** tradition on **archetypal-ectypal theology**, **divine simplicity**, and the primacy of **Scripture** as **principium cognoscendi**. Points of tension arise where his descriptive account of early modern **natural theology** may be pressed beyond what a **Van Tilian** epistemology would permit under the antithesis of **revelation** and **autonomous reason**. A WTS-friendly appropriation will receive Mullers historical claims while norming **philosophical** usage by a robust doctrine of **covenant epistemology** and the **self-attestation** of Scripture, guarding against any putative independence of **reason** from **revelation**. Likewise, his emphasis on diversity must be held together with confessional **consensus** on **covenant**, **Christ**, and **grace**, lest plurality be mistaken for doctrinal indifference. Within those principial constraints, Muller’s work stands as a major ally in recovering classical **Reformed orthodoxy**, offering tested categories for contemporary exegesis and dogmatics under the authority of the **triune** God who speaks.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Setting**: Late 20th–early 21st century reassessment of **Post-Reformation Reformed orthodoxy** shaped by **Steinmetz** and **Oberman**, taught at **Calvin Theological Seminary**.
- **Core focus**: Rehabilitation of **Reformed scholasticism** as method and retrieval of continuity and diversity without **central-dogma** myths.
- **Major works**: **Dictionary** (1985; rev. 2017), **Christ and the Decree** (1986), **Arminius** study (1991), **PRRD** 4 vols. (1987–2003), **Unaccommodated Calvin** (2000), **After Calvin** (2003), **Calvin and the Reformed Tradition** (2012), **Divine Will and Human Choice** (2017), **Grace and Freedom** (2020).
- **Distinctives**: Emphasis on **archetypal/ectypal theology**, **principia** doctrine, **divine simplicity and immutability**, calibrated **natural theology**, and **synchronic contingency** in freedom.
- **Interlocutors**: Corrects **Kendall** and **Armstrong**, engages **Barth** and **T. F. Torrance**, retrieves **Aquinas**, **Scotus**, **Turretin**, **Voetius**, **Owen**, **Perkins**.
- **Reformed critique**: Affirmed for classical **theism** and confessional method; debated on scope of **natural theology** and how diversity relates to **confessional consensus** in a **Van Tilian** frame.
- **Legacy**: Standardized reference for **Reformed orthodoxy**; durable influence on contemporary **dogmatics**, **seminary curricula**, and renewed interest in early modern **scholastic** sources.
