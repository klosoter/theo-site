### TITLE
Christ in all of Scripture and the Church in all of Christ: redemptive-historical preaching ordered to Reformed ecclesiology.

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Mid- to late-twentieth-century **Westminster Seminary** context shaped by **Geerhardus Vos**, **Cornelius Van Til**, and postwar debates with **neo-orthodoxy** and **historical criticism**.
- Method: **presuppositional, covenantal epistemology** joined to **Vosian biblical theology**, **typology** governed by **divine authorial intent**, and **Christocentric expository preaching**.
- Key works: **Preaching and Biblical Theology** 1961; **Called to the Ministry** 1964; **The Message of 1 Peter** 1988; **The Unfolding Mystery** 1988; **The Church** 1995; **Preaching Christ in All of Scripture** 2003.
- Central categories: **covenant and kingdom**, **promise and fulfillment**, **type and antitype**, **prophet-priest-king**, **means of grace**, **one holy catholic apostolic church**.
- Interlocutors and influences: **Vos**, **Murray**, **Van Til**, **Ridderbos**, **Calvin**, conversing with **Karl Barth**, **Rudolf Bultmann**, **Brevard Childs**, and homiletical colleagues like **Sidney Greidanus**.
- Reformed reception cue: Integrates **Westminster Standards** with **redemptive-historical preaching**, influential for **Timothy Keller** and contemporary **Reformed homiletics**.

### ESSAY
Edmund P. Clowney 1917â€“2005 stands at the nexus of **Reformed biblical theology** and **ecclesial practice**, forming a generation of pastors at **Westminster Theological Seminary** who learned to preach **Christ in all of Scripture** and to order congregational life by **covenant** and **means of grace**. Educated in the orbit of **Wheaton**, **Westminster**, and **Yale**, and ordained in the **Orthodox Presbyterian Church**, he taught at Westminster from the early 1950s, becoming the institution's first **president** in 1966 and serving until 1984. His career unfolded against the background of postwar American **evangelicalism**, the rise of **neo-orthodoxy**, and ongoing clashes over **historical criticism**. His response was neither retreat into biblicism nor accommodation to dialectical theology, but a constructive retrieval of **Vosian redemptive history** welded to **Van Tilian presuppositionalism**, producing a hermeneutic in which **divine revelation** is historically enacted, canonically inscripturated, and homiletically proclaimed.

The architectonics of Clowney's method rest on three interlocking convictions: first, that **covenant** structures the movement of biblical revelation from **creation** through **redemption** to **consummation**; second, that **typology** connects epochs by God-ordained correspondences that climax in **Christ as antitype**; third, that **divine authorial intent** guarantees the unity of Scripture beyond what any single human author foresaw. Against both **allegory** and reductive **grammatical-historical** isolation, he argued that every text has a dual horizon, the immediate human horizon and the canonical horizon directed by the **Spirit**. This hermeneutic anchors his homiletic: exegesis identifies a text's place in the **covenantal drama**, ascends to **Christological fulfillment**, and descends with Spirit-wrought application to the **church**. Thus preaching is not a moralistic lecture nor a free-floating kerygma, but **Word-and-Spirit ministry** located in the ordo salutis and the historia salutis.

Clowney's early programmatic volume, **Preaching and Biblical Theology** 1961, set the template for a generation of Reformed preachers. He contended that **biblical theology** is indispensable for **expository preaching**, because the Bible is a coherent narrative of **promise and fulfillment** rather than a storehouse of timeless anecdotes. He pressed a sharp critique of **exemplaristic moralism** that moves from David to the believer without passing through **David's Son**, and he rejected **dialectical theology's** separation of the **Word-event** from historical acts of God. His definition of **type** highlights God-initiated historical correspondences with escalation in the **antitype**, so that **exodus**, **sacrifice**, **temple**, and **kingship** project forward to **Christ's cross**, **resurrection**, and **ascension**. Yet he refused an autonomous **sensus plenior**, locating the deeper sense in the one **divine author** who speaks through the many human authors, thereby safeguarding the **analogia fidei** without collapsing into arbitrary associations.

The pastoral face of this method shines in **The Unfolding Mystery** 1988, where Clowney narrates the Bible's Christological center for the people of God. In chapters tracing **Adam**, **Noah**, **Abraham**, **Moses**, **David**, **wisdom**, and **temple**, he shows how **covenant** and **kingdom** trajectories converge on **Immanuel**. Adamic headship finds its true image in the **last Adam**; the **Noahic** preservation covenant prefigures the cosmic scope of **new creation**; **Abrahamic promise** blossoms in **justification by faith** and the **nations**; **Mosaic exodus** and **tabernacle** find completion in **the Lamb** and **God-with-us**; **Davidic kingship** is exalted in **Messiah's reign**; and **wisdom** is personified in **Christ** crucified and risen. The book exhibits his characteristic restraint: every christological move is text-governed, covenantally staged, and ecclesially directed toward **worship and mission**.

Clowney's mature homiletic appears again in **Preaching Christ in All of Scripture** 2003, a capstone volume blending hermeneutical argument with model sermons. Here he articulates a sequence often summarized by students as the **Clowney triangle**: from a text's **expository point** one traces its redemptive-historical **trajectory to Christ**, then applies the **gospel** to the **hearer's world**. He dissects homiletical missteps such as **atomistic exegesis**, **theological proof-texting** detached from literary context, and **therapeutic application** that bypasses **union with Christ**. Preaching for him is **sacramental in character** in the older Reformed sense, because the **Spirit** uses the **Word** as means of **grace** to unite sinners to **Christ** and conform them to his image under the **covenant of grace**. His sermons on themes like **temple**, **priesthood**, and **banquet** are case-studies in how **biblical theology** yields doxology, catechesis, and pastoral care.

While known for preaching, Clowney also produced a systematic, pastoral ecclesiology in **The Church** 1995 within the Contours of Christian Theology series. There he expounds the church's **attributes** one, holy, catholic, apostolic as properties of **covenantal union** with the **Triune God**. The **marks of the church** Word, **sacrament**, and **discipline** are means by which the risen **Christ** rules his body by the **Spirit**. He aligns **baptism** and the **Lord's Supper** with **covenant signs and seals**, rejecting **baptismal regeneration** while defending **infant baptism** as the proper sign for children of the covenant. On **polity** he defends **presbyterial government**, grounding **eldership** and **diaconate** in the apostolic pattern and affirming the **ministerial office** as servant-leadership under the **Word**. The church's **mission** unfolds from its worship; gathered doxology overflows in scattered witness to the **kingdom**, yet he carefully distinguishes **church** from **kingdom**, warding off both ecclesiastical triumphalism and privatized pietism. Engagements with **Vatican II**, the **World Council of Churches**, and **charismatic renewal** demonstrate a catholic spirit that remains confessionally **Reformed**, receptive to the whole church's gifts while wary of compromising the **gospel**.

His exegetical work, notably **The Message of 1 Peter** 1988, complements his ecclesiology by detailing the church's identity as **exiles** whose pathway is patterned by **Christ's suffering and glory**. The commentary foregrounds **royal priesthood** and **spiritual house** imagery, relocating temple categories in the **people of God** indwelt by the **Spirit**. Household codes are reframed under **eschatological hope**, and the letter's paraenesis becomes an outflow of **union with Christ** rather than law abstracted from **grace**. This exegetical discipline undergirds his homiletic: application emerges from the redemptive-historical texture of the text, not from topical agendas imposed on it. In the same idiom, **Called to the Ministry** 1964 clarifies the **internal and external call**, the role of the **church courts** in testing gifts, and the spirituality of the pastor as one who ministers the **Word and sacrament** under the **covenant**. Vocation for Clowney is not entrepreneurial self-expression but humble service within the **communion of saints**.

Clowney's intellectual influences and adversaries illuminate his precise location in twentieth-century theology. From **Geerhardus Vos** he inherits the grammar of **history of special revelation** and the conviction that **eschatology** is already present in **Christ**. From **Herman Ridderbos** he assimilates a **kingdom** dynamic that binds **Pauline theology** to the **gospels**, producing his persistent emphasis on **already and not yet**. From **Cornelius Van Til** he takes a **covenantal epistemology**: fallen reason is not neutral, the **Word of God** is self-authenticating, and the **Spirit** must illumine the mind. Consequently, Clowney rejects **Bultmann's** demythologizing and **Barth's** separation of **kerygma** from stable **history**, even as he shares with **Barth** a fierce **Christocentrism**. He appreciates **Brevard Childs** for his **canonical** sensibilities but insists that the canon's unity is doxologically ordered to **Christ**, not merely a literary final form. In conversation with **Sidney Greidanus** and later **Graeme Goldsworthy**, he contributes a distinctly confessional and pastoral voice to the wider **redemptive-historical** homiletic.

The shape of Clowney's preaching theology bears special weight for Reformed praxis. He maintains the **regulative principle of worship**, yet refuses to let it shrink preaching into mere didactics. Instead, he frames preaching as **covenantal address**, the **risen Christ** speaking through his **ambassadors** by the **Spirit**. This posture yields a practice that is profoundly **Trinitarian**, explicitly **Christocentric**, and rigorously **text-driven**. The preacher must trace the text's **covenantal horizon**, articulate its **Christological telos**, and make **grace-driven application** that does not relapse into moralism. For Clowney, the pastoral aim is **faith formed by love** in union with **Christ**, which entails concrete admonition and consolation grounded in the **gospel**. His classroom legacy, preserved in lecture notes and in students such as **Timothy Keller**, demonstrates how **biblical theology** can renew **catechesis**, **evangelism**, and **urban mission** without jettisoning **confessional** depth.

Clowney's ecclesiology also engages modern pressures on the church's identity. He resists **sectarianism** by retrieving the **catholicity** of the Reformed tradition, situating **Presbyterian polity** within the broader **body of Christ**. He opposes **sacramentalism** that makes rites efficacious ex opere operato, yet equally critiques **memorialism** that hollows out the **Lord's Supper** as a mere mental recollection. His account of **discipline** insists that the church's holiness is guarded by moral clarity and pastoral patience, a blend he sees exemplified in **Calvin's Geneva** and enshrined in the **Westminster Standards**. On **mission**, he advances a churchly missiology: the church as **embassy of the kingdom** bears the **keys** and speaks **reconciliation**, while believers as **royal priests** offer spiritual sacrifices in their vocations. The resulting model avoids both **two-kingdoms withdrawal** and **transformationalist triumphalism** by centering the church's public life on **Word and sacrament** and the believer's public life on **wisdom** and **love of neighbor**.

In terms of dogmatic development, Clowney consistently deploys **Trinitarian economy** to integrate **exegesis**, **dogmatics**, and **homiletics**. Revelation flows from the **Father**, through the **Son**, by the **Spirit**; the **covenant of grace** is administered by **means of grace**; and the **church** is constituted by **union with Christ**. His typological repertoire turns on the central axes of **prophet, priest, and king** and the literary-spatial themes of **temple**, **sacrifice**, **exodus**, and **wisdom**. He treats **law and gospel** not as competing dispensations but as distinct yet united voices within the **covenant**, where **law** exposes sin and guides gratitude, and **gospel** announces Christ's finished work. His pastoral counsel marries **assurance** grounded in **Christ's righteousness** with **sanctification** grounded in **union**, aiming to produce mature disciples who can suffer as **exiles** with hope.

Clowney's legacy is visible in the resurgence of **Christ-centered expository preaching** across confessional **Reformed** and broader **evangelical** circles. Through students, conferences, and books, his method has shaped pulpits from **Philadelphia** to **Manhattan**, informing networks that prize **urban mission** and **doctrinal fidelity**. His books remain curricular staples where **biblical theology** is taught in service of the church, and his commentary on **1 Peter** continues to model exegesis that is pastoral, catholic, and **Reformed**. The durability of his influence lies in the deep simplicity of his vision: the **Triune God** making and redeeming a people through **covenant**, revealing himself in **Christ**, and gathering the nations into one **church** by the **Spirit** through **Word and sacrament**.

Reformed and WTS evaluation. Within a **Westminster** frame, Clowney's synthesis of **biblical theology** and **homiletics** is exemplary, yet several tensions invite refinement. First, his robust use of **divine authorial intent** is necessary to secure canonical unity, but it requires constant discipline to avoid **eisegetical typology**; the proper alternative remains the classic **Reformed analogia Scripturae**, where clearer texts govern and the **rule of faith** constrains inference. Second, his acute **Christocentrism** can, in less careful hands, eclipse the **Trinitarian fullness** of Scripture; the remedy is to preserve the **missions of the Son and Spirit** together so that preaching is not functionally **Christomonist**. Third, in ecclesiology, his warmly **missional** stance risks blurring **church and kingdom** in practice; a sharper twofold distinction can guard the church's spiritual mission while affirming the breadth of **common grace** in culture. Finally, his resistance to **moralism** must be paired with explicit **third-use of the law** so that grace-driven preaching yields concrete, commanded holiness. These are not rejections but intra-family calibrations, confirming that Clowney's project, rightly ordered by **covenant**, **revelation**, and **grace**, is a durable gift to Reformed theology and ministry.
