### TITLE
Doctrine as Direction: Theodramatic, speech‑act‑shaped, canonical‑linguistic theology for a mere Protestant church

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late‑modern and **postmodern hermeneutical** context shaped by **Ricoeur**, **Derrida**, and the **Yale school**; evangelical institutional base at **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School** and British training at **Cambridge**.
- Method: **canonical‑linguistic** and **speech‑act** approach; **theodramatic** metaphors; retrieval via **remythologizing**; emphasis on **triune communicative action** and ecclesial performance.
- Key works: **Biblical Narrative in the Philosophy of Paul Ricoeur** (1990); **Is There a Meaning in This Text?** (1998); **First Theology** (2002); **Dictionary for Theological Interpretation of the Bible** ed. (2005); **The Drama of Doctrine** (2005); **Everyday Theology** ed. (2007); **Remythologizing Theology** (2010); **Faith Speaking Understanding** (2014); **Theology and the Mirror of Scripture** with D. Treier (2015); **The Pastor as Public Theologian** with O. Strachan (2015); **Biblical Authority after Babel** (2016); **Pictures at a Theological Exhibition** (2016); **Hearers and Doers** (2019).
- Central terms: **authorial intention**, **illocutionary acts**, **canonical authority**, **doctrine as direction**, **improvisation under authority**, **triune discourse**, **mere Protestant**, **interpretive virtues**, **inerrancy as communicative perfection**.
- Interlocutors: **Paul Ricoeur**, **Hans Frei**, **George Lindbeck**, **Karl Barth**, **J. L. Austin**, **John Searle**, **E. D. Hirsch**, **Anthony Thiselton**, plus critical engagements with **Derrida**, **Stanley Fish**, **Foucault**, and **Bultmann**.
- Reformed cue: alignment on **sola Scriptura**, **providence**, and **classical theism** with questions about **metaphysical density**, **confessional specificity**, and the reach of **improvisation** within **covenant theology**.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, late‑modern setting, and methodological wager
Kevin Vanhoozer emerged as a leading evangelical voice within a late‑twentieth‑century struggle over **meaning, truth, and authority** in the wake of **postmodern literary theory**. Trained at **Wheaton**, **Westminster Theological Seminary**, and the **University of Cambridge**, and teaching at **New College, Edinburgh** before his long tenure at **Trinity Evangelical Divinity School**, he positions theology as a disciplined response to **divine communicative action** rather than as a merely academic exercise in **religious studies**. His early academic focus on **Paul Ricoeur** in Biblical Narrative in the Philosophy of Paul Ricoeur (1990) located him in an ongoing conversation with **hermeneutical phenomenology** and the **Yale school** of **Hans Frei** and **George Lindbeck**, even as he sharpened a distinctive evangelical trajectory committed to **authorial intention**, **canonical form**, and **dogmatic truth**.

Against the postmodern suspicion of **metanarrative** and the postliberal retrenchment to **community grammar**, Vanhoozer wagers that the church may receive Scripture as **the canon** of God’s **illocutionary acts**, a stable speech that nevertheless summons fresh **perlocutionary** obedience in every age. He recasts theology’s task as a **canonical‑linguistic** undertaking: to attend to the **linguistic action** of Scripture in order to render doctrine as **direction** for fitting participation in the **theodrama** of **triune redemption**. This wager trades on a decisive appropriation of **speech‑act theory** from **J. L. Austin** and **John Searle**, chastened by **E. D. Hirsch** regarding **meaning** and **significance**, and honed by evangelical commitments to **sola Scriptura** and **the church catholic**.

#### 2. Hermeneutics after theory: meaning, authorship, and the covenant of discourse
Is There a Meaning in This Text? (1998) made Vanhoozer a central interlocutor in debates with **deconstruction** and **reader‑response theory**. He confronts **Derrida’s** endless différance and **Stanley Fish’s** interpretive communities with a robust account of **meaning as what an author does in saying something**, grounding interpretation in **illocutionary force** discernible through **canonical** and **literary** conventions. The book proposes a **covenant of discourse** that binds authors, texts, and readers to norms of honesty, humility, and charity, naming **interpretive virtues** as the fitting habitus for reading **Scripture**. He thereby resists both **foundationalist positivism** and **hermeneutical anarchism**, claiming a chastened **realism** wherein God, as **communicative author**, can be heard through **human authors** without collapsing **dual authorship**.

First Theology: God, Scripture & Hermeneutics (2002) consolidates this stance by arguing that **theology proper** and **bibliology** are reciprocally conditioning. Because God is **triune communicative agency** who speaks *ad extra* through **covenant** and **Christ the Logos**, Scripture cannot be reduced to information but functions as **performative discourse** that effects what it says. The resultant defense of **inerrancy as communicative perfection** belongs to a broader **economy of revelation**, not merely to textual infallibility. In Vanhoozer’s hands, **inerrancy** names the fittingness of **God’s illocutions** in Scripture to accomplish faithful **promise**, **command**, and **testimony** within the **canon’s** literary diversity.

#### 3. Doctrine as drama: the canonical‑linguistic turn
The Drama of Doctrine (2005) supplies Vanhoozer’s signature proposal: doctrine exists to direct the church’s **participation in the theodrama**. Drawing on **Hans Urs von Balthasar’s** theatrical imagination while decisively **Protestantizing** its grammar, he construes **Scripture** as the authoritative **script**, **doctrine** as **stage directions**, the **church** as the company, and **pastors** as **director‑theologians** who cultivate performances that are faithful to **the canonical script**. Doctrine is therefore both **cognitive** and **pragmatic**: it renders the **illocutions** of Scripture in ecclesial practices that fit the **gospel plot** of **creation, covenant, cross, and consummation**.

Faith Speaking Understanding (2014) returns to the theme with catechetical clarity, extending the performative idiom to **discipleship**, **catechesis**, and **ecclesial habitus**. Whereas **The Drama of Doctrine** elaborates the metaphors, this later work shows how **confession**, **petition**, and **praise** function as **speech‑acts** that configure the church’s identity in **union with Christ**. The result is a thick account of **theologia prima** as the church’s Scripture‑saturated performance, and **theologia secunda** as reflective **dogmatics** that renders judgments to safeguard and guide that performance under **canonical authority**.

#### 4. Remythologizing God: retrieving classical theism through communicative action
Remythologizing Theology: Divine Action, Passion, and Authorship (2010) answers **Bultmann’s** demythologizing and contemporary **process** and **open theist** revisions by retraining metaphysics on **Scripture’s theodrama**. To remythologize is to let **biblical discourse** about God’s speaking, promising, judging, and pardoning supply the primary metaphysical purchase on **triune being and agency**. Vanhoozer maintains **divine aseity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** while describing God’s **impassioned** covenantal freedom as the energetic integrity of **triune love**. He leverages **speech‑act** distinctions to show how God can be **responsive** in the order of **perlocution** without implying **passibility** in God’s **simple** being *ad intra*. The idiom of **authorship** secures **providence** as **comprehensive yet noncompetitive causality**, fitting classic **concursus** but framed in narrative‑linguistic terms.

Pictures at a Theological Exhibition (2016) collects and extends these themes, clarifying the **theo‑ontology** implied by **triune discourse**. Vanhoozer resists both **univocal voluntarism** and **apophatic quietism**, arguing that **God’s communicative presence** in **Word and Spirit** enables analogical talk of **divine attributes** that is governed by **canonical usage** and **doctrinal rules**. His constructive vision invites a retrieval of **classical theism** that speaks biblical prose, treats **covenant** as metaphysical idiom, and keeps **Christology** and **pneumatology** at the heart of **theology proper**.

#### 5. Theological interpretation and the ecclesial location of reading
As general editor of the Dictionary for Theological Interpretation of the Bible (2005), Vanhoozer became a catalyst for the **theological interpretation of Scripture** movement. Against both **historicism** that evacuates the canon’s theological force and **biblicist proof‑texting** that ignores literary form, he orchestrated a collective retrieval of **rule‑governed, churchly exegesis**. The Dictionary’s programmatic entries commend **figural reading**, **canon consciousness**, and **creedal alignment** as interpretive norms, while maintaining a high view of **authorial intention** and the intellectual tools of **literary theory**. With Daniel Treier he later argued in The Theology and the Mirror of Scripture (2015) that **Scripture and doctrine** reflect and regulate each other as **norming norm** and **normed norms**, placing the **church’s confession** within the mirror of **canonical discourse**.

The pastoral payoff is explicit in The Pastor as Public Theologian (2015) and Hearers and Doers (2019). Here Vanhoozer insists that **pastors** are **public theologians** whose primary stage is the **congregation** and whose public is the **polis**. The tools remain **canonical‑linguistic**: sermons are **illocutionary acts** that, by the **Spirit’s** agency, effect **perlocutionary** conversions, moral transformations, and communal reconciliations consonant with the **gospel’s** telos. This pastoral emphasis keeps his hermeneutics tethered to **ecclesial formation**, not merely to academic interpretation.

#### 6. Protestant principle, catholic substance: authority after Babel
Biblical Authority after Babel (2016) addresses the Protestant crisis of **interpretive fragmentation**. Vanhoozer retrieves the **solas** as interlocking **hermeneutical principles**: **sola Scriptura** as the **suprema Scriptura** rule within a pattern of **sola fide**, **sola gratia**, **solus Christus**, and **soli Deo gloria** that together discipline interpretation. He argues for a **mere Protestant** posture: robustly confessional yet intentionally **catholic**, centered in **Scripture’s** **claritas** and the **Spirit’s** guidance of the **communion of saints**. The proposal is neither **magisterial centralization** nor **individualist biblicism**, but a polity of **interpretive accountability** marked by **confession**, **catechesis**, and **correction** in the church.

This vision extends earlier claims about **interpretive virtues** by articulating **ecclesial practices** that sustain them: common **lection**, **creedal** rehearsal, **discipline**, and **synodality** without conceding **Roman** claims. The wager is that **sola Scriptura**, rightly construed, yields **Reformed catholicity** where **dogma** directs communion’s performance of the **gospel**. Vanhoozer thus reframes authority as **distributed responsibility under the canon**, secured by God’s **triune speech** rather than by hierarchical fiat.

#### 7. Doctrinal loci in communicative key: Scripture, Trinity, Christ, church
Throughout his corpus, the loci of **dogmatics** are rewritten in communicative terms. For **Scripture**, inspiration is the **Spirit’s** superintendence of the authors’ **illocutions**, canon is a **covenant constitution**, and **inerrancy** is the fittingness of God’s speech to its ends. For the **Trinity**, God is the **communicative agent** whose one **will** enacts multiple **illocutions** as **Father**, **Son**, and **Spirit** without dividing the **simple** act of God *ad intra*. For **Christology**, **incarnation** is the climactic **speech‑act** wherein the **Logos** is God’s perlocutionary self‑giving for our salvation, calibrating the church’s **confession** as responsive speech. For **ecclesiology**, the church is the **company of the baptized** whose vocation is to **perform the script** by **Word, sacrament, and discipline**, cultivating a **holy habitus** that bears witness before the **powers**.

Ethics likewise takes a dramatic turn: improvisation is not creative license but **disciplined fittingness** that extends **apostolic pattern** into new cultural scenes. Everyday Theology (2007) models cultural exegesis as a mode of discerning **rival liturgies** and **counter‑scripts**, equipping congregations to inhabit the **gospel’s** dramaturgy rather than the **market’s** or **state’s**. In all of this, doctrines are **directive judgments** that safeguard the **identity** of the **triune God** and the **gospel**, enabling the church to live faithfully between the **ascension** and the **parousia**.

#### 8. Interlocutors, tensions, and disciplinary positioning
Vanhoozer’s work stands at the crossroads of **postliberal theology** and **evangelical dogmatics**, dialoguing with **Frei** and **Lindbeck** while resisting the reduction of doctrine to **community grammar**. He receives **Barth’s** insistence on **revelation’s primacy** and **Christocentrism**, yet argues for a clearer account of **canonical authorial intention** and the integrity of **Scripture** as **norming norm**. Alongside **Thiselton** and **Wolterstorff**, he appropriates **analytic philosophy of language** for **hermeneutics**, translating philosophical distinctions into ecclesial usefulness. In contest with **Derrida** and **Fish**, he articulates a chastened **critical realism**, insisting that the **Spirit** enables **understanding** without collapsing it into **immediacy**. Critics have pressed whether **speech‑act theory** overdetermines the ontology of **revelation** or insufficiently addresses **historical criticism’s** empirical claims; Vanhoozer typically responds by reframing the field’s rules in **canonical** rather than **positivist** terms.

A related tension arises in the use of **theatrical metaphors**. While the **theodrama** brilliantly renders the participatory character of doctrine, some fear that the **stage direction** idiom can deemphasize the ontic fixity of **dogma** or encourage **revisionist improvisation**. Vanhoozer’s repeated refrain of **improvisation under authority** aims to prevent such slippage: the performance must fit the **canonical script**, the **creedal rules**, and the **gospel plot**. His mature work on **remythologizing** further shows that the metaphors are tethered to **classical theism**, not deployed for conceptual innovation at the expense of **aseity** or **simplicity**.

#### 9. Contribution and consolidation: a mere Protestant program for our time
Across three decades, Vanhoozer has given evangelicals a **method** that is neither biblicist nor liberal, a **hermeneutic** that is neither foundationalist nor relativist, and a **dogmatics** that is catholic without surrendering **sola Scriptura**. His **canonical‑linguistic** approach protects **authorial meaning** and **canonical form** while encouraging the church’s **active obedience**; his **speech‑act** framework reframes **inspiration**, **illumination**, and **authority** in terms that integrate **exegesis**, **systematics**, and **pastoral ministry**. By retrieving **classical theism** via **remythologizing**, he keeps **simplicity**, **immutability**, and **impassibility** in play amid contemporary concerns for **relationality** and **divine action**. The cumulative result is a credible **mere Protestant** project: **Scripture** as **canon**, **doctrine** as **direction**, **church** as **company**, **pastors** as **public theologians**, all under the **triune Lord** whose **Word** does what it says.

In a fragmented academy and an anxious church, his call for **interpretive virtue**, **ecclesial accountability**, and **performative fidelity** provides a generative path forward. His corpus supplies tools for catechesis, preaching, cultural analysis, and doctrinal retrieval that resist both **technocratic pragmatism** and **ideological captivity**, aiming instead at the **wisdom** that corresponds to the **gospel**.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a classical **Reformed** and **Westminster Theological Seminary** vantage, Vanhoozer’s project wins strong commendation for its defense of **sola Scriptura**, its retrieval of **classical theism**, and its insistence that **doctrine directs practice**. His accounts of **dual authorship**, **concursus**, and **inerrancy as communicative perfection** cohere with **Warfield’s** Scripture principle, while his stress on **triune authorship** resonates with **Bavinck’s** organic inspiration. Yet several tensions warrant notice. First, the **speech‑act** idiom, though salutary, can appear to thin out **metaphysical density**; Reformed orthodoxy will press beyond **linguistic action** to God’s **actus purus**, reinforcing **divine simplicity** and **aseity** as the formal principles that guarantee God’s **immutability** and **impassibility** apart from any communicative economy *ad extra*. Second, the **theodramatic** metaphor risks being over‑productive in hands less disciplined than Vanhoozer’s; **improvisation under authority** must be explicitly bounded by **confessional standards** including **covenant of works and grace**, **federal headship**, **law‑gospel distinction**, and **forensic justification**, lest performance eclipse **propositional content**. Third, the **mere Protestant** proposal can, in practice, flatten **confessional particularity**; a Westminster path will commend **Reformed catholicity** in which **sola Scriptura** normatively yields not only general catholic substance but determinate **covenant theology**, **means of grace**, and **Sabbath** practice. Finally, on **divine passibility**, Vanhoozer rightly retrieves **impassibility** in communicative key, but a Reformed assessment will prefer the unambiguous grammar of **ad intra impassibilis, ad extra passibilis non est** while locating all talk of divine responsiveness in the register of **anthropopathism** and **covenantal condescension**. Even so, within these clarifications, Vanhoozer’s **canonical‑linguistic** program aligns deeply with **Reformed orthodoxy’s** principia of **revelation** and **covenant**, offering a fruitful vernacular for confessing **the triune God** and directing the church’s **pilgrim obedience**.
