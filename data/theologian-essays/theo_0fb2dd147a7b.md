### TITLE
Scripture as theopneustos and theology as a scientific exposition of revealed facts

### PREVIEW NOTES
- **Old Princeton Presbyterianism**, the **Gilded Age** and **Progressive Era** United States, and the **fundamentalist modernist controversy** form the horizon for **B. B. Warfield** 1851–1921, heir to **Charles Hodge** and guardian of **confessional Reformed orthodoxy** against **German higher criticism**.
- A **scientific inductive method** grounded in **Common Sense Realism**, rigorous **exegesis**, and a positive **evidentialist apologetics** that employs **natural theology** and the **theistic proofs** under **Sola Scriptura** guide Warfield's epistemic stance.
- Key works include **The Lord of Glory** 1907, **The Plan of Salvation** 1915, **Counterfeit Miracles** 1918, **Biblical Doctrines** 1929, **Perfectionism** 1931, **Calvin and Calvinism** 1931, **Revelation and Inspiration** 1927, and **The Inspiration and Authority of the Bible** 1948, within **The Works of Benjamin B. Warfield** 1927–1932.
- Central categories are **verbal plenary inspiration**, **theopneustos**, **concursus**, **autographic inerrancy**, **monergism**, **immediate imputation**, **cessationism**, **Augustinianism**, and the unity of **biblical and systematic theology**.
- Interlocutors and influences include **A. A. Hodge**, **Geerhardus Vos**, **Abraham Kuyper**, **Julius Wellhausen**, **Albrecht Ritschl**, **Adolf von Harnack**, **Charles A. Briggs**, and later heirs **J. Gresham Machen** and **Cornelius Van Til**.
- Reformed reception centers on **Old Princeton's** defense of **classical theism** and **inerrancy**, its legacy at **Westminster Theological Seminary**, and debates over **evidentialism** versus **presuppositionalism** within a shared **Reformed confessional** frame.

### ESSAY
#### 1. Formation, Princeton setting, and method
Born near Lexington, Kentucky in 1851, **Benjamin Breckinridge Warfield** matured at the high tide of **Old School Presbyterianism** and entered **Princeton Seminary** in 1873, absorbing **Charles Hodge's** vision of theology as a science of God. His early post at **Western Theological Seminary** in Allegheny placed him within an American ecclesial world confronting **German historicism**, the diffusion of **Darwinian science**, and the rise of **liberal Protestantism**. Succeeding **A. A. Hodge** at Princeton in 1887 as professor of **Didactic and Polemic Theology**, Warfield became the last great architect of the **Old Princeton** synthesis, uniting **confessional Reformed orthodoxy**, **Common Sense Realism**, and a disciplined **exegesis** that served dogmatics. The personal crucible of his wife's long invalidism constrained his travel and deepened his scholarly concentration, yet his classroom and pen radiated a confident **classical theism** and a polemically capable **Catholic-Reformed** catholicity.

Warfield's method reflects the **Princeton apologetic** ideal. He insisted that **Sola Scriptura** supplies the principium cognoscendi, yet he also argued that reason, operating under common grace, can recognize the marks of **revelation** and the credibility of **miracle** and **prophecy**. He affirmed **natural theology** as a legitimate preamble, defended **a posteriori theistic proofs**, and maintained that theology is an ordered account of the facts God has given. This is the Old Princeton grammar of science, where the **analogia fidei** directs interpretation and reason serves faith, but without collapsing into **fideism** or accepting the **Ritschlian** reduction of theology to value judgments. The result is a confident **evidentialism** coupled with a high view of **inspiration** and a thick **confessional** ecclesial location.

#### 2. Scripture as theopneustos, concursus, and autographic inerrancy
Warfield's most enduring contribution is his doctrine of **Scripture**, elaborated across essays later gathered in **Revelation and Inspiration** 1927 and **The Inspiration and Authority of the Bible** 1948, and framed programmatically in the joint 1881 article **Inspiration** with **A. A. Hodge**. Exegeting 2 Timothy 3.16, he fixed the term **theopneustos** not as a quality breathed into antecedent writings but as the divine act by which Scripture is breathed out by God. This led to the classic pairing of **verbal plenary inspiration** with a rich **concursus** theory. God is the primary author, employing human writers in an **organic** mode that elevates rather than suppresses their faculties, styles, and historical circumstances. The human is real and varied, yet all the words are truly the words of God.

From this ontology of Scripture Warfield argued the **inerrancy of the autographs**, or **autographic inerrancy**. Truthfulness attaches to what God caused to be written. He did not deny transmissional errancy in copies, which he addressed through robust **textual criticism**, nor did he fear modern philology. He held that the providential preservation of the text, the multiplicity of manuscripts, and the canons of **Westcott and Hort** type criticism bring us near the original. Because inspiration is verbal and plenary, inerrancy is a natural implication, a moral attribute of the God who speaks. The appeal is decisively theological, yet it is buttressed by philological study and historical argumentation. This is the nerve center of **Old Princeton**, where **Scripture's authority** is dogmatically secure and empirically defensible.

#### 3. Engagement with higher criticism and liberal theology
Warfield's defence of **inspiration** was forged in combat with **German higher criticism** and **liberal Protestantism**. He challenged **Julius Wellhausen's** reconstruction of Israel's religion and date of the Pentateuch through essays that paralleled **W. H. Green's** labors on the Old Testament while targeting the principles at stake, namely the denial of **supernatural revelation** and the imposition of an evolutionary religious a priori. He resisted **Albrecht Ritschl's** program that relegated doctrine to the realm of value judgments and moral community, and he refuted **Adolf von Harnack's** essence of Christianity as a simple fatherhood of God and brotherhood of man. In American Presbyterianism he confronted **Charles A. Briggs**, whose critique of inerrancy culminated in ecclesial trials, by showing that a confessional **Westminster doctrine of Scripture** implies plenary inspiration and that historical difficulties do not overturn the theological axiom that **God cannot lie**.

The polemics were paired with constructive labor. Warfield parsed the development of dogma historically while insisting on the constancy of **apostolic truth**. He sometimes deployed **German historical-critical tools** against rationalist conclusions, a method evident in his careful treatment of alleged contradictions, his defense of the **resurrection** as a real historical miracle, and his analysis of the canon as a providentially recognized collection of inspired writings. The result is a posture both critical and credal, a learned **orthodoxy** that receives **Scripture** as divine speech yet does not retreat from the academy.

#### 4. Christology, Trinity, and the defense of the supernatural
Warfield's **Christology** in **The Lord of Glory** 1907 assembles biblical data, linguistic analysis, and dogmatic clarity to affirm the full **deity of Christ**. He follows the classic **homoousios** confession by tracing how New Testament titles, divine prerogatives, and worship converge on the identity of the Son. He counters late nineteenth-century **kenotic Christologies** that posited a self-emptying involving a surrender of divine attributes. For Warfield, Philippians 2 narrates the assumption of the form of a servant without ontological diminishment. In **The Emotional Life of Our Lord** 1912 he limns the true humanity of Christ through an exegetical theology of the Savior's affections, demonstrating a finely grained account of **two-natures Christology** in which complete deity and complete humanity are united in one person.

His Trinitarian essays, notably **The Biblical Doctrine of the Trinity**, argue that the **Trinity** is progressively revealed and formally defined in the post-apostolic church, yet already structurally present in Scripture. His work on the **Holy Spirit** treats the Spirit as fully divine and personally active in revelation, regeneration, and sanctification. Against revivalist excess and sacramentalist claims, Warfield's **Counterfeit Miracles** 1918 defends **cessationism**, making a historical and biblical case that attesting signs clustered around epochs of revelation and ceased with the close of the apostolic age. He denies ongoing **revelatory gifts** while affirming the continued supernatural work of the Spirit in regeneration and providence. This coheres with his doctrine of **Sola Scriptura** and the sufficiency of Scripture for faith and life.

#### 5. Soteriology, Augustinianism, and imputation
In **The Plan of Salvation** 1915 Warfield maps the soteriological universe with the clarity of a Reformed polemicist. He distinguishes **monergism** and **synergism**, contrasts sacramental instrumentalism with evangelical trust in the Word, and claims that **Calvinism** is just **Augustinianism** at its purest, the full expression of **sola gratia**. He draws crisp lines between **Roman Catholicism**, **Lutheranism**, **Arminianism**, and **Reformed** soteriology, especially on election, effectual calling, and perseverance. His essay on **imputation**, often cited as **Imputation and the Guilt of Adam's First Sin**, defends **immediate imputation** within a federal framework. Adam's guilt is reckoned to his posterity by covenantal appointment, and corruption is vitally transmitted. The twofold gift of Christ, righteousness and life, answers to the twofold harm of Adam, guilt and corruption. The logic is juridical and vital, covenantal and realistic, grounded in Romans 5 and tethered to **federal headship**.

Warfield's commitment to **Augustinianism** extends to the doctrines of effectual grace and perseverance. He prizes **Calvin's autotheos** of the Son as an expression of robust deity and follows **Turretin** and **Hodge** in a scholastic Reformed retrieval. He values precision, evident in his attention to the ordo salutis and the distinctions among calling, regeneration, faith, and justification. Yet he refuses to let ordo become an abstraction, insisting that all saving benefits are united in the **Christus totus** applied by the Spirit.

#### 6. Sanctification, perfectionism, and the shape of piety
Warfield's long campaign against **Perfectionism**, culminating in the posthumous two-volume **Perfectionism** 1931, critiques **Wesleyan**, **Keswick**, and **Higher Life** schemes of two-stage sanctification. He argues that these models mistake the nature of sin, underestimate indwelling remaining corruption, and misconstrue faith as a second blessing that catapults the believer into a state beyond conflict. Warfield's positive doctrine centers on **definitive sanctification** in union with Christ and **progressive sanctification** through ordinary means, undergirded by the Spirit's continual work. He is an ecclesial theologian of piety, in the line of **Reformed experiential Calvinism**, yet he refuses the anti-intellectualism and subjectivism that often displace **Word and sacrament**.

His pastoral essays, including the famous address **The Religious Life of Theological Students** 1911, call for devotion that is intellectual and humble, rooted in prayer and rigorous study. The same concern is visible in his rejection of romanticized **mysticism** detached from doctrine. For Warfield, the spiritual life flourishes where the **doctrines of grace** are preached and believed, and where the church's ordered ministry dispenses the means of grace faithfully.

#### 7. Calvin, catholicity, and the unity of biblical and systematic theology
Warfield is a learned reader of **Calvin** and the **Reformed Orthodox**. The essays gathered as **Calvin and Calvinism** 1931 present Calvin as the theologian of the **majesty of God** in salvation, the best expression of the catholic Christian faith purified of medieval accretions. He champions **Reformed catholicity**, praising **Augustine** as the father of evangelical theology and arguing that the Reformation's innovations are evangelical rather than sectarian. His work on **the development of doctrine** aims to exhibit a principled continuity between apostolic truth and Reformed dogmatics.

His friendship with **Geerhardus Vos** models the Old Princeton synergy of **biblical theology** and **systematic theology**. He welcomed Vos to Princeton in 1893, and he cited Vos's redemptive-historical insights while insisting that the fruit of exegesis must be synthesized dogmatically. Warfield did not isolate the historical from the dogmatic. He used historical consciousness to read sources and critics, yet he kept the **rule of faith** and the **Westminster Standards** central to theological construction. The result is a catholic Reformed scholasticism attuned to modern challenges.

#### 8. Science, modernity, and apologetics
Warfield confronted **modern science** with a careful confidence. He refused the false dichotomy of faith and fact that dominated many debates. He affirmed **providence** as universally active, distinguished **creation** and **providence** in classic terms, and allowed that **evolutionary processes** at the level of secondary causality could be compatible with divine design, provided the doctrines of **creation ex nihilo**, **imago Dei**, and the **historical Adam** were maintained. His reading of **Darwin** is critical rather than dismissive, and he holds that scientific theories, when properly bounded, can be integrated within a theistic worldview.

His **apologetics** is decisively **evidentialist**, heir to **Aquinas** and **Reid** through **Hodge**. He defends **miracles** as signs, **prophecy** as confirmatory, and **the resurrection of Christ** as the linchpin event. He rejects **Kantian** reduction and **Ritschlian** value theory, contending that Christianity rests on public revelation and public events. The Holy Spirit's internal witness brings conviction, yet the faith is not irrational. Warfield thus stands as a bridge between an older **natural theology** and a robust **confessional Protestantism** that prizes the historicity and factuality of redemption.

#### 9. Legacy, institutional horizon, and works
Warfield's death in 1921 closed the Old Princeton era. The reorganization of Princeton in 1929 and the founding of **Westminster Theological Seminary** by **J. Gresham Machen** carried forward his devotion to **inerrancy**, **Reformed orthodoxy**, and learned polemics against modernism. His essays, collected in **The Works of Benjamin B. Warfield** 1927–1932, continued to shape twentieth-century debates. The **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy** 1978 reflects Warfieldian grammar, notably **autographic inerrancy**, **verbal plenary inspiration**, and the organic **concursus** of divine and human authorship. His **Counterfeit Miracles** seeded a standard Reformed **cessationism**, and his **Plan of Salvation** remains a paradigmatic map of soteriological options. The Warfield legacy is an ethos as much as a set of theses, a posture that combines historical erudition, exegetical acuity, and doctrinal firmness in service of the church.

Warfield's influence also includes the cultivation of a scholarly culture that honors the **fathers and the Reformed scholastics**, values companion disciplines like **philology** and **textual criticism**, and maintains that doctrinal clarity fuels pastoral power. His name now anchors debates about **Old Princeton** versus **Dutch neo-Calvinism**, about **evidentialism** versus **presuppositionalism**, and about the terms on which Reformed theology engages the modern world while staying deeply planted in its confessional roots.

#### 10. Reformed and WTS evaluation
From a **Reformed and Westminster Theological Seminary** angle, Warfield is a towering ally and a complex interlocutor. His strong **doctrine of Scripture**, with **verbal plenary inspiration**, **theopneustos**, and **autographic inerrancy**, remains exemplary, and his **concursus** model still frames the best accounts of the divine human relation in biblical authorship. His **Augustinian monergism**, defense of **federal headship** and **immediate imputation**, and critique of **perfectionism** are firmly aligned with **Westminster Standards**. Yet tensions appear at the level of method. His **evidentialist** posture, trust in **Common Sense Realism**, and appeal to **theistic proofs** risk the impression of a neutral rationality that **presuppositional** heirs at Westminster challenge in the name of the noetic effects of sin and the **antithesis** between belief and unbelief. A Van Tilian critique would insist that apologetics be explicitly **covenantal** and **revelationally presuppositional**, though it can appropriate Warfield's historical evidences as ministerial, not magisterial. On nature and grace, his qualified openness to **theistic evolution** invites scrutiny where it might loosen **special creation** and **historical Adam**. His robust **cessationism** is largely received, though some today press for a more redemptive-historical framing of gifts in light of **inaugurated eschatology**. The best Reformed path receives Warfield's doctrinal core and his exegetical rigor, recalibrates his epistemology within a fully self-consciously **revelational presuppositionalism**, and sustains his catholic scholarly habit while reaffirming the primacy of **covenant revelation** over any autonomous account of reason, history, or nature.

### RECAP NOTES
- **Old Princeton** theologian, 1851–1921, formed by **Charles Hodge** and contending with **German higher criticism** and the **modernist** challenge in an American Presbyterian setting.
- Core focus was the defense of **Scripture's authority** through **verbal plenary inspiration**, **theopneustos**, and **autographic inerrancy**, joined to a **scientific** theological method.
- Major works include **The Lord of Glory** 1907, **The Plan of Salvation** 1915, **Counterfeit Miracles** 1918, **Biblical Doctrines** 1929, **Perfectionism** 1931, **Calvin and Calvinism** 1931, **Revelation and Inspiration** 1927, and **The Inspiration and Authority of the Bible** 1948.
- Defining doctrines and tools were **concursus**, **immediate imputation**, **cessationism**, **Augustinian monergism**, and the unity of **biblical and systematic theology** under **Sola Scriptura**.
- Key interlocutors included **Wellhausen**, **Ritschl**, **Harnack**, and **Charles A. Briggs**, with allies **A. A. Hodge**, **Geerhardus Vos**, and later **Machen** and **Van Til**.
- Reformed critique affirms his **inerrancy** and **monergism**, but recalibrates his **evidentialism** with **presuppositional** insights and tests his limited openness to **theistic evolution**.
- Legacy endures in the **Chicago Statement on Biblical Inerrancy**, in standard **cessationist** arguments, and in the scholarly ethos of **Old Princeton** carried forward by **Westminster Theological Seminary**.
