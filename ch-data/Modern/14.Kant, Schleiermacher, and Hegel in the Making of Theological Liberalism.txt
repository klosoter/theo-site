### TITLE
Kant, Schleiermacher, and Hegel in the Making of Theological Liberalism

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Late eighteenth to early twentieth century, German Protestant thought in the wake of the Enlightenment and Romanticism
- Aim: explain how Kant, Schleiermacher, and Hegel supplied foundations and frameworks for theological liberalism
- Immanuel Kant, critical philosopher of reason and moral faith
- Friedrich Schleiermacher, theologian of piety, church, and experience

### ESSAY
When students first meet the idea of theological liberalism, the question arises at once. How did Christian theology come to speak the language of modern freedom, historical method, and ethical culture without surrendering its gospel? The answer takes shape around three figures whose dates bracket the turn of the nineteenth century. Kant, 1724 to 1804, set critical limits and a moral ground. Schleiermacher, 1768 to 1834, rebuilt theology around lived piety and the church. Hegel, 1770 to 1831, cast religion within a sweeping philosophy of spirit and history. Together they supplied the grammar, the affective center, and the historical logic that made theological liberalism conceivable and persuasive.

The context mattered. The Enlightenment had already unsettled orthodoxy by the 1750s through historical criticism, natural philosophy, and political upheaval. The French Revolution after 1789 brought questions of autonomy and authority to the parish door. German Pietism preserved religion as heartfelt devotion, while Romanticism dignified experience, art, and community. Prussian reforms founded the University of Berlin in 1810 and consolidated a learned clergy. In this ferment the older Protestant scholasticism, with its confident metaphysical proofs and fixed systems, struggled to persuade. Theological liberalism did not begin as unbelief. It began as the attempt to make Christian truth intelligible under new conditions of knowledge, culture, and historical awareness.

Kant provided the enabling threshold. In the Critique of Pure Reason, 1781, he argued that theoretical reason cannot know the noumenal, thus cannot prove God or immortality by speculative metaphysics. He refused to hand religion over to skepticism. In the Critique of Practical Reason, 1788, he located God, freedom, and immortality as postulates of practical reason, necessary to the moral life. In Religion within the Limits of Reason Alone, 1793, he reframed Christianity around the moral law, radical evil, and the need for an ethical commonwealth. Scripture became a vehicle of moral truth rather than a set of metaphysical propositions. Revelation remained possible, but it must accord with the highest demands of autonomous reason. For later liberals, two Kantian moves were decisive. First, the delimitation of knowledge, which underwrote the shift from dogmatic speculation to critical method. Second, the elevation of ethics, which made the kingdom of God a moral task within history rather than chiefly a metaphysical reality beyond it.

Schleiermacher stepped into this cleared terrain and gave churchly form to a post critical faith. His On Religion, Speeches to Its Cultured Despisers, 1799, sought reconciliation between Christianity and the educated elite of Berlin. He insisted that religion is neither metaphysics nor morality, but a distinct province of life grounded in intuition and feeling. In The Christian Faith, 1821 to 1822, he articulated the famous definition of piety as the feeling of absolute dependence, a pre reflective awareness of our existence as grounded in God. This was not mere emotion. It was a basal consciousness that could be analyzed, disciplined, and formed in the community. Doctrines, for Schleiermacher, were accounts of the modifications of this God consciousness in the life of the church. Thus Christology turns on the Redeemer as the one in whom God consciousness was original and victorious, whose influence generates a new corporate life. Sin is the universal obstruction of this consciousness, redemption is its restoration through participation in Christ. The Trinity and providence, sacraments and Scripture, are recast as articulations of the churchs experience of dependence and grace.

Schleiermacher was a pastor and a professor at Berlin from 1810, and his hermeneutical lectures educated a generation in historical and philological reading. He treated the Bible as a classic record of the original Christian piety, to be interpreted with the same rigor as other texts, yet within the living horizon of the church. He did not abandon doctrine. He re grounded it. The result was a liberal dogmatics that held together modern criticism and ecclesial life. The centrality of experience in community, the prioritizing of redemption over metaphysics, and the methodological role of historical interpretation, these became hallmarks of theological liberalism.

Hegel then gave the movement a speculative and historical backbone. In the Phenomenology of Spirit, 1807, and the Science of Logic, 1812 to 1816, he advanced a dialectical account of reason unfolding toward self knowledge. In the Lectures on the Philosophy of Religion, delivered from 1821 to 1831, he treated the history of religions as stages in the self manifestation of absolute spirit. Religion expresses truth in picture thinking, while philosophy states the same truth conceptually. Christianity is for Hegel the absolute religion because in it God is known as self giving and reconciled with finitude, epitomized in the incarnation and the cross. Reconciliation is realized in ethical life, in institutions of freedom and law. Hence history is not a detour from revelation. History is the medium of revelation.

This Hegelian vision energized a new kind of theological history. The Tubingen School under F. C. Baur, 1792 to 1860, applied dialectic to New Testament and early church history, seeing conflicts of Jewish Christianity and Gentile Christianity resolved in Catholic consolidation. David Strauss, 1808 to 1874, trained in Tubingen and influenced by Hegel, published Life of Jesus in 1835. He argued that the gospel narratives are mythic expressions of communal faith rather than direct records of facts, a thesis that scandalized the public and tested the liberal wager. If Christianity is true in its idea, Strauss asked, does it need miraculous events as traditionally conceived? Even those who rejected his conclusions adopted his historical and literary methods.

By mid century a Kantian correction tempered Hegelian excess. Albrecht Ritschl, 1822 to 1889, retrieved Kant to ground theology in value judgments rather than speculative theses. In The Christian Doctrine of Justification and Reconciliation, 1870 to 1874, he made the kingdom of God, the ethical community of love under God, the center of doctrine. He insisted that knowledge of God is practical and historical, bound to the revelation of Jesus and the communal life that springs from it. Adolf von Harnack, 1851 to 1930, translated this program into historical work and public theology. His What Is Christianity, 1900, defined the gospel as the fatherhood of God, the infinite value of the human soul, and the higher righteousness, an agenda that marked liberal Protestant preaching well into the early twentieth century.

The orbit of Kant, Schleiermacher, and Hegel thus defined three axes of liberal theology. Kant gave the critical boundaries and the moral heart. Schleiermacher endowed the church with a language of experience that could live with criticism. Hegel granted confidence that history discloses Gods truth in progressive form. Together they re located authority. Scripture became witness, not foundation. Doctrine became the churchs evolving articulation of experienced redemption. Revelation became Gods self communication in and through historical life. Christology shifted from metaphysical natures to the concrete person who mediates God consciousness and reconciliation. Ecclesiology moved from juridical forms to ethical community.

Critics arose quickly. The Old Lutherans in the 1830s warned that Schleiermacher reduced dogma to psychology. Kierkegaard in the 1840s attacked Hegelian system as erasing the offense of the gospel. Catholic authorities condemned Modernism in 1907, worried that historical method dissolved dogma. The sharpest Protestant rebuke came with Karl Barth, 1886 to 1968. His Epistle to the Romans, 1919, thundered that liberalism had turned God into a projection of culture and ethics. Yet even Barth worked with a Kantian sense of limit and a Schleiermacherian attention to the text and to experience, transformed by a doctrine of revelation. In the later twentieth century, Bultmanns demythologizing, Tillichs method of correlation, and liberation theologies adopted and contested the same inheritance, applying critical reason, existential experience, and historical critique to new questions of meaning and justice.

The place of Kant, Schleiermacher, and Hegel in the development of theological liberalism is therefore foundational but differentiated. Kant secured a disciplined space for faith after the collapse of speculative proof. Schleiermacher reconstructed the house of theology from the hearth of redeemed experience in the church. Hegel supplied the confidence that the history of spirit, including the history of doctrine, is not alien to God but the very arena of divine self manifestation. Liberal theology, in its best moments, is the conversation their three projects made possible.
