### TITLE
Schleiermacher, Ritschl, and Loisy as Architects of Theological Modernism

### PREVIEW NOTES
- Nineteenth to early twentieth century, German Protestant academies and French Catholic exegesis confronting Rome
- Aim, explain how key thinkers enabled theological modernism by reframing sources, methods, and authority
- Friedrich Schleiermacher, architect of modern Protestant theology
- Albrecht Ritschl, ethical and historical system builder
- Alfred Loisy, Catholic biblical critic and modernist catalyst
- Historical criticism, the past as a dynamic process of meaning
- Immanence, divine action discerned within human consciousness and history
- Authority and tradition, reinterpreted as historically mediated and developing
- Value judgments, normative assessments grounded in communal moral experience
- On Religion 1799 and The Christian Faith 1821 to 1822, programmatic turn to experience
- Ritschl, Justification and Reconciliation 1870 to 1874, ethics and the kingdom of God
- Loisy, The Gospel and the Church 1902, sparks Catholic crisis culminating in Pascendi 1907
- Wider context, research universities, German historicism, laicization in France, scientific philology
- Legacy cue, from condemnations and crises to lasting changes in exegesis and twentieth century renewal

### ESSAY
Theological modernism arose where faith met the modern critical conscience, and where older patterns of authority collided with the new prestige of history and experience. The question was never simply whether one could believe. The sharper question was how to believe after the historical disciplines, the sciences, and modern notions of subjectivity had altered the very way Western culture asked and answered questions. Three figures, Friedrich Schleiermacher in the early nineteenth century, Albrecht Ritschl later in that century, and Alfred Loisy at the turn of the twentieth, helped to make that question unavoidable. Each reconfigured theology's sources and aims, and in doing so each facilitated the rise of modernism.

The context was primed by the Enlightenment and by German historicism. Gotthold Lessing, in the 1770s, lamented the ugly ditch between contingent historical events and necessary truths of reason. Immanuel Kant's critical project, 1781 to 1790, constrained claims about God to the limits of practical reason and fostered a turn to moral autonomy. Meanwhile the modern university, reshaped in Berlin in 1810, elevated research, philology, and source criticism. In France, the Revolution and later the Third Republic, 1870 to 1940, pressed laicization, while within Catholicism the centralization of teaching authority at Vatican I, 1869 to 1870, sharpened tensions over scripture, tradition, and magisterium. Into this world stepped Schleiermacher, Ritschl, and Loisy, each fluent in the idiom of history and each eager to articulate Christianity intelligibly within it.

Friedrich Schleiermacher, 1768 to 1834, framed a new starting point. In On Religion, 1799, he spoke to cultured despisers, proposing that religion is not metaphysics and not ethics, but immediate consciousness of the Infinite. He later gave this intuition systematic shape in The Christian Faith, 1821 to 1822. There he defined piety as the feeling of absolute dependence, a primal awareness that our finite agency is grounded in a higher causality. Doctrine, on this account, is a second order articulation of the states of Christian consciousness that arise within the community founded by Jesus. Christ is not primarily the bearer of speculative natures but the Redeemer whose God consciousness generates a new corporate life of faith and love. This reorientation did three things that proved decisive for modernism. It relocated the norm of theology from external authority to the correlated discernment of experience and scripture as read within the community. It rendered historical criticism not an enemy but an ally, since the historical Jesus and the apostolic witness become the original matrix of Christian experience. And it licensed the concept of doctrinal development, not as arbitrary change, but as the disciplined unfolding of the community's God consciousness under ever new cultural conditions. Schleiermacher did not deny dogma. He reconceived it as descriptive of life. The result was a theology capable of engaging philology, criticism, and philosophy without surrendering its center.

Albrecht Ritschl, 1822 to 1889, carried that program forward with a stricter historical and ethical accent. Educated within the German research tradition, Ritschl worked up a full scale system in Justification and Reconciliation, three volumes published from 1870 to 1874. He appropriated Kant's distinction of theoretical and practical reason in order to move theological truth claims out of the realm of speculative essence into the realm of value judgments. The essence of Christianity, he argued, is the realization of the kingdom of God, a moral community of love and righteousness established by Jesus. Justification and reconciliation are not metaphysical changes in divine substance but judgments that grasp the believer's standing in a community normed by the will of God disclosed in Christ. To secure this account, Ritschl insisted on a strict historical method, distinguishing the historical Jesus from later dogmatic accretions and reading New Testament texts within the concrete conflicts of the early church. His school at Gottingen formed Adolf von Harnack, whose What Is Christianity lectures, 1899 to 1900, disseminated the Ritschlian vision to a wider European audience. Theology thus became a positive science of historical Christian facts assessed for their ethical value. This was modernism's lifeblood on the Protestant side. It grounded authority in historical research and communal praxis, elevated ethics over metaphysics, and cut a path for Protestant engagement with culture, education, and the modern state.

Across the confessional divide, Alfred Loisy, 1857 to 1940, pressed similar questions within Roman Catholicism through the medium of biblical criticism. A gifted exegete trained in Semitic philology, Loisy wrote La Religion d Israel in 1901 and then The Gospel and the Church in 1902, the latter a direct response to Harnack. If Harnack reduced Christianity to the fatherhood of God and the brotherhood of man, Loisy insisted that the preaching of the kingdom necessarily gave rise to a church with sacrament and structure. He formulated the famous aphorism, Jesus announced the kingdom, and what came was the church. In this claim lay a powerful historical principle. Revelation is not a set of timeless propositions dropped into the world. It is God's self communication mediated through a people and their institutions as these evolve across time. Doctrines develop as the community wrestles with scripture and experience, translating the apostolic kerygma into new linguistic and social forms. Loisy's exegesis, in works on the synoptic Gospels published in 1907 and 1908, deployed the full arsenal of critical method, source criticism, literary analysis, and attention to redaction, and thereby challenged harmonizing readings that had buttressed a static view of tradition.

The turning point came swiftly. In 1907 the Holy Office issued Lamentabili, a syllabus of condemned modernist propositions, followed the same year by Pius X's encyclical Pascendi dominici gregis. These documents denounced immanentism, historicism, and the tendency to reduce dogma to symbols of religious experience. Loisy was excommunicated in 1908. An oath against modernism followed in 1910. For Catholicism, the modernist crisis institutionalized suspicion toward historical criticism for a generation, yet it could not stop the slow diffusion of critical methods in seminaries and universities. On the Protestant side, the outbreak of World War I in 1914 exposed the optimistic cultural synthesis of Ritschl and Harnack to devastating critique, and Karl Barth's commentary on Romans in 1919 signaled a reassertion of divine transcendence. Yet even Barth worked within a world in which historical criticism and the university model of research had become permanent fixtures.

What, then, did Schleiermacher, Ritschl, and Loisy contribute to the rise of theological modernism. First, they reframed the locus of authority. Schleiermacher placed it within the regenerated consciousness of the community as it attends to scripture. Ritschl located it in the ethical telos of the kingdom, discerned through historical study and value judgment. Loisy relocated it in the historically developing life of the church that receives and interprets revelation across time. Second, they normalized method. Historical criticism moved from the margins to the center of theological work. Third, they recalibrated doctrine. Dogma now functioned as conceptualized description of life with God, as ethical norm for community, and as historically evolved symbol of revealed truth. Finally, they made room for development. The static picture gave way to a dynamic one in which church, scripture, and theology grow while remaining accountable to apostolic origins.

Reception was mixed and consequential. Modernist methods seeded the field of biblical studies across confessions. Catholic resistance was sharp in 1907, but Pius XII's encouragement of biblical studies in 1943 and the Second Vatican Council's document on revelation in 1965 marked a qualified reception of insights that Loisy had pressed prematurely and polemically. Protestant liberalism lost its cultural confidence after 1914, yet it bequeathed a pastoral and ethical attentiveness that shaped social Christianity, ecumenism, and public theology. The longer legacy is methodological. No serious theological work now proceeds without historical consciousness, without attention to communal experience and practice, and without awareness that doctrine lives through time. In that sense, the modernist wager succeeded. Theology learned to speak in a historical key and thus to stay in conversation with modern culture, even as later thinkers rebalanced immanence with transcendence and history with revelation.

The rise of theological modernism, whatever its flaws, was made possible by a few bold reconfigurations of source, method, and aim, and by the courage to let faith face the facts of history.
