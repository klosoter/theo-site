### TITLE
The Synod of Dort and the Shape of Reformed Orthodoxy

### ESSAY
A national church at war with itself is a perilous sight. In the Dutch Republic, between 1609 and 1621, the Twelve Years Truce with Spain gave the young state a pause from external conflict, only to reveal internal fracture. Ministers and magistrates lined up on opposing sides of a doctrinal and political struggle. Out of that struggle came the Synod of Dort, convened in 1618 to 1619 in Dordrecht, whose Canons became a defining charter of Reformed orthodoxy and a lasting answer to the sustained challenge of the Remonstrant movement.

The roots of the synod lie in the rise of Jacobus Arminius, 1560 to 1609, professor of theology at Leiden. Wrestling with the exegesis of Romans 7 and 9, and facing pastoral questions about assurance and blame, Arminius questioned what many in the Dutch churches regarded as settled doctrine. He emphasized conditional election based on foreseen faith, a universal intent in the atonement, resistible grace, and the need for further inquiry on perseverance. Franciscus Gomarus, 1563 to 1641, his colleague, pressed the traditional Reformed reading of unconditional election and efficacious grace. Their disputations in 1603 to 1609 foreshadowed a wider rift.

After Arminius died in 1609, his followers articulated their program in the Remonstrance of 1610, five articles that called for a moderated predestinarianism, softened views of depravity and grace, and a more inclusive church. Jan Uytenbogaert, court preacher at The Hague, helped lead the movement. Festus Hommius and other opponents responded with the Counter Remonstrance of 1611, defending what they saw as the settled confession of the churches. A conference at The Hague in 1611 failed to reconcile the parties. By then the controversy had entangled the highest levels of Dutch politics.

The States of Holland, guided by Johan van Oldenbarnevelt, preferred tolerance of Remonstrant teaching and asserted stronger magistrate control of ecclesiastical appointments. Many city councils placed Remonstrant ministers in contested pulpits. The Counter Remonstrants, backed increasingly by Stadtholder Maurice of Nassau, demanded a national synod to adjudicate doctrine and to secure a unified church order. The wider European context mattered. The Reformed churches in England, Scotland, the Palatinate, Geneva, and the Swiss cities watched closely, since Dutch disarray imperiled a key Protestant ally in the struggle against Habsburg power.

Maurice made his move in 1618, arresting Oldenbarnevelt in August and clearing the way for a national synod authorized by the States General. The Synod of Dort opened on 13 November 1618 under the presidency of Johan Bogerman, a Frisian minister with a firm hand and a legal mind. The Dutch provincial synods sent ministers and elders. Professors from Leiden, Franeker, and Groningen joined. Most notable was the international delegation, which gave the synod weight far beyond the Netherlands. England sent George Carleton, John Davenant, Samuel Ward, and Thomas Goade. Scotland sent Walter Balcanquhal. Bremen sent Matthias Martinius, a moderate voice. The Palatinate sent Abraham Scultetus and Heinrich Alting. Geneva sent Giovanni Diodati. Zurich, Bern, and Basel sent representatives. The Reformed world had gathered.

The Remonstrants, led by Simon Episcopius, entered the synod with confidence, asking for a free colloquy and scripture based argument. The synod insisted on a judicial procedure, written theses, and answers to specific points. The clash of procedure revealed the deeper conflict. Episcopius sought to shift the burden of proof and to reopen the confessional settlement. Bogerman pressed the status of existing confessions, the Belgic Confession of 1561 and the Heidelberg Catechism of 1563, as the doctrinal baseline. After weeks of wrangling, the synod dismissed the Remonstrants on 14 January 1619, judging them contumacious. Bogerman's dismissal was memorable and harsh, but it cleared the floor for the assembly to draft its determinations.

From January to April 1619 the synod composed the Canons of Dort. They did not aim to be a full dogmatic system. Rather, they set sustained answers to five contested themes, arranged in four heads of doctrine. First came divine election and reprobation. The canons taught that God, from eternity, chose a definite multitude to salvation in Christ, out of the same fallen mass. The ground of election was the good pleasure of God, not foreseen faith, merit, or human decision. Reprobation was taught soberly and asymmetrically, as a decree to leave some in their misery and to judge them for their sins, never as a mirror opposite to election. This head also spoke pastorally of the assurance of election and warned against prying into secret things.

The second head dealt with the death of Christ and human redemption by it. Here the synod walked carefully. Christ's death was of infinite worth and sufficient to atone for the sins of the whole world. Yet by divine intention, it was efficacious only for the elect, for whom the Father gave the Son. The synod also affirmed the promiscuous, that is, universal, preaching of the gospel and the well meant offer. This combination held together a broad summons with a particular design.

The third and fourth heads were combined, treating human corruption and the conversion of man. The synod asserted total depravity, not as the worst possible wickedness, but as a pervasive inability to turn oneself to God. The preaching of the gospel is a means of grace, but the saving change is the work of the Spirit who effectually calls and regenerates. Grace does not violate or coerce the will. Rather, by the Spirit's power, the will is made new and willingly comes to Christ. The canons rejected the view that grace is a moral persuasion that can be set aside by autonomous choice.

The fifth head taught the perseverance of the saints. Those whom God has called and justified he will also glorify. Believers may fall grievously for a time, and the canons name David and Peter as biblical examples. Yet God preserves them through word, sacrament, discipline, and prayer, restoring them to repentance. The synod spoke richly about assurance, locating it in the promises of God, the inward witness of the Spirit, and the observable fruits of faith, while warning against carnal security.

Alongside these heads, the synod issued Rejections of Errors, targeted refutations of specific Remonstrant theses. It also attended to church governance, passing a Church Order in 1619 that addressed consistorial oversight, catechism preaching, and the regulation of ministers. It urged a new translation of the Bible into Dutch from the original tongues. The States General later financed the Statenvertaling, completed in 1637, whose cadences shaped Dutch piety and preaching for generations.

The outcomes were both doctrinal and political. The synod deposed Remonstrant ministers and barred them from pulpits. Oldenbarnevelt was executed in May 1619. Hugo Grotius was condemned to life imprisonment, after which he famously escaped in 1621. The Remonstrants organized as a separate communion, the Remonstrant Brotherhood, and produced a revised confession in 1621 under Episcopius. Within the Reformed world, Dort became a benchmark. Its careful formulations allowed diversity on the order of the decrees and on the manner of the gospel offer, yet drew a clear line against conditional election and resistible grace.

The theological significance of the Canons of Dort lies in their balance of precision and pastoral tone. They consolidate a doctrine of unconditional grace that is nevertheless tied to the means of grace and to universal proclamation. They distinguish sharply between sufficiency and efficiency in the atonement, a distinction older than Dort, now given confessional shape. They insist that predestination does not negate responsibility or the call to holiness, and they offer comfort concerning covenant infants, telling godly parents not to doubt the election and salvation of their children whom God takes in infancy.

Reception varied. English delegates such as Davenant later wrote on hypothetical universalism, seeking to honor Dort while elaborating a broader view of the intent of the atonement. Swiss and German churches largely embraced the canons. In the Netherlands, political winds shifted after 1625, and limited toleration returned, though the Canons remained a confessional standard alongside the Belgic Confession and Heidelberg Catechism. In later centuries, Dort's doctrine was compressed into the TULIP acronym, a modern mnemonic that captures the five themes but not the nuance of the canons themselves.

The Synod of Dort was thus a turning point for the Reformation's second generation, linking doctrine, discipline, and translation, stabilizing a church under pressure, and articulating a theology of grace that still orders Reformed confession.
